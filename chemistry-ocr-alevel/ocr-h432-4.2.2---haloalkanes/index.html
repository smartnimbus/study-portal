Haloalkanes are organic compounds containing a polar C-X bond, making the δ⁺ carbon susceptible to nucleophilic attack, particularly hydrolysis to form alcohols. 🧠 Conceptual Understanding

Haloalkanes, also known as alkyl halides, are organic compounds containing at least one halogen atom (F, Cl, Br, I) covalently bonded to an alkyl group. They are essentially alkanes where one or more hydrogen atoms have been replaced by halogen atoms. These compounds are important intermediates in organic synthesis due to the polarity of the carbon-halogen bond. The halogen atom is more electronegative than carbon, creating a δ⁺ carbon atom that is susceptible to attack by electron-rich species, known as nucleophiles. This reaction type is called nucleophilic substitution, where the halogen 'leaving group' is replaced by the nucleophile. Understanding the mechanism and the factors affecting the rate of this substitution, particularly the strength of the carbon-halogen bond, is crucial for predicting their chemical behaviour and applications.

📌 Core Theory

  • Haloalkanes are saturated organic compounds containing a carbon-halogen (C-X) bond, where X is F, Cl, Br, or I.
  • The C-X bond is polar because halogens are more electronegative than carbon, making the carbon atom partially positive (δ⁺) and the halogen atom partially negative (δ⁻).
  • This δ⁺ carbon is an electrophilic centre, attracting nucleophiles (electron pair donors).
  • A nucleophile is an electron pair donor, typically an ion or molecule with a lone pair of electrons (e.g., OH⁻, H₂O, NH₃).
  • Hydrolysis is a type of nucleophilic substitution where the halogen atom is replaced by a hydroxyl (OH) group, forming an alcohol.
  • The rate of hydrolysis of haloalkanes depends on the strength of the carbon-halogen bond; weaker bonds hydrolyse faster.
  • The trend in C-X bond enthalpy is C-F > C-Cl > C-Br > C-I, meaning the C-I bond is the weakest and hydrolyses the fastest.
  • The mechanism for the hydrolysis of primary haloalkanes with aqueous alkali is nucleophilic substitution, typically an Sɴ2 mechanism (bimolecular nucleophilic substitution).
  • Experimental comparison of hydrolysis rates can be done using aqueous silver nitrate in ethanol, observing the rate of silver halide precipitate formation.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

Haloalkanes undergo nucleophilic substitution reactions because the electronegativity difference between carbon and the halogen creates a partial positive charge on the carbon atom, making it an ideal target for nucleophiles. A nucleophile, such as the hydroxide ion (OH⁻) from aqueous alkali, donates its lone pair of electrons to the δ⁺ carbon atom. Simultaneously, the carbon-halogen bond breaks heterolytically, with the halogen atom taking both electrons from the bond to form a halide ion (X⁻), which acts as the leaving group. For primary haloalkanes, this often occurs via a concerted Sɴ2 mechanism, where the nucleophile attacks from the opposite side to the leaving group, forming a transition state where both the incoming nucleophile and the leaving group are partially bonded to the carbon. The rate of hydrolysis is determined by the ease with which the C-X bond breaks, which directly correlates with its bond enthalpy. As we go down Group 17, the atomic radius of the halogen increases, leading to longer and weaker C-X bonds (C-F being the strongest, C-I the weakest). Consequently, iodoalkanes hydrolyse fastest, followed by bromoalkanes, chloroalkanes, and finally fluoroalkanes which are very unreactive under these conditions.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Describing the nucleophilic substitution mechanism of bromoethane hydrolysis

Problem: Describe the mechanism of the reaction between bromoethane and aqueous sodium hydroxide, showing all relevant curly arrows, lone pairs, and partial charges.

Steps: 1. Draw the bromoethane molecule, showing the δ⁺ carbon and δ⁻ bromine, and all hydrogen atoms. 2. Draw the hydroxide ion (OH⁻) with its negative charge and a lone pair on the oxygen atom. 3. Draw a curly arrow from the lone pair on the hydroxide ion's oxygen atom to the δ⁺ carbon atom of bromoethane. 4. Draw a second curly arrow from the C-Br bond to the bromine atom, indicating the breaking of the bond and the departure of the bromide ion. 5. Show the transition state (optional but good for higher marks) where OH is partially bonded and Br is partially bonded, with square brackets and a δ⁻ charge. 6. Draw the products: ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH) and a bromide ion (Br⁻).

Answer: The hydroxide ion (OH⁻), acting as a nucleophile, attacks the partially positive carbon atom of bromoethane. A lone pair from the oxygen atom of OH⁻ forms a new covalent bond with this carbon. Simultaneously, the C-Br bond breaks, and the bromine atom leaves as a bromide ion (Br⁻). This results in the formation of ethanol and bromide ions.

Teacher's Tip

Always ensure your curly arrows start from a lone pair or a bond and point towards a positively charged atom or where a new bond is forming. Show partial charges (δ⁺, δ⁻) on the C-X bond and the full charge on the nucleophile and leaving group.

2. Explaining the trend in hydrolysis rates of primary haloalkanes

Problem: Explain why iodoethane hydrolyses faster than chloroethane when reacted with aqueous silver nitrate in ethanol.

Steps: 1. Identify the compounds: iodoethane (CH₃CH₂I) and chloroethane (CH₃CH₂Cl). 2. Recall the C-X bond present in each: C-I and C-Cl. 3. Compare the bond enthalpies of C-I and C-Cl. State that the C-I bond is weaker than the C-Cl bond. 4. Explain why the C-I bond is weaker: Iodine is a larger atom than chlorine, leading to a longer bond length and less effective overlap between the carbon and iodine orbitals, making the bond easier to break. 5. Relate bond strength to reaction rate: A weaker bond requires less energy to break, thus it breaks more readily. 6. Conclude that because the C-I bond breaks more easily, iodoethane undergoes nucleophilic substitution (hydrolysis) at a faster rate than chloroethane.

Answer: Iodoethane hydrolyses faster than chloroethane because the carbon-iodine (C-I) bond is significantly weaker than the carbon-chlorine (C-Cl) bond. Iodine atoms are larger than chlorine atoms, resulting in a longer bond length and less effective orbital overlap with the carbon atom, which reduces the bond enthalpy. Since less energy is required to break the C-I bond, it breaks more easily during the nucleophilic substitution reaction, leading to a faster rate of hydrolysis.

Teacher's Tip

When comparing rates based on bond enthalpy, always explicitly state which bond is stronger/weaker, relate it to atomic size/bond length, and then link it directly to the energy required to break the bond and thus the reaction rate.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Refrigerants and Air Conditioning: Historically, chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) were widely used due to their stability and non-toxicity, but their role in ozone depletion led to their phasing out. Hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs) are now common replacements.
  • Solvents: Dichloromethane (CH₂Cl₂) and tetrachloromethane (CCl₄) are effective solvents for organic compounds, used in dry cleaning, paint removers, and pharmaceutical manufacturing.
  • Anaesthetics: Halogenated ethers like halothane (a bromo-chloro-fluoroethane) were once widely used as general anaesthetics in medicine.
  • Pesticides: Organochlorine compounds, a class of haloalkanes, were historically used as pesticides (e.g., DDT), though many are now banned due to their environmental persistence and toxicity.
  • Fire Extinguishers: Halons, containing bromine and fluorine, were used in some fire suppression systems due to their ability to inhibit combustion, but their use is now restricted due to environmental concerns.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing nucleophiles with electrophiles: Nucleophiles are electron-rich, electrophiles are electron-deficient.
  • ❌ Incorrect curly arrow placement: Arrows must start from an electron source (lone pair or bond) and point to an electron-deficient atom or where a new bond is forming.
  • ❌ Not showing lone pairs on nucleophiles or intermediate species where relevant.
  • ❌ Assuming all haloalkanes hydrolyse at the same rate; failing to link rate to C-X bond enthalpy.
  • ❌ Incorrectly identifying the leaving group; it's always the halide ion, not the halogen atom.
  • ❌ Believing that the C-F bond is the strongest because fluorine is the most reactive halogen overall. Its bond with carbon is strongest, making fluoroalkanes least reactive to hydrolysis.
Memory Anchor

Think of a nucleophile as a 'love-at-first-site' electron donor, seeking out the partially positive carbon 'partner' in the haloalkane. The halogen is the 'ex-partner' that gets kicked out.

✏️ Answer: When bromoethane reacts with an excess of aqueous ammonia, a mixture of products is formed due to successive nucleophilic substitution reactions. A lone pair from the nitrogen attacks the δ⁺ carbon, displacing the bromide ion (Br⁻), to form an ethylammonium ion (CH₃CH₂NH₃⁺). This ion is then deprotonated by another molecule of ammonia to yield ethylamine (CH₃CH₂NH₂), a primary amine.

However, ethylamine itself is also a nucleophile, as the nitrogen still possesses a lone pair of electrons. If there is unreacted bromoethane present, this newly formed ethylamine can act as a nucleophile and attack another bromoethane molecule. This second nucleophilic substitution forms a diethylammonium ion [(CH₃CH₂)₂NH₂⁺], which can then be deprotonated by ammonia to produce diethylamine [(CH₃CH₂)₂NH], a secondary amine. This process can continue, with diethylamine attacking bromoethane to form a tertiary amine (triethylamine) and even a quaternary ammonium salt, leading to a complex mixture of primary, secondary, tertiary amines, and ammonium salt.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Initial nucleophilic substitution: NH₃ attacks bromoethane, Br⁻ leaves, forming ethylammonium ion (CH₃CH₂NH₃⁺).
  • ✅ Deprotonation: Another NH₃ molecule deprotonates CH₃CH₂NH₃⁺ to form ethylamine (CH₃CH₂NH₂).
  • ✅ Secondary attack: Ethylamine (which is also a nucleophile) can then attack another molecule of bromoethane.
  • ✅ Br⁻ leaves, forming a diethylammonium ion [(CH₃CH₂)₂NH₂⁺].
  • ✅ Further deprotonation: Another NH₃ molecule deprotonates [(CH₃CH₂)₂NH₂⁺] to form diethylamine [(CH₃CH₂)₂NH], a secondary amine.
  • ✅ Mention that this process can continue to form tertiary amine and quaternary ammonium salt, leading to a mixture.

Q7. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

A student carries out the hydrolysis experiment using 1-bromobutane and notices that the precipitate forms faster when the water bath temperature is increased from 25 °C to 50 °C. Explain this observation using collision theory.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Increasing the water bath temperature from 25 °C to 50 °C increases the kinetic energy of both the 1-bromobutane molecules and the nucleophile (hydroxide ions from hydrolysis, or water molecules). This leads to two main effects according to collision theory. Firstly, the particles move faster, resulting in a higher frequency of collisions between the reacting species per unit time. Secondly, and more significantly, a much greater proportion of these collisions will possess energy equal to or exceeding the activation energy (Eₐ) for the hydrolysis reaction. Since only collisions with energy greater than or equal to Eₐ can lead to a reaction, an increase in the proportion of such 'successful' collisions directly translates to a faster rate of reaction, hence the faster formation of the precipitate.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Increasing temperature increases the kinetic energy of reactant particles (1-bromobutane and OH⁻/H₂O).
  • ✅ This leads to more frequent collisions between reacting particles per unit time.
  • ✅ More importantly, a greater proportion of these collisions will have energy equal to or greater than the activation energy (Eₐ).
  • ✅ This results in a higher frequency of successful collisions.
  • ✅ Therefore, the rate of hydrolysis increases, and the precipitate forms faster.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: Ensure students cover both aspects of collision theory (frequency and energy) and link them explicitly to the rate of reaction. A common error is only mentioning collision frequency.

Q8. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

Compare and contrast the nucleophilic substitution reactions of primary haloalkanes with aqueous alkali and with water in the presence of silver nitrate and ethanol. Include similarities, differences in conditions, and purpose.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Both reactions are fundamentally nucleophilic substitution reactions where a haloalkane is hydrolysed to form an alcohol, with the halogen atom being replaced by a hydroxyl group. In both cases, the C-X bond is broken, and the relative rates of reaction across the different haloalkanes (iodoalkanes > bromoalkanes > chloroalkanes) are governed by the strength of the C-X bond enthalpy.

However, there are key differences. When reacting with aqueous alkali, the nucleophile is the hydroxide ion (OH⁻), which is a strong nucleophile. This reaction directly yields an alcohol and a halide salt. The primary purpose is often synthetic, producing alcohols from haloalkanes.

In contrast, the reaction with water in the presence of silver nitrate and ethanol uses water (H₂O) as the nucleophile. Water is a weaker nucleophile than OH⁻. The immediate products are an alcohol and a hydrogen halide, which then dissociates. The key analytical aspect is that the halide ion (X⁻) produced then reacts with the silver ions (Ag⁺) from the silver nitrate to form an insoluble silver halide precipitate (AgCl, AgBr, or AgI). The rate of hydrolysis in this method is easily compared by timing how long it takes for a visible precipitate to form. This method's primary purpose is therefore analytical – to experimentally compare the relative rates of hydrolysis of different haloalkanes.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Similarities: Both are nucleophilic substitution reactions where the halogen atom is replaced by a hydroxyl group, forming an alcohol.
  • ✅ Both involve the breaking of the C-X bond.
  • ✅ The relative rates in both cases follow the trend I > Br > Cl (due to bond enthalpy).
  • ✅ Differences - Nucleophile: Aqueous alkali uses OH⁻ as the nucleophile (strong nucleophile). Water uses H₂O as the nucleophile (weak nucleophile, but also the solvent).
  • ✅ Differences - Product/Observation: Aqueous alkali directly forms alcohol + halide salt. Water/AgNO₃ produces alcohol + H⁺ + halide ion, with the halide ion then reacting with Ag⁺ to form a silver halide precipitate (AgX).
  • ✅ Differences - Rate indication: With aqueous alkali, rate could be monitored by pH change or conductivity, but it's not a standard comparative method. With water/AgNO₃, the rate is easily monitored by the time taken for the precipitate to appear.
  • ✅ Differences - Purpose: Aqueous alkali is primarily a synthetic method to produce alcohols. Water/AgNO₃ is primarily an analytical method to compare relative rates of hydrolysis.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This requires a comprehensive comparison, touching on nucleophile identity, observable products, and the practical utility of each method. Emphasise that Ag⁺ isn't a nucleophile, it just 'traps' the leaving group.

✏️ Answer: The novel haloalkane, X, with a C-X bond enthalpy of 200 kJ mol⁻¹, would be significantly more reactive towards hydrolysis than both 1-iodobutane (C-I bond enthalpy ~234 kJ mol⁻¹) and 1-bromobutane (C-Br bond enthalpy ~276 kJ mol⁻¹). B) The carbon-halogen bond breaks and the C-OH bond forms. 🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The history of haloalkanes is intertwined with both scientific breakthroughs and significant environmental challenges. Early in the 20th century, compounds like chloroform (CHCl₃) and halothane were crucial as anaesthetics. The mid-20th century saw the widespread adoption of chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) like CCl₂F₂ (Dichlorodifluoromethane) as refrigerants, aerosol propellants, and solvents, praised for their non-toxicity and stability. However, groundbreaking work by Sherwood Rowland and Mario Molina in the 1970s revealed that CFCs were responsible for stratospheric ozone depletion, leading to the signing of the Montreal Protocol in 1987, an international treaty to phase out ozone-depleting substances. The search for safer alternatives, like hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs), continues today.

⚖️ Ethics

The ethical considerations surrounding haloalkanes are profound. The historical use of CFCs exemplifies how a seemingly benign chemical can have devastating global environmental consequences, highlighting the responsibility of scientists and policymakers. While replacement compounds like HFCs don't deplete ozone, many are potent greenhouse gases, contributing to climate change. The use of certain organochlorine pesticides, such as DDT, despite their effectiveness in disease control (e.g., malaria), raised severe ethical questions due to their environmental persistence, bioaccumulation, and toxicity to wildlife. Balancing the benefits of these chemicals in areas like medicine and agriculture against their potential environmental and health risks is a continuous ethical challenge.

🚀 Advanced Theory

While the OCR H432 specification focuses on primary haloalkanes and Sɴ2 mechanisms, a deeper understanding involves the distinction between Sɴ1 and Sɴ2 pathways. Primary haloalkanes undergo Sɴ2, a concerted, one-step mechanism where the nucleophile attacks from the backside, leading to an inversion of configuration at the carbon. The rate is dependent on both haloalkane and nucleophile concentrations. Tertiary haloalkanes, however, favour the Sɴ1 mechanism, which is a two-step process: first, the slow, rate-determining heterolytic cleavage of the C-X bond to form a planar carbocation, followed by rapid attack of the nucleophile. Tertiary carbocations are stable due to hyperconjugation and inductive effects from alkyl groups. Secondary haloalkanes can undergo either Sɴ1 or Sɴ2, depending on the solvent and nucleophile strength. Polar protic solvents favour Sɴ1 by stabilising the carbocation, while polar aprotic solvents favour Sɴ2.

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

The experimental comparison of the rates of hydrolysis of different carbon-halogen bonds (PAG7) involves heating equal volumes of different haloalkanes (e.g., 1-chlorobutane, 1-bromobutane, 1-iodobutane) with aqueous silver nitrate in ethanol in separate test tubes. The test tubes should be placed in a water bath to maintain a constant temperature, as temperature significantly affects reaction rate. A timer is started as soon as the haloalkane is added. Observations are made for the time taken for the first appearance of a precipitate. Silver chloride (AgCl) is a white precipitate, silver bromide (AgBr) is cream, and silver iodide (AgI) is yellow. Safety notes: Haloalkanes are often volatile and toxic, so work in a fume cupboard. Ethanol is flammable. Silver nitrate is corrosive and can stain skin. Wear eye protection. Equipment list: Test tubes, test tube rack, water bath with thermometer, measuring cylinders/pipettes, dropper, stirring rods, different primary haloalkanes, aqueous silver nitrate solution, ethanol, dilute nitric acid (for acidification after hydrolysis).

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Haloalkanes: Many are volatile, toxic, and some are carcinogens. Work in a fume cupboard. Avoid skin contact and inhalation.
  • 🛑 Ethanol: Flammable. Keep away from naked flames.
  • 🛑 Aqueous Silver Nitrate: Corrosive and an irritant. Causes permanent brown/black stains on skin and clothing. Wear gloves and eye protection.
  • 🛑 Dilute Nitric Acid: Corrosive and an irritant. Wear eye protection and gloves.
  • 🛑 Heating: Use a water bath for controlled heating; avoid direct flame on flammable organic solvents.

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. Nucleophilic Substitution (Sɴ2) in primary haloalkanes: This is a concerted, one-step mechanism. The nucleophile (e.g., OH⁻) attacks the δ⁺ carbon atom from the opposite side to the halogen. Simultaneously, the C-X bond breaks, and the halide ion leaves. A transition state is formed where both the incoming nucleophile and the leaving group are partially bonded to the carbon. This leads to an inversion of configuration at the carbon centre. Curly arrows show the lone pair from the nucleophile attacking the carbon, and the C-X bond breaking towards the halogen.

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Hydrolysis with aqueous alkali (e.g., NaOH/KOH): Aqueous solution (water as solvent), often heated under reflux to ensure complete reaction and prevent volatile organic products from escaping. No specific pressure, but usually atmospheric.
  • Hydrolysis with water in the presence of AgNO₃ and ethanol (for rate comparison): Aqueous solution (water as solvent), with ethanol added as a co-solvent to mix the haloalkane and water. Usually carried out in a water bath at a controlled, constant temperature. Silver nitrate is present to precipitate the halide ion, allowing visual comparison of rates.

🔦 Element Spotlight

The carbon-halogen bond (C-X) is the central feature of haloalkanes. Its properties determine the reactivity of the entire molecule. The bond is polar due to the higher electronegativity of halogens compared to carbon, making the carbon atom partially positive (δ⁺). This polarity makes the carbon an electrophilic centre, attracting nucleophiles. Crucially, the bond enthalpy of the C-X bond varies significantly down Group 17: C-F (strongest, ~485 kJ mol⁻¹) > C-Cl (~338 kJ mol⁻¹) > C-Br (~276 kJ mol⁻¹) > C-I (weakest, ~234 kJ mol⁻¹). This trend in bond strength directly dictates the rate of hydrolysis and other nucleophilic substitution reactions. A weaker bond is easier to break, leading to faster reaction rates. The increasing atomic size of halogens down the group leads to longer bond lengths and less effective orbital overlap with carbon, hence the decreasing bond enthalpy.
