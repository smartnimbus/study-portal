Phenols: Acidity and Electrophilic Substitution

📖 Overview

🤜 In a Nutshell

Phenols are aromatic compounds with a hydroxyl group directly attached to a benzene ring, giving them unique properties. They are weakly acidic (react with NaOH but not carbonates) and highly activated towards electrophilic substitution due to electron donation from the oxygen atom, directing incoming electrophiles to the 2- and 4- positions. Key reactions include the formation of 2,4,6-tribromophenol with aqueous bromine and a mixture of 2- and 4-nitrophenol with dilute nitric acid.

🧠 Conceptual Understanding

Phenols are organic compounds where a hydroxyl (-OH) group is directly bonded to a benzene ring. This direct attachment profoundly influences their chemical properties compared to simple alcohols or benzene itself. The key differences lie in their acidity and their reactivity towards electrophilic substitution. The oxygen atom's lone pair electrons on the -OH group can delocalise into the benzene ring's π-system through resonance. This delocalisation makes the phenolic proton more acidic than an alcohol proton because the resulting phenoxide ion is stabilised by spreading the negative charge around the ring. Furthermore, this electron donation activates the benzene ring, making it significantly more susceptible to electrophilic substitution than benzene, particularly at the 2- (ortho) and 4- (para) positions. Understanding this electron donation and resonance is crucial to predicting phenol's unique reactivity.

📌 Core Theory

  • Phenols contain a hydroxyl (-OH) group directly bonded to a benzene ring.
  • The -OH group in phenols is weakly acidic, meaning phenols react with strong bases like sodium hydroxide (NaOH) to form a salt (sodium phenoxide) and water.
  • Phenols do not react with carbonates (e.g., Na₂CO₃) or hydrogencarbonates (e.g., NaHCO₃) because they are weaker acids than carbonic acid, unlike carboxylic acids.
  • The -OH group activates the benzene ring towards electrophilic substitution reactions by donating electron density into the π-system via resonance.
  • This activation makes phenols much more reactive towards electrophiles than benzene.
  • The -OH group is a 2- and 4- (ortho-para) directing group, meaning incoming electrophiles will substitute at these positions on the benzene ring.
  • Phenol reacts readily with aqueous bromine at room temperature to form 2,4,6-tribromophenol (a white precipitate), often without a catalyst.
  • Phenol reacts with dilute nitric acid at room temperature to form a mixture of 2-nitrophenol and 4-nitrophenol.
  • Electron-donating groups (e.g., -OH, -NH₂) are activating and 2,4-directing.
  • Electron-withdrawing groups (e.g., -NO₂) are deactivating and 3- (meta) directing.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

The unusual reactivity of phenols stems from the interaction between the lone pair of electrons on the oxygen atom of the hydroxyl group and the delocalised π-electron system of the benzene ring. Through resonance, these lone pair electrons can be delocalised into the ring, increasing the electron density within the benzene ring, particularly at the 2-, 4-, and 6- (ortho and para) positions. This increased electron density stabilises the intermediate carbocation formed during electrophilic substitution, thus lowering the activation energy and making the reaction much faster than with benzene. This 'activation' effect is also responsible for the directing nature of the -OH group; because electron density is highest at the ortho and para positions, electrophiles preferentially attack these sites. The weak acidity of phenols is also a direct consequence of this resonance stabilisation. When phenol loses its proton to form a phenoxide ion (C₆H₅O⁻), the negative charge on the oxygen can be delocalised into the benzene ring, spreading the charge and making the phenoxide ion more stable than an alkoxide ion (R-O⁻) from an alcohol. However, this stabilisation is not as strong as the stabilisation in a carboxylate ion, which is why phenols are weaker acids than carboxylic acids and do not react with carbonates.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Comparing Acidity: Phenol vs. Carboxylic Acid vs. Alcohol

Problem: Explain why phenol reacts with aqueous sodium hydroxide but not with sodium carbonate, whereas ethanoic acid reacts with both. Butanol does not react with either.

Steps: 1. Recall the relative acid strengths: Carboxylic acids > Phenols > Alcohols. 2. Recall that a stronger acid will displace a weaker acid from its salt. Carbonic acid (H₂CO₃) is formed when CO₂ dissolves in water, and it's a stronger acid than phenol but weaker than carboxylic acids. 3. Ethanoic acid (a carboxylic acid) is strong enough to react with both NaOH (a strong base) and Na₂CO₃ (a weaker base, forming carbonic acid). 4. Phenol is acidic enough to react with NaOH (a strong base) but is weaker than carbonic acid, so it cannot react with Na₂CO₃. 5. Butanol (an alcohol) is very weakly acidic, much weaker than water, and therefore does not react with either NaOH or Na₂CO₃.

Answer: Ethanoic acid reacts with both NaOH and Na₂CO₃ because it is a stronger acid than carbonic acid. Phenol reacts with NaOH to form sodium phenoxide and water, demonstrating its weak acidic nature. However, phenol is a weaker acid than carbonic acid, so it cannot react with sodium carbonate. Butanol, as a typical alcohol, is extremely weakly acidic (weaker than water) and thus does not react with either NaOH or Na₂CO₃.

Teacher's Tip

Remember the hierarchy of acidity: Carboxylic acids are stronger than carbonic acid, which is stronger than phenols, which are stronger than alcohols and water.

2. Predicting Products of Electrophilic Substitution of Phenol

Problem: Predict the major organic products when phenol reacts separately with: (i) aqueous bromine at room temperature and (ii) dilute nitric acid at room temperature. Explain the directing effect.

Steps: 1. Identify the reactant: Phenol (C₆H₅OH). 2. Identify the substituent on the benzene ring: -OH group. 3. Recall the directing effect of the -OH group: it is an activating and 2,4-directing group. 4. For (i) reaction with aqueous bromine: Since -OH is strongly activating, multiple brominations can occur, typically at all available ortho and para positions. 5. For (ii) reaction with dilute nitric acid: Nitration occurs, with the nitro group (-NO₂) substituting at the ortho and para positions. Since dilute acid is used, multiple nitrations are less likely than with conc. acid. 6. Draw the structures of the predicted products for each reaction. 7. Explain that the -OH group's lone pair electrons delocalise into the ring, increasing electron density at 2 and 4 positions, hence directing electrophiles there.

Answer: (i) When phenol reacts with aqueous bromine at room temperature, the major product is 2,4,6-tribromophenol. A white precipitate is observed. (ii) When phenol reacts with dilute nitric acid at room temperature, a mixture of 2-nitrophenol and 4-nitrophenol is formed. The -OH group is an activating group that donates electron density into the benzene ring's π-system via resonance. This electron donation is concentrated at the 2- (ortho) and 4- (para) positions, making these sites more susceptible to electrophilic attack by the incoming electrophiles (Br⁺ from bromine water, or NO₂⁺ from nitric acid).

Teacher's Tip

When a strongly activating group like -OH or -NH₂ is present, be aware that multiple substitutions can occur, especially with strong electrophiles or excess reagent (e.g., aqueous bromine).

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Antiseptics and Disinfectants: Early uses of phenol (carbolic acid) by Joseph Lister revolutionized surgery by reducing infections. Derivatives are still used in some throat sprays and household disinfectants.
  • Polymer Production: Phenols are key monomers in the production of phenolic resins (e.g., Bakelite), which are used in electrical components, insulation, and adhesives.
  • Pharmaceuticals: Many pharmaceuticals, including aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid), are synthesised from phenol derivatives. Paracetamol (acetaminophen) is also a phenolic compound.
  • Dye Industry: Phenolic compounds are important intermediates in the synthesis of a wide range of dyes and pigments.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Treating phenols as alcohols: Students often confuse phenols with alcohols, leading to incorrect predictions about their acidity or reactivity with electrophiles.
  • ❌ Phenols react with carbonates: Mistaking phenol's weak acidity for carboxylic acid strength, leading to the incorrect belief that it reacts with carbonates.
  • ❌ Incorrect directing effects: Confusing activating/deactivating groups or their directing effects (e.g., thinking -OH is meta-directing).
  • ❌ Underestimating phenol's reactivity: Assuming phenol requires the same harsh conditions as benzene for electrophilic substitution.
  • ❌ Mechanism errors: Incorrectly drawing curly arrows or intermediates in electrophilic substitution mechanisms.
Memory Anchor

Think of the -OH group in phenol as a 'generous neighbour' to the benzene ring. It 'donates' its electron resources (lone pair) to the ring, making the ring 'richer' in electrons. This makes the ring more attractive to 'electron-hungry' electrophiles, and the donation is mainly focused on the 'houses' at positions 2 and 4.

The oxygen atom of the -OH group has a lone pair of electrons that can be delocalised into the benzene ring's π-system through resonance. This electron donation significantly increases the electron density within the ring, making phenol much more nucleophilic and thus far more susceptible to electrophilic attack than benzene. Furthermore, this electron donation helps to stabilise the positively charged sigma complex intermediate, lowering the activation energy for the reaction and accelerating the rate of substitution. Q7. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

Consider the conversion of phenol to 4-bromophenol. Explain why this synthesis is challenging if attempting to avoid further bromination and suggest a strategy to obtain predominantly 4-bromophenol.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: The direct conversion of phenol to 4-bromophenol, avoiding further bromination, is challenging because the hydroxyl (-OH) group is a very strong activating and 2,4-directing group. When phenol is reacted with aqueous bromine, even at room temperature, its high reactivity typically leads to multiple substitutions, forming 2,4,6-tribromophenol as a white precipitate, not selectively 4-bromophenol.

To obtain predominantly 4-bromophenol, a common strategy involves temporarily reducing the activating effect of the -OH group. This can be achieved by converting the hydroxyl group into an ester, such as a phenyl acetate, using ethanoyl chloride or ethanoic anhydride. The resulting ester group (–OCOCH₃) is still an activating group, but it is much weaker than the free hydroxyl group, and it also directs to the ortho/para positions. Bromination of phenyl acetate under controlled conditions (e.g., using a non-polar solvent and limited bromine) would predominantly yield 4-bromophenyl acetate. Following this, the ester group can be hydrolysed back to a hydroxyl group (e.g., using dilute NaOH followed by acidification) to yield 4-bromophenol. This 'protecting group' strategy allows for better control over the regioselectivity and extent of substitution.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Challenge: Phenol is highly activated; direct bromination often leads to tribromination (2,4,6-tribromophenol) (1 mark).

  • ✅ Strategy 1 (protecting group): Convert -OH to an ester (e.g., acetate) to reduce activation (1 mark).
  • ✅ Strategy 2 (less activating): Brominate the less activated intermediate, then hydrolyse the ester back to -OH (1 mark).
  • ✅ Alternatively: Use a less powerful brominating agent or carefully control stoichiometry/temperature (but often less selective) (1 mark).
  • ✅ Preferred strategy: Protecting group approach (e.g. acetylation) to control activation (1 mark).

🧩 Quick Quiz (MCQ)

1. Which of the following statements correctly describes the acidity of phenol compared to carboxylic acids? A) Phenol is a stronger acid than carboxylic acids, reacting with both NaOH and carbonates. B) Phenol is a weaker acid than carboxylic acids, reacting with NaOH but not with carbonates. C) Phenol is a stronger acid than carboxylic acids, reacting with carbonates but not with NaOH. D) Phenol is a weaker acid than carboxylic acids, reacting with neither NaOH nor carbonates.

Answer

B) Phenol is a weaker acid than carboxylic acids, reacting with NaOH but not with carbonates. Phenol is a weak acid, strong enough to react with strong bases like NaOH to form a salt (sodium phenoxide) and water. However, it is not acidic enough to react with weak bases like sodium carbonate, which distinguishes it from carboxylic acids.

2. When phenol reacts with aqueous bromine (bromine water) at room temperature, what is the major product formed? A) Bromobenzene B) 2-Bromophenol C) 2,4,6-Tribromophenol D) 4-Bromophenol

Answer

C) 2,4,6-Tribromophenol The hydroxyl group (-OH) in phenol strongly activates the benzene ring towards electrophilic substitution and is a 2,4-director. With bromine water, this activation is so strong that substitution occurs rapidly at all available 2, 4, and 6 positions to form 2,4,6-tribromophenol, typically resulting in a white precipitate.

3. Why is phenol more reactive towards electrophilic substitution than benzene? A) The -OH group withdraws electrons from the benzene ring, making it more susceptible to electrophiles. B) The oxygen atom in the -OH group donates a lone pair of electrons into the π-system of the benzene ring, increasing its electron density. C) The -OH group is a bulky group that creates steric hindrance, making substitution easier. D) Phenol has a lower boiling point than benzene, increasing its reactivity.

Answer

B) The oxygen atom in the -OH group donates a lone pair of electrons into the π-system of the benzene ring, increasing its electron density. The oxygen atom in the hydroxyl group of phenol has a lone pair of electrons in a p-orbital that can be delocalized into the π-electron system of the benzene ring. This electron donation increases the electron density of the ring, particularly at the 2, 4, and 6 positions, making it much more susceptible to attack by electrophiles.

4. Which of the following describes the directing effect of the hydroxyl (-OH) group in phenol during electrophilic substitution reactions? A) It is a 3-director and deactivating. B) It is a 2- and 4-director and activating. C) It is a 3-director and activating. D) It is a 2- and 4-director and deactivating.

Answer

B) It is a 2- and 4-director and activating. Electron-donating groups like -OH increase the electron density in the benzene ring, making it more reactive (activating) towards electrophilic substitution. The electron donation specifically increases density at the ortho (2) and para (4) positions, directing incoming electrophiles to these positions.

5. When phenol reacts with dilute nitric acid at room temperature, which products are primarily formed? A) Only 2-nitrophenol B) Only 4-nitrophenol C) A mixture of 2-nitrophenol and 4-nitrophenol D) 2,4,6-Trinitrophenol

Answer

C) A mixture of 2-nitrophenol and 4-nitrophenol The -OH group is an ortho- and para-directing group. When phenol reacts with dilute nitric acid, nitration occurs primarily at the 2 (ortho) and 4 (para) positions, leading to a mixture of 2-nitrophenol and 4-nitrophenol. Concentrated nitric acid would lead to trinitrophenol.

6. A student wants to distinguish between phenol and ethanoic acid. Which reagent would provide a clear distinction? A) Aqueous sodium hydroxide B) Universal indicator solution C) Aqueous sodium carbonate D) Bromine water

Answer

C) Aqueous sodium carbonate Both phenol and ethanoic acid are acidic and will react with strong bases like NaOH. However, ethanoic acid (a carboxylic acid) is strong enough to react with sodium carbonate, producing effervescence (CO2 gas), while phenol is not acidic enough to react with carbonates. This difference allows for their distinction.

7. Which of the following groups, when attached to a benzene ring, is most likely to be a 3-director? A) -NH2 (amino) B) -OH (hydroxyl) C) -CH3 (methyl) D) -NO2 (nitro)

Answer

D) -NO2 (nitro) Electron-withdrawing groups such as the nitro group (-NO2) deactivate the benzene ring and direct incoming electrophiles to the meta (3) position. Electron-donating groups like -NH2, -OH, and -CH3 are 2- and 4-directors.

8. The neutralisation reaction between phenol and sodium hydroxide results in the formation of: A) Phenol chloride and water B) Sodium phenoxide and hydrogen gas C) Sodium phenoxide and water D) Benzene and sodium hydroxide

Answer

C) Sodium phenoxide and water Phenol is a weak acid and reacts with a strong base like sodium hydroxide in a neutralisation reaction to form a salt, sodium phenoxide, and water. This is a typical acid-base reaction.

9. Why is the formation of 2,4,6-tribromophenol from phenol and bromine water considered a very rapid reaction compared to the bromination of benzene? A) Benzene needs a Lewis acid catalyst for bromination. B) Phenol has a smaller molecular mass than benzene. C) The -OH group in phenol increases the electron density of the ring, making it much more attractive to electrophilic bromine. D) Bromine water is a stronger electrophile than liquid bromine used with benzene.

Answer

C) The -OH group in phenol increases the electron density of the ring, making it much more attractive to electrophilic bromine. The -OH group in phenol strongly activates the benzene ring by donating electron density, particularly to the 2, 4, and 6 positions. This significantly increases the ring's nucleophilicity, making it highly reactive towards electrophiles like bromine, even without a catalyst.

10. In organic synthesis, understanding directing effects of substituents on aromatic rings is important for: A) Determining the solubility of the final product. B) Predicting the exact position of new substituents and controlling the reaction outcome. C) Calculating the activation energy of the reaction. D) Choosing the most appropriate solvent for the reaction.

Answer

B) Predicting the exact position of new substituents and controlling the reaction outcome. Directing effects are crucial in organic synthesis because they allow chemists to predict where a new substituent will attach to an aromatic ring. This control over regioselectivity is essential for synthesizing specific isomers and avoiding unwanted by-products, thus making the synthesis more efficient and predictable.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

Phenol, also known as carbolic acid, has a rich history. It was first isolated from coal tar in 1834 by Friedlieb Ferdinand Runge. However, its most famous historical application comes from Joseph Lister, who, in the 1860s, pioneered antiseptic surgery by using carbolic acid to sterilise surgical instruments and wounds, dramatically reducing post-operative infections and mortality. This marked a monumental shift in medical practice. Later, its derivatives became crucial in the burgeoning plastics industry with the development of Bakelite (a phenol-formaldehyde resin) by Leo Baekeland in the early 20th century, which was one of the first commercially successful synthetic plastics.

⚖️ Ethics

Despite its utility, phenol is a corrosive and toxic substance. Historically, its widespread use as an antiseptic led to accidental poisonings and skin burns. Today, while controlled, its industrial production and use require stringent safety measures to prevent environmental contamination and exposure to workers. Concerns also exist about phenolic compounds leaching from plastics or being present as pollutants, affecting ecosystems and human health. Ethical considerations involve balancing the industrial benefits of phenol in materials and pharmaceuticals with the imperative to minimise environmental impact and ensure worker and public safety through responsible handling, disposal, and regulation.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond qualitative explanations, the acidity of phenols can be quantified using pKa values (phenol has a pKa of ~10, compared to ethanol's ~16 and ethanoic acid's ~4.7). The electron-donating and directing effects can be more rigorously explained by Hammett equation and substituent constants (σ values), which provide a quantitative measure of how substituents affect reaction rates and equilibrium constants. Further, the precise ratio of ortho to para products in nitration or bromination is influenced by steric hindrance and solvent effects, not just electronic directing effects. For example, intramolecular hydrogen bonding can play a role in separating ortho and para isomers, such as in 2-nitrophenol. More complex syntheses might involve reversibility of certain electrophilic substitutions (e.g., sulfonation) to achieve specific positional isomers.

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. Electrophilic Substitution (e.g., Bromination):
  2. Electrophile generation: Bromine molecule (Br-Br) is polarised by the electron-rich phenol ring, or in the presence of trace H⁺, can form Br⁺.
  3. Attack by Phenol: The delocalised π-electrons of the phenol ring (specifically at the 2- or 4- position) attack the slightly positive end of the bromine molecule (or Br⁺). A curly arrow moves from the π-bond to the bromine. A second curly arrow moves from the Br-Br bond to the other bromine atom, forming a bromide ion (Br⁻).
  4. Formation of Sigma Complex (Arenium ion): This forms a positively charged, non-aromatic intermediate (sigma complex) where the positive charge is delocalised across the ring. The -OH group's lone pair further stabilises this carbocation through resonance by forming a quinoidal structure with a positive charge on oxygen (all atoms having a complete octet).
  5. Deprotonation: A base (e.g., another Br⁻ ion or water molecule) removes a proton (H⁺) from the carbon atom where the electrophile attacked. A curly arrow moves from the C-H bond back into the ring to reform the aromatic π-system. This restores aromaticity and yields the brominated phenol product. This process repeats until all available ortho and para positions are substituted (2,4,6-tribromophenol).

  • Neutralisation with NaOH: Aqueous sodium hydroxide, room temperature.
  • Non-reaction with Carbonates: Aqueous sodium carbonate, room temperature.
  • Bromination: Aqueous bromine (bromine water), room temperature, no catalyst required (due to strong activation).
  • Nitration: Dilute nitric acid, room temperature, no catalyst required (due to strong activation). Concentrated nitric acid would lead to multiple substitutions and oxidation.

🔦 Element Spotlight

Phenol (C₆H₅OH) itself is the star here. It's a compound that bridges aromatic chemistry and alcohol chemistry but has its own unique class properties. The key is the direct bond between the hydroxyl oxygen and the sp² hybridised carbon of the benzene ring. This allows for direct resonance interaction, where the oxygen's lone pair electrons can be delocalised into the ring. This delocalisation is responsible for: 1. Enhanced Acidity: The resulting phenoxide ion (C₆H₅O⁻) is stabilised by spreading its negative charge across the ring, making it easier for phenol to lose a proton than an alcohol. 2. Activated Ring: The increased electron density within the ring (especially at the 2- and 4- positions) makes phenol significantly more reactive towards electrophiles than benzene. 3. Directing Effect: The resonance directs incoming electrophiles specifically to the 2- (ortho) and 4- (para) positions.
