Preparation of a Soluble Salt from an Insoluble Reactant (RP1)

📖 Overview

Ever wondered how chemists make pure salts for everything from fertilisers to medicines? This practical shows you how to transform an insoluble powder into a sparkling, soluble salt crystal!
Level Standard
Study Time ⏱️ 45 min
🤜 In a Nutshell

This practical teaches the preparation of a pure, dry soluble salt from an insoluble reactant like a metal oxide or carbonate. It involves reacting the acid with an excess of the insoluble reactant, filtering off the unreacted solid, then evaporating the solution to concentrate it, allowing pure crystals to form upon slow cooling, and finally washing and drying the crystals.

🧠 Conceptual Understanding

This practical teaches you how to prepare a pure, dry sample of a soluble salt. It hinges on a neutralisation reaction where an acid reacts with an insoluble metal oxide or carbonate. Since the reactant (oxide/carbonate) is insoluble, you can add it in excess to ensure all the acid reacts, and then easily remove the unreacted solid by filtration. The resulting solution contains only the desired soluble salt and water. To get the pure, dry salt, you then evaporate most of the water and allow the crystals to form upon cooling, a process called crystallisation. This method is crucial because it allows us to create salts from reactants that don't dissolve, making it easy to separate the products.

📌 Core Theory

  • Choose an appropriate acid and an insoluble metal oxide or carbonate to produce the desired soluble salt. For example, copper oxide (insoluble) + sulfuric acid (acid) -> copper sulfate (soluble salt) + water.
  • Heat the dilute acid using a Bunsen burner to speed up the reaction.
  • Add the insoluble metal oxide or carbonate in excess to the hot acid, stirring constantly, until no more dissolves (i.e., the solid stops disappearing and remains at the bottom). This ensures all the acid has reacted.
  • Filter the mixture to remove the unreacted excess insoluble reactant. The filtrate is a solution of the pure soluble salt.
  • Transfer the filtrate to an evaporating basin and heat it gently using a water bath or electric heater. This evaporates most of the water, concentrating the salt solution.
  • Stop heating when crystals begin to form on the edge of the solution or when a drop of solution on a glass rod crystallises quickly when cooled (testing for the 'point of crystallisation').
  • Allow the saturated solution to cool slowly. As it cools, the salt becomes less soluble and crystallises out.
  • Filter off the pure salt crystals and wash them with a small amount of cold distilled water to remove any soluble impurities.
  • Dry the crystals using filter paper (patting dry) or by leaving them in a warm oven or desiccator.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

The preparation of a soluble salt from an insoluble reactant involves several key chemical and physical processes. First, a neutralization reaction occurs between the dilute acid (e.g., sulfuric acid) and the insoluble base (e.g., copper oxide) or insoluble carbonate (e.g., copper carbonate). The acid reacts with the base to form a salt and water. If a carbonate is used, carbon dioxide gas is also produced. Heating the acid initially increases the rate of reaction. Adding the insoluble reactant in excess ensures that all the acid is consumed, preventing contamination of the final salt product with unreacted acid. The excess insoluble solid is then removed by filtration, leaving a pure solution of the soluble salt. This solution is then concentrated by evaporating most of the water using a gentle heat source (water bath or electric heater) to prevent 'bumping' and decomposition of the salt. Slow cooling allows for the formation of large, pure crystals. Finally, washing with cold distilled water removes any remaining soluble impurities on the surface of the crystals, and then the crystals are dried.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Preparing Copper Sulfate from Copper Oxide

Problem: Describe the chemical equation for the reaction of copper(II) oxide with sulfuric acid, and explain why copper(II) oxide is added in excess.

Steps: 1. Identify the reactants: copper(II) oxide (CuO) and sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄). 2. Identify the products of a metal oxide + acid reaction: a salt (copper sulfate, CuSO₄) and water (H₂O). 3. Write the balanced chemical equation: CuO(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → CuSO₄(aq) + H₂O(l). 4. Explain the purpose of adding copper(II) oxide in excess: Adding excess insoluble copper(II) oxide ensures that all the sulfuric acid is reacted completely. This is crucial because any unreacted acid would contaminate the final copper sulfate product. Since copper(II) oxide is insoluble, the unreacted excess can easily be removed by filtration.

Answer: Equation: CuO(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → CuSO₄(aq) + H₂O(l). Excess CuO is added to ensure all acid reacts and can then be filtered off easily.

Teacher's Tip

Always check the solubility rules! Using an insoluble reactant is key for easy separation of excess reactant.

2. Producing Zinc Nitrate from Zinc Carbonate

Problem: A student wants to prepare pure, dry zinc nitrate crystals from zinc carbonate and nitric acid. Outline the key steps after the initial reaction to obtain pure, dry crystals.

Steps: 1. After the initial reaction between zinc carbonate and nitric acid (producing zinc nitrate solution, water, and carbon dioxide), filter the mixture to remove any unreacted, insoluble zinc carbonate. 2. Transfer the filtrate (zinc nitrate solution) to an evaporating basin. 3. Gently heat the solution using a water bath or electric heater to evaporate most of the water until a saturated solution is formed (point of crystallisation). 4. Remove the evaporating basin from the heat and allow the solution to cool slowly at room temperature. 5. Filter the crystals formed from the solution. 6. Wash the crystals with a small amount of cold distilled water to remove any soluble impurities. 7. Dry the crystals by pressing them between sheets of filter paper or in a warm oven/desiccator.

Answer: Filter off excess zinc carbonate. Heat the filtrate gently to evaporate water until saturated. Allow to cool slowly to crystallise. Filter and wash crystals with cold distilled water. Dry crystals between filter paper.

Teacher's Tip

Remember, slow cooling makes larger, purer crystals. Rapid cooling makes smaller, less pure crystals.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Production of fertilisers: Ammonium nitrate is a soluble salt produced by neutralisation, vital for agriculture.
  • Manufacturing of medicines: Many active pharmaceutical ingredients are soluble salts, synthesised through controlled reactions and crystallisation.
  • Industrial wastewater treatment: Neutralisation reactions are used to treat acidic or alkaline effluents before discharge.
  • Food additives: Sodium chloride (table salt) is purified via crystallisation. Other salts like potassium iodide are added to fortify foods.
  • Pigment production: Salts like lead chromate (yellow pigment) can be formed by precipitation reactions involving soluble salts.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Heating to dryness: Many students heat the salt solution until all the water has evaporated. This can lead to anhydrous salt, decomposition of the salt, or smaller, impure crystals. It's crucial to stop heating at the 'point of crystallisation' and allow slow cooling.
  • ❌ Not using excess insoluble reactant: If not enough insoluble reactant is added, some acid will remain in the solution, contaminating the final salt product. The whole point of using an insoluble reactant is that excess can be filtered off easily.
  • ❌ Washing crystals with hot or too much water: Washing with hot water or too much cold water can dissolve the product crystals, reducing the yield. Cold distilled water is used sparingly to remove surface impurities.
  • ❌ Confusing soluble and insoluble: Students sometimes mix up which reactant needs to be insoluble. The key is that the reactant you add in excess and need to filter off must be insoluble.
Memory Anchor

Think of it like making a special kind of 'soup': You're adding solid 'ingredients' (insoluble reactant) to a liquid 'broth' (acid). You add extra solid to make sure all the broth is 'used up'. Then, you 'strain' out the extra solid, 'cook down' the broth to concentrate it, and finally, 'cool' it slowly to get beautiful, pure 'flavour crystals' (the salt!).

Safety: Standard laboratory safety precautions should be followed, including wearing safety goggles, handling hot apparatus with care, and appropriate disposal of chemicals.

Q11. (3 Marks | Short Answer | ⏱️ ~3 min)

A student measures the pH of the salt solution immediately after filtration. If the pH is found to be 2, what does this indicate about the preparation, and what remedial action should be taken?

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: A pH of 2 indicates that the solution is still acidic, meaning not all the acid reacted with the insoluble reactant. This suggests that insufficient insoluble reactant (e.g., metal oxide or carbonate) was added initially, or it was not added in excess. The remedial action would be to add more of the insoluble reactant to the solution, stirring and gently heating, until the pH becomes neutral (pH 7), indicating complete neutralisation.

  • ✅ Remedial action: Add more insoluble reactant (oxide/carbonate) to the solution until the pH is neutral (7) / no more fizzing (if carbonate) / the reactant stops dissolving (1 mark).

🧩 Quick Quiz (MCQ)

1. Why is the dilute acid often heated before adding the insoluble reactant when preparing a soluble salt? A) To make the salt precipitate faster. B) To increase the solubility of the insoluble reactant. C) To speed up the rate of reaction. D) To sterilise the acid before use.

Answer

C) To speed up the rate of reaction. Heating increases the kinetic energy of the particles, leading to more frequent and energetic collisions, thus increasing the rate at which the insoluble reactant reacts with the acid.

2. In the preparation of a soluble salt, why is it crucial to add an excess of the insoluble oxide or carbonate to the dilute acid? A) To ensure all the acid reacts completely. B) To make the solution saturated with the salt. C) To prevent the salt from decomposing during the reaction. D) To make the solution easier to filter after the reaction.

Answer

A) To ensure all the acid reacts completely. Adding excess insoluble reactant ensures that all the acid is neutralised. The unreacted insoluble solid can then be easily removed by filtration, leaving a pure solution of the salt.

3. After the reaction between an insoluble oxide and a dilute acid, what is the most appropriate technique to remove any unreacted solid from the salt solution? A) Decantation B) Distillation C) Filtration D) Crystallisation

Answer

C) Filtration Filtration is used to separate an insoluble solid from a liquid. The unreacted solid is retained on the filter paper, and the salt solution passes through as the filtrate.

4. When concentrating the salt solution by evaporation, why is a water bath or electric heater preferred over direct heating with a Bunsen burner flame? A) To prevent the salt from becoming too concentrated. B) To ensure a slower, more controlled evaporation to prevent the salt from decomposing or spitting. C) To remove impurities more effectively from the solution. D) To encourage the formation of larger salt crystals during evaporation.

Answer

B) To ensure a slower, more controlled evaporation to prevent the salt from decomposing or spitting. A water bath or electric heater provides gentler and more even heating, which prevents the solution from boiling too vigorously (spitting) and reduces the risk of thermal decomposition of the salt or burning of the solution, helping to obtain purer crystals.

5. After concentrating the salt solution, it is allowed to cool slowly to crystallise. Why is it generally not recommended to evaporate all the water completely to dryness? A) To increase the overall yield of the salt. B) To obtain larger, purer crystals of the salt. C) To make the salt less soluble for easier collection. D) To prevent the salt from re-dissolving when exposed to air.

Answer

B) To obtain larger, purer crystals of the salt. Allowing the solution to cool slowly promotes the formation of larger, purer crystals. Evaporating to complete dryness can result in a fine powder that includes impurities that were dissolved in the remaining water.

6. Which method is most appropriate for drying purified salt crystals after they have been filtered and washed? A) Heating them strongly with a Bunsen burner. B) Leaving them uncovered in direct sunlight for several hours. C) Pressing them gently between sheets of filter paper. D) Placing them in a freezer to solidify any remaining water.

Answer

C) Pressing them gently between sheets of filter paper. Pressing the crystals gently between filter papers or placing them in a desiccator removes surface moisture without heating, which could decompose the salt or cause loss of water of crystallisation.

7. A student is heating dilute sulfuric acid in a beaker over a Bunsen burner before adding copper(II) oxide. Which equipment combination is essential for safely and effectively heating the beaker? A) A clamp and retort stand. B) A conical flask and a stopper. C) A tripod, gauze, and heat-resistant mat. D) A watch glass and a evaporating dish.

Answer

C) A tripod, gauze, and heat-resistant mat. A tripod provides a stable platform, the gauze supports the beaker and spreads the heat evenly, and a heat-resistant mat protects the workbench. This is standard safe practice for heating beakers with a Bunsen burner.

8. To prepare pure, dry magnesium sulfate from an insoluble magnesium compound, which acid should be reacted with magnesium oxide or magnesium carbonate? A) Hydrochloric acid B) Nitric acid C) Sulfuric acid D) Ethanoic acid

Answer

C) Sulfuric acid To produce magnesium sulfate, sulfuric acid must be used, as the acid provides the sulfate ions for the salt. Magnesium oxide + Sulfuric acid → Magnesium sulfate + Water.

9. When preparing a salt from an insoluble carbonate, vigorous effervescence is observed as carbon dioxide gas is produced. What is the safest way to add the carbonate to prevent spillage? A) Add all the carbonate at once and stir vigorously. B) Add the carbonate slowly in small portions. C) Pour the acid slowly into the carbonate while stirring. D) Heat the mixture strongly while adding the carbonate.

Answer

B) Add the carbonate slowly in small portions. Adding the carbonate slowly in small portions allows the carbon dioxide gas to escape gradually, preventing a sudden surge of bubbles that could cause the solution to overflow from the beaker.

10. Which of the following salts would typically be prepared using the method of reacting an insoluble oxide or carbonate with a dilute acid? A) Silver chloride B) Lead(II) sulfate C) Zinc chloride D) Barium carbonate

Answer

C) Zinc chloride Silver chloride and lead(II) sulfate are insoluble salts, so this method (which forms a soluble salt solution) is not appropriate. Barium carbonate is an insoluble reactant, not a soluble salt product. Zinc chloride is a soluble salt and can be prepared by reacting insoluble zinc oxide or zinc carbonate with dilute hydrochloric acid.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The history of salt making is ancient, primarily focusing on sodium chloride for food preservation and seasoning. However, the preparation of other specific salts for medical, dyeing, or industrial purposes became central to early chemistry. Alchemists and early chemists often used similar neutralisation principles, though without the rigorous purification techniques. The development of crystallisation as a purification method, distinguishing it from simple evaporation to dryness, was a significant step in the 17th and 18th centuries, crucial for obtaining pure compounds for analysis and further reactions. This practical's methodology reflects a refined version of techniques developed over centuries to ensure purity.

⚖️ Ethics

Ethical considerations in modern large-scale salt production, particularly for fertilisers, involve responsible sourcing of raw materials, energy consumption for heating/evaporation, and waste management. For example, the production of nitric acid (for nitrates) involves high temperatures and can produce nitrogen oxides, pollutants contributing to acid rain and smog. Ensuring worker safety in environments handling corrosive chemicals and hot processes is paramount. The 'excess' reactant principle in the practical highlights a micro-scale version of a challenge in industrial chemistry: how to ensure complete reaction of expensive or hazardous reagents while minimising waste.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond GCSE, the crystallisation process involves supersaturation, nucleation, and crystal growth kinetics. The 'point of crystallisation' is when the solution reaches supersaturation, and nucleation (formation of initial crystal seeds) begins. Slow cooling favours crystal growth over further nucleation, leading to larger, more ordered crystals. Conversely, rapid cooling leads to a high degree of supersaturation and rapid nucleation, resulting in many small, often less pure crystals. Washing with cold distilled water is a practical application of the common ion effect and solubility product principle (Ksp), where impurities are dissolved while minimising loss of the desired salt. For some salts, anhydrous forms can be problematic, and controlling the water of crystallisation is critical (e.g., heating copper sulfate too strongly removes its 5 waters of crystallisation, turning it white).

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

  1. Set up a Bunsen burner, tripod, and gauze mat. Place a beaker containing dilute acid (e.g., sulfuric acid) on the gauze.
  2. Gently heat the dilute acid using a small Bunsen flame (or electric heater) to increase reaction rate.
  3. Add the insoluble metal oxide or carbonate (e.g., copper(II) oxide) slowly, in small portions, to the hot acid, stirring continuously with a glass rod. Continue adding until no more solid dissolves and a small amount of solid remains at the bottom of the beaker (indicating excess).
  4. Remove the beaker from the heat and allow it to cool slightly.
  5. Set up a filtration apparatus: a filter funnel with folded filter paper, collecting the filtrate in a clean conical flask or beaker.
  6. Pour the mixture into the filter funnel to separate the unreacted excess insoluble solid (residue) from the salt solution (filtrate).
  7. Transfer the filtrate (pure salt solution) into an evaporating basin.
  8. Place the evaporating basin over a water bath (or use an electric heater) and gently heat it to evaporate most of the water. Stop heating when crystals just begin to form around the edge of the solution or when a drop of solution on a glass rod crystallises quickly on cooling.
  9. Remove the evaporating basin from the heat and allow the saturated solution to cool slowly at room temperature without disturbance. This promotes the growth of larger, purer crystals.
  10. Once crystals have formed, filter the crystals using a clean filter funnel and filter paper.
  11. Wash the collected crystals with a very small amount of cold distilled water using a wash bottle to remove any soluble impurities adhering to their surface.
  12. Gently press the crystals between two sheets of fresh filter paper to dry them, or place them in a warm oven or desiccator to fully dry.

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Dilute acids (e.g., sulfuric, hydrochloric, nitric acid): Corrosive/Irritant. Wear eye protection. Handle with care. Spills should be diluted with water and neutralised.
  • 🛑 Hot apparatus (Bunsen burner, hot beaker, evaporating basin, hot plate): Risk of burns. Use heatproof mats and tongs/gloves.
  • 🛑 Glassware: Risk of cuts from broken glassware. Handle with care, dispose of broken glass in designated bins.
  • 🛑 Metal oxides/carbonates (e.g., copper(II) oxide): Harmful if ingested or inhaled, some are irritants. Avoid contact with skin and eyes. Wash hands after use.
  • 🛑 Bumping: Sudden, vigorous boiling can cause hot solution to splash out. Heat gently, especially during evaporation.

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. The core reaction is neutralisation. For a metal oxide (a base), the oxide ions (O²⁻) react with hydrogen ions (H⁺) from the acid to form water: O²⁻(s) + 2H⁺(aq) → H₂O(l). The metal cation combines with the acid's anion to form the salt.
  2. For a metal carbonate, the carbonate ions (CO₃²⁻) react with hydrogen ions (H⁺) from the acid to form water and carbon dioxide: CO₃²⁻(s) + 2H⁺(aq) → H₂O(l) + CO₂(g). Again, the metal cation and acid's anion form the salt.

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Temperature: The acid is heated (gentle Bunsen flame) initially to increase the rate of reaction. Evaporation is done gently, typically over a water bath or electric heater, to prevent decomposition and ensure controlled crystallisation.
  • Reactant concentration: Dilute acid is used.
  • Reactant amount: Insoluble metal oxide or carbonate is added in excess to ensure complete reaction of the acid.
  • Cooling: Slow cooling of the saturated salt solution (at room temperature, undisturbed) is essential for the formation of large, pure crystals.

🔦 Element Spotlight

While no single element is spotlighted, the 'salt' itself is the key focus. Salts are ionic compounds formed when the hydrogen ion of an acid is replaced by a metal ion or ammonium ion. They typically consist of a positively charged metal ion (or ammonium ion) and a negatively charged non-metal ion (or polyatomic ion like sulfate, nitrate, carbonate). Their properties (e.g., solubility, crystal structure) depend on the specific ions they contain. In this practical, we produce a soluble salt, meaning it dissolves in water to form a solution, which is crucial for its purification via filtration and crystallisation.
