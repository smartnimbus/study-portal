Carbon–carbon bond formation is a cornerstone of organic synthesis, enabling the construction of larger molecules from smaller precursors. Key reactions include nucleophilic substitution of haloalkanes with cyanide and nucleophilic addition of HCN to carbonyls, both adding one carbon. 🧠 Conceptual Understanding

In organic chemistry, the ability to form new carbon-carbon (C–C) bonds is absolutely fundamental for synthesising larger, more complex molecules from smaller ones. Think of it like adding bricks to build a bigger structure. This topic focuses on several key reactions that allow chemists to extend carbon chains, introduce new functional groups, and create substituted aromatic compounds. We'll explore nucleophilic substitution with cyanide ions to add a carbon atom and nucleophilic addition with hydrogen cyanide to carbonyl compounds, both of which add a carbon. We'll then see how the nitrile group (–CN) formed can be further transformed into amines or carboxylic acids. Finally, we'll look at how we can add carbon atoms directly to benzene rings using Friedel–Crafts reactions, which are essential for creating a vast array of aromatic compounds used in industries like pharmaceuticals and dyes.

📌 Core Theory

  • C–C bond formation is crucial for increasing the length of a carbon chain in organic synthesis.
  • Haloalkanes react with cyanide ions (CN⁻) in ethanolic solution via nucleophilic substitution to form nitriles, adding one carbon atom.
  • Carbonyl compounds (aldehydes and ketones) react with hydrogen cyanide (HCN) via nucleophilic addition to form hydroxynitriles, adding one carbon atom.
  • Nitriles can be reduced using hydrogen and a nickel catalyst (H₂/Ni) to form primary amines.
  • Nitriles can be hydrolysed by heating with dilute acid to form carboxylic acids.
  • Friedel–Crafts reactions are used to form new C–C bonds by attaching alkyl groups (alkylation) or acyl groups (acylation) to a benzene ring.
  • Friedel–Crafts alkylation involves a haloalkane and a halogen carrier (e.g., AlCl₃ or FeCl₃) as a catalyst.
  • Friedel–Crafts acylation involves an acyl chloride and a halogen carrier (e.g., AlCl₃ or FeCl₃) as a catalyst.
  • The halogen carrier acts as a Lewis acid, reacting with the haloalkane/acyl chloride to generate a strong electrophile (carbocation or acylium ion) that attacks the benzene ring.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

Extending carbon chains is a core strategy in organic synthesis. One common method involves using the highly nucleophilic cyanide ion (CN⁻), typically from potassium cyanide, to displace a halogen from a haloalkane. This nucleophilic substitution reaction, often carried out in ethanol to ensure solubility for both reactants, increases the carbon chain by one atom and introduces a nitrile (–CN) functional group. Aldehydes and ketones can also have their carbon chain extended by one carbon through reaction with hydrogen cyanide (HCN). This is a nucleophilic addition reaction where the CN⁻ nucleophile attacks the electrophilic carbon of the carbonyl group, forming a hydroxynitrile. Once a nitrile is formed, its versatility becomes apparent: it can be reduced to a primary amine (containing –CH₂NH₂) or hydrolysed to a carboxylic acid (containing –COOH), providing routes to different functional groups while maintaining the increased carbon chain length. For aromatic compounds, Friedel–Crafts reactions are indispensable. These electrophilic substitution reactions allow for the introduction of alkyl (alkylation) or acyl (acylation) groups onto a benzene ring, effectively forming a new C–C bond. They require a halogen carrier, such as aluminium chloride (AlCl₃), which acts as a Lewis acid to generate the highly reactive electrophile needed to attack the electron-rich benzene ring.

📖 Key Definitions

Nucleophilic substitution A reaction in which a nucleophile replaces another group (the leaving group) on a carbon atom.
Nucleophilic addition A reaction where a nucleophile adds across a double bond (or triple bond), typically a C=O bond in carbonyl compounds.
Nitrile An organic compound containing the –C≡N functional group.
Amine An organic compound containing a nitrogen atom bonded to one or more alkyl or aryl groups; primary amines have the formula RNH₂.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Converting bromoethane to propanoic acid

Problem: Outline a two-step synthesis to convert bromoethane (CH₃CH₂Br) into propanoic acid (CH₃CH₂COOH), including reagents and conditions for each step.

Steps: 1. Step 1: Convert bromoethane to propanenitrile via nucleophilic substitution. 2. Reagent: Potassium cyanide (KCN) or sodium cyanide (NaCN). 3. Conditions: Ethanolic solution, heated under reflux. 4. Equation: CH₃CH₂Br + CN⁻ → CH₃CH₂CN + Br⁻ 5. Step 2: Hydrolyse propanenitrile to propanoic acid. 6. Reagent: Dilute hydrochloric acid (HCl(aq)) or dilute sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄(aq)). 7. Conditions: Heat under reflux. 8. Equation: CH₃CH₂CN + 2H₂O + H⁺ → CH₃CH₂COOH + NH₄⁺

Answer: Step 1: CH₃CH₂Br → CH₃CH₂CN using KCN (or NaCN) in ethanol, heated under reflux. Step 2: CH₃CH₂CN → CH₃CH₂COOH using dilute HCl(aq) (or H₂SO₄(aq)), heated under reflux.

Teacher's Tip

Remember that the nitrile reaction adds one carbon, so the product will have one more carbon atom than the haloalkane starting material.

2. Reacting propanal with hydrogen cyanide

Problem: Describe the reaction of propanal (CH₃CH₂CHO) with hydrogen cyanide (HCN), including the name of the product and the mechanism.

Steps: 1. Identify the functional group: Propanal is an aldehyde, containing a carbonyl (C=O) group. 2. Identify the nucleophile: The cyanide ion (CN⁻) from HCN is the nucleophile. 3. Draw the nucleophilic addition mechanism: The lone pair on the cyanide carbon attacks the electrophilic carbon of the C=O group. The π-electrons of the C=O bond shift onto the oxygen atom, forming an intermediate alkoxide ion. This alkoxide then rapidly protonates from HCN or a solvent proton to form the hydroxynitrile. 4. Name the product: The product will be a hydroxynitrile. Propanal has 3 carbons, so the product will have 4 carbons with a hydroxyl and a nitrile group on the same carbon. It is 2-hydroxybutanenitrile.

Answer: Propanal reacts with hydrogen cyanide (HCN) via nucleophilic addition to form 2-hydroxybutanenitrile. The cyanide ion acts as a nucleophile, attacking the partially positive carbonyl carbon. The double bond breaks, and the oxygen gains a negative charge, which is then protonated to form the hydroxyl group.

Teacher's Tip

Always remember the stereochemistry if the product has a chiral centre. Nucleophilic addition to an unsymmetrical carbonyl compound will often produce a racemic mixture.

3. Friedel–Crafts acylation of benzene

Problem: Explain the role of aluminium chloride (AlCl₃) in the Friedel–Crafts acylation of benzene with ethanoyl chloride (CH₃COCl), including the relevant equation for its action.

Steps: 1. Identify the type of reaction: Friedel–Crafts acylation is an electrophilic aromatic substitution. 2. Recall the need for an electrophile: Benzene is electron-rich and requires a very strong electrophile to react. 3. Explain AlCl₃'s role as a Lewis acid: AlCl₃ is a Lewis acid, meaning it can accept an electron pair. 4. Show how it interacts with the acyl chloride: AlCl₃ reacts with ethanoyl chloride by accepting the lone pair from the chlorine atom. 5. Formulate the electrophile: This interaction generates a highly reactive acylium ion (CH₃C⁺=O), which is the electrophile. 6. Write the equation: CH₃COCl + AlCl₃ → CH₃C⁺=O + AlCl₄⁻

Answer: Aluminium chloride (AlCl₃) acts as a Lewis acid catalyst in Friedel–Crafts acylation. It reacts with ethanoyl chloride to generate a highly reactive electrophile, the acylium ion (CH₃C⁺=O). The equation is: CH₃COCl + AlCl₃ → CH₃C⁺=O + AlCl₄⁻. This acylium ion then attacks the electron-rich benzene ring.

Teacher's Tip

Halogen carriers like AlCl₃ are essential to make the electrophile strong enough to react with benzene. Without it, the reaction won't proceed.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Pharmaceutical synthesis: Many drugs are complex organic molecules built up using C–C bond formation reactions to create their intricate structures.
  • Polymer production: Monomers are joined together by forming C–C bonds to create long polymer chains, essential for plastics and fibres.
  • Agrochemicals: Insecticides, herbicides, and fungicides often rely on specific carbon skeletons formed through C–C bond formation.
  • Dye and pigment manufacturing: Substituted aromatic compounds made via Friedel–Crafts reactions are crucial intermediates in the production of colourful dyes.
  • Fragrances and flavourings: Specific aroma compounds are synthesised through controlled C–C bond formations to achieve desired scents and tastes.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Students often forget the ethanolic solvent for haloalkane reactions with CN⁻, or confuse it with aqueous conditions for hydrolysis.
  • ❌ Incorrect curly arrows or omission of charges in reaction mechanisms, especially in the intermediate steps of nucleophilic addition/substitution.
  • ❌ Confusing Friedel–Crafts alkylation with acylation, or using the wrong starting material (e.g., acyl chloride for alkylation).
  • ❌ Forgetting the halogen carrier catalyst in Friedel–Crafts reactions, or not understanding its role in generating the electrophile.
  • ❌ Incorrectly counting carbons when converting between functional groups (e.g., forgetting nitriles add one carbon).
Memory Anchor

Think of C-C bond formation as 'Building Blocks for Bigger Molecules'. Cyanide acts as a 'carbon-adding brick' (CN⁻) to haloalkanes and carbonyls. Friedel-Crafts are 'fancy add-ons' for benzene rings, needing an 'aluminium architect' (AlCl₃) to make the building blocks reactive.

Q4. (3 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~3 min)

When benzene reacts with 1-chloropropane in the presence of AlCl₃, a mixture of products is often formed. Explain why.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: In Friedel–Crafts alkylation, the primary carbocation (CH₃CH₂CH₂⁺) formed from 1-chloropropane and AlCl₃ is relatively unstable. This primary carbocation can undergo a hydride shift (a hydrogen atom with its bonding electrons moves) to form a more stable secondary carbocation (CH₃CH⁺CH₃). Both the primary and the rearranged secondary carbocation can then act as electrophiles and attack the benzene ring, leading to the formation of both propylbenzene (from the primary carbocation) and isopropylbenzene (from the secondary carbocation), hence a mixture of products.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ The primary carbocation (CH₃CH₂CH₂⁺) initially formed can rearrange.
  • ✅ It rearranges to a more stable secondary carbocation (CH₃CH⁺CH₃) via a hydride shift.
  • ✅ Both primary and secondary carbocations can then react with benzene, leading to a mixture of propylbenzene and isopropylbenzene.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This delves into carbocation stability and rearrangements, a common feature in electrophilic substitution reactions. Good for higher-level thinking.

Q9. (2 Marks | Short Answer | ⏱️ ~2 min)

Explain why ketones generally react slower than aldehydes with nucleophiles like cyanide.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Ketones have two electron-donating alkyl groups attached to the carbonyl carbon, whereas aldehydes only have one (the other group being a hydrogen atom). These alkyl groups increase the electron density on the carbonyl carbon, making it less electrophilic and thus less susceptible to nucleophilic attack. Additionally, the two alkyl groups in ketones cause greater steric hindrance, making it harder for the nucleophile to approach the carbonyl carbon, further slowing down the reaction.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Ketones have two alkyl groups attached to the carbonyl carbon, aldehydes have one alkyl and one hydrogen (1 mark).
  • ✅ Alkyl groups are electron-donating and also sterically hinder the attack of the nucleophile, making ketones less reactive (1 mark).

Q3. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

Discuss the advantages and disadvantages of using Friedel–Crafts alkylation versus acylation for attaching a carbon chain to a benzene ring.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Friedel–Crafts alkylation and acylation both form C–C bonds with benzene, but have distinct advantages and disadvantages:

Friedel–Crafts Alkylation (using haloalkanes): * Advantages: Uses relatively simple haloalkane reagents. * Disadvantages: 1. Carbocation Rearrangements: The carbocation formed from the haloalkane can rearrange to a more stable carbocation (e.g., primary to secondary), leading to a mixture of alkylated products. 2. Polyalkylation: The alkyl group introduced is electron-donating, which activates the benzene ring more than the original benzene. This makes the alkylated product more reactive to further alkylation, leading to the formation of multiple alkyl groups on the ring (polyalkylation), which is difficult to control.

Friedel–Crafts Acylation (using acyl chlorides): * Advantages: 1. No Rearrangements: The acylium ion electrophile does not undergo rearrangement, so only a single acylated product is formed. 2. No Polyacylation: The acyl group (ketone) is electron-withdrawing and deactivates the benzene ring. This makes the acylated product less reactive than benzene, preventing further acylation (polyacylation). * Disadvantages: 1. Requires an acyl chloride, which can be more expensive and reactive than haloalkanes. 2. If a simple alkyl group is ultimately desired, an additional reduction step (e.g., Clemmensen reduction or Wolff-Kishner reduction) is required to convert the ketone to an alkane, adding complexity to the synthesis.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Alkylation advantages: Simpler reagents (haloalkane).
  • ✅ Alkylation disadvantages: Carbocation rearrangements can lead to mixed products (e.g., propylbenzene and isopropylbenzene).
  • ✅ Alkylation disadvantages: Product is more reactive than benzene, leading to polyalkylation (multiple alkyl groups attached).
  • ✅ Acylation advantages: No carbocation rearrangements, so only one product formed.
  • ✅ Acylation advantages: Product (ketone) is less reactive than benzene, preventing polyacylation.
  • ✅ Acylation disadvantages: Requires acyl chloride (often more expensive, reactive), and subsequent reduction is needed if an alkyl group is desired (Clemmensen or Wolff-Kishner).

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This is a detailed comparison, crucial for understanding synthetic strategy in aromatic chemistry. Focus on rearrangements and reactivity differences for poly-substitution.

Q5. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

Using curly arrows, draw the full mechanism for the acid hydrolysis of propanenitrile (CH₃CH₂CN) to propanoic acid (CH₃CH₂COOH). Include all intermediates and show proton transfers.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: The acid hydrolysis of nitriles is a multi-step process involving protonation and nucleophilic attack by water.

  1. Protonation of Nitrile: The nitrogen atom of the nitrile is protonated by H⁺ from the acid, activating the carbon for nucleophilic attack. CH₃CH₂C≡N + H⁺ ⇌ CH₃CH₂C≡N⁺H₂. Nucleophilic Attack by Water: A water molecule attacks the electrophilic carbon of the protonated nitrile. CH₃CH₂C≡N⁺H + H₂O → CH₃CH₂C(OH)=N⁺H₂
  2. Proton Transfer (Tautomerism): A series of proton transfers leads to the formation of a protonated amide (or its tautomer). CH₃CH₂C(OH)=N⁺H₂ ⇌ CH₃CH₂C(=O)NH₂ + H⁺ (via several proton transfer steps involving water)
  3. Protonation of Amide: The oxygen of the amide carbonyl is protonated, making the carbonyl carbon more electrophilic. CH₃CH₂C(=O)NH₂ + H⁺ ⇌ CH₃CH₂C(OH)⁺=NH₂
  4. Nucleophilic Attack by Water: Another water molecule attacks the carbonyl carbon. CH₃CH₂C(OH)⁺=NH₂ + H₂O → CH₃CH₂C(OH)₂-N⁺H₃

Q6. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

When synthesising a primary amine from a haloalkane using cyanide followed by reduction, explain why it is crucial to use an excess of ammonia during the subsequent reduction step if performing reductive amination (alternative to H₂/Ni). What is a common pitfall if this is not done?

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: The question specifies 'using cyanide followed by reduction', which implies nitrile reduction. If this refers to the reductive amination of a carbonyl compound that might follow some C-C bond formation, the reasoning is as follows:

If we were considering the reductive amination of a carbonyl compound to an amine (which is a related C-N bond formation, often achieved via imine formation then reduction), using an excess of ammonia is crucial to minimise the formation of secondary and tertiary amines.

  1. Nucleophilicity: The primary amine product (RNH₂) is a stronger nucleophile than ammonia (NH₃) due to the electron-donating effect of the alkyl group(s).
  2. Over-alkylation/Over-amination: If ammonia is not in excess, the newly formed primary amine can itself react with any unreacted carbonyl compound (or an imine intermediate) or even the starting alkylating agent, leading to the formation of secondary (R₂NH) and tertiary (R₃N) amines.
  3. Yield: Using an excess of ammonia ensures that the primary amine product has a much higher chance of reacting with H⁺ (to be protonated and rendered non-nucleophilic) or simply not finding another carbonyl/alkylating agent to react with, thereby favouring the formation of the desired primary amine and maximising its yield, while minimising undesired by-products.

  • ✅ The primary amine product is a better nucleophile than ammonia (due to alkyl group activation).
  • ✅ If ammonia is not in excess, the primary amine can react further with the imine (or alkylating agent) to form secondary and tertiary amines.
  • ✅ This leads to a mixture of products, reducing the yield of the desired primary amine.

Q7. (3 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~3 min)

Explain why Friedel–Crafts alkylation is generally not used for synthesising alkylbenzenes with straight-chain alkyl groups longer than two carbons.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Friedel–Crafts alkylation is generally not suitable for preparing straight-chain alkylbenzenes longer than two carbons due to carbocation rearrangements. When a primary haloalkane (like 1-chloropropane or 1-chlorobutane) reacts with the halogen carrier (e.g., AlCl₃), it initially forms a primary carbocation. However, primary carbocations are less stable than secondary or tertiary carbocations. The primary carbocation can undergo a hydride shift (a migration of a hydrogen atom with its bonding electrons) to form a more stable secondary or tertiary carbocation. This rearranged carbocation then attacks the benzene ring, leading to the formation of a branched alkylbenzene instead of the desired straight-chain product, or a mixture of both.

  • ✅ Carbocation rearrangement occurs with primary carbocations.
  • ✅ This leads to the formation of more stable secondary or tertiary carbocations.
  • ✅ Resulting in a mixture of branched alkylbenzenes, not the desired straight-chain product.

Q9. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

Explain, with the aid of a mechanism, how the presence of an electron-donating group on a benzene ring affects its reactivity in Friedel–Crafts acylation.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Electron-donating groups (EDGs), such as alkyl groups (e.g., -CH₃) or -OH, increase the electron density of the benzene ring through inductive effects or resonance. This enhanced electron density makes the benzene ring a stronger nucleophile, making it more attractive and reactive towards the electrophilic acylium ion (RCO⁺) generated in Friedel–Crafts acylation.

Mechanistically, the rate-determining step in electrophilic aromatic substitution is the attack of the electrophile on the benzene ring to form a sigma complex (or arenium ion). The positive charge in this sigma complex is delocalised around the ring. An electron-donating group can stabilise this positive charge by pushing electron density into the ring, particularly when it's located at ortho or para positions. This stabilisation lowers the activation energy for the formation of the sigma complex, thereby increasing the rate of the Friedel–Crafts acylation reaction. The benzene ring becomes 'activated' towards electrophilic attack.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Electron-donating groups (EDGs) increase electron density in the benzene ring.
  • ✅ This makes the benzene ring a stronger nucleophile.
  • ✅ The EDG stabilises the intermediate sigma complex (arenium ion) formed during electrophilic attack.
  • ✅ Stabilisation lowers the activation energy for the rate-determining step, increasing the reaction rate.

C) Reaction of a haloalkane with cyanide ions Reaction of haloalkanes with cyanide ions introduces a -CN group, adding a carbon atom to the chain, which can then be further converted into other functional groups like carboxylic acids or amines.

Answer

C) Nucleophile The cyanide ion (CN-) has a lone pair of electrons on the carbon atom, which it donates to the partially positive carbon atom attached to the halogen, acting as a nucleophile in a nucleophilic substitution reaction.

4. When ethanal reacts with hydrogen cyanide in the presence of a small amount of KCN, what type of product is formed? A) An amine B) A nitrile C) A hydroxynitrile D) A carboxylic acid

Answer

C) A hydroxynitrile Carbonyl compounds (aldehydes and ketones) undergo nucleophilic addition with HCN to form hydroxynitriles (also known as cyanohydrins). The C=O double bond breaks, and -OH and -CN groups are added to the carbonyl carbon.

Answer

B) The CN- ion attacks the carbon atom of the carbonyl group. The negatively charged cyanide ion acts as a nucleophile, attacking the partially positive carbon atom of the polar carbonyl (C=O) group. This causes the pi bond of the C=O to break, with electrons moving to the oxygen atom.

B) Reduction with H2/Ni catalyst Nitriles can be reduced to primary amines using reducing agents such as hydrogen gas with a nickel catalyst (H2/Ni) or lithium aluminium hydride (LiAlH4).

7. What is the organic product formed when propanenitrile undergoes acid hydrolysis? A) Propanamide B) Propanoic acid C) Propan-1-ol D) Propan-1-amine

Answer

B) Propanoic acid Acid hydrolysis of a nitrile (typically by refluxing with dilute acid) converts the -CN functional group into a -COOH (carboxylic acid) functional group, retaining the original carbon skeleton length.

B) A haloalkane and FeCl3 Friedel-Crafts alkylation involves the reaction of an aromatic compound (like benzene) with a haloalkane (e.g., chloroethane) in the presence of a halogen carrier catalyst such as aluminium chloride (AlCl3) or iron(III) chloride (FeCl3) to add an alkyl group.

9. What is the primary purpose of using an acyl chloride (e.g., ethanoyl chloride) in a Friedel-Crafts reaction? A) To add an alkyl group to the aromatic ring. B) To reduce the aromatic ring. C) To add an acyl group to the aromatic ring. D) To oxidise the aromatic ring.

C) To add an acyl group to the aromatic ring. Friedel-Crafts acylation specifically uses an acyl chloride or an acid anhydride in the presence of a halogen carrier catalyst to introduce an acyl group (-COR) onto an aromatic ring.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The Friedel–Crafts reactions, named after Charles Friedel and James Crafts, were discovered in 1877. This landmark discovery provided chemists with a powerful tool for synthesising a vast array of aromatic compounds, significantly impacting the dye, pharmaceutical, and polymer industries. The development of nitrile chemistry also has a rich history, with key discoveries in the 19th and early 20th centuries revealing their versatility as synthetic intermediates. Early work on 'Grignard reagents' (discovered by Victor Grignard in 1900, earning him a Nobel Prize) also revolutionised C–C bond formation, offering even more versatile routes than the ones covered in core curricula.

⚖️ Ethics

The use of cyanide reagents (KCN, HCN) raises significant ethical and safety concerns due to their extreme toxicity. Industrial processes using these reagents require stringent safety protocols and waste management to prevent environmental contamination and human exposure. Green chemistry principles encourage the exploration of less toxic alternatives or the development of catalytic processes that avoid hazardous reagents. For example, some 'green' C–C bond formations aim to use renewable feedstocks and minimise waste, aligning with sustainable development goals.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond the core reactions, advanced C–C bond formation includes powerful methods like Grignard reactions (using organometallic reagents R-MgX to attack carbonyls), Wittig reactions (forming alkenes from carbonyls), and various cross-coupling reactions (e.g., Suzuki, Heck, Stille coupling) that link aryl or vinyl halides with organometallic reagents, often catalysed by palladium complexes. These reactions offer exquisite control over regioselectivity and stereoselectivity, allowing for the construction of highly complex and stereochemically defined molecules. For Friedel–Crafts reactions, understanding the precise nature of the Lewis acid catalysis, including the formation of intermediate complexes and their effect on ring activation, becomes crucial at an advanced level.

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

For reactions involving potassium cyanide (KCN) or hydrogen cyanide (HCN), all work must be conducted in a fume cupboard with appropriate PPE (gloves, eye protection, lab coat). Avoid contact with acids. Disposal of cyanide waste must follow strict protocols. Friedel–Crafts reactions require anhydrous conditions, typically achieved by drying glassware and using freshly distilled, dry solvents to prevent deactivation of the halogen carrier catalyst and hydrolysis of reagents.

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Potassium cyanide (KCN) and hydrogen cyanide (HCN) are highly toxic. Inhalation, ingestion, or skin absorption can be fatal.
  • 🛑 HCN gas is extremely volatile and is generated if cyanide salts come into contact with acids.
  • 🛑 Ethanol is flammable; reactions heated under reflux require appropriate heating methods (e.g., electric heating mantle, not open flame).
  • 🛑 Halogen carriers like AlCl₃ are corrosive and react vigorously with water, releasing HCl gas. Handle with dry apparatus in a fume cupboard.
  • 🛑 Haloalkanes can be irritants and have varying toxicities; handle in a fume cupboard.

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. Nucleophilic Substitution (R-X + CN⁻): The cyanide ion (CN⁻) acts as a nucleophile, attacking the partially positive carbon atom attached to the halogen in the haloalkane. Simultaneously, the carbon-halogen bond breaks, and the halogen leaves as a halide ion. For primary haloalkanes, this is typically an SN2 mechanism (concerted, one-step, transition state).
  2. Nucleophilic Addition (R₂C=O + HCN): The cyanide ion (CN⁻) acts as a nucleophile, attacking the electrophilic carbon atom of the carbonyl (C=O) group. The π-electrons of the C=O bond shift onto the oxygen atom, forming an alkoxide intermediate. This alkoxide then rapidly picks up a proton from HCN or the solvent to form the hydroxynitrile.
  3. Friedel–Crafts Alkylation/Acylation (Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution):
    1. Electrophile Generation: The halogen carrier (e.g., AlCl₃) reacts with the haloalkane (for alkylation, forming a carbocation, R⁺) or acyl chloride (for acylation, forming an acylium ion, RCO⁺), generating a powerful electrophile.
    2. Electrophilic Attack: The electrophile attacks the electron-rich benzene ring, breaking its aromaticity temporarily and forming a resonance-stabilised sigma complex (arenium ion).
    3. Deprotonation and Aromatisation: A proton is removed from the carbon where the electrophile attacked (usually by the AlCl₄⁻ ion), reforming the aromatic system and regenerating the catalyst.

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Haloalkane to Nitrile (R-X → R-CN): Potassium cyanide (KCN) or Sodium cyanide (NaCN) in an ethanolic solution, heated under reflux.
  • Carbonyl to Hydroxynitrile (R₂C=O → R₂C(OH)CN): Hydrogen cyanide (HCN) generated in situ (e.g., KCN/H₂SO₄(aq)), at room temperature.
  • Nitrile to Amine (R-CN → R-CH₂NH₂): Hydrogen gas (H₂) with a Nickel (Ni) catalyst, under heat and pressure (catalytic hydrogenation).
  • Nitrile to Carboxylic Acid (R-CN → R-COOH): Dilute strong acid (e.g., HCl(aq) or H₂SO₄(aq)), heated under reflux (acid hydrolysis); alternatively, dilute strong alkali (e.g., NaOH(aq)), heated under reflux, followed by acidification (alkaline hydrolysis).
  • Friedel–Crafts Alkylation/Acylation: Anhydrous conditions, halogen carrier catalyst (e.g., AlCl₃ or FeCl₃), and heating (often under reflux).

🔦 Element Spotlight

Carbon is the star of the show! Its ability to form four stable covalent bonds, including strong C–C single, double, and triple bonds, is what allows for the incredible diversity and complexity of organic molecules. In C–C bond formation reactions, we are essentially harnessing carbon's versatility to link smaller carbon fragments together. Whether it's the sp-hybridised carbon of the cyanide ion attacking an electrophilic centre, or an acylium ion's sp-hybridised carbon forming a new bond with an sp²-hybridised benzene carbon, the flexibility of carbon bonding underpins all these synthetic strategies.
