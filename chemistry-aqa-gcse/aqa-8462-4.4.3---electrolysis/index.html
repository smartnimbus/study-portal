🤜 In a Nutshell

Electrolysis uses electrical energy to decompose molten or dissolved ionic compounds. Positive ions are reduced at the cathode, and negative ions are oxidised at the anode. 🧠 Conceptual Understanding

Electrolysis is a powerful chemical process where an electric current is passed through a molten or dissolved ionic compound, causing it to decompose. This process is crucial because it allows us to extract highly reactive metals from their ores that cannot be extracted by reduction with carbon, or to produce non-metal elements and compounds. For electrolysis to occur, the ions in the compound must be free to move, which is why the ionic compound needs to be molten (melted) or dissolved in water. These liquids, called electrolytes, can conduct electricity because of these mobile ions. When the current flows, positive ions (cations) are attracted to the negative electrode (cathode) and gain electrons (reduction), while negative ions (anions) are attracted to the positive electrode (anode) and lose electrons (oxidation). This controlled electron transfer forces non-spontaneous chemical changes, yielding new elements or compounds.

📌 Core Theory

  • Ionic compounds contain positively charged ions (cations) and negatively charged ions (anions) held in a giant ionic lattice structure.
  • When an ionic compound is molten or dissolved in water, the ions become mobile and are free to move.
  • Liquids containing free-moving ions are called electrolytes and are capable of conducting electricity.
  • Electrolysis is the process of using electrical energy to break down (decompose) an ionic compound into its constituent elements or simpler compounds.
  • During electrolysis, positive ions (cations) are attracted to the negative electrode (cathode), where they gain electrons (reduction).
  • Negative ions (anions) are attracted to the positive electrode (anode), where they lose electrons (oxidation).
  • At the anode, non-metal ions like halide ions (Cl-, Br-, I-) lose electrons to form diatomic elements (Cl2, Br2, I2). Hydroxide ions (OH-) or water molecules may lose electrons to form oxygen gas.
  • At the cathode, metal ions (e.g., Cu2+) gain electrons to form metal atoms. If the metal is more reactive than hydrogen, or if the electrolyte is an aqueous solution, hydrogen gas (from H+ ions or water) may be produced instead of the metal.
  • Half-equations (Higher Tier) represent the electron gain or loss at each electrode, showing the oxidation and reduction processes separately.
  • Predicting products in aqueous solutions involves considering the reactivity series for metals (at cathode) and the presence of halide ions vs. hydroxide/sulfate ions (at anode).
  • If halide ions are present in aqueous solution and are concentrated, they will be discharged at the anode. Otherwise, oxygen gas will be produced from hydroxide ions or water.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

Electrolysis fundamentally relies on the movement and discharge of ions. When an electric current is supplied to an electrolyte, the circuit is completed by the external power supply, which acts as an 'electron pump'. The negative terminal of the power supply pushes electrons onto the cathode, making it negatively charged. Conversely, the positive terminal pulls electrons away from the anode, making it positively charged. Positive ions (cations) in the electrolyte are electrostatically attracted to the negative cathode, where they gain electrons – this is reduction. For example, Cu²⁺ ions gain two electrons to form Cu atoms. Negative ions (anions) migrate towards the positive anode, where they lose electrons – this is oxidation. For instance, Cl⁻ ions lose one electron to form Cl atoms, which then pair up to form Cl₂ gas. The specific products formed depend on whether the electrolyte is molten or aqueous, and the relative reactivity of the ions present. In molten ionic compounds, it's straightforward: the metal forms at the cathode and the non-metal at the anode. However, in aqueous solutions, water itself can ionise to produce H⁺ and OH⁻ ions, which can also be discharged. At the cathode, if the metal ion is more reactive than hydrogen (e.g., Na⁺, K⁺), hydrogen gas is produced. If the metal ion is less reactive than hydrogen (e.g., Cu²⁺, Ag⁺), the metal itself is produced. At the anode, if halide ions (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) are present in significant concentration, they will be oxidised to form the halogen gas. Otherwise, hydroxide ions (from water) will be oxidised to form oxygen gas and water. These processes are vital for industrial applications, from purifying metals to producing essential industrial chemicals.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Electrolysis of Molten Lead Bromide (PbBr₂)

Problem: Describe what happens during the electrolysis of molten lead(II) bromide using inert graphite electrodes, including the products formed and relevant half-equations.

Steps: 1. Identify the ions present: In molten lead(II) bromide, the ions are Pb²⁺ (lead ions) and Br⁻ (bromide ions). 2. Determine electrode attraction: Pb²⁺ ions are positive, so they are attracted to the negative cathode. Br⁻ ions are negative, so they are attracted to the positive anode. 3. Predict products at cathode (reduction): Pb²⁺ ions gain electrons to form lead metal. The half-equation is: Pb²⁺(l) + 2e⁻ → Pb(l). 4. Predict products at anode (oxidation): Br⁻ ions lose electrons to form bromine molecules. The half-equation is: 2Br⁻(l) → Br₂(g) + 2e⁻. 5. State observations: At the cathode, a grey liquid metal (lead) will form. At the anode, brown-orange fumes (bromine gas) will be produced.

Answer: During the electrolysis of molten lead(II) bromide, lead(II) ions (Pb²⁺) are attracted to the cathode and gain two electrons each to form molten lead metal. Bromide ions (Br⁻) are attracted to the anode and each lose one electron to form bromine atoms, which then combine to form bromine gas.The half-equation at the cathode is: Pb²⁺(l) + 2e⁻ → Pb(l).The half-equation at the anode is: 2Br⁻(l) → Br₂(g) + 2e⁻.

Teacher's Tip

Always remember 'PANIC' for electrodes: Positive Anode, Negative Is Cathode (for ions). Then apply OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain.

2. Electrolysis of Aqueous Copper(II) Sulfate (CuSO₄) with Inert Electrodes

Problem: Predict the products at the anode and cathode when aqueous copper(II) sulfate is electrolysed using inert graphite electrodes. Write the half-equations for the reactions.

Steps: 1. Identify all ions present: From CuSO₄: Cu²⁺ and SO₄²⁻. From water (H₂O): H⁺ and OH⁻. 2. Determine ions attracted to cathode (negative electrode): Cu²⁺ and H⁺. 3. Predict product at cathode: Compare reactivity of Cu and H. Copper is less reactive than hydrogen, so copper metal will be deposited. Half-equation: Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s). 4. Determine ions attracted to anode (positive electrode): SO₄²⁻ and OH⁻. 5. Predict product at anode: Compare discharge priority. Sulfate ions are rarely discharged. Hydroxide ions (or water) are discharged to form oxygen gas and water. Half-equation: 4OH⁻(aq) → O₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) + 4e⁻ (or 2H₂O(l) → O₂(g) + 4H⁺(aq) + 4e⁻). 6. State observations: At the cathode, a pink-brown solid (copper) will deposit. At the anode, bubbles of a gas that relights a glowing splint (oxygen) will be produced. The blue colour of the copper sulfate solution will fade.

Answer: At the cathode, copper(II) ions (Cu²⁺) are discharged in preference to hydrogen ions, producing copper metal. The half-equation is: Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s).At the anode, hydroxide ions (OH⁻) from the water are discharged in preference to sulfate ions, producing oxygen gas and water. The half-equation is: 4OH⁻(aq) → O₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) + 4e⁻.

Teacher's Tip

For aqueous solutions, remember the reactivity series for the cathode and the priority order for the anode (halides > hydroxide/water).

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Extraction of Aluminium: Bauxite ore (aluminium oxide) is electrolysed when molten, providing a cost-effective way to obtain pure aluminium metal, which is highly reactive and cannot be extracted by reduction with carbon.
  • Purification of Copper: Impure copper can be refined by electrolysis. Impure copper acts as the anode, and pure copper forms at the cathode, separating copper from less reactive impurities.
  • Electroplating: Covering the surface of one metal with a thin layer of another more desirable metal (e.g., chrome plating on steel, silver plating on cutlery) using electrolysis to improve appearance or corrosion resistance.
  • Chlor-alkali process: The electrolysis of concentrated aqueous sodium chloride (brine) produces chlorine gas (Cl2), hydrogen gas (H2), and sodium hydroxide (NaOH), all vital industrial chemicals.
  • Manufacture of Sodium: Molten sodium chloride is electrolysed in the Down's cell to produce elemental sodium and chlorine gas.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing anode with cathode, or thinking negative ions go to the positive electrode and vice-versa (they do, but students sometimes mix up which electrode is which charge from the power supply).
  • ❌ Believing that electrons travel through the electrolyte. Electrons travel through the external circuit; ions move in the electrolyte.
  • ❌ Forgetting about water's role in aqueous electrolysis and only considering the ions from the dissolved salt.
  • ❌ Incorrectly predicting products in aqueous solutions, especially if the metal is more reactive than hydrogen (producing H₂ not metal) or if chloride ions are present (producing Cl₂ not O₂).
  • ❌ Mixing up oxidation and reduction, particularly regarding electron gain/loss (OIL RIG is crucial here).
Memory Anchor

Remember 'OIL RIG' for oxidation and reduction: Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons), Reduction Is Gain (of electrons). For electrodes in an electrolytic cell, 'PANIC': Positive Anode, Negative Is Cathode.

This is due to the high concentration of chloride ions, which makes them preferentially discharged over hydroxide ions (or water) at the positive electrode.

Q7. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

Explain, using relevant half-equations, how electrolysis could be used to remove nickel impurities from a sample of copper. (HT)

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: To remove nickel impurities from copper using electrolysis, an electrolytic cell would be set up with the impure copper acting as the anode (positive electrode), and a thin strip of pure copper as the cathode (negative electrode). The electrolyte would be an aqueous solution containing copper ions, such as copper(II) sulfate. At the anode, both the copper atoms and the nickel impurity atoms in the impure block would lose electrons (oxidise) to form their respective ions, Cu²⁺ and Ni²⁺, which then dissolve into the electrolyte. The half-equations are: Cu(s) → Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ and Ni(s) → Ni²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻. At the cathode, both Cu²⁺ and Ni²⁺ ions from the solution would be attracted. However, only the copper(II) ions are preferentially discharged (reduced) to form pure copper metal. This is because copper is less reactive than nickel, meaning Cu²⁺ ions are more easily reduced from the solution than Ni²⁺ ions. Therefore, pure copper metal deposits on the cathode, while the nickel ions remain in solution as impurities.

Q9. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

Explain why the volume of hydrogen gas collected at the cathode is typically double the volume of oxygen gas collected at the anode during the electrolysis of water (or dilute acid/alkali). (HT)

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: During the electrolysis of water, hydrogen gas is produced at the cathode and oxygen gas at the anode. The half-equation for hydrogen production is typically 2H⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → H₂(g). The half-equation for oxygen production is 4OH⁻(aq) → O₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) + 4e⁻. From these equations, it can be seen that for every 4 electrons transferred, 2 molecules of hydrogen are produced, while only 1 molecule of oxygen is produced. According to Avogadro's Law, equal moles of gas occupy equal volumes under the same conditions. Therefore, twice as many moles of hydrogen gas are produced compared to oxygen gas, leading to a volume ratio of hydrogen to oxygen of 2:1.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Half-equation for H₂ production: 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂ (or 2H₂O + 2e⁻ → H₂ + 2OH⁻) (1 mark).

  • ✅ From the half-equations, 2 moles of electrons produce 1 mole of H₂, while 4 moles of electrons produce 1 mole of O₂ (1 mark).
  • ✅ Therefore, for every one mole of oxygen produced, two moles of hydrogen are produced, resulting in a 2:1 volume ratio of hydrogen to oxygen (according to Avogadro's law) (1 mark).

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The term 'electrolysis' was coined by Michael Faraday in the early 19th century. Humphry Davy was one of the first to use electrolysis to isolate elements previously thought to be compounds, like sodium and potassium, from their molten hydroxides in 1807. His pioneering work involved using a voltaic pile (early battery) to generate the electric current, marking a significant milestone in modern chemistry by demonstrating the elemental nature of these substances.

⚖️ Ethics

Industrial electrolysis processes, particularly those involving the production of chlorine (chlor-alkali process), raise significant ethical and environmental concerns. Chlorine production can generate hazardous byproducts like mercury (in older cell designs) or contribute to global warming through energy consumption. The release of toxic chlorine gas into the atmosphere or wastewater has severe ecological impacts and poses health risks to human populations. Modern industries strive to implement more environmentally friendly technologies (e.g., membrane cells) and strict safety protocols to mitigate these risks, balancing economic benefits with environmental responsibility.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond GCSE, the quantitative aspects of electrolysis are governed by Faraday's Laws. The first law states that the mass of a substance produced at an electrode is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity (charge) passed through the electrolyte. The second law relates the masses of different substances produced by the same quantity of electricity to their chemical equivalents. This involves calculations using the Faraday constant (the charge of one mole of electrons, approximately 96,485 C/mol) and molar masses. Furthermore, A-level chemistry delves into electrode potentials, which provide a quantitative measure of the tendency for a species to gain or lose electrons, allowing for more precise predictions of discharge order in complex mixtures.

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. Oxidation at the Anode: Negative ions (anions) are attracted to the positively charged anode. Each anion loses one or more electrons to the anode, becoming a neutral atom or molecule. For example, 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻. This is an electron-loss process.
  2. Reduction at the Cathode: Positive ions (cations) are attracted to the negatively charged cathode. Each cation gains one or more electrons from the cathode, becoming a neutral atom or molecule. For example, Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu. This is an electron-gain process.

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Electrolyte state: Must be molten (for pure ionic compounds) or dissolved in water (aqueous solution) to allow for free-moving ions.
  • Electrodes: Typically inert (e.g., graphite, platinum) to prevent them from reacting and affecting the products. Reactive electrodes (e.g., copper anode in copper refining) are used for specific purposes.
  • Temperature: For molten electrolytes, high temperatures (e.g., 950°C for Al₂O₃ in cryolite) are necessary to keep the compound molten. Aqueous solutions are usually electrolysed at room temperature.
  • Pressure: Standard atmospheric pressure for most laboratory experiments. Industrial processes may use specific pressures.
  • Current/Voltage: A direct current (DC) supply is required to drive the non-spontaneous reaction. The voltage must be sufficient to overcome the decomposition potential of the electrolyte.
  • Concentration: In aqueous solutions, the concentration of halide ions can determine whether the halogen or oxygen is produced at the anode (concentrated halide favors halogen).

🔦 Element Spotlight

Aluminium (Al) Properties: A light, strong, silvery-white metal. Good conductor of electricity and heat. Resistant to corrosion due to a protective layer of aluminium oxide that forms on its surface. It has a relatively low density. Electron Configuration: 1s²2s²2p⁶3s²3p¹ (or [Ne] 3s²3p¹). It typically forms Al³⁺ ions by losing its three valence electrons. Reactivity: Aluminium is a very reactive metal (higher than carbon in the reactivity series). Despite its reactivity, its strong, impermeable oxide layer makes it appear unreactive in everyday conditions. Because of its high reactivity, it cannot be extracted from its ore (aluminium oxide, Al₂O₃) by reduction with carbon. Therefore, electrolysis (the Hall-Héroult process) is the only industrial method for its extraction, highlighting the importance of this process for such a widely used metal.
