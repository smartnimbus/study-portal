🤜 In a Nutshell

Condensation polymerisation is a process where monomers, each with at least two functional groups, link together to form a polymer while eliminating a small molecule, typically water. This creates characteristic linkages like ester (-COO-) in polyesters or amide (-CONH-) in polyamides, enabling the synthesis of important materials like Terylene and Nylon.

🧠 Conceptual Understanding

Condensation polymerisation is a type of polymerisation reaction where monomers join together to form a polymer, with the simultaneous elimination of a small molecule, typically water. Imagine building a long LEGO chain, but each time you connect two blocks, a tiny water molecule pops off. This process requires monomers that each have at least two functional groups, allowing them to link repeatedly. For example, a dicarboxylic acid has two -COOH groups, and a diol has two -OH groups. When they react, one -OH from the acid and one -H from the alcohol combine to form water, leaving a new ester linkage. This is distinct from addition polymerisation, where unsaturated monomers simply add together without losing any atoms.

📌 Core Theory

  • Condensation polymerisation involves monomers with at least two functional groups.
  • During the reaction, monomers join together, eliminating a small molecule (e.g., water, HCl) in each linking step.
  • The most common examples involve the elimination of water, hence 'condensation'.
  • Polyesters are formed from dicarboxylic acids and diols, eliminating water to form ester linkages.
  • Polyamides are formed from dicarboxylic acids and diamines, eliminating water to form amide linkages.
  • The repeating unit in the polymer is different from the monomers, as it lacks the atoms that formed the small molecule.
  • This type of polymerisation is crucial for creating synthetic fibres like Nylon and Terylene.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

In condensation polymerisation, the joining of monomer units occurs via a condensation reaction, which is characterised by the removal of a small molecule. For instance, when a dicarboxylic acid (containing two -COOH groups) reacts with a diol (containing two -OH groups), an ester linkage (-COO-) is formed between them, and a molecule of water (H2O) is released. This reaction repeats across numerous monomer units, leading to a long polymer chain known as a polyester. Similarly, if a dicarboxylic acid reacts with a diamine (containing two -NH2 groups), an amide linkage (-CONH-) is formed, again with the elimination of water, producing a polyamide. Unlike addition polymers which contain only carbon-carbon single bonds in their backbone, condensation polymers have ester or amide linkages as part of their main chain structure, making them generally more susceptible to hydrolysis.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Identifying Monomers and Linkages in a Polyester

Problem: A condensation polymer has the repeating unit shown below (assume R and R' are hydrocarbon chains). Identify the functional groups on the monomers that would form this polymer and the small molecule eliminated.

Steps: 1. Observe the repeating unit: -COO-R'-COO-R-. 2. Identify the ester linkages (-COO-). 3. Break the ester linkages: An ester linkage is formed from a carboxylic acid (-COOH) and an alcohol (-OH). 4. Therefore, one monomer must have two carboxylic acid groups (dicarboxylic acid, e.g., HOOC-R-COOH). 5. The other monomer must have two alcohol groups (diol, e.g., HO-R'-OH). 6. The small molecule eliminated during the formation of an ester linkage is water (H2O).

Answer: The monomers would be a dicarboxylic acid (e.g., HOOC-R-COOH) and a diol (e.g., HO-R'-OH). The small molecule eliminated is water (H2O).

Teacher's Tip

To 'deconstruct' a condensation polymer, look for the linking functional groups (ester or amide) and imagine adding the atoms of the eliminated small molecule back to break the link and reveal the original functional groups on the monomers.

2. Drawing a Repeating Unit of a Polyamide (Nylon-6,6)

Problem: Nylon-6,6 is formed from hexanedioic acid (HOOC(CH2)4COOH) and 1,6-diaminohexane (H2N(CH2)6NH2). Draw the repeating unit of Nylon-6,6 and show the small molecule eliminated.

Steps: 1. Identify the functional groups: Hexanedioic acid has two -COOH groups. 1,6-diaminohexane has two -NH2 groups. 2. Consider the reaction: An acid reacts with an amine to form an amide linkage (-CONH-) and eliminate water. 3. Remove -OH from one -COOH and -H from one -NH2 to form water (H2O) and the amide linkage. 4. Join the remaining parts: HOOC-(CH2)4-C(=O)NH-(CH2)6-NH2 becomes -C(=O)-(CH2)4-C(=O)NH-(CH2)6-NH-. 5. Draw the repeating unit, ensuring the 'ends' are ready to link to more monomers. 6. The repeating unit will be: [-C(=O)-(CH2)4-C(=O)NH-(CH2)6-NH-].

Answer: The repeating unit of Nylon-6,6 is: [-CO-(CH2)4-CONH-(CH2)6-NH-]. The small molecule eliminated is H2O.

Teacher's Tip

When drawing repeating units, ensure the bonds at the 'ends' of your drawn unit indicate continuation of the polymer chain. Remember to remove the atoms of the small molecule from the functional groups before linking.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Polyesters (e.g., Terylene): Used extensively in synthetic fibres for clothing, ropes, and sails due to their strength and resistance to creasing.
  • Polyamides (e.g., Nylon): Famous for their strength, elasticity, and durability, Nylon is used in ropes, fishing lines, carpets, and parachutes.
  • Kevlar: An extremely strong polyamide used in bulletproof vests, aerospace composites, and racing car bodies.
  • Biodegradable Plastics: Some condensation polymers are designed to be biodegradable, such as polylactic acid (PLA), used in packaging and medical implants, as their ester linkages can be hydrolysed.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing condensation polymerisation with addition polymerisation: Students often forget that a small molecule is eliminated in condensation, whereas in addition polymerisation, nothing is lost.
  • ❌ Incorrectly identifying the functional groups required: For condensation, monomers must have two reactive functional groups (e.g., dicarboxylic acid, diol, diamine), not just one.
  • ❌ Forgetting to show the elimination of the small molecule (usually water) in diagrams or explanations.
  • ❌ Misidentifying the repeating unit: Students might include the atoms of the eliminated small molecule or not correctly show the repeating pattern.
Memory Anchor

Think of 'condensation' like water condensing on a cold surface – water is 'lost' or 'removed'. In condensation polymerisation, monomers link up and 'condense' by losing a small molecule like water.

Q11. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

You are given a polymer and told it is a condensation polymer. How could you experimentally determine if it is a polyester or a polyamide?

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: To determine if a condensation polymer is a polyester or a polyamide, one key difference is the presence of nitrogen in polyamides (amide linkage -CONH-) which is absent in polyesters (ester linkage -COO-). An experimental approach could involve: 1. Elemental analysis: A sample of the polymer could be analysed for the presence of nitrogen. If nitrogen is detected, it is likely a polyamide. 2. Hydrolysis: Hydrolysing the polymer (e.g., using strong acid or alkali) would break the ester or amide linkages. Polyamides would yield a diamine (which is basic) and a dicarboxylic acid, while polyesters would yield a diol (alcohol) and a dicarboxylic acid. The presence of an amine in the hydrolysis products could be confirmed by testing the pH (basic) or using an appropriate chemical test for amines.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Polyester contains ester linkages (-COO-); polyamide contains amide linkages (-CONH-).
  • ✅ Amide linkages contain nitrogen; ester linkages do not.
  • ✅ Perform a chemical test for nitrogen: e.g., burning/decomposition test to release ammonia (alkaline gas) or Mass Spectrometry.
  • ✅ Hydrolysis test: Both can be hydrolysed, but polyamides release amines (basic) and dicarboxylic acids, while polyesters release alcohols and dicarboxylic acids. Test pH of hydrolysed product.

🧩 Quick Quiz (MCQ)

1. Which statement correctly describes a key feature of condensation polymerisation? A) A) It involves monomers adding together without any atoms being lost. B) B) Small molecules, such as water, are lost during the reaction. C) C) Only one type of monomer with a single functional group is required. D) D) It results in polymers that can be easily broken down by heat.

Answer

B) B) Small molecules, such as water, are lost during the reaction. Condensation polymerisation is characterized by the joining of monomers with the simultaneous loss of a small molecule, most commonly water, as the bond forms between the monomers.

2. What type of functional groups must monomers typically possess to undergo condensation polymerisation? A) A) One functional group on each monomer. B) B) At least two functional groups on each monomer. C) C) Exactly two different functional groups on each monomer. D) D) Only double bonds between carbon atoms.

Answer

B) B) At least two functional groups on each monomer. For monomers to link repeatedly and form a polymer chain via condensation, they must each have at least two functional groups, allowing them to react at both ends.

3. During the formation of a polyester by condensation polymerisation, what small molecule is typically lost? A) A) Hydrogen gas (H₂) B) B) Oxygen gas (O₂) C) C) Water (H₂O) D) D) Carbon dioxide (CO₂)

Answer

C) C) Water (H₂O) In condensation polymerisation, especially when forming polyesters or polyamides, a hydroxyl group (-OH) from one monomer and a hydrogen atom (-H) from another typically combine to form a water molecule, which is eliminated.

4. How does condensation polymerisation fundamentally differ from addition polymerisation? A) A) Condensation polymerisation requires high temperatures, while addition polymerisation does not. B) B) In condensation polymerisation, the polymer has a higher mass than the sum of its monomers, unlike addition polymerisation. C) C) Condensation polymerisation involves the loss of a small molecule, whereas addition polymerisation does not. D) D) Addition polymerisation uses monomers with two different functional groups, while condensation polymerisation uses only one.

Answer

C) C) Condensation polymerisation involves the loss of a small molecule, whereas addition polymerisation does not. The defining characteristic of condensation polymerisation is the elimination of a small molecule (like water) when monomers join. Addition polymerisation, conversely, involves monomers adding directly to each other without any loss of atoms.

5. A student models condensation polymerisation using two different monomers. Monomer A has two -COOH groups, and Monomer B has two -OH groups. What type of polymer will be formed? A) A) Poly(ethene) B) B) Polyamide C) C) Polyester D) D) Poly(propene)

Answer

C) C) Polyester A dicarboxylic acid (two -COOH groups) reacting with a diol (two -OH groups) forms ester linkages (-COO-) and eliminates water, which is the definition of forming a polyester.

6. Consider a condensation polymer formed from monomers X and Y. If monomer X has two amine (-NH₂) groups and monomer Y has two carboxylic acid (-COOH) groups, what is the repeating linkage in the polymer? A) A) Ester linkage (-COO-) B) B) Amide linkage (-CONH-) C) C) Ether linkage (-O-) D) D) Carbon-carbon single bond (-C-C-)

Answer

B) B) Amide linkage (-CONH-) The reaction between an amine group and a carboxylic acid group results in the formation of an amide linkage (-CONH-) and the elimination of a water molecule. This forms a polyamide.

7. Which statement best describes the repeating unit in a condensation polymer? A) A) It is identical to the original monomer molecule. B) B) It is formed from the monomer units minus the atoms of the small molecule lost during polymerisation. C) C) It contains all the functional groups of the original monomers. D) D) It is an unstable intermediate that quickly breaks down.

Answer

B) B) It is formed from the monomer units minus the atoms of the small molecule lost during polymerisation. The repeating unit in a condensation polymer is derived from the monomer units but has lost the atoms that formed the small molecule (e.g., water) during the condensation reaction.

8. Nylon is an example of a polyamide. What type of functional groups would the monomers used to make nylon typically possess? A) A) Carbon-carbon double bonds. B) B) Hydroxyl groups (-OH) and carboxylic acid groups (-COOH). C) C) Amine groups (-NH₂) and carboxylic acid groups (-COOH). D) D) Only aldehyde groups (-CHO).

Answer

C) C) Amine groups (-NH₂) and carboxylic acid groups (-COOH). Polyamides like nylon are formed from monomers with amine (-NH₂) groups and carboxylic acid (-COOH) groups reacting to form amide linkages, with the loss of water.

9. A model showing two different molecules, each with two reactive ends, combining to form a longer chain and releasing a tiny sphere representing water, best illustrates which process? A) A) Cracking of hydrocarbons. B) B) Addition polymerisation. C) C) Fractional distillation. D) D) Condensation polymerisation.

Answer

D) D) Condensation polymerisation. The description clearly points to condensation polymerisation: two reactive monomers (molecules with two reactive ends) combine to form a longer chain (polymer), and a small molecule (tiny sphere representing water) is released.

10. Why is the term 'condensation' used to describe this type of polymerisation? A) A) Because the reaction must occur at low temperatures, causing condensation. B) B) Because a small molecule is 'condensed out' or eliminated from the reacting monomers. C) C) Because the resulting polymer is always a solid, condensed state. D) D) Because it involves the compression of monomers under high pressure.

Answer

B) B) Because a small molecule is 'condensed out' or eliminated from the reacting monomers. The term 'condensation' refers to the process of forming a substance from a gas or vapor, usually by cooling, which involves the removal of a small molecule. In polymerisation, it refers to the removal of a small molecule (like water) as the monomers join, similar to how water vapour condenses to liquid water.

⚡ Cram Sheet (3-3-1)

Key Facts
  • 📌 Condensation polymerisation requires monomers to each have two functional groups.
  • 📌 Monomers react by joining and simultaneously eliminating a small molecule, most commonly water.
  • 📌 The simplest condensation polymers are formed from two different monomers, each possessing two identical functional groups.
Red Flags
  • 🚩 Forgetting to explicitly state that a small molecule (e.g., water) is eliminated during the polymerisation process.
  • 🚩 Confusing condensation polymerisation with addition polymerisation, which involves one monomer type and no byproduct.
  • 🚩 Incorrectly identifying the functional groups on the monomers or failing to deduce the correct repeating unit in the polymer.
Main Takeaway

Condensation polymerisation links difunctional monomers, always producing a polymer and a small molecule byproduct.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The history of condensation polymers is rich, beginning with Wallace Carothers at DuPont in the 1930s, who famously synthesised Nylon, the first truly synthetic fibre, in 1935. His work on polyamides revolutionised textiles. Later, polyesters like Terylene (Dacron in the US) were developed in the 1940s by John Whinfield and James Dickson, expanding the range of strong synthetic fibres. These discoveries were pivotal in the development of modern polymer chemistry and led to a boom in synthetic materials.

⚖️ Ethics

The widespread use of condensation polymers, while providing numerous benefits, also raises ethical considerations. The reliance on fossil fuels for monomer production contributes to climate change. The biodegradability (or lack thereof) of many condensation polymers, particularly polyesters and polyamides, contributes to plastic waste and microplastic pollution in ecosystems. Ethical considerations include finding sustainable sources for monomers, developing truly biodegradable alternatives, and improving recycling infrastructure to minimise environmental impact.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond GCSE, condensation polymerisation can involve a wider array of functional groups (e.g., carbonates, urethanes) leading to polymers like polycarbonates and polyurethanes. The kinetics of condensation polymerisation can be studied using step-growth mechanisms. Understanding molecular weight control through stoichiometric balance of monomers is crucial for industrial polymer synthesis. Furthermore, the reversibility of some condensation reactions (e.g., hydrolysis of esters and amides) is key to the degradation of these polymers and is exploited in chemical recycling processes.

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. Polyester Formation (Esterification): 1. A lone pair of electrons on the oxygen of the alcohol's -OH group attacks the electrophilic carbon of the carboxylic acid's -COOH group. 2. A proton transfer occurs. 3. Water is eliminated as the carbon-oxygen double bond reforms, creating an ester linkage (-COO-). This process repeats at both ends of the monomers.
  2. Polyamide Formation (Amidation): 1. The lone pair of electrons on the nitrogen of the amine's -NH2 group attacks the electrophilic carbon of the carboxylic acid's -COOH group. 2. A proton transfer occurs. 3. Water is eliminated as the carbon-oxygen double bond reforms, creating an amide linkage (-CONH-). This process repeats at both ends of the monomers.

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Temperature: Often requires heating to provide activation energy and drive off the small molecule (e.g., water) to shift equilibrium towards polymer formation.
  • Catalyst: Acid catalysts (e.g., concentrated sulfuric acid) can be used to speed up esterification reactions.
  • Solvent: Depending on the solubility of monomers and polymer, suitable organic solvents may be used.
