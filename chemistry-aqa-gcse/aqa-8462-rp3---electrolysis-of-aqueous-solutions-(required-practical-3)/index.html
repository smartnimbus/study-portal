Electrolysis of Aqueous Solutions (Required Practical 3)

📖 Overview

Ever wondered how we can break down compounds using just electricity? Dive into the world of electrolysis and discover what happens when we pass current through aqueous solutions!
Level Higher
Study Time ⏱️ 75 min
🤜 In a Nutshell

Electrolysis of aqueous solutions uses electrical energy to decompose ionic compounds. The products at the inert electrodes depend on the competition between ions from the dissolved salt and from water (H+, OH-). At the cathode, the less reactive positive ion (metal or hydrogen) is reduced. At the anode, halide ions are oxidised to halogens, otherwise hydroxide ions are oxidised to oxygen. This required practical involves developing a hypothesis and experimentally identifying these products.

🧠 Conceptual Understanding

Electrolysis is a powerful process that uses electrical energy to force non-spontaneous chemical reactions to occur, specifically the decomposition of ionic compounds. When you're dealing with aqueous solutions, it's not just the ions from the dissolved salt that matter; water itself (H₂O) can also be electrolysed, producing hydrogen ions (H+) and hydroxide ions (OH-). This means there's often a 'competition' at both the positive and negative electrodes to see which ion will be discharged. At the cathode (negative electrode), the less reactive positive ion (either a metal ion or H+) will gain electrons and be reduced. At the anode (positive electrode), if halide ions (like Cl-, Br-, I-) are present, they will lose electrons and be oxidised; otherwise, hydroxide ions (or water) will be oxidised to produce oxygen gas. Understanding these rules is key to predicting the products and is the core of this required practical investigation.

📌 Core Theory

  • Electrolysis is the decomposition of an ionic compound when molten or in aqueous solution, using electrical energy.
  • Aqueous solutions contain ions from the dissolved ionic compound AND hydrogen ions (H+) and hydroxide ions (OH-) from the slight dissociation of water.
  • At the cathode (negative electrode), positive ions (cations) are attracted. Either a metal will be deposited (if the metal is less reactive than hydrogen), or hydrogen gas will be produced (if the metal is more reactive than hydrogen).
  • At the anode (positive electrode), negative ions (anions) are attracted. If halide ions (Cl-, Br-, I-) are present, they will be discharged to form halogen gas. If no halide ions are present, oxygen gas will be produced from hydroxide ions (or water).
  • Inert electrodes (e.g., graphite or platinum) are used because they do not react themselves; they simply provide a surface for electron transfer.
  • Developing a hypothesis for this investigation involves predicting the specific products formed at each electrode based on the ions present and the rules of discharge.
  • The products can be identified using chemical tests: hydrogen (lit splint 'squeaky pop'), oxygen (glowing splint relights), chlorine (damp blue litmus paper turns red, then bleaches white).

🔬 Detailed Explanation

When an electric current is passed through an aqueous solution of an ionic compound using inert electrodes, the positively charged cations move towards the negative cathode, and the negatively charged anions move towards the positive anode. The key to predicting the products lies in the competition at each electrode. At the cathode, if the metal ion is less reactive than hydrogen (e.g., copper, silver), the metal will be deposited. If the metal ion is more reactive than hydrogen (e.g., sodium, potassium), then hydrogen gas will be produced from the H+ ions in the water, as hydrogen is easier to reduce. At the anode, if halide ions are present (chloride, bromide, iodide), they will be oxidised to form the corresponding halogen gas. If no halide ions are present, then hydroxide ions (from water) will be oxidised to produce oxygen gas and water. This competition is crucial and often misunderstood. The concentration of the solution can sometimes influence the outcome, especially if the halide ion is present in very low concentration.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Predicting Products for Aqueous Copper(II) Chloride

Problem: Predict the products formed at the anode and cathode when aqueous copper(II) chloride is electrolysed using inert electrodes. Write half-equations for the reactions.

Steps: 1. Identify all ions present in aqueous copper(II) chloride: Cu²⁺(aq), Cl⁻(aq), H⁺(aq), OH⁻(aq). 2. At the cathode (negative electrode), positive ions are attracted: Cu²⁺ and H⁺. Copper is less reactive than hydrogen, so Cu²⁺ ions will be discharged. 3. Write the half-equation for the cathode: Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s). Product: Copper metal. 4. At the anode (positive electrode), negative ions are attracted: Cl⁻ and OH⁻. Halide ions (Cl⁻) are present, so Cl⁻ ions will be discharged. 5. Write the half-equation for the anode: 2Cl⁻(aq) → Cl₂(g) + 2e⁻. Product: Chlorine gas. 6. Therefore, copper metal forms at the cathode and chlorine gas forms at the anode.

Answer: Cathode: Copper metal (Cu(s)). Anode: Chlorine gas (Cl₂(g)). Half-equations: Cathode: Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s); Anode: 2Cl⁻(aq) → Cl₂(g) + 2e⁻.

Teacher's Tip

Always list all four possible ions (metal, anion, H+, OH-) first before applying the discharge rules.

2. Predicting Products for Aqueous Sodium Sulfate

Problem: Predict the products formed at the anode and cathode when aqueous sodium sulfate is electrolysed using inert electrodes. Write half-equations for the reactions.

Steps: 1. Identify all ions present in aqueous sodium sulfate: Na⁺(aq), SO₄²⁻(aq), H⁺(aq), OH⁻(aq). 2. At the cathode (negative electrode), positive ions are attracted: Na⁺ and H⁺. Sodium is more reactive than hydrogen, so H⁺ ions will be discharged. 3. Write the half-equation for the cathode: 2H⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → H₂(g). Product: Hydrogen gas. 4. At the anode (positive electrode), negative ions are attracted: SO₄²⁻ and OH⁻. No halide ions are present, so OH⁻ ions will be discharged. 5. Write the half-equation for the anode: 4OH⁻(aq) → O₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) + 4e⁻. Product: Oxygen gas. 6. Therefore, hydrogen gas forms at the cathode and oxygen gas forms at the anode.

Answer: Cathode: Hydrogen gas (H₂(g)). Anode: Oxygen gas (O₂(g)). Half-equations: Cathode: 2H⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → H₂(g); Anode: 4OH⁻(aq) → O₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) + 4e⁻.

Teacher's Tip

Remember that sulfate ions (SO₄²⁻) and nitrate ions (NO₃⁻) are never discharged at the anode; instead, oxygen gas is produced.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Chlor-alkali industry: Electrolysis of concentrated aqueous sodium chloride (brine) produces chlorine gas (for sterilisation, plastics), hydrogen gas (fuel), and sodium hydroxide (soap, paper).
  • Electroplating: Using electrolysis to coat a cheaper metal with a thin layer of a more expensive or corrosion-resistant metal (e.g., silver plating cutlery, chromium plating car parts).
  • Purification of copper: Impure copper can be purified by making it the anode in an electrolytic cell with a pure copper cathode and copper(II) sulfate electrolyte.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Forgetting about the H⁺ and OH⁻ ions from water when considering aqueous solutions.
  • ❌ Confusing which electrode is positive/negative or which process (oxidation/reduction) occurs at each.
  • ❌ Incorrectly stating that the sulfate ion (SO₄²⁻) or nitrate ion (NO₃⁻) is discharged at the anode, when it should be oxygen from OH⁻.
  • ❌ Mixing up the reactivity series rule for the cathode (less reactive metal deposited, more reactive results in hydrogen production).
  • ❌ Failing to balance half-equations correctly, especially for the production of oxygen from hydroxide ions.
Memory Anchor

PANC: Positive Anode, Negative Cathode. OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain. AQA Cathode Rule: More Reactive = Hydrogen, Less Reactive = Metal. AQA Anode Rule: Halide = Halogen, Other = Oxygen.

Q10. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

Evaluate the statement: 'The volume of gas collected at the cathode is always double the volume of gas collected at the anode during the electrolysis of any aqueous solution.'

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: The statement 'The volume of gas collected at the cathode is always double the volume of gas collected at the anode during the electrolysis of any aqueous solution' is incorrect. It is only true in specific cases, such as the electrolysis of dilute sulfuric acid or other solutions where hydrogen gas is produced at the cathode and oxygen gas at the anode (effectively electrolysing water). In these cases, hydrogen and oxygen are produced in a 2:1 molar ratio, hence a 2:1 volume ratio.

However, if the products are different, the 2:1 ratio does not hold. For example, during the electrolysis of concentrated aqueous sodium chloride, hydrogen gas is produced at the cathode, but chlorine gas is produced at the anode. The half-equations are $2H^{+}(aq) + 2e^{-} \rightarrow H_{2}(g)$ and $2Cl^{-}(aq) \rightarrow Cl_{2}(g) + 2e^{-}$. Here, hydrogen and chlorine are produced in a 1:1 molar ratio, meaning their volumes would be equal, not a 2:1 ratio.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ The statement is generally true only when H₂ and O₂ are the products (i.e., when water is effectively being electrolysed). (1 mark)
  • ✅ Example where it's true: Electrolysis of dilute sulfuric acid, where H₂ and O₂ are produced in a 2:1 ratio. (1 mark)
  • ✅ Example where it's false: Electrolysis of aqueous NaCl (concentrated), where H₂ is produced at cathode and Cl₂ at anode (not a 2:1 ratio). (1 mark)
  • ✅ Explanation: The ratio depends on the specific half-equations and products formed at each electrode, which vary with the electrolyte. (1 mark)

📋 Strategy: State if true/false, then provide one example where it holds and one clear counter-example where it doesn't, explaining why.

⚠️ Examiner Note: Students often generalise the 2:1 H2:O2 ratio, failing to consider other products like Cl2.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: Challenges students to critically analyse a statement, requiring both general knowledge and specific counter-examples.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The history of electrolysis is deeply intertwined with the development of electrochemistry. Humphry Davy, in the early 19th century, was a pioneer, using electrolysis to isolate highly reactive metals like sodium and potassium from their molten salts for the first time. Later, Michael Faraday's quantitative laws of electrolysis established fundamental relationships between the amount of electricity passed and the amount of substance produced, laying the groundwork for much of modern electrochemistry.

⚖️ Ethics

Industrial electrolysis processes, while vital for producing essential chemicals and materials, come with ethical considerations. They are often highly energy-intensive, contributing to greenhouse gas emissions if powered by fossil fuels. The production of toxic chlorine gas (from brine electrolysis) requires stringent safety measures and careful waste management to prevent environmental contamination and health risks to workers and nearby communities.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond GCSE, the prediction of electrolysis products becomes more quantitative using standard electrode potentials (E° values). The species with the most positive E° value will be reduced at the cathode, and the species with the most negative E° value (or most easily oxidised) will be oxidised at the anode. However, factors like overpotential (the extra voltage required for a reaction to occur) and concentration effects (as seen with concentrated NaCl) can cause deviations from these predictions, making the actual outcome more complex. The formation of oxygen often requires a significant overpotential compared to halide discharge.

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

Aim: To investigate what happens when aqueous solutions are electrolysed using inert electrodes and develop a hypothesis. Hypothesis Example: When aqueous copper(II) chloride is electrolysed, copper metal will be produced at the cathode and chlorine gas at the anode. Equipment: DC power supply, 2 graphite electrodes, beaker, 2 test tubes (inverted), connecting leads with crocodile clips, aqueous solutions (e.g., CuSO₄, NaCl, H₂SO₄, KI), universal indicator, splints (lit and glowing), damp blue litmus paper. Procedure: 1. Set up the apparatus: electrolyte in a beaker, two graphite electrodes partially submerged, connected to a DC power supply. 2. Place inverted test tubes over each electrode to collect any gases produced. 3. Turn on the power supply and observe changes at each electrode (e.g., gas bubbles, metal deposition). 4. Test any collected gases: hydrogen (lit splint - 'squeaky pop'), oxygen (glowing splint - relights), chlorine (damp blue litmus - red then bleaches). 5. Record all observations and results. Safety Notes: Wear eye protection at all times. Ensure good ventilation, especially when producing chlorine (toxic) or hydrogen (flammable). Handle solutions carefully as some may be irritants (e.g., copper sulfate). Disconnect the power supply before handling electrodes or solutions.

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Electrical shock: Ensure power supply is DC and switched off before touching apparatus. Use insulated wires.
  • 🛑 Corrosive/Irritant solutions: Copper(II) sulfate, concentrated acids/alkalis can be irritants or corrosive. Wear eye protection (goggles) and avoid skin contact.
  • 🛑 Toxic gas (Chlorine, Cl₂): Produced from electrolysis of chlorides. Work in a fume cupboard or well-ventilated area.
  • 🛑 Flammable gas (Hydrogen, H₂): Produced at the cathode. Keep away from naked flames, use lit splint test carefully.
  • 🛑 Hot apparatus: Electrodes and electrolyte can become warm, especially with high currents or prolonged electrolysis.

  • $$Cathode (Hydrogen gas): $2H^{+}(aq) + 2e^{-} \rightarrow H_{2}(g)$$$
  • $$Anode (Halogen gas): $2X^{-}(aq) \rightarrow X_{2}(g) + 2e^{-}$$$
  • $$Anode (Oxygen gas): $4OH^{-}(aq) \rightarrow O_{2}(g) + 2H_{2}O(l) + 4e^{-}$$$

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. At the Cathode (Negative Electrode - Reduction): Positive ions (cations) are attracted. Competition occurs between the metal ions from the dissolved salt and hydrogen ions (H⁺) from water. The ion that is easier to reduce (less reactive metal or H⁺ if metal is more reactive than H) will gain electrons and be discharged. For example, $Cu^{2+}(aq) + 2e^{-} \rightarrow Cu(s)$ or $2H^{+}(aq) + 2e^{-} \rightarrow H_{2}(g)$.
  2. At the Anode (Positive Electrode - Oxidation): Negative ions (anions) are attracted. Competition occurs between halide ions (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) and hydroxide ions (OH⁻) from water. Halide ions, if present in sufficient concentration, are preferentially oxidised to form halogen gas. If no halide ions are present, or if they are very dilute, hydroxide ions are oxidised to form oxygen gas and water. For example, $2Cl^{-}(aq) \rightarrow Cl_{2}(g) + 2e^{-}$ or $4OH^{-}(aq) \rightarrow O_{2}(g) + 2H_{2}O(l) + 4e^{-}$.

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Aqueous solution (electrolyte): The ionic compound must be dissolved in water.
  • Direct current (DC) power supply: Essential for unidirectional flow of ions and specific product formation.
  • Inert electrodes: Electrodes (e.g., graphite or platinum) that do not react with the electrolyte or products.
  • Room temperature and pressure: Typically, no special heating or pressure conditions are required for these practicals, though reaction rates increase with temperature.
