🤜 In a Nutshell

Electrolysis uses electrical energy to decompose molten ionic compounds. In the molten state, mobile ions conduct electricity, with positive metal ions (cations) moving to the negative cathode for reduction, and negative non-metal ions (anions) moving to the positive anode for oxidation. This process is vital for extracting reactive metals like aluminium and is distinguished by specific products forming at each electrode.

🧠 Conceptual Understanding

Electrolysis is the process of using electrical energy to break down (decompose) an ionic compound. For this to happen, the ions in the compound must be free to move. In solid ionic compounds, the ions are locked in a lattice and cannot move, so they don't conduct electricity. However, when an ionic compound is melted (becomes 'molten'), its ions become mobile. This molten ionic compound, called an electrolyte, can then conduct electricity. When an electric current is passed through it, the positively charged metal ions (cations) move towards the negative electrode (cathode), where they gain electrons and are reduced to form the pure metal. Simultaneously, the negatively charged non-metal ions (anions) move towards the positive electrode (anode), where they lose electrons and are oxidised to form the non-metal element. This process is crucial for extracting reactive metals like aluminium from their ores and for other industrial applications.

📌 Core Theory

  • Electrolysis is the decomposition of an ionic compound using electrical energy.
  • For electrolysis to occur, the ionic compound must be in a molten (liquid) state, allowing its ions to be free to move and carry charge.
  • The molten ionic compound is called an electrolyte.
  • The power supply provides a direct current (DC) to two inert electrodes immersed in the electrolyte.
  • The positive electrode is called the anode; the negative electrode is called the cathode.
  • At the cathode (negative electrode), positively charged metal ions (cations) migrate, gain electrons (reduction), and are deposited as neutral metal atoms.
  • At the anode (positive electrode), negatively charged non-metal ions (anions) migrate, lose electrons (oxidation), and form neutral non-metal atoms/molecules.
  • The products of electrolysis of a molten binary ionic compound are always the metal at the cathode and the non-metal at the anode.
  • Half-equations represent the electron transfer at each electrode.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

When an ionic compound like lead bromide (PbBr₂) is heated to its melting point, the strong electrostatic forces of attraction between the positive lead ions (Pb²⁺) and negative bromide ions (Br⁻) are overcome, allowing the ions to move freely. This molten state is essential because the mobile ions are what carry the electrical charge through the electrolyte. A direct current is then supplied by a power source to two graphite electrodes, which are inert, meaning they do not react themselves. The negative terminal of the power supply attracts the positive lead ions (Pb²⁺) to the cathode. Here, each lead ion gains two electrons (reduction) to form a neutral lead atom, which collects as molten lead. Concurrently, the positive terminal attracts the negative bromide ions (Br⁻) to the anode. At the anode, each bromide ion loses one electron (oxidation) to form a neutral bromine atom. These bromine atoms then combine in pairs to form diatomic bromine molecules (Br₂), which appear as brown/orange fumes. The overall reaction is driven by the external electrical energy, forcing a non-spontaneous chemical change.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Predicting Products for Molten Lead Bromide Electrolysis

Problem: Molten lead bromide (PbBr₂) is electrolysed using inert electrodes. Identify the products formed at each electrode and write the half-equations.

Steps: 1. Identify the ions present in molten lead bromide: Pb²⁺ and Br⁻. 2. Determine which electrode each ion will migrate to: Pb²⁺ (positive ion) will go to the cathode (negative electrode); Br⁻ (negative ion) will go to the anode (positive electrode). 3. Predict the reaction at the cathode: Pb²⁺ ions gain electrons (reduction) to form lead metal. Write the half-equation: Pb²⁺(l) + 2e⁻

Pb(l). 4. Predict the reaction at the anode: Br⁻ ions lose electrons (oxidation) to form bromine gas. As bromine is diatomic, two Br⁻ ions are needed for one Br₂ molecule. Write the half-equation: 2Br⁻(l)

Br₂(g) + 2e⁻. 5. State the final products: Lead metal is formed at the cathode and bromine gas is formed at the anode.

Answer: At the cathode: Lead metal (Pb). Half-equation: Pb²⁺(l) + 2e⁻

Pb(l). At the anode: Bromine gas (Br₂). Half-equation: 2Br⁻(l)

Br₂(g) + 2e⁻.

Teacher's Tip

Remember 'P-ANIC' (Positive Anode, Negative Is Cathode) and 'OIL RIG' (Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain) for electron transfer at electrodes.

2. Predicting Products for Molten Potassium Iodide Electrolysis

Problem: Describe the electrolysis of molten potassium iodide (KI), stating the products at each electrode and writing relevant half-equations.

Steps: 1. Identify the ions present: K⁺ and I⁻. 2. Positive ions (K⁺) go to the negative electrode (cathode). Negative ions (I⁻) go to the positive electrode (anode). 3. At the cathode, K⁺ ions gain electrons (reduction): K⁺(l) + e⁻

K(l). The product is molten potassium. 4. At the anode, I⁻ ions lose electrons (oxidation): 2I⁻(l)

I₂(g) + 2e⁻. The product is iodine gas (which would solidify upon cooling to purple solid or vaporize to purple gas).

Answer: At the cathode, molten potassium is formed: K⁺(l) + e⁻

K(l). At the anode, iodine gas is formed: 2I⁻(l)

I₂(g) + 2e⁻.

Teacher's Tip

Always remember that non-metal elements like halogens (Cl₂, Br₂, I₂) are diatomic, so you need two halide ions to form one molecule and lose two electrons in total for the half-equation.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Extraction of reactive metals: The Hall-Heroult process uses electrolysis to extract aluminium from its molten ore (aluminium oxide dissolved in cryolite) because aluminium is too reactive to be extracted by reduction with carbon.
  • Production of pure sodium and chlorine: Molten sodium chloride can be electrolysed to produce liquid sodium metal and chlorine gas, both valuable industrial chemicals.
  • Purification of metals: Impure metals can be refined by electrolysis, where the impure metal acts as the anode and the pure metal is deposited at the cathode.
  • Electroplating: Although typically done in aqueous solutions, the principle of using electricity to deposit a layer of metal (like chromium onto steel) relies on the movement and reduction of metal ions.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Thinking that electrolysis can happen with solid ionic compounds – ions must be mobile.
  • ❌ Confusing the products at the anode and cathode, e.g., putting the metal at the anode.
  • ❌ Forgetting that non-metal products like halogens (Cl, Br, I) form diatomic molecules (Cl₂, Br₂, I₂).
  • ❌ Assuming water is present; this process is for molten compounds, not aqueous solutions, so H⁺ and OH⁻ ions are not involved.
  • ❌ Not balancing the charges and electrons correctly in half-equations.
Memory Anchor

P-ANIC: Positive Anode, Negative Is Cathode. OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons), Reduction Is Gain (of electrons). This helps you remember which reaction happens at which electrode.

Q1. (3 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~3 min)

Explain why solid lead bromide does not conduct electricity, but molten lead bromide does.

If the temperature drops below its melting point, the ions (Zn²⁺ and I⁻) would solidify into a rigid lattice and become immobile. Without mobile ions, the molten compound would no longer conduct electricity, and the electrolysis process would stop.

  • ✅ So that they can move to the electrodes and conduct electricity, allowing electrolysis to continue (1 mark).

Q12. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

What are two distinct differences between electrolysis and a chemical cell (voltaic cell)?

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Firstly, electrolysis requires an external power supply to drive a non-spontaneous chemical reaction by converting electrical energy into chemical energy. In contrast, a chemical cell (voltaic cell) generates electricity from a spontaneous chemical reaction, converting chemical energy into electrical energy. Secondly, in an electrolytic cell, the anode is the positive electrode and the cathode is the negative electrode. However, in a chemical cell, the anode is the negative electrode and the cathode is the positive electrode.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Electrolysis requires an external power supply / electrical energy input; chemical cells generate electricity (1 mark).
  • ✅ Electrolysis drives non-spontaneous reactions; chemical cells involve spontaneous reactions (1 mark).
  • ✅ In electrolysis, anode is positive and cathode is negative; in chemical cells, anode is negative and cathode is positive (1 mark).
  • ✅ In electrolysis, the purpose is to decompose a compound; in a chemical cell, it is to generate electricity (1 mark).

📋 Strategy: Compare the cells based on energy flow, spontaneity of reaction, and electrode polarity.

⚠️ Examiner Note: Students often struggle to articulate the differences clearly, especially regarding spontaneity and electrode polarity.

Q13. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

A student sets up an experiment to electrolyse molten sodium bromide (NaBr). What safety precautions should be taken given the likely products?

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Given that molten sodium bromide would produce molten sodium metal at the cathode and bromine gas at the anode, several safety precautions are vital. The experiment requires very high temperatures to keep NaBr molten, so a heat-resistant mat should be used, and the apparatus must be stable to prevent spills. Molten ionic compounds are corrosive, so eye protection (safety goggles) must be worn at all times, and tongs used for handling hot apparatus. Molten sodium is extremely reactive with water and air, posing a fire risk; therefore, the experiment should be conducted in a dry environment, and a sand bucket should be kept nearby for emergencies. Bromine gas is toxic and corrosive, necessitating the use of a fume cupboard to extract the harmful fumes and prevent inhalation. Finally, as electricity is involved, all wiring must be secure and insulated, and the power supply should be switched off before making or breaking connections.

Q14. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

An ionic compound, AZ, is electrolysed in its molten state. At the cathode, a shiny, silvery liquid metal is produced. At the anode, a pungent, green gas is produced. Identify the elements A and Z, and write the overall balanced chemical equation for the electrolysis.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: The pungent, green gas produced at the anode is characteristic of chlorine gas (Cl₂). This indicates that the anion Z⁻ is a chloride ion (Cl⁻). The production of a shiny, silvery liquid metal at the cathode suggests a reactive metal with a low melting point, such as sodium (Na). Therefore, the ionic compound AZ is likely sodium chloride (NaCl). The overall balanced chemical equation for its electrolysis is: 2NaCl(l)

2Na(l) + Cl₂(g).

  • ✅ Pungent, green gas at anode suggests Chlorine (Cl₂) (1 mark).
  • ✅ This means Z is the chloride ion (Cl⁻) (1 mark).
  • ✅ Shiny, silvery liquid metal at cathode suggests a Group 1 metal with a low melting point, e.g., Sodium (Na) or Potassium (K). Sodium is a common example. (1 mark for identifying Na/K as A).

2Na(l) + Cl₂(g) (or 2KCl(l)

🧩 Quick Quiz (MCQ)

1. When molten lead bromide (PbBr₂) is electrolysed using inert electrodes, what are the products formed at the cathode and anode, respectively? A) A) Lead at the anode, bromine at the cathode B) B) Lead at the cathode, bromine at the anode C) C) Lead(II) ions at the cathode, bromide ions at the anode D) D) Lead oxide at the cathode, bromine at the anode

Answer

B) B) Lead at the cathode, bromine at the anode In molten ionic compounds, the positive metal ions (Pb²⁺) move to the negative cathode where they gain electrons to form the metal (Pb). The negative non-metal ions (Br⁻) move to the positive anode where they lose electrons to form the non-metal (Br₂).

2. Which statement correctly describes the products formed during the electrolysis of a molten simple ionic compound? A) A) The non-metal is formed at the cathode and the metal at the anode. B) B) The metal is formed at the cathode and the non-metal at the anode. C) C) Both the metal and non-metal are formed at the cathode. D) D) Both the metal and non-metal are formed at the anode.

Answer

B) B) The metal is formed at the cathode and the non-metal at the anode. Positive metal ions are attracted to the negative cathode, where they gain electrons to form the neutral metal. Negative non-metal ions are attracted to the positive anode, where they lose electrons to form the neutral non-metal.

3. Molten sodium chloride (NaCl) is electrolysed. What are the products at the negative electrode and positive electrode, respectively? A) A) Chlorine gas at the negative electrode, sodium metal at the positive electrode B) B) Sodium metal at the negative electrode, chlorine gas at the positive electrode C) C) Sodium chloride at both electrodes D) D) Oxygen gas at the negative electrode, hydrogen gas at the positive electrode

Answer

B) B) Sodium metal at the negative electrode, chlorine gas at the positive electrode At the negative electrode (cathode), positive sodium ions (Na⁺) gain electrons to form sodium metal (Na). At the positive electrode (anode), negative chloride ions (Cl⁻) lose electrons to form chlorine gas (Cl₂).

4. During the electrolysis of molten potassium iodide (KI), what substance is produced at the anode? A) A) Potassium metal B) B) Potassium ions C) C) Iodine gas D) D) Iodide ions

Answer

C) C) Iodine gas At the anode (positive electrode), the negative iodide ions (I⁻) are attracted. They lose electrons to form iodine molecules (I₂), which is a non-metal gas/solid (depending on temperature).

5. Why must an ionic compound be molten for it to undergo electrolysis? A) A) To increase the reactivity of the ions. B) B) To allow the ions to become mobile and conduct electricity. C) C) To prevent the electrodes from corroding. D) D) To lower the energy required for the reactions.

Answer

B) B) To allow the ions to become mobile and conduct electricity. Ionic compounds in the solid state have their ions held in fixed positions within a lattice. When molten, the ions are free to move and can carry charge, thus allowing the compound to conduct electricity and undergo electrolysis.

6. Which of the following compounds, when molten, would produce magnesium metal at the cathode upon electrolysis? A) A) Magnesium sulfate B) B) Magnesium carbonate C) C) Magnesium chloride D) D) Magnesium hydroxide

Answer

C) C) Magnesium chloride For electrolysis of a molten binary ionic compound, the metal is always produced at the cathode. Magnesium chloride (MgCl₂) is a simple binary ionic compound, and when molten, Mg²⁺ ions will be attracted to the cathode to form magnesium metal. The other options are not simple binary ionic compounds.

7. If molten aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃) is electrolysed, what product would you expect to form at the anode? A) A) Aluminium metal B) B) Aluminium ions C) C) Oxygen gas D) D) Oxide ions

Answer

C) C) Oxygen gas At the anode (positive electrode), the negative oxide ions (O²⁻) are attracted. They lose electrons to form oxygen gas (O₂), which is the non-metal product.

8. Consider the electrolysis of molten calcium bromide (CaBr₂). Which half-equation represents the reaction occurring at the cathode? A) A) Ca²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Ca B) B) 2Br⁻ → Br₂ + 2e⁻ C) C) Ca → Ca²⁺ + 2e⁻ D) D) Br₂ + 2e⁻ → 2Br⁻

Answer

A) A) Ca²⁺ + 2e⁻ → CaAt the cathode (negative electrode), positive metal ions (Ca²⁺) gain electrons (reduction) to form neutral metal atoms (Ca). Option A shows this correctly.

9. What happens to the ions in a molten ionic compound during electrolysis? A) A) Both positive and negative ions move towards the cathode. B) B) Positive ions move towards the anode, negative ions move towards the cathode. C) C) Positive ions move towards the cathode, negative ions move towards the anode. D) D) The ions remain stationary as the electrons move through the melt.

Answer

C) C) Positive ions move towards the cathode, negative ions move towards the anode. Oppositely charged ions are attracted to oppositely charged electrodes. Positive metal ions (cations) move to the negative cathode, and negative non-metal ions (anions) move to the positive anode.

10. During the electrolysis of molten lithium fluoride (LiF), what are the products formed and where? A) A) Lithium at the anode, fluorine at the cathode B) B) Lithium at the cathode, fluorine at the anode C) C) Lithium fluoride at both electrodes D) D) Lithium ions and fluoride ions at the electrodes

Answer

B) B) Lithium at the cathode, fluorine at the anode Lithium ions (Li⁺) are positive, so they move to the negative cathode to gain electrons and form lithium metal. Fluoride ions (F⁻) are negative, so they move to the positive anode to lose electrons and form fluorine gas (F₂).

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

Electrolysis was pioneered in the early 19th century by scientists like Humphry Davy, who used it to isolate reactive elements like sodium, potassium, and calcium for the first time. He famously used a voltaic pile (an early battery) to decompose molten potash (potassium carbonate) and soda (sodium hydroxide). Later, in 1886, the Hall-Héroult process was independently developed by Charles Martin Hall and Paul Héroult, revolutionizing aluminium production by electrolysing aluminium oxide dissolved in molten cryolite, drastically reducing its cost and making it a common metal.

⚖️ Ethics

The industrial scale of electrolysis, particularly for reactive metals, carries significant ethical considerations. The energy demand is enormous, leading to a large carbon footprint if electricity is generated from fossil fuels. This raises questions about sustainable energy sourcing. Additionally, by-products such as corrosive gases (e.g., chlorine, fluorine compounds from cryolite) can pose environmental and health risks if not managed properly. There's an ongoing ethical debate about balancing the economic benefits of these essential materials with their environmental and social costs.

🚀 Advanced Theory

While GCSE focuses on simple predictions, A-level Chemistry delves into quantitative electrolysis using Faraday's laws (Q = It, where Q is charge, I is current, t is time, and Q = nF, where n is moles of electrons and F is Faraday's constant). It also explores standard electrode potentials, which provide a quantitative measure of how easily a substance is oxidised or reduced. These potentials allow for more precise predictions of electrode reactions, especially in complex aqueous solutions where multiple species might be present. Factors like overpotential and electrode surface area also play a role in reaction rates and product purity, which are critical in industrial applications.

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

The electrolysis of molten lead(II) bromide (PbBr₂) is a common required practical demonstration. Setup: Place solid lead(II) bromide in a ceramic crucible. Insert two inert graphite electrodes into the solid, ensuring they don't touch. Connect the electrodes to a DC power supply via crocodile clips and wires. Heat the crucible strongly using a Bunsen burner until the lead(II) bromide melts. Once molten, observe the electrodes: molten silver lead will collect at the cathode, and brown/orange bromine fumes will be seen at the anode. Safety: Wear eye protection and work in a fume cupboard due to toxic bromine fumes. Molten lead(II) bromide is corrosive, and the high temperatures pose a burn risk. Ensure electrical connections are secure and safe.

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 High temperatures: Molten ionic compounds require very high temperatures (e.g., lead bromide melts at 373 °C), posing a severe burn risk. Use heat-resistant mats, tongs, and safety goggles.
  • 🛑 Corrosive molten salts: Molten ionic compounds are often corrosive and can cause severe skin and eye damage. Wear eye protection and avoid skin contact.
  • 🛑 Toxic/Corrosive gaseous products: Products like chlorine (Cl₂), bromine (Br₂), and iodine (I₂) are toxic and corrosive gases. The experiment must be carried out in a well-ventilated fume cupboard.
  • 🛑 Reactive metal products: Metals like sodium and potassium produced at the cathode are highly reactive and can ignite or react violently with water/air. Handle with extreme care in a dry, inert atmosphere if possible.
  • 🛑 Electrical hazards: Direct current power supplies pose an electrical shock risk. Ensure all wiring is insulated and connections are secure. Switch off the power supply before adjusting apparatus.

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. At the cathode (negative electrode): Positively charged metal cations (e.g., Pb²⁺) are attracted to the negative charge. They migrate to the cathode, where they gain electrons from the external circuit (reduction) to become neutral metal atoms. These atoms then collect as molten metal.
  2. At the anode (positive electrode): Negatively charged non-metal anions (e.g., Br⁻) are attracted to the positive charge. They migrate to the anode, where they lose electrons to the external circuit (oxidation) to become neutral non-metal atoms. These atoms often combine to form diatomic molecules (e.g., Br₂).

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Molten state: The ionic compound must be heated above its melting point to allow its ions to become mobile.
  • Inert electrodes: Electrodes made of materials like graphite or platinum are used to ensure they do not react with the electrolyte or the products.
  • Direct current (DC) power supply: An external electrical power source is required to drive the non-spontaneous redox reactions.
  • Sufficient voltage: The applied voltage must be greater than the decomposition voltage of the molten compound.
  • High temperature: Maintained throughout the process to keep the electrolyte molten.
