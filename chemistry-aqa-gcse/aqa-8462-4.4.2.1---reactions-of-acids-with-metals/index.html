📖 Overview

🤜 In a Nutshell

Reactive metals react with acids to produce a salt and hydrogen gas, observable as fizzing. This is a redox reaction where the metal atoms lose electrons (oxidised) and hydrogen ions gain electrons (reduced). The hydrogen gas produced can be identified by the 'squeaky pop' test with a lit splint, and only metals more reactive than hydrogen will undergo this reaction.

🧠 Conceptual Understanding

When an acid reacts with a metal, it's essentially a competition. The acid wants to donate its hydrogen ions (H+), and reactive metals want to lose electrons to become positive ions. In this reaction, the metal atoms lose electrons to form positive metal ions, which then combine with the acid's anion to form a salt. At the same time, the hydrogen ions from the acid gain these electrons to form neutral hydrogen atoms, which pair up to become hydrogen gas (H2). This exchange of electrons means it's a redox reaction: the metal is oxidised (loses electrons) and the hydrogen ions are reduced (gain electrons). This only happens with metals above hydrogen in the reactivity series.

📌 Core Theory

  • Acids react with some metals to produce a salt and hydrogen gas.
  • The general word equation for this reaction is: Acid + Metal " Salt + Hydrogen.
  • Examples include magnesium, zinc, and iron reacting with dilute hydrochloric acid and sulfuric acid.
  • For example, magnesium + hydrochloric acid "> magnesium chloride + hydrogen.
  • Hydrogen gas can be tested for using a lit splint, which will produce a 'squeaky pop' sound.
  • These reactions are redox reactions: the metal loses electrons (oxidation) and the hydrogen ions gain electrons (reduction).
  • Only metals that are more reactive than hydrogen will react with acids to produce hydrogen gas.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

The reaction of an acid with a metal is a single displacement reaction and specifically a redox process. Consider a metal like magnesium reacting with hydrochloric acid. Magnesium atoms (Mg) are neutral, but highly reactive. The hydrochloric acid (HCl) dissociates in water to produce hydrogen ions (H+) and chloride ions (Cl-). The magnesium atoms readily lose two electrons to become Mg2+ ions. These electrons are then picked up by two H+ ions to form two neutral hydrogen atoms, which immediately combine to form a molecule of hydrogen gas (H2). The Mg2+ ions then combine with the Cl- ions to form magnesium chloride (MgCl2), which is the salt. The key is the transfer of electrons: the metal is oxidised (loss of electrons) and the hydrogen ions are reduced (gain of electrons). This reaction only proceeds if the metal is more reactive than hydrogen, allowing it to displace hydrogen from the acid.

📖 Key Definitions

Term Definition
Acid A substance that produces hydrogen ions (H+) in aqueous solution.
Salt An ionic compound formed when the hydrogen of an acid is replaced by a metal ion or an ammonium ion.
Oxidation Loss of electrons. This occurs for the metal in acid-metal reactions.
Reduction Gain of electrons. This occurs for the hydrogen ions in acid-metal reactions.
Redox Reaction A chemical reaction in which both oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously.

📚 Glossary

  • Acid — A substance that produces hydrogen ions (H+) in aqueous solution.
  • Oxidation — Loss of electrons. This occurs for the metal in acid-metal reactions.
  • Redox Reaction — A chemical reaction in which both oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously.
  • Reduction — Gain of electrons. This occurs for the hydrogen ions in acid-metal reactions.
  • Salt — An ionic compound formed when the hydrogen of an acid is replaced by a metal ion or an ammonium ion.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Writing a Balanced Equation for Magnesium and Hydrochloric Acid

Problem: Write a balanced symbol equation, including state symbols, for the reaction between solid magnesium and dilute hydrochloric acid.

Steps: 1. Identify the reactants: Magnesium (Mg) and Hydrochloric acid (HCl). 2. Identify the products: Magnesium chloride (MgCl2) and Hydrogen gas (H2). 3. Write the unbalanced equation with state symbols: Mg(s) + HCl(aq) "> MgCl2(aq) + H2(g). 4. Balance the equation by counting atoms: There are 2 Cl atoms on the right and 1 on the left, so add a '2' coefficient in front of HCl. This also balances H atoms. 5. Final balanced equation: Mg(s) + 2HCl(aq) "> MgCl2(aq) + H2(g).

Answer: Mg(s) + 2HCl(aq) "> MgCl2(aq) + H2(g)

Teacher's Tip

Always remember that hydrogen gas exists as diatomic molecules (H2), not single atoms, and ensure state symbols are included when asked.

2. Identifying Oxidation and Reduction in a Redox Reaction (HT)

Problem: In the reaction between zinc and sulfuric acid, Zn(s) + H2SO4(aq) "> ZnSO4(aq) + H2(g), identify which species is oxidised and which is reduced, explaining your answer in terms of electron transfer.

Steps: 1. Break down the reactants and products into their ionic forms where applicable: Zn(s), 2H+(aq) from H2SO4, SO42-(aq) from H2SO4, Zn2+(aq) from ZnSO4, SO42-(aq) from ZnSO4, H2(g). 2. Identify the species that change oxidation state/electron count: Zn changes from neutral atom to Zn2+ ion. H+ changes from ion to neutral H atom (in H2 molecule). 3. Write the half-equations for each species changing: Zn "> Zn2+ + 2e- and 2H+ + 2e- "> H2. 4. Apply OIL RIG: Zinc loses electrons (Zn "> Zn2+ + 2e-), so Zinc is oxidised. 5. Apply OIL RIG: Hydrogen ions gain electrons (2H+ + 2e- "> H2), so Hydrogen ions are reduced.

Answer: Zinc is oxidised because it loses electrons (from Zn to Zn2+). Hydrogen ions (H+) are reduced because they gain electrons (from H+ to H2).

Teacher's Tip

Remember 'OIL RIG' (Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain) to help you distinguish between oxidation and reduction in terms of electrons.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Corrosion of metal structures (e.g., iron bridges) by acid rain, which is dilute sulfuric or nitric acid, leading to the formation of rust and hydrogen gas.
  • The 'pickling' process in industry, where dilute acids are used to remove rust (iron oxides) and scale from the surface of metals before processes like galvanizing or electroplating.
  • Production of hydrogen gas in laboratories for various experiments or as a precursor for other chemical syntheses.
  • Some older types of batteries (e.g., lead-acid batteries) involve acid-metal reactions, though the specific mechanisms are more complex.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Believing that all metals react with acids; only metals more reactive than hydrogen do so.
  • ❌ Assuming hydrogen gas is H when it should always be H2 (a diatomic molecule).
  • ❌ Confusing oxidation (loss of electrons) with reduction (gain of electrons), particularly in Higher Tier questions.
  • ❌ Thinking that the 'fizzing' is the acid boiling, rather than the production of hydrogen gas.
Memory Anchor

Remember 'OIL RIG' to keep oxidation and reduction straight: Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons), Reduction Is Gain (of electrons). For the products, it's always 'Salt + Hydrogen' (S+H!).

Q1. (3 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~3 min)

Explain why the reaction between magnesium and hydrochloric acid is an example of a redox reaction. (Higher Tier)

Q6. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

Using a diagram, explain how the amount of hydrogen gas collected can be measured accurately in a school laboratory. (Higher Tier)

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: To accurately measure hydrogen gas, two main methods can be used: a gas syringe or displacement over water. For a gas syringe, the reaction apparatus (e.g., conical flask with metal and acid) is connected directly to the gas syringe via a delivery tube. As hydrogen is produced, it pushes the plunger of the syringe out, and the volume can be read directly from the scale on the syringe. This method is generally more accurate. Alternatively, hydrogen can be collected by displacement over water. The delivery tube from the reaction is placed under an inverted measuring cylinder or burette filled with water in a trough. As hydrogen gas is produced, it displaces the water, and its volume can be read from the scale on the measuring cylinder/burette. To ensure accuracy, especially when measuring a rate, a stopwatch should be used to record the time taken for specific volumes of gas to be collected or the volume collected within fixed time intervals. Care must be taken to ensure gas is not lost and that the syringe is free-moving or that the water level is adjusted to atmospheric pressure before reading.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Diagram showing gas syringe or displacement over water method with measurements indicated.
  • ✅ Description of gas syringe use: plunger pushed in, collected directly.
  • ✅ Description of displacement over water: inverted measuring cylinder/burette, volume read from scale.
  • ✅ Accuracy: Gas syringe is more accurate, less soluble gases preferred for displacement.
  • ✅ Time measurement: Use a stopwatch to measure volume over time.

Q8. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

A student carries out the reaction of zinc with hydrochloric acid. They notice that the rate of reaction slows down considerably over time, even though there is still plenty of zinc and acid present. Suggest and explain two possible reasons for this observation. (Hard)

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: One reason the reaction rate might slow down is that the reactants are being used up. As zinc and hydrochloric acid are consumed in the reaction, their concentrations decrease. A lower concentration means there are fewer reactant particles per unit volume, leading to less frequent successful collisions between them, thus slowing down the reaction rate. Another possible reason is that the reaction could be exothermic, producing heat. However, as the heat dissipates into the surroundings, the temperature of the reaction mixture would decrease over time. A lower temperature means the reactant particles have less kinetic energy, resulting in fewer collisions having sufficient energy to overcome the activation energy, therefore decreasing the rate of reaction.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Reason 1: Reactants are being used up.
  • ✅ Explanation 1: As acid/metal is used up, concentration decreases, leading to fewer successful collisions and slower rate.
  • ✅ Reason 2: Temperature decreases.
  • ✅ Explanation 2: Reaction is exothermic, but heat dissipates. If reaction cools, particles have less kinetic energy, fewer successful collisions.
  • ✅ Reason 3: Surface area of metal decreases.
  • ✅ Explanation 3: As metal reacts, its surface area reduces, fewer sites for reaction, slower rate.

⚡ Cram Sheet (3-3-1)

Key Facts
  • 📌 Acids react with metals more reactive than hydrogen to produce a salt and hydrogen gas.
  • 📌 For GCSE, focus on reactions of magnesium, zinc, and iron with hydrochloric or sulfuric acids.
  • 📌 These reactions are redox processes where the metal is oxidised (loses electrons) and hydrogen ions are reduced (gain electrons) (HT only).
Red Flags
  • 🚩 Less reactive metals, such as copper, do not react with dilute acids to produce hydrogen gas.
  • 🚩 Incorrectly identifying the products or failing to test for hydrogen gas (squeaky pop) will lose marks.
  • 🚩 Confusing oxidation as loss of electrons and reduction as gain of electrons is a common and penalised error (HT only).
Main Takeaway

Understand the general word equation, the specific reactants and products for common metals, and for HT, the electron transfer mechanism of these redox reactions.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The reaction of acids with metals to produce a 'flammable air' was observed by many early chemists, but it was Henry Cavendish in the 18th century who definitively identified this gas as a distinct substance, which he called 'inflammable air from metals'. Antoine Lavoisier later named it 'hydrogen' (meaning 'water-former') when he showed that water was produced when hydrogen burned in oxygen. This discovery was a significant step in developing modern chemistry, moving away from alchemical concepts and towards elemental analysis.

⚖️ Ethics

The reactivity of metals with acids has ethical implications related to environmental corrosion and waste disposal. Acid rain (containing sulfuric and nitric acids) accelerates the corrosion of metal infrastructure, like bridges and statues, requiring costly maintenance and impacting historical sites. Industrially, processes involving acid-metal reactions generate waste streams containing dissolved heavy metal ions, which can be toxic. Responsible disposal and treatment of these acidic wastes are crucial to prevent environmental pollution and harm to ecosystems and human health.

🚀 Advanced Theory

At a more advanced level, the reactivity of metals with acids is quantified using standard electrode potentials (E"). A metal will react with an acid to produce hydrogen if its standard electrode potential is more negative than that of hydrogen (0.00 V). The larger the negative value, the more readily the metal is oxidised and thus the more reactive it is. For example, Mg (E" = -2.37 V) reacts vigorously, while Cu (E" = +0.34 V) does not. This electrochemical series provides a quantitative basis for the qualitative reactivity series and underpins the operation of electrochemical cells and batteries.

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

To react a metal with an acid and collect the hydrogen gas, a common setup involves a test tube or conical flask containing the metal and dilute acid, sealed with a bung and a delivery tube. The delivery tube is then inverted into a trough of water, leading to an inverted test tube or measuring cylinder to collect the hydrogen gas by downward displacement of water. Safety precautions include wearing eye protection, using a small amount of acid, ensuring good ventilation, and keeping naked flames away from the area due to hydrogen's flammability.

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Corrosive/Irritant Acids: Dilute hydrochloric acid and sulfuric acid can cause skin and eye irritation or burns.
  • 🛑 Flammable Hydrogen Gas: Hydrogen is highly flammable and explosive when mixed with air. Naked flames must be avoided.
  • 🛑 Suck-back: If heated, sudden cooling can cause apparatus to suck back water, potentially cracking hot glass.

🔧 Equipment

  • Test tubes / Conical flask
  • Bung with delivery tube
  • Trough (e.g., plastic basin)
  • Water
  • Inverted test tube / Measuring cylinder (for gas collection)
  • Metal samples (magnesium ribbon, zinc granules, iron filings)
  • Dilute hydrochloric acid
  • Dilute sulfuric acid
  • Lit splint
  • Safety goggles
  • Fume cupboard (recommended)

📐 Key Formulae

  • $$\text{Acid} + \text{Metal} \rightarrow \text{Salt} + \text{Hydrogen}$$
  • $$\text{Mg(s)} + 2\text{HCl(aq)} \rightarrow \text{MgCl}{2}\text{(aq)} + \text{H}$$}\text{(g)
  • $$\text{Zn(s)} + \text{H}{2}\text{SO}}\text{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{ZnSO{4}\text{(aq)} + \text{H}$$}\text{(g)
  • $$\text{Fe(s)} + \text{H}{2}\text{SO}}\text{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{FeSO{4}\text{(aq)} + \text{H}$$}\text{(g)
  • $$\text{Oxidation half-equation (general): } \text{M(s)} \rightarrow \text{M}^{n+}\text{(aq)} + n\text{e}^{-}$$
  • $$\text{Reduction half-equation: } 2\text{H}^{+}\text{(aq)} + 2\text{e}^{-} \rightarrow \text{H}_{2}\text{(g)}$$

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

    1. Dissociation of Acid: The acid dissociates in water, producing hydrogen ions (H+). For example, HCl(aq) "> H+(aq) + Cl-(aq).
    1. Oxidation of Metal: The reactive metal atoms lose electrons to form positive metal ions. This is oxidation. For example, Mg "> Mg2+ + 2e-.
    1. Reduction of Hydrogen Ions: The hydrogen ions from the acid gain the electrons released by the metal to form neutral hydrogen atoms. These then combine to form diatomic hydrogen molecules (H2). This is reduction. For example, 2H+ + 2e- "> H2.
    1. Formation of Salt: The newly formed metal ions combine with the anion from the acid (e.g., Cl- or SO42-) to form the ionic salt.

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Temperature: Typically room temperature, though increasing temperature will increase reaction rate.
  • Pressure: Standard atmospheric pressure.
  • Catalyst: No specific catalyst is usually required for these direct reactions.
  • Solvent: Aqueous (water is the solvent for the acid).
  • Concentration: Dilute acids are typically used; higher concentrations increase reaction rate.

🔦 Element Spotlight

Hydrogen (H): As a product of these reactions, hydrogen is a diatomic gas (H2) at standard conditions. It is the lightest element and highly flammable, hence its characteristic 'squeaky pop' test. In acids, it exists as a positively charged ion (H+), essentially a proton, which readily accepts electrons to become a neutral atom. This electron affinity is why it is reduced in reactions with more reactive metals.
