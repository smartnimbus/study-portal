🤜 In a Nutshell

Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons crucial as fuels and organic building blocks, characterised by their tetrahedral carbon centres and CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ general formula. Their physical properties, like boiling point, are governed by London forces, while their low reactivity stems from strong, non-polar bonds. They undergo combustion and radical substitution reactions, the latter being limited by forming product mixtures.

🧠 Conceptual Understanding

Alkanes are the simplest family of organic compounds, serving as the foundation upon which more complex organic chemistry is built. They are saturated hydrocarbons, meaning they contain only carbon and hydrogen atoms, and all their carbon-carbon bonds are single bonds. This 'saturation' makes them relatively unreactive compared to other organic families. Understanding alkanes is crucial because they are excellent examples of molecules where shape, bonding, and intermolecular forces directly dictate physical properties like boiling point. They are also vital as fuels and as starting materials in many industrial processes. By studying alkanes, you learn about fundamental concepts such as tetrahedral geometry, sigma bonds, London forces, and radical reactions, which are building blocks for understanding all other organic compounds.

📌 Core Theory

  • Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons, containing only single C–C and C–H bonds, which are sigma (σ) bonds.
  • The σ-bonds in alkanes allow for free rotation, meaning the molecule can adopt various conformations.
  • Each carbon atom in an alkane is surrounded by four electron pairs (in four single bonds), resulting in a tetrahedral shape around each carbon with bond angles of approximately 109.5°, as explained by electron pair repulsion theory.
  • The boiling points of alkanes increase with increasing carbon-chain length due to stronger induced dipole–dipole interactions (London forces) between larger molecules, which require more energy to overcome.
  • Branching in alkanes reduces the surface area for contact between molecules, leading to weaker London forces and thus lower boiling points compared to their straight-chain isomers.
  • Alkanes have low reactivity with many reagents due to the high bond enthalpy (strength) and very low polarity of their C–C and C–H σ-bonds.
  • Complete combustion of alkanes, typically used as fuels, produces carbon dioxide (CO₂) and water (H₂O) when oxygen is in plentiful supply.
  • Incomplete combustion occurs in a limited supply of oxygen, producing carbon monoxide (CO) (a toxic gas), carbon (soot), and water, which poses significant health dangers.
  • Alkanes react with halogens (like chlorine and bromine) in the presence of ultraviolet (UV) radiation via a radical substitution mechanism.
  • The radical substitution mechanism involves three stages: initiation (homolytic fission of the halogen molecule to form radicals), propagation (radicals react to form products and new radicals), and termination (radicals combine to form stable molecules).
  • A limitation of radical substitution in synthesis is the formation of a mixture of organic products due to multiple substitutions and reactions at different positions on the carbon chain.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

Alkanes are fundamentally defined by their single covalent bonds and their composition of only carbon and hydrogen. Each carbon atom forms four single covalent bonds, leading to a tetrahedral arrangement around it. This geometry is a direct consequence of the repulsion between electron pairs, which arrange themselves to be as far apart as possible. The non-polar nature and high bond strength of C-C and C-H bonds explain the generally unreactive nature of alkanes. However, they readily undergo combustion, releasing significant energy, making them crucial as fuels. The extent of oxygen available determines whether combustion is complete (producing CO₂ and H₂O) or incomplete (producing CO and/or C, which are hazardous). Furthermore, alkanes can react with halogens through a specific radical substitution mechanism initiated by UV light. This process involves highly reactive radicals, which are species with unpaired electrons, leading to a chain reaction. While synthetically useful, this mechanism often yields a mixture of products, limiting its precision in forming a single desired compound.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Explaining Boiling Point Differences in Alkanes

Problem: Explain why pentane (C₅H₁₂) has a higher boiling point than 2,2-dimethylpropane (C₅H₁₂), even though they have the same molecular formula.

Steps: 1. Identify that both pentane and 2,2-dimethylpropane are isomers, meaning they have the same molecular formula but different structural formulae. 2. Draw or visualise the structures: Pentane is a straight-chain alkane, while 2,2-dimethylpropane is a highly branched alkane (a central carbon bonded to four methyl groups). 3. Consider the type of intermolecular forces present: Both are non-polar alkanes, so the only intermolecular forces are induced dipole–dipole interactions (London forces). 4. Relate structure to the strength of London forces: Straight-chain pentane molecules can pack more closely together and have a larger surface area for contact between molecules, allowing for more effective London forces. 5. Relate branching to the strength of London forces: 2,2-dimethylpropane molecules are more spherical and branched, which reduces the surface area available for close contact between molecules, thus leading to weaker London forces. 6. Conclude how intermolecular force strength affects boiling point: Stronger London forces in pentane require more energy to overcome during boiling, resulting in a higher boiling point. Weaker London forces in 2,2-dimethylpropane require less energy, resulting in a lower boiling point.

Answer: Pentane has a higher boiling point than 2,2-dimethylpropane because although they are isomers with the same molecular formula and molecular mass, their structures differ. Pentane is a straight-chain alkane, allowing its molecules to pack closely together and maximise the surface area for contact between molecules. This leads to stronger induced dipole–dipole interactions (London forces). 2,2-dimethylpropane, being a highly branched molecule, has a more spherical shape, which reduces the effective surface area for intermolecular contact. Consequently, the London forces between 2,2-dimethylpropane molecules are weaker. More energy is required to overcome the stronger intermolecular forces in pentane, resulting in its higher boiling point.

Teacher's Tip

Always link the type of intermolecular force (London forces for alkanes) to the structure (chain length, branching) and then to the energy required to overcome them, which dictates the boiling point.

2. Writing a Balanced Equation for Complete Combustion

Problem: Write a balanced chemical equation for the complete combustion of propane (C₃H₈).

Steps: 1. Write out the reactants: propane (C₃H₈) and oxygen (O₂). 2. Write out the products of complete combustion: carbon dioxide (CO₂) and water (H₂O). The unbalanced equation is: C₃H₈ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O. 3. Balance the carbon atoms: There are 3 carbons on the left, so place a '3' in front of CO₂ on the right: C₃H₈ + O₂ → 3CO₂ + H₂O. 4. Balance the hydrogen atoms: There are 8 hydrogens on the left, so place a '4' in front of H₂O on the right (4 × 2 = 8): C₃H₈ + O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O. 5. Balance the oxygen atoms: Count oxygen atoms on the right: (3 × 2 in CO₂) + (4 × 1 in H₂O) = 6 + 4 = 10 oxygen atoms. Therefore, 10 oxygen atoms are needed on the left. Since oxygen exists as O₂, place a '5' in front of O₂ (5 × 2 = 10): C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O. 6. Verify all atoms are balanced: Carbon: 3 on left, 3 on right. Hydrogen: 8 on left, 8 on right. Oxygen: 10 on left, 10 on right. The equation is balanced.

Answer: C₃H₈(g) + 5O₂(g) → 3CO₂(g) + 4H₂O(l)

Teacher's Tip

When balancing combustion equations, always balance carbon first, then hydrogen, and finally oxygen. This order often simplifies the process.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Fuels: Alkanes are the primary components of fossil fuels such as natural gas (mainly methane), petrol (gasoline), diesel, and kerosene. Their high energy content upon combustion makes them essential for heating, transport, and electricity generation.
  • Lubricants: Longer-chain alkanes, like those found in lubricating oils and greases, are used to reduce friction between moving parts in machinery due to their low reactivity and slippery nature.
  • Raw Materials for Polymers: Alkanes can be cracked into smaller alkenes (unsaturated hydrocarbons), which are then used as monomers to produce polymers like poly(ethene) (polyethylene) and poly(propene) (polypropylene).
  • Solvents: Some alkanes (e.g., hexane) are used as non-polar solvents in laboratories and industries for dissolving greases, oils, and other non-polar substances where water is ineffective.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing homolytic fission (forming radicals) with heterolytic fission (forming ions).
  • ❌ Believing alkanes are reactive because they contain C–C bonds (they are unreactive due to high bond enthalpy and low polarity).
  • ❌ Not understanding that branching decreases boiling points by reducing surface contact for London forces, rather than increasing them due to increased complexity.
  • ❌ Incorrectly stating conditions for radical substitution (e.g., heat instead of UV light).
  • ❌ Forgetting the full range of products possible in radical substitution reactions due to further substitution or reaction at different carbon atoms.
Memory Anchor

For radical substitution, remember 'IPT': Initiation (makes radicals), Propagation (radicals make products and new radicals), Termination (radicals combine).

Q1. (3 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~3 min)

Explain, in terms of electron pair repulsion, why the bond angle around each carbon atom in an alkane is approximately 109.5°.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Each carbon atom in an alkane is surrounded by four electron pairs (in the four single covalent bonds). According to VSEPR theory, these electron pairs repel each other. To minimise this repulsion, they arrange themselves as far apart as possible in a three-dimensional space, which results in a tetrahedral arrangement. This tetrahedral geometry gives bond angles of approximately 109.5°.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Each carbon atom is surrounded by four bonding pairs of electrons.
  • ✅ These electron pairs repel each other equally.
  • ✅ They arrange themselves as far apart as possible to minimise repulsion, leading to a tetrahedral shape with 109.5° bond angles.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: Ensure students link 'four electron pairs' to 'minimising repulsion' and then to 'tetrahedral' and '109.5°'. This is a common explanation question.

Q3. (3 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~3 min)

Explain why alkanes generally have low reactivity with many reagents.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Alkanes exhibit low reactivity because their C–C and C–H bonds are very strong, possessing high bond enthalpies, which require a significant amount of energy to break. Furthermore, the electronegativity difference between carbon and hydrogen is very small, making the C–H bonds almost non-polar. C–C bonds are entirely non-polar. This lack of polarity means there are no δ+ or δ- centres that would attract nucleophiles (electron-rich species) or electrophiles (electron-deficient species), which are common in many reaction mechanisms.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ C–C and C–H bonds have high bond enthalpies (are strong).
  • ✅ Requires a large amount of energy to break these bonds.
  • ✅ C–C bonds are non-polar, and C–H bonds have very low polarity, so they are not readily attacked by nucleophiles or electrophiles.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: Students often just say 'they are stable'. Encourage them to explain why they are stable (strong bonds, low polarity).

Q1. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

A student attempts to synthesise 1-chloropropane from propane using radical substitution with chlorine. Explain why a pure sample of 1-chloropropane is difficult to obtain.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Obtaining a pure sample of 1-chloropropane from the radical substitution of propane with chlorine is difficult due to several limitations of the reaction. Firstly, propane has different types of hydrogen atoms (primary on C1/C3 and secondary on C2). During propagation, a chlorine radical can abstract a hydrogen from either the C1 (or C3) or the C2 position, leading to the formation of both 1-chloropropane and its constitutional isomer, 2-chloropropane. Secondly, once a chloropropane molecule is formed, it can undergo further radical substitution with more chlorine radicals. This results in the formation of polyhalogenated products, such as dichloropropanes, trichloropropanes, and so on. The reaction is largely indiscriminate. All these different organic products have very similar physical properties, especially boiling points, making their separation by techniques like fractional distillation challenging and inefficient.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Propagation steps can lead to substitution at different carbon atoms (C1 or C2 in propane).
  • ✅ This produces a mixture of constitutional isomers, e.g., 1-chloropropane and 2-chloropropane.
  • ✅ Further substitution can occur on the already halogenated products.
  • ✅ This leads to polyhalogenated products, e.g., dichloropropanes, trichloropropanes.
  • ✅ Separating these products is difficult due to similar physical properties (e.g., boiling points).

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This is a key 'limitation' question. Ensure students cover both 'different positions' and 'multiple substitutions', and the consequence of difficult separation.

Q3. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

Explain why the combustion of alkanes is considered an exothermic reaction, relating this to bond breaking and bond making.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: The combustion of alkanes is an exothermic reaction because the overall process involves a net release of energy. Energy is always required to break existing bonds in the reactants (C–C and C–H bonds in the alkane, and O=O bonds in oxygen); this is an endothermic process. Conversely, energy is always released when new bonds are formed in the products (C=O bonds in carbon dioxide and O–H bonds in water); this is an exothermic process. In the combustion of alkanes, the total energy released during the formation of the stronger bonds in CO₂ and H₂O is significantly greater than the total energy absorbed to break the bonds in the alkane and oxygen. This difference results in a net release of energy to the surroundings, which is observed as heat and light, characterising the reaction as exothermic.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Energy is required to break bonds in reactants (C-C, C-H, O=O) – an endothermic process.
  • ✅ Energy is released when new bonds are formed in products (C=O in CO₂, O-H in H₂O) – an exothermic process.
  • ✅ The energy released from forming new bonds is greater than the energy absorbed for breaking bonds.
  • ✅ Resulting in a net release of energy (exothermic reaction).

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This is a fundamental energy change question. Stress the comparison of energy absorbed vs. energy released. Students often just say 'releases heat'.

Q4. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

Evaluate the effectiveness of radical substitution as a synthetic method for producing specific halogenoalkanes in industry.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Radical substitution is generally considered to be a poor synthetic method for producing specific halogenoalkanes in industry, primarily due to a lack of selectivity. Firstly, the reaction lacks regioselectivity; if an alkane has different types of hydrogen atoms (e.g., primary, secondary, or tertiary), the halogen radical can abstract any of these hydrogens. This results in the formation of a mixture of structural isomers (e.g., 1-chloropropane and 2-chloropropane from propane). Secondly, the reaction also lacks control over the extent of substitution. Once a hydrogen atom is replaced by a halogen, the resulting halogenoalkane is still susceptible to further radical substitution, leading to the formation of di-, tri-, and even tetrahalogenated products. These mixtures of isomers and multi-substituted products have very similar physical properties, such as boiling points, making their separation by fractional distillation extremely difficult and economically unfeasible on an industrial scale. Therefore, while useful for understanding mechanisms, it is rarely the method of choice for targeted synthesis.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Low effectiveness/poor synthetic route for specific products.
  • ✅ Lack of regioselectivity: Reaction occurs at various positions on the carbon chain.
  • ✅ Results in a mixture of structural isomers (e.g., 1-chloropropane and 2-chloropropane).
  • ✅ Lack of control over extent of substitution: Multiple substitutions can occur.
  • ✅ Leads to a mixture of mono-, di-, tri-halogenated products.
  • ✅ Separation of these mixtures is difficult and costly due to similar boiling points, leading to low yields of the desired product.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: A strong answer will explicitly state the two main limitations (regioselectivity, extent of substitution) and the practical consequence (difficult/costly separation, low yield).

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Beyond their combustion products, the extraction and transport of alkanes (crude oil and natural gas) carry significant environmental impacts. Drilling operations, particularly offshore, can lead to habitat disruption and destruction for marine life. The risk of accidental oil spills during extraction, transportation (via pipelines or tankers), or refining is a major concern, causing devastating pollution to marine and coastal ecosystems. Oil spills coat wildlife, impairing their ability to fly, swim, and regulate body temperature, leading to mass mortality. They also contaminate beaches, wetlands, and fishing grounds, impacting biodiversity and local economies. Furthermore, the extraction and processing of natural gas can lead to the release of methane, a potent greenhouse gas, into the atmosphere, contributing to climate change. The extensive infrastructure required, such as pipelines, roads, and processing plants, also contributes to habitat fragmentation and land degradation.

Q8. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

Explain, with reference to the specific stages, how the presence of nitrogen dioxide (NO₂) can deplete the ozone layer through radical reactions.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: While often associated with CFCs, nitrogen dioxide (NO₂) can indirectly contribute to ozone depletion through radical reactions, though its direct impact is usually less pronounced than chlorine radicals. NO₂ is formed from nitrogen monoxide (NO) which is produced in internal combustion engines at high temperatures (N₂ + O₂ → 2NO). NO then reacts with oxygen to form NO₂. NO₂ itself can photolyse under UV light: Initiation: NO₂ + UV → NO• + O• This O• radical can then react with O₂ to form O₃, or participate in other cycles. More significantly, NO can react with ozone directly in a catalytic cycle: Propagation 1: NO• + O₃ → NO₂• + O₂Propagation 2: NO₂• + O• → NO• + O₂Net reaction: O₃ + O• → 2O₂In this cycle, NO• acts as a catalyst, regenerated in the second step, allowing it to destroy many ozone molecules. Additionally, NO₂ can react with ClO• radicals (formed from CFCs) to form chlorine nitrate (ClONO₂), which acts as a temporary reservoir for chlorine, but can later release the chlorine radical again.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Initiation: NO₂ can be formed from combustion in engines (N₂ + O₂ → 2NO, then 2NO + O₂ → 2NO₂).
  • ✅ NO₂ absorbs UV light and breaks into NO• and O• radicals.
  • ✅ Propagation 1: Cl• (from CFCs) + O₃ → ClO• + O₂
  • ✅ Propagation 2: ClO• + O• → Cl• + O₂ (this regenerates the Cl radical)
  • ✅ Net effect: O₃ + O• → 2O₂ (ozone depletion)
  • ✅ NO acts as a catalyst in a similar cycle or can react with ClO• to form ClONO₂ (temporary reservoir).

Q9. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

Butane is used as a fuel in camping stoves. Explain the potential dangers if the stove is used in a poorly ventilated tent, including relevant chemical equations.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: If a butane camping stove is used in a poorly ventilated tent, the oxygen supply will be limited. This leads to the incomplete combustion of butane, rather than complete combustion. The chemical equation for the incomplete combustion producing carbon monoxide is: 2C₄H₁₀(g) + 9O₂(g) → 8CO(g) + 10H₂O(l) The primary danger arises from the production of carbon monoxide (CO). Carbon monoxide is a highly toxic, colourless, and odourless gas, making it very difficult to detect. When inhaled, CO binds irreversibly to haemoglobin in red blood cells, forming carboxyhaemoglobin. This prevents the haemoglobin from binding to and transporting oxygen around the body, leading to oxygen deprivation in tissues and organs. Symptoms include headache, dizziness, nausea, and eventually unconsciousness and death. Additionally, incomplete combustion can also produce fine particles of carbon (soot), which can cause respiratory irritation and contribute to global dimming, though the immediate danger from CO is far greater.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ In a poorly ventilated tent, oxygen supply is limited.
  • ✅ This leads to incomplete combustion of butane.
  • ✅ Equation for incomplete combustion: 2C₄H₁₀ + 9O₂ → 8CO + 10H₂O (or producing carbon soot).
  • ✅ Produces carbon monoxide (CO), which is a highly toxic gas.
  • ✅ CO binds irreversibly to haemoglobin, preventing oxygen transport.
  • ✅ Can lead to unconsciousness and death.
  • ✅ Soot (carbon) can also be produced, causing respiratory irritation and leaving deposits.

Q10. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

Discuss the reasons why the boiling point of 2,2,3-trimethylbutane is lower than that of its straight-chain isomer, heptane.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Both 2,2,3-trimethylbutane and heptane are alkanes with the same molecular formula (C₇H₁₆), meaning they are constitutional isomers. Therefore, the only intermolecular forces present between their molecules are London forces (induced dipole–dipole interactions). Heptane is a straight-chain alkane, allowing its molecules to pack together efficiently and providing a large surface area for intermolecular contact, leading to relatively strong London forces. In contrast, 2,2,3-trimethylbutane is a highly branched alkane. Its compact, more spherical shape significantly reduces the effective surface area available for contact between adjacent molecules. This reduction in surface contact results in weaker London forces between 2,2,3-trimethylbutane molecules. Consequently, less energy is required to overcome these weaker forces during boiling, leading to a lower boiling point for 2,2,3-trimethylbutane compared to heptane.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Both are alkanes, so only London forces (induced dipole–dipole interactions) are present.
  • ✅ Heptane is a straight-chain alkane, allowing for more extensive surface contact between molecules.
  • ✅ 2,2,3-trimethylbutane is highly branched, giving it a more compact/spherical shape.
  • ✅ This reduced surface area for intermolecular contact leads to weaker London forces in 2,2,3-trimethylbutane, requiring less energy to overcome, resulting in a lower boiling point.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: A clear explanation of how branching reduces surface area and therefore weakens London forces is vital. Avoid simply stating 'branching lowers boiling point' without the 'why'.

🧩 Quick Quiz (MCQ)

1. Which of the following statements correctly describes alkanes? A) They are unsaturated hydrocarbons containing at least one C=C double bond. B) They are saturated hydrocarbons containing only C-C and C-H single bonds. C) They are compounds that always contain a hydroxyl (-OH) group. D) They are highly polar molecules due to the electronegativity difference between carbon and hydrogen.

Answer

B) They are saturated hydrocarbons containing only C-C and C-H single bonds. Alkanes are defined as saturated hydrocarbons, meaning they contain only single covalent bonds between carbon atoms and between carbon and hydrogen atoms, forming C-C and C-H sigma bonds.

2. What is the typical shape and bond angle around each carbon atom in an alkane molecule, such as methane? A) Trigonal planar with a bond angle of 120° B) Linear with a bond angle of 180° C) Tetrahedral with a bond angle of 109.5° D) Pyramidal with a bond angle of 107°

Answer

C) Tetrahedral with a bond angle of 109.5° Around each carbon atom in an alkane, there are four electron pairs (in the C-C and C-H bonds). According to VSEPR theory, these electron pairs repel each other to be as far apart as possible, resulting in a tetrahedral shape with bond angles of approximately 109.5°.

3. Why do alkanes with longer carbon chains generally have higher boiling points? A) Longer chains have stronger covalent bonds that require more energy to break. B) Longer chains have more hydrogen bonds between molecules. C) Longer chains have larger surface areas, leading to stronger induced dipole-dipole interactions (London forces). D) Longer chains are more polar, resulting in stronger permanent dipole-dipole interactions.

Answer

C) Longer chains have larger surface areas, leading to stronger induced dipole-dipole interactions (London forces). Alkanes are non-polar molecules, so the only intermolecular forces present are induced dipole-dipole interactions (London forces). Longer carbon chains have a larger surface area and more electrons, leading to more frequent and stronger temporary dipoles, hence stronger London forces that require more energy to overcome, resulting in higher boiling points.

4. Which of the following C5H12 isomers would have the lowest boiling point? A) Pentane B) 2-methylbutane C) 2,2-dimethylpropane D) All C5H12 isomers have the same boiling point.

Answer

C) 2,2-dimethylpropane Branching in alkanes reduces the surface area available for intermolecular contact between molecules, weakening the induced dipole-dipole interactions (London forces). 2,2-dimethylpropane is the most branched isomer of C5H12, therefore it has the lowest boiling point.

5. Alkanes are generally unreactive with many common reagents. What is the primary reason for this low reactivity? A) They can easily form hydrogen bonds. B) They possess strong C=C double bonds. C) The high bond enthalpy and very low polarity of the C-C and C-H sigma bonds. D) They are strong acids, making them resistant to nucleophilic attack.

Answer

C) The high bond enthalpy and very low polarity of the C-C and C-H sigma bonds. The C-C and C-H bonds in alkanes are strong (high bond enthalpy) and have very low polarity. This makes them difficult to break and less susceptible to attack by polar reagents or electrophiles/nucleophiles.

6. What are the main products of the complete combustion of an alkane, such as methane (CH4)? A) Carbon (soot) and water B) Carbon monoxide and water C) Carbon dioxide and water D) Carbon dioxide and hydrogen

Answer

C) Carbon dioxide and water Complete combustion of any alkane in a sufficient supply of oxygen produces carbon dioxide and water as the only products.

7. Why is the incomplete combustion of alkane fuels considered dangerous? A) It releases large amounts of sulfur dioxide, causing acid rain. B) It produces a significant amount of toxic soot (carbon particles). C) It generates carbon monoxide, a highly poisonous gas. D) It consumes oxygen at a faster rate than complete combustion, leading to oxygen depletion.

Answer

C) It generates carbon monoxide, a highly poisonous gas. Incomplete combustion occurs in a limited supply of oxygen and can produce carbon monoxide (CO), which is a colourless, odourless, and highly toxic gas that binds irreversibly to haemoglobin in the blood, preventing oxygen transport.

8. What conditions are required for the reaction between an alkane and chlorine (radical substitution)? A) High temperature and pressure only B) Aqueous solution at room temperature C) Presence of a transition metal catalyst D) Ultraviolet (UV) radiation

Answer

D) Ultraviolet (UV) radiation The reaction of alkanes with halogens like chlorine or bromine occurs via a radical substitution mechanism, which is initiated by homolytic fission of the halogen molecule caused by ultraviolet (UV) radiation.

9. Which of the following steps is an example of an 'initiation' step in the radical substitution mechanism of methane with chlorine? A) CH4 + Cl• → •CH3 + HCl B) Cl• + Cl• → Cl2 C) Cl2 → 2Cl• D) •CH3 + Cl2 → CH3Cl + Cl•

Answer

C) Cl2 → 2Cl• The initiation step in radical substitution involves the homolytic fission of the halogen molecule (e.g., Cl2) into two halogen radicals (Cl•) by ultraviolet light.

10. What is a major limitation of using radical substitution to synthesize a specific halogenoalkane? A) The reaction only occurs at extremely high temperatures. B) It is a very slow reaction, making it impractical. C) It produces a mixture of organic products, making separation difficult. D) It only works with fluorine, not other halogens.

Answer

C) It produces a mixture of organic products, making separation difficult. During radical substitution, once a halogenoalkane is formed, further substitution can occur on the same molecule, leading to di-substituted, tri-substituted, and even tetra-substituted products. This results in a mixture of organic products, making it difficult to isolate a single desired halogenoalkane.

Alkanes are fundamental saturated hydrocarbons, primarily used as fuels due to their combustibility, and their low reactivity with many reagents stems from the high enthalpy and low polarity of their sigma bonds.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The study of alkanes began with the early days of organic chemistry in the 19th century, particularly with the discovery and isolation of components from petroleum. August Kekulé, famous for the benzene structure, also contributed significantly to the theory of carbon's valency and the ability of carbon atoms to link together, forming chains and rings, which laid the groundwork for understanding alkanes. The development of crude oil refining processes in the late 19th and early 20th centuries highlighted the commercial importance of alkanes as fuels and raw materials, driving further research into their properties and reactions.

⚖️ Ethics

The widespread use of alkanes as fossil fuels presents significant ethical dilemmas. Their combustion releases large amounts of CO₂, contributing to climate change, a global issue with disproportionate impacts on vulnerable communities. The extraction processes (e.g., fracking, offshore drilling) raise concerns about environmental degradation, water contamination, and ecosystem disruption. There are ethical considerations regarding resource depletion, energy justice (equitable access to energy), and the transition to renewable energy sources, which often involves balancing economic stability with environmental responsibility and intergenerational equity.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond basic tetrahedral geometry, alkanes exhibit conformational isomerism due to free rotation around C–C single bonds. Concepts like Newman projections and Sawhorse projections are used to visualise and analyse different conformations (e.g., staggered vs. eclipsed) and their relative energies. This leads to an understanding of torsional strain and steric strain, which dictate the preferred conformations of larger alkanes. In advanced organic synthesis, C–H activation, a field that aims to selectively functionalise strong C–H bonds, offers a highly active area of research to overcome the inherent low reactivity of alkanes without resorting to unselective radical processes.

🔦 Element Spotlight

Carbon is the central element in alkanes, exhibiting its remarkable ability to form four strong covalent bonds. Its electron configuration (1s²2s²2p²) allows for sp³ hybridisation, leading to the characteristic tetrahedral geometry around each carbon atom in alkanes. This versatility in bonding, particularly its ability to catenate (form long chains and rings with itself), is what underpins the vast field of organic chemistry. In alkanes, carbon's moderate electronegativity means its bonds with hydrogen are largely non-polar, contributing to the overall stability and low reactivity of the molecules.
