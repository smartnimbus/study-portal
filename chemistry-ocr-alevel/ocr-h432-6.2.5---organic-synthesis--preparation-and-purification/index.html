🧠 Conceptual Understanding

Organic synthesis is the deliberate construction of organic compounds through chemical reactions. It's like molecular Lego, where chemists use known reactions to transform one functional group into another, or to connect smaller molecules into larger, more complex structures. This topic is crucial because it underpins the creation of almost all manufactured organic materials, from life-saving drugs to synthetic fabrics. It combines theoretical knowledge of reaction mechanisms and functional group chemistry with practical laboratory skills to successfully prepare and purify target compounds. Understanding multi-stage synthetic routes involves not just knowing individual reactions, but also thinking strategically about the order of steps, yields, atom economy, and the overall efficiency and practicality of the synthesis.

📌 Core Theory

  • Organic preparation involves setting up apparatus for reactions, commonly using Quickfit glassware for heating under reflux and distillation.
  • Heating under reflux allows reactions to be heated for prolonged periods without loss of volatile reactants or products.
  • Distillation is used to separate a product from a reaction mixture based on differences in boiling points.
  • Purification of an organic solid often involves filtration under reduced pressure (Büchner filtration) to isolate the crude product.
  • Recrystallisation is a key technique for purifying impure organic solids, relying on differential solubility in a hot solvent.
  • Measurement of melting points is used to assess the purity of a recrystallised organic solid, as pure substances have sharp melting points.
  • For organic molecules with multiple functional groups, each group's individual reactions and properties must be identified and predicted.
  • Multi-stage synthetic routes involve a sequence of reactions, each transforming a functional group or adding structural complexity, to reach a desired target molecule.
  • Efficiency in synthesis is evaluated using concepts like percentage yield (actual product obtained versus theoretical maximum) and atom economy (the proportion of reactant atoms incorporated into the desired product).

🔬 Detailed Explanation

Organic synthesis is a blend of scientific understanding and practical skill. When designing a synthetic route, chemists consider the starting materials available, the functional groups present, and the desired functional groups and overall structure of the target molecule. Each step in a multi-stage synthesis must be carefully chosen to achieve a specific transformation while avoiding unwanted side reactions or protecting other functional groups if necessary. Techniques like heating under reflux are vital for increasing reaction rates without losing volatile components, ensuring maximum yield. Distillation separates components based on volatility, often used after reflux to isolate the crude product. For solid products, filtration under reduced pressure provides efficient separation, followed by recrystallisation, which capitalises on the principle that most substances are more soluble in hot solvents than cold ones. Impurities typically remain in solution upon cooling or are insoluble when the desired product dissolves. Finally, a sharp melting point range is indicative of high purity, confirming the success of the purification process. Understanding the reactivity of different functional groups is paramount, especially when planning multi-stage syntheses where the order of reactions can dictate the feasibility and success of the overall route.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Designing a Two-Stage Synthetic Route

Problem: Outline a two-stage synthetic route to convert ethene (CH₂=CH₂) into ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH). State the reagents and conditions for each step.

Steps: 1. Step 1: Convert ethene to ethanol. This is an electrophilic addition reaction. 2. Reagent: Steam (H₂O(g)) 3. Conditions: High temperature (300 °C), high pressure (60-70 atm), concentrated phosphoric(V) acid catalyst (H₃PO₄). 4. Reaction: CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O(g) → CH₃CH₂OH₅. Step 2: Convert ethanol to ethanoic acid. This is an oxidation reaction. 6. Reagent: Acidified potassium dichromate(VI) (K₂Cr₂O₇/H₂SO₄) or acidified potassium permanganate(VII) (KMnO₄/H₂SO₄). 7. Conditions: Heat under reflux for complete oxidation. 8. Reaction: CH₃CH₂OH + 2[O] → CH₃COOH + H₂O

Answer: Stage 1: Ethene to Ethanol. Reagent: Steam. Conditions: 300 °C, 60-70 atm, concentrated H₃PO₄ catalyst. Stage 2: Ethanol to Ethanoic Acid. Reagent: Acidified potassium dichromate(VI) (or acidified potassium permanganate(VII)). Conditions: Heat under reflux.

Teacher's Tip

When designing synthetic routes, work backwards from the target molecule to simpler precursors, and then forwards from the starting material. Always consider functional group transformations.

2. Calculating Percentage Yield

Problem: In an experiment, 7.4 g of butan-1-ol (Mᵣ = 74.12 g/mol) was reacted with excess concentrated sulfuric acid to produce 5.6 g of an alkene mixture. Assuming the primary product is but-1-ene (Mᵣ = 56.11 g/mol), calculate the percentage yield of but-1-ene.

Steps: 1. Write the balanced chemical equation for the reaction: CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₂OH → CH₃CH₂CH=CH₂ + H₂O. 2. Calculate the moles of butan-1-ol used: Moles = Mass / Mᵣ. 3. Moles of butan-1-ol = 7.4 g / 74.12 g/mol = 0.0998 mol. 4. From the stoichiometry (1:1 ratio), the theoretical moles of but-1-ene produced is 0.0998 mol. 5. Calculate the theoretical mass of but-1-ene: Theoretical mass = Moles × Mᵣ. 6. Theoretical mass of but-1-ene = 0.0998 mol × 56.11 g/mol = 5.60 g. 7. Calculate the percentage yield: Percentage yield = (Actual yield / Theoretical yield) × 100%. 8. Percentage yield = (5.6 g / 5.60 g) × 100% = 100%.

Answer: Percentage yield = 100%.

Teacher's Tip

Carefully distinguish between actual yield (what you measured) and theoretical yield (what you calculated should have been produced). Ensure the stoichiometry is correct.

3. Purifying an Organic Solid by Recrystallisation

Problem: Describe the steps involved in purifying an impure sample of aspirin (a solid) using recrystallisation, including how to assess its purity.

Steps: 1. Choose a suitable solvent: The solvent should dissolve the crude solid when hot but not when cold, and impurities should either be insoluble in the hot solvent or remain soluble in the cold solvent. 2. Dissolve the impure solid: Add the minimum amount of hot solvent to the crude aspirin in a conical flask to completely dissolve it. Heat gently if necessary. 3. Hot filter (optional): If there are insoluble impurities, hot filter the solution through a pre-heated funnel to remove them. 4. Cool the solution: Allow the hot, saturated solution to cool slowly, ideally in an ice bath, to induce the formation of pure crystals. Scratching the side of the flask with a glass rod can help initiate crystallisation. 5. Filter under reduced pressure: Once crystals have formed, filter them using a Büchner funnel and flask with reduced pressure to separate the crystals from the cold solvent (filtrate), which contains soluble impurities. 6. Wash the crystals: Wash the collected crystals with a small amount of ice-cold pure solvent to remove any adhering impurities. 7. Dry the crystals: Dry the purified crystals thoroughly, for example, by leaving them in a desiccator, on filter paper in a warm oven, or between sheets of filter paper. 8. Assess purity: Determine the melting point of the dried crystals. A pure sample will melt sharply over a narrow temperature range, close to the literature value. Impurities broaden the melting point range and lower the melting point.

Answer: The steps involve dissolving the impure solid in minimum hot solvent, hot filtering (if needed), slow cooling to recrystallise, filtering under reduced pressure, washing with cold solvent, and drying. Purity is assessed by a sharp melting point close to the literature value.

Teacher's Tip

The key to good recrystallisation is using the minimum volume of hot solvent and allowing slow cooling for large, pure crystals to form. Washing with cold solvent prevents dissolving the purified product.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing reflux with distillation: Reflux returns vapours to the flask, distillation separates components by collecting vapours.
  • ❌ Using too much solvent in recrystallisation: This leads to poor yield, as less product crystallises out upon cooling.
  • ❌ Washing recrystallised crystals with hot solvent: This will redissolve the product, leading to significant loss of yield.
  • ❌ Not understanding the purpose of each practical step: Each step (e.g., reflux, washing, drying) has a specific chemical or physical reason.
  • ❌ Mistaking percentage yield for atom economy: Yield relates to how much product is obtained, atom economy to how efficiently reactants are converted into the desired product, minimising waste.
Memory Anchor

Think of organic synthesis as baking a complex cake: each ingredient is a reactant, each mixing or heating step is a reaction, and the oven (reflux/distillation) provides the conditions. Purification is like letting the cake cool and decorating it nicely to make it presentable!

Draw a labelled diagram of the Quickfit apparatus used for heating under reflux.

The pure compound and the impurity will not melt simultaneously, resulting in a gradual softening and melting over a wider temperature span, rather than a sharp, distinct melting point characteristic of a pure substance.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Impurities disrupt the regular crystal lattice structure of the pure compound.
  • ✅ Less energy is required to break these disrupted intermolecular forces, leading to a lower melting point.
  • ✅ The impurity and pure substance will not melt at the same temperature, causing melting to occur over a range rather than sharply at a single temperature.

✏️ Answer: To confirm the presence of both a hydroxyl (-OH) group and a carbonyl (C=O) group in the same molecule using chemical tests, a systematic approach is needed.

First, for the hydroxyl (-OH) group: Add a small piece of sodium metal to the compound. If an -OH group is present, vigorous effervescence will be observed due to the production of hydrogen gas. (Alternatively, the addition of phosphorus pentachloride (PCl₅) would produce steamy white fumes of HCl gas).

Second, for the carbonyl (C=O) group: Add 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazine (2,4-DNPH) solution to the compound. The formation of an orange or yellow precipitate would confirm the presence of a carbonyl group, indicating either an aldehyde or a ketone.

To further distinguish between an aldehyde and a ketone, a test like Tollens' reagent or Fehling's solution could be used. If the compound gives a silver mirror with Tollens' reagent or a brick-red precipitate with Fehling's solution, an aldehyde group is present. If neither test gives a positive result after the 2,4-DNPH test, then a ketone is present.

Care must be taken that one test does not interfere with or mask the other. For instance, the 2,4-DNPH test is generally quite robust, while the sodium test should be performed separately or carefully to avoid complications from any side reactions.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Test for -OH: Add a small piece of sodium metal; effervescence (H₂ gas) indicates an -OH group. (Alternatively, add PCl₅; steamy fumes indicate -OH).
  • ✅ Test for C=O: Add 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazine (2,4-DNPH) solution; an orange/yellow precipitate confirms a C=O group (aldehyde or ketone).
  • ✅ Distinguish aldehyde/ketone: Use Tollens' reagent (silver mirror for aldehyde) or Fehling's solution (brick-red precipitate for aldehyde).
  • ✅ Considerations: Ensure the chosen tests don't interfere with each other or mask the presence of the other group. Order of tests might be important.

A student attempts to synthesise propanone from propan-2-ol using acidified potassium dichromate(VI) and heating under reflux. They obtain a good yield but their product has a broadened melting point range. What is the most likely reason for this, and how could they improve the purity?

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Propan-2-ol is a secondary alcohol, and its oxidation product, propanone, is a ketone. Ketones are typically liquid at room temperature (propanone has a boiling point of 56 °C, and a melting point of -95 °C). Therefore, the most likely reason the student observed a 'broadened melting point range' is that they were trying to determine the melting point of a liquid, or they mistook another solid impurity for the product. Propanone does not have a melting point that can be easily observed or used as a purity check in this manner.

To improve the purity of the propanone, the student should purify it via simple distillation. Propanone (b.p. 56 °C) would be distilled off, separating it from any unreacted propan-2-ol (b.p. 82 °C) and water. The purity of the collected distillate could then be assessed by measuring its boiling point, which should be sharp and match the literature value for propanone, or by using spectroscopic techniques such as IR or NMR.

Q8. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

A student aims to synthesise chloroethane from ethanol. Outline the steps involved, including the reagents and conditions, and a brief description of how the product could be isolated and purified. Include any key safety considerations.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Synthesis of chloroethane from ethanol typically involves reacting ethanol with concentrated hydrochloric acid in the presence of an anhydrous zinc chloride catalyst. The reaction is: CH₃CH₂OH + HCl → CH₃CH₂Cl + H₂O. The mixture is usually heated gently or under reflux to drive the reaction forward.

Isolation and Purification: 1. Distillation: As chloroethane is volatile (b.p. 12.3 °C), it can be distilled from the reaction mixture, often directly as it forms, or after a initial work-up. It might be collected in an ice-cooled receiver. If it forms a separate organic layer, it can be separated using a separating funnel. 2. Washing: The organic layer (containing chloroethane) is washed with dilute sodium hydrogen carbonate (NaHCO₃) solution to neutralise any residual unreacted HCl acid and remove any sulfur dioxide if thionyl chloride was used. This removes water-soluble impurities. 3. Drying: The organic layer is then dried using an anhydrous drying agent, such as anhydrous magnesium sulfate (MgSO₄) or calcium chloride (CaCl₂), to remove any dissolved water. 4. Redistillation: Finally, the dried chloroethane can be redistilled to obtain a pure sample with a sharp boiling point.

Safety Considerations: * Concentrated HCl: Corrosive. Handle in a fume cupboard with gloves and eye protection. * Zinc Chloride: Corrosive and irritant. * Chloroethane: Highly volatile and flammable. Handle in a fume cupboard, away from naked flames. Vapours can form explosive mixtures with air. Use of a heating mantle or water bath is essential for heating.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Reaction: Ethanol + HCl (or PCl₅/SOCl₂ for better yield).
  • ✅ Reagents & Conditions: Ethanol, concentrated HCl, ZnCl₂ catalyst, heat (e.g., warm, or heat under reflux depending on method). (Alternatively, PCl₅ at room temp or SOCl₂ with gentle heating).
  • ✅ Isolation: Chloroethane is volatile, so typically distil the product off. It may form a separate organic layer.
  • ✅ Purification: Wash with water (removes acid), then NaHCO₃ solution (neutralises excess acid), then saturated NaCl solution (salting out). Dry with anhydrous drying agent. Redistil.
  • ✅ Safety: Concentrated acids are corrosive. Chloroethane is volatile and flammable. Fume cupboard, eye protection, gloves.

Q9. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

Consider the synthesis of an ester, ethyl ethanoate, from ethanol and ethanoic acid. Outline the reagents, conditions, isolation, and purification of the product. Explain the role of each purification step.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: The synthesis of ethyl ethanoate involves the esterification of ethanoic acid with ethanol. The reagents are ethanoic acid, ethanol, and concentrated sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) as a catalyst. The reaction is typically carried out by heating the mixture under reflux to increase the reaction rate and prevent the loss of volatile reactants/products. The reaction is reversible.

Isolation and Purification: 1. Initial separation: After reflux, the mixture is cooled and transferred to a separating funnel. The organic layer (containing ethyl ethanoate) is less dense than the aqueous layer (containing unreacted acid, alcohol, water, and catalyst) and will form the top layer. The aqueous layer is run off. 2. Washing with sodium carbonate/hydrogen carbonate: The organic layer is then washed with dilute sodium carbonate (Na₂CO₃) or sodium hydrogen carbonate (NaHCO₃) solution. This neutralises and removes any unreacted ethanoic acid and the sulfuric acid catalyst, both of which are soluble in the aqueous phase and would contaminate the ester. CO₂ gas will be evolved. 3. Washing with water/brine: The organic layer is washed with distilled water or saturated sodium chloride (brine) solution to remove any remaining dissolved salts or water. Brine can also help 'salt out' the ester, reducing its solubility in the aqueous phase and improving separation. 4. Drying: The organic layer is then transferred to a dry conical flask, and an anhydrous drying agent, such as anhydrous magnesium sulfate (MgSO₄) or calcium chloride (CaCl₂), is added. This absorbs any remaining dissolved water, making the ester opaque then clear when dry. 5. Redistillation: Finally, the dried ester is carefully distilled. Ethyl ethanoate has a boiling point of 77 °C. The fraction boiling at this temperature is collected, ensuring a pure sample, separated from any remaining alcohol, acid, or higher boiling point impurities.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Reagents: Ethanol, ethanoic acid, concentrated H₂SO₄ catalyst.
  • ✅ Conditions: Heat under reflux.
  • ✅ Isolation: Use a separating funnel to separate the organic layer (ester) from the aqueous layer (acid, alcohol, water).
  • ✅ Purification (Washing 1): Wash organic layer with dilute Na₂CO₃/NaHCO₃ solution to remove unreacted ethanoic acid and catalyst.
  • ✅ Purification (Washing 2): Wash with water or brine to remove dissolved salts/further water.
  • ✅ Drying: Add anhydrous drying agent (e.g., MgSO₄) to remove water.
  • ✅ Final purification: Redistil to collect pure ethyl ethanoate (b.p. 77 °C).

Q10. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

An organic compound contains a chiral centre. Explain what a chiral centre is and why its presence is significant in organic synthesis, particularly for pharmaceuticals.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: A chiral centre is a carbon atom bonded to four different atoms or groups of atoms. The presence of a chiral centre means that the molecule can exist as two non-superimposable mirror images, known as enantiomers. These enantiomers have identical physical properties (like melting point or boiling point) but can differ in their interaction with plane-polarised light and, critically, with other chiral molecules.

In organic synthesis, especially for pharmaceuticals, the presence of chiral centres is highly significant because biological systems (enzymes, receptors) are themselves chiral. This means that often, only one enantiomer of a drug will have the desired therapeutic effect, while the other enantiomer may be inactive, less effective, or even harmful (e.g., thalidomide). Therefore, pharmaceutical synthesis often requires stereoselective synthesis — methods designed to produce predominantly or exclusively one specific enantiomer — to ensure the efficacy and safety of the drug product.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ A chiral centre (or stereocentre) is a carbon atom bonded to four different groups.
  • ✅ This leads to the existence of enantiomers (non-superimposable mirror images).
  • ✅ Enantiomers can have different biological activities (e.g., one therapeutic, one inactive or toxic).
  • ✅ Therefore, synthesis must be stereoselective to produce only the desired enantiomer, crucial for drug efficacy and safety.

🧩 Quick Quiz (MCQ)

1. Why is heating under reflux often preferred over direct heating in the preparation of some organic compounds? A) It allows for faster evaporation of the product. B) It ensures that volatile reactants and products are not lost from the reaction mixture. C) It prevents the reaction vessel from overheating. D) It makes it easier to monitor the reaction temperature.

Answer

B) It ensures that volatile reactants and products are not lost from the reaction mixture. Heating under reflux involves continuously boiling and condensing volatile substances back into the reaction vessel, preventing their loss while maintaining a high temperature for the reaction.

2. In the purification of an organic liquid, what is the primary purpose of simple distillation? A) To remove insoluble solid impurities. B) To separate liquids with significantly different boiling points. C) To increase the yield of the desired product. D) To convert a liquid product into a solid.

Answer

B) To separate liquids with significantly different boiling points. Distillation separates liquids based on their boiling points. The desired product can be vaporized and condensed, separating it from unreacted reagents or impurities with different boiling points.

3. Which of the following statements best describes the primary goal of recrystallisation in organic synthesis? A) To convert a liquid organic product into a solid form. B) To separate a mixture of organic liquids with different boiling points. C) To purify an impure solid compound by selectively dissolving and then precipitating the desired compound. D) To determine the melting point of a solid organic compound.

Answer

C) To purify an impure solid compound by selectively dissolving and then precipitating the desired compound. Recrystallisation is a purification technique for solids. It involves dissolving the impure solid in a minimum amount of hot solvent, then cooling to allow the pure compound to crystallise out, leaving soluble impurities in solution.

4. How is the melting point used to assess the purity of an organic solid after recrystallisation? A) A pure sample will melt over a broad temperature range. B) A pure sample will have a lower melting point than an impure sample. C) A pure sample will melt sharply at a specific temperature or over a narrow range. D) The melting point determines the yield of the recrystallisation process.

Answer

C) A pure sample will melt sharply at a specific temperature or over a narrow range. Pure crystalline solids melt sharply at a distinct, characteristic temperature or over a very narrow temperature range. Impurities typically broaden the melting range and depress the melting point.

5. What is a significant advantage of using filtration under reduced pressure (Büchner funnel and flask) compared to gravity filtration in organic synthesis? A) It allows for the separation of immiscible liquids. B) It is faster and more efficient for separating a solid product from a solvent. C) It is specifically designed for filtering hot solutions during recrystallisation. D) It prevents the loss of volatile solvents during filtration.

Answer

B) It is faster and more efficient for separating a solid product from a solvent. Filtration under reduced pressure creates a pressure difference that pulls the solvent through the filter paper more quickly, making the separation of solid products from solvents much faster and more efficient.

6. An organic molecule contains both a hydroxyl (-OH) group and a carboxyl (-COOH) group. Which of the following statements about this molecule is most likely true? A) It will react with both sodium carbonate and ethanol. B) It will only react with a strong oxidizing agent. C) It will act as a base due to the presence of -OH. D) It will be insoluble in water.

Answer

A) It will react with both sodium carbonate and ethanol. The carboxyl (-COOH) group is acidic and will react with sodium carbonate. The carboxyl group can also react with an alcohol like ethanol via esterification.

7. A compound with the formula CH3CH(OH)COOH is reacted with an excess of ethanol in the presence of an acid catalyst. Which functional group(s) will react in this process? A) Only the hydroxyl (-OH) group. B) Only the carboxyl (-COOH) group. C) Both the hydroxyl (-OH) and the carboxyl (-COOH) groups. D) Neither functional group will react.

Answer

B) Only the carboxyl (-COOH) group. Esterification occurs between a carboxylic acid and an alcohol. In this reaction, the carboxyl (-COOH) group of the given compound will react with ethanol (an alcohol) to form an ester. The hydroxyl (-OH) group within the compound itself is an alcohol, but it will not react with another alcohol (ethanol) in an esterification reaction.

C) Alcohol In Step 1, the hydration of an alkene (prop-1-ene) forms an alcohol (propan-2-ol, following Markovnikov's rule). Therefore, Product A is an alcohol.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The history of organic synthesis is rich, dating back to Friedrich Wöhler's 1828 synthesis of urea from inorganic precursors, which debunked the 'vital force' theory. This opened the floodgates for chemists to synthesise organic compounds in the lab. Key figures like William Perkin, who synthesised the first synthetic dye (mauveine), pioneered industrial organic synthesis. The 20th century saw a revolution with the development of sophisticated reaction mechanisms and catalysts, enabling the synthesis of highly complex natural products and pharmaceuticals. Total synthesis, the complete synthesis of complex molecules from simple, readily available precursors, became a hallmark of organic chemistry, with Nobel Prizes awarded to chemists like Robert Burns Woodward and E.J. Corey for their groundbreaking work.

⚖️ Ethics

Ethical considerations in organic synthesis primarily revolve around Green Chemistry principles. This includes designing syntheses that minimise waste (high atom economy), use less hazardous chemical syntheses, design safer chemicals, use safer solvents and auxiliary substances, increase energy efficiency, use renewable feedstocks, and prevent accidents. For example, the pharmaceutical industry faces immense pressure to develop more sustainable synthetic routes that reduce environmental impact while maintaining drug efficacy and affordability. The ethical responsibility extends to ensuring the safety of workers, the public, and the environment throughout the chemical production lifecycle.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond introductory concepts, advanced organic synthesis delves into stereoselective and stereospecific reactions, where the creation of specific enantiomers or diastereomers is controlled. This is critical for drug synthesis due to the chiral nature of biological systems. Protecting groups are also a key advanced concept, used when a molecule has multiple reactive functional groups and only one needs to be modified. A protecting group temporarily blocks a functional group, preventing it from reacting, and is then removed later in the synthesis. Furthermore, the use of transition metal catalysts (e.g., palladium-catalysed cross-coupling reactions like Heck, Suzuki, Sonogashira) has revolutionised the formation of carbon-carbon bonds, enabling the construction of intricate molecular architectures with high efficiency and selectivity.

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

Heating under Reflux:

  1. Set up the Quickfit apparatus: A round-bottom flask (with magnetic stirrer bar if needed) is clamped securely. A Liebig condenser is fitted vertically into the neck of the flask.
  2. Add reactants and solvent to the flask.
  3. Attach rubber tubing to the condenser: Water enters at the lower inlet and exits from the upper outlet.
  4. Heat the flask using a heating mantle or water bath (for flammable liquids). Avoid naked flames unless specified for non-flammable solvents.
  5. Adjust heating so the mixture boils gently, and a 'reflux ring' (condensation line) is visible about one-third of the way up the condenser. Continue heating for the specified reaction time.

Distillation:

  1. Set up the Quickfit apparatus: A distillation flask (or round-bottom flask) is clamped, containing the reaction mixture and anti-bumping granules. A thermometer is placed in the neck of the flask such that its bulb is level with the side arm leading to the condenser.
  2. A Liebig condenser is attached horizontally, cooled by water entering at the lower inlet and exiting at the upper outlet.
  3. A receiver adapter leads to a collection flask (e.g., conical flask or round-bottom flask), which may be cooled in an ice bath for volatile products.
  4. Heat the distillation flask gently. The liquid will boil, vapours rise, condense in the condenser, and the distillate flows into the collection flask.
  5. Collect the fraction that distils over a narrow temperature range corresponding to the boiling point of the desired product.

Filtration under Reduced Pressure (Büchner Filtration):

  1. Set up: A Büchner funnel is placed into the neck of a Büchner flask (or filter flask). A piece of filter paper is placed in the Büchner funnel, ensuring it covers all holes.
  2. Attach the side arm of the Büchner flask to a vacuum pump or water aspirator via thick-walled tubing.
  3. Moisten the filter paper with a small amount of the solvent used in the precipitation/recrystallisation, and turn on the vacuum to ensure the paper seals properly.
  4. Pour the mixture containing the solid crystals and solvent into the Büchner funnel.
  5. Wash the collected crystals with a small amount of ice-cold pure solvent to remove adhering impurities.
  6. Continue drawing air through the crystals for a few minutes to partially dry them.

Recrystallisation:

  1. Choose a suitable solvent (dissolves solid when hot, not when cold; impurities either insoluble hot or soluble cold).
  2. Dissolve the impure solid in the minimum volume of hot solvent in a conical flask.
  3. (Optional) If insoluble impurities are present, hot filter the solution using a fluted filter paper in a pre-heated funnel.
  4. Allow the hot, clear solution to cool slowly to room temperature, then place in an ice bath to maximise crystal formation.
  5. Filter the crystals using filtration under reduced pressure.
  6. Wash the crystals with a small amount of ice-cold pure solvent.
  7. Dry the crystals thoroughly (e.g., in a desiccator, warm oven, or between sheets of filter paper).

Melting Point Determination:

  1. Load a small amount of the dried, powdered solid into a capillary tube.
  2. Place the capillary tube in a melting point apparatus.
  3. Heat slowly and observe the temperature at which the solid begins to melt and when it completely melts. Record this temperature range.
  4. Compare the observed melting point range with the literature value for the pure substance. A pure substance melts sharply over a narrow range.

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Flammable solvents (e.g., ethanol, diethyl ether): Keep away from naked flames, use heating mantles, work in fume cupboard.
  • 🛑 Corrosive acids/bases (e.g., concentrated H₂SO₄, NaOH): Wear eye protection and gloves, handle in fume cupboard, clean up spills immediately.
  • 🛑 Toxic or irritant reagents/products: Handle in a fume cupboard, avoid inhalation, wear gloves, dispose of waste properly.
  • 🛑 Heating under reflux/distillation: Ensure apparatus is securely clamped, avoid boiling dry, use anti-bumping granules.
  • 🛑 Vacuum filtration: Ensure glassware is robust enough for reduced pressure, check tubing for cracks.

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. Many organic synthesis reactions involve fundamental mechanisms like nucleophilic substitution (e.g., forming haloalkanes from alcohols), electrophilic addition (e.g., hydrating alkenes to alcohols), elimination (e.g., dehydrating alcohols to alkenes), and oxidation/reduction (e.g., alcohols to carbonyls/carboxylic acids, carbonyls to alcohols). Students should understand how these mechanisms facilitate functional group interconversions within a synthetic route. For example, the oxidation of primary alcohols involves the removal of hydrogen atoms from the carbon bearing the hydroxyl group and an adjacent carbon, and the addition of oxygen.
  2. Esterification, often used in synthesis, is typically a nucleophilic acyl substitution reaction where the carboxylic acid is protonated, making the carbonyl carbon more electrophilic, followed by nucleophilic attack by the alcohol, proton transfers, and elimination of water.

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Heating under reflux: Used for reactions requiring prolonged heating to increase reaction rate without losing volatile components (e.g., esterification, oxidation to carboxylic acids).
  • Distillation: Used for separating liquids with different boiling points, often involves heating to boiling point and collecting distillate (e.g., separating an aldehyde product from a primary alcohol reaction, purifying an ester).
  • Acid catalyst: Often required for esterification (conc. H₂SO₄) and hydration of alkenes (conc. H₃PO₄/H₂SO₄).
  • Base catalyst: Can be used for certain condensation reactions or dehydrohalogenation reactions.
  • Specific temperatures and pressures: E.g., high T and P for industrial hydration of ethene; gentle heating for controlled oxidation of primary alcohols to aldehydes.
  • Anhydrous conditions: Essential for water-sensitive reagents (e.g., Grignard reagents, acid chlorides).
  • Solvents: Choice of solvent is crucial, affecting solubility, reaction rate, and temperature (e.g., non-polar solvents for non-polar reactants, polar aprotic for Sₙ2 reactions).

🔦 Element Spotlight

While not a single element, Carbon is the backbone of all organic synthesis. Its ability to form stable bonds with itself (catenation) and with a wide variety of other elements (H, O, N, S, halogens) in diverse bonding arrangements (single, double, triple bonds) allows for the creation of an immense array of molecular structures. The understanding of carbon's valency (typically 4 bonds), hybridisation (sp³, sp², sp), and its electron configuration (1s²2s²2p²) is fundamental to predicting the structure, reactivity, and synthetic transformations of organic compounds. Organic synthesis essentially manipulates these carbon frameworks and their attached functional groups to build desired molecules.
