🧠 Conceptual Understanding

In chemistry, when we talk about a 'solution', we mean a homogeneous mixture made up of a 'solute' (the substance that is dissolved) and a 'solvent' (the substance that does the dissolving, often water). The 'concentration' of a solution tells us how much solute is dissolved in a given volume of solvent. Think of making squash: adding a little cordial to a lot of water gives a dilute solution, while adding a lot of cordial to a little water gives a concentrated one. Understanding concentration is crucial because the amount of reactant available in a solution directly affects how quickly and effectively chemical reactions occur, and it's essential for preparing solutions of a specific strength in labs and industry.

📌 Core Theory

  • A solution is formed when a solute dissolves in a solvent.
  • Concentration measures the amount of solute present in a specific volume of solution.
  • The most common unit for concentration at GCSE level is grams per cubic decimetre (g/dm3).
  • To calculate concentration, divide the mass of the solute (in grams) by the volume of the solution (in cubic decimetres).
  • A higher concentration means there is more solute dissolved in the same volume of solution.
  • (Higher Tier only) The relationship between mass of solute, volume of solution, and concentration is directly proportional. If you increase the mass of solute, the concentration increases (for a fixed volume). If you increase the volume of solution (for a fixed mass of solute), the concentration decreases.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

The concentration of a solution quantifies its 'strength' or 'dilution'. It's defined as the mass of the solute divided by the volume of the solution. The standard unit for this at GCSE is grams per cubic decimetre (g/dm3). One cubic decimetre (dm3) is equivalent to 1 litre (L), or 1000 cubic centimetres (cm3). Therefore, if you are given a volume in cm3, you must convert it to dm3 by dividing by 1000 before using it in concentration calculations. This relationship is direct: a larger mass of solute in the same volume leads to a higher concentration, and a smaller volume containing the same mass of solute also leads to a higher concentration. Conversely, diluting a solution by adding more solvent increases the volume, thus decreasing the concentration, assuming the mass of solute remains constant. This proportional relationship is key for understanding how to prepare solutions of desired strengths and how to interpret experimental results involving solutions.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Calculating Concentration

Problem: A student dissolves 15 g of sodium chloride in water to make 500 cm3 of solution. Calculate the concentration of the solution in g/dm3.

Steps: 1. Convert the volume from cm3 to dm3: 500 cm3 7 1000 = 0.5 dm3. 2. Use the formula: Concentration = Mass of solute / Volume of solution. 3. Substitute the values: Concentration = 15 g / 0.5 dm3. 4. Calculate the result.

Answer: 30 g/dm3

Teacher's Tip

Always check your units! Volume usually needs converting from cm3 to dm3.

2. Calculating Mass of Solute

Problem: A laboratory technician needs to prepare 2 dm3 of a potassium hydroxide solution with a concentration of 28 g/dm3. What mass of potassium hydroxide is needed?

Steps: 1. Rearrange the formula: Mass of solute = Concentration imes Volume of solution. 2. Substitute the given values: Mass of solute = 28 g/dm3 imes 2 dm3. 3. Calculate the result.

Answer: 56 g

Teacher's Tip

It's often easier to rearrange the formula first before substituting numbers. Remember 'Mass = Con imes Vol'.

3. Calculating Volume of Solution (Higher Tier)

Problem: How much volume (in cm3) of a 40 g/dm3 sodium carbonate solution can be made using 10 g of sodium carbonate?

Steps: 1. Rearrange the formula to solve for volume: Volume of solution = Mass of solute / Concentration. 2. Substitute the values: Volume of solution = 10 g / 40 g/dm3. 3. Calculate the volume in dm3. 4. Convert the volume from dm3 to cm3: result in dm3 imes 1000.

Answer: 250 cm3

Teacher's Tip

If the question asks for the volume in cm3, don't forget the final conversion from dm3.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Household Cleaning Products: The effectiveness of disinfectants or bleach depends on their concentration. Too dilute, and they won't kill germs; too concentrated, and they can be dangerous.
  • Medical Drips & Injections: Hospitals meticulously control the concentration of medicines in intravenous drips or injections to ensure patients receive the correct dosage, avoiding under-dosing or toxicity.
  • Food & Drink Industry: The 'strength' of coffee, juice, or cordial is a measure of its concentration. Manufacturers control this for consistent taste and safety. For example, sugar syrup concentration impacts preservation.
  • Environmental Monitoring: Scientists measure the concentration of pollutants (e.g., heavy metals, pesticides) in water bodies or air samples to assess environmental health and safety.
  • Agriculture: Farmers prepare solutions of fertilisers or pesticides at specific concentrations to ensure optimal plant growth and pest control without harming crops or the environment.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing concentration with the total amount of solute: A concentrated solution in a small beaker might have less total solute than a dilute solution in a very large swimming pool.
  • ❌ Forgetting to convert units: Students often forget to convert volume from cm3 to dm3 before performing calculations, leading to incorrect answers.
  • ❌ Thinking that adding more solvent increases concentration: Adding solvent dilutes the solution, meaning the concentration decreases.
  • ❌ Mixing up solute and solvent: Incorrectly identifying which substance is the solute and which is the solvent.
Memory Anchor

Think of making a cup of tea: The stronger the tea, the more tea 'solute' you've got in the 'solvent' (water). If you add more water, it becomes less concentrated, or 'diluted'.

Q9. (4 Marks | Calculation | ⏱️ ~4 min)

A student dissolves 12g of magnesium sulfate in 400 cm3 of water. Later, they add an additional 100 cm3 of water to the solution. Calculate the new concentration of the magnesium sulfate solution in g/dm3.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: First, calculate the total volume of the solution after adding more water:Total volume = 400 cm3 + 100 cm3 = 500 cm3.Convert this total volume to dm3: 500 cm3 7 1000 = 0.5 dm3.The mass of magnesium sulfate (solute) remains constant at 12 g.Now, calculate the new concentration:Concentration = Mass of solute / Total volume of solution = 12 g / 0.5 dm3 = 24 g/dm3.The new concentration of the magnesium sulfate solution is 24 g/dm3.

🔢 Working: 1. Initial volume = 400 cm3 2. Added volume = 100 cm3 3. Total volume = 400 + 100 = 500 cm3 4. Total volume in dm3 = 500 / 1000 = 0.5 dm3 5. New concentration = 12 g / 0.5 dm3 = 24 g/dm3

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Total volume after addition = 400 cm3 + 100 cm3 = 500 cm3 (1 mark)
  • ✅ Convert total volume to dm3: 500 cm3 = 0.5 dm3 (1 mark)
  • ✅ Mass of solute remains 12 g (1 mark)
  • ✅ Calculate new concentration: 12 g / 0.5 dm3 = 24 g/dm3 (1 mark)

Q10. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

(Higher Tier) Explain the difference between a concentrated solution and a saturated solution, using an example.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: A 'concentrated solution' simply means it has a relatively large amount of solute dissolved in a given volume of solution. It's a comparative term. For example, a 200 g/dm3 salt solution is concentrated compared to a 50 g/dm3 solution.A 'saturated solution', on the other hand, is one in which the maximum possible amount of solute has been dissolved in a given volume of solvent at a specific temperature. No more solute can dissolve at that temperature; any additional solute would remain undissolved.A concentrated solution is not necessarily saturated. You can have a concentrated solution that could still dissolve more solute (i.e., it's concentrated but unsaturated). For instance, a salt solution containing 200 g/dm3 might be considered concentrated, but if the solubility of that salt is 360 g/dm3, then the 200 g/dm3 solution is concentrated but unsaturated. A saturated solution must, by definition, also be concentrated (unless the solute has very low solubility).

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Concentrated refers to a high amount of solute per unit volume (1 mark).
  • ✅ Saturated means the maximum possible amount of solute has dissolved in a given volume of solvent at a specific temperature (no more can dissolve) (1 mark).
  • ✅ A concentrated solution might not be saturated (it could still dissolve more solute) (1 mark).
  • ✅ Example: A solution with 300 g/dm3 of salt might be concentrated, but a saturated solution might be 360 g/dm3. So, 300 g/dm3 is concentrated but unsaturated (1 mark).

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This addresses a common conceptual confusion. Emphasise that 'saturated' is a specific point where 'concentrated' is a relative description.

🧩 Quick Quiz (MCQ)

1. Which of the following correctly defines the standard unit for concentration in terms of mass per given volume at GCSE level? A) grams per litre (g/L) B) kilograms per cubic metre (kg/m³) C) grams per cubic decimetre (g/dm³) D) moles per decimetre cubed (mol/dm³)

Answer

C) grams per cubic decimetre (g/dm³) At GCSE level, concentration is typically expressed in grams per cubic decimetre (g/dm³), where 1 dm³ is equivalent to 1 litre.

2. A student dissolves 25 g of potassium chloride in enough water to make 500 cm³ of solution. What is the concentration of the solution in g/dm³? A) 0.05 g/dm³ B) 50 g/dm³ C) 12.5 g/dm³ D) 250 g/dm³

Answer

B) 50 g/dm³ First, convert the volume to dm³: 500 cm³ = 0.5 dm³. Then, concentration = mass/volume = 25 g / 0.5 dm³ = 50 g/dm³.

3. A solution has a concentration of 15 g/dm³. What mass of solute is present in 200 cm³ of this solution? A) 3 g B) 30 g C) 75 g D) 0.03 g

Answer

A) 3 g First, convert the volume to dm³: 200 cm³ = 0.2 dm³. Then, mass = concentration × volume = 15 g/dm³ × 0.2 dm³ = 3 g.

4. How much volume of a 40 g/dm³ solution is needed to obtain 10 g of solute? A) 0.25 dm³ B) 4 dm³ C) 400 cm³ D) 250 cm³

Answer

D) 250 cm³ Volume = mass / concentration = 10 g / 40 g/dm³ = 0.25 dm³. To convert to cm³, multiply by 1000: 0.25 dm³ × 1000 cm³/dm³ = 250 cm³.

5. If you add more sodium hydroxide pellets to an already concentrated sodium hydroxide solution, without changing the total volume of the solution, what happens to its concentration? A) It decreases. B) It increases. C) It stays the same. D) It depends on the initial concentration.

Answer

B) It increases. Concentration is mass of solute per unit volume. Adding more solute (sodium hydroxide pellets) while keeping the volume constant will increase the mass of solute in that volume, thus increasing the concentration.

6. A dilute solution of copper sulfate is prepared. If more water is added to this solution, what effect does this have on its concentration? A) The concentration increases. B) The concentration decreases. C) The concentration remains constant. D) The concentration initially decreases then increases.

Answer

B) The concentration decreases. Adding more water (solvent) increases the total volume of the solution while the mass of solute (copper sulfate) remains the same. Since concentration = mass/volume, an increase in volume with constant mass leads to a decrease in concentration.

7. A student leaves a beaker of sugar solution exposed to air for several days. Over this period, some of the water evaporates. How does this affect the concentration of the remaining solution? A) It decreases because the volume decreases. B) It increases because the mass of solute stays the same but volume decreases. C) It stays the same because the amount of sugar doesn't change. D) It decreases as the solution becomes saturated.

Answer

B) It increases because the mass of solute stays the same but volume decreases. Evaporation removes solvent (water) but leaves the solute (sugar) behind. This means the mass of solute remains constant, but the volume of the solution decreases. Therefore, the concentration (mass/volume) increases.

8. A solution contains 0.8 g of solute in 20 cm³ of solution. What is its concentration in g/dm³? A) 40 g/dm³ B) 4 g/dm³ C) 0.04 g/dm³ D) 16 g/dm³

Answer

A) 40 g/dm³ First, convert the volume to dm³: 20 cm³ = 0.02 dm³. Then, concentration = mass/volume = 0.8 g / 0.02 dm³ = 40 g/dm³.

9. (Higher Tier) Which statement correctly describes the relationship between the mass of a solute, the volume of a solution, and its concentration? A) Doubling the mass of solute while keeping the volume constant halves the concentration. B) Doubling the volume of solution while keeping the mass of solute constant doubles the concentration. C) Halving the mass of solute and halving the volume of solution results in no change in concentration. D) Halving the volume of solution while keeping the mass of solute constant halves the concentration.

Answer

C) Halving the mass of solute and halving the volume of solution results in no change in concentration. Concentration = mass/volume. If both the mass of solute and the volume of the solution are halved (mass/2 and volume/2), the concentration becomes (mass/2) / (volume/2) = mass/volume, which is the original concentration. Therefore, there is no change.

10. Solution X has a concentration of 30 g/dm³. Solution Y contains 10 g of solute in 250 cm³ of solution. Which solution is more concentrated? A) Solution X B) Solution Y C) Both have the same concentration D) Cannot be determined without knowing the solute.

Answer

B) Solution YFirst, calculate the concentration of Solution Y. Convert volume to dm³: 250 cm³ = 0.25 dm³. Concentration Y = 10 g / 0.25 dm³ = 40 g/dm³. Comparing the concentrations: Solution X = 30 g/dm³, Solution Y = 40 g/dm³. Therefore, Solution Y is more concentrated.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The concept of concentration has been implicitly understood for centuries, but its formal mathematical expression and precise measurement developed with the rise of quantitative chemistry in the 18th and 19th centuries. Key figures like Antoine Lavoisier established the importance of accurate measurements, paving the way for understanding stoichiometry and solution chemistry. The development of volumetric glassware (pipettes, burettes, volumetric flasks) was crucial for standardising solution preparation and analysis.

⚖️ Ethics

The precise control of solution concentration has significant ethical implications. In medicine, incorrect concentrations of drugs can lead to serious patient harm or death. In environmental science, monitoring pollutant concentrations is vital for public health and ecosystem protection; misreporting or lax standards can have devastating effects. In industry, ensuring correct concentrations in products (e.g., food additives, cleaning agents) is a matter of consumer safety and product efficacy. Waste disposal of concentrated chemical solutions also requires careful management to prevent environmental damage.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond mass per volume (g/dm3), chemists often use 'molar concentration' or 'molarity', expressed in moles per cubic decimetre (mol/dm3) or moles per litre (mol/L). This unit is more fundamental because it directly relates to the number of particles (atoms, ions, molecules) of solute present, which is crucial for stoichiometric calculations in reactions. Molarity allows chemists to easily determine the exact mole ratios of reactants in solution. Other advanced measures include molality (moles of solute per kg of solvent), mole fraction, and parts per million (ppm) or parts per billion (ppb) for very dilute solutions (e.g., pollutants).

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

To prepare a solution of known concentration (e.g., 20 g/dm3):1. Calculate the required mass of solute for the desired volume (e.g., 20g for 1 dm3).2. Accurately weigh out the calculated mass of solute using a balance.3. Transfer the solute into a volumetric flask (e.g., 1 dm3 flask).4. Add a small amount of distilled water to dissolve the solid, swirling the flask.5. Once dissolved, carefully add more distilled water up to the calibration mark on the neck of the volumetric flask. Ensure the bottom of the meniscus touches the line.6. Stopper the flask and invert it several times to ensure the solution is thoroughly mixed and homogeneous.Safety notes: Always wear eye protection. If using corrosive or irritating chemicals, wear gloves and work in a fume cupboard. Ensure proper disposal of chemical waste.

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 General Chemical Handling: Always wear safety goggles to protect eyes from splashes.
  • 🛑 Corrosive Solutions: Many concentrated solutions (e.g., strong acids or alkalis) are corrosive and can cause severe burns to skin and eyes. Wear gloves and use a fume cupboard.
  • 🛑 Irritants: Some solutions, even when dilute, can be irritating to skin or respiratory tract. Avoid direct contact and inhalation of vapours.
  • 🛑 Slippery Spills: Spilled solutions can make floors slippery, posing a fall hazard. Clean spills immediately according to lab protocol.
  • 🛑 Broken Glassware: Handle glassware carefully; broken glassware can cause cuts. Dispose of broken glass in designated bins.
