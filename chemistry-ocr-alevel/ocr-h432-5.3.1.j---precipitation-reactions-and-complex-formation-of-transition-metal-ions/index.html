🤜 In a Nutshell

This topic explores the characteristic reactions of aqueous transition metal ions (Cu²⁺, Fe²⁺, Fe³⁺, Mn²⁺, Cr³⁺) with aqueous sodium hydroxide and aqueous ammonia. Students learn to identify these ions based on the colour of their insoluble hydroxide precipitates and their subsequent behaviour (redissolution to form soluble complex ions) with excess reagent, mastering the relevant ionic equations and understanding the principles of ligand exchange and amphoteric behaviour.

🧠 Conceptual Understanding

As a teacher, I'd explain that precipitation reactions are fundamental in chemistry, especially when dealing with transition metal ions. When you mix two aqueous solutions, sometimes a new, insoluble solid forms – we call this a precipitate. This happens because the ions from the two solutions combine to form a compound that doesn't dissolve in water. For transition metals like copper, iron, manganese, and chromium, these precipitation reactions often involve hydroxide ions (from sodium hydroxide or ammonia) forming metal hydroxides. What makes it extra interesting is that some of these metal hydroxides, particularly those of copper and chromium, can then react further with excess ammonia or sodium hydroxide to form soluble complex ions, leading to a colour change and the precipitate redissolving. This phenomenon is critical for identifying these specific metal ions in unknown solutions and understanding their coordination chemistry.

📌 Core Theory

  • Transition metal ions in aqueous solution exist as hydrated ions (e.g., [Cu(H₂O)₆]²⁺).
  • Adding aqueous sodium hydroxide (NaOH) or aqueous ammonia (NH₃) to solutions of Cu²⁺, Fe²⁺, Fe³⁺, Mn²⁺, and Cr³⁺ ions causes precipitation of insoluble metal hydroxides.
  • The general ionic equation for precipitation is Mⁿ⁺(aq) + nOH⁻(aq) → M(OH)ₙ(s).
  • Each transition metal ion produces a characteristic coloured precipitate.
  • With excess aqueous sodium hydroxide or aqueous ammonia, some metal hydroxides (specifically Cr(OH)₃ and Cu(OH)₂ respectively, and Cr(OH)₃ with both) can redissolve to form soluble complex ions.
  • Complex ion formation involves ligand exchange, where water ligands are replaced by hydroxide ions (OH⁻) or ammonia molecules (NH₃).
  • The characteristic colours of the precipitates and the complex ions are crucial for distinguishing between these different transition metal ions.
  • Fe(OH)₂ is a green precipitate which readily oxidises in air to reddish-brown Fe(OH)₃.
  • Mn(OH)₂ is a light brown precipitate which also darkens on standing due to oxidation.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

When aqueous solutions containing transition metal ions like Cu²⁺, Fe²⁺, Fe³⁺, Mn²⁺, and Cr³⁺ are treated with aqueous sodium hydroxide or aqueous ammonia, an initial precipitation reaction occurs. This is because the added hydroxide ions (OH⁻) from NaOH, or hydroxide ions formed by the dissociation of ammonia (NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻), react with the positively charged metal ions to form insoluble metal hydroxides. For example, Cu²⁺(aq) reacts with 2OH⁻(aq) to form Cu(OH)₂(s), a blue precipitate. The unique colours of these precipitates (blue for copper, green for Fe²⁺, reddish-brown for Fe³⁺, light brown for Mn²⁺, and grey-green for Cr³⁺) serve as a diagnostic test. However, the reactions don't always stop there. With the addition of excess reagent, some of these metal hydroxides can undergo ligand exchange, where the water ligands around the metal ion are replaced by hydroxide or ammonia ligands, forming a new, soluble complex ion. For instance, Cu(OH)₂(s) will redissolve in excess aqueous ammonia to form the deep blue tetraamminecopper(II) complex, [Cu(NH₃)₄(H₂O)₂]²⁺. Similarly, Cr(OH)₃(s) is amphoteric and will redissolve in excess aqueous NaOH to form the hexahydroxochromate(III) complex, [Cr(OH)₆]³⁻, and in excess aqueous NH₃ to form the hexaamminechromium(III) complex, [Cr(NH₃)₆]³⁺. The ability of these precipitates to redissolve and form characteristic complex ions with specific reagents is key to distinguishing between different transition metal ions.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Identifying an unknown solution containing a transition metal ion

Problem: A student is given an unknown aqueous solution 'X'. When a few drops of aqueous sodium hydroxide are added, a green precipitate forms. Upon adding excess aqueous sodium hydroxide, the precipitate remains insoluble. Identify the transition metal ion present in solution 'X' and write the ionic equation for the initial precipitation.

Steps: 1. Recall the characteristic colours of precipitates formed by transition metal ions with aqueous sodium hydroxide. 2. A green precipitate is characteristic of Fe²⁺ ions (iron(II) hydroxide). 3. Consider the behaviour with excess aqueous sodium hydroxide. Fe(OH)₂ does not redissolve in excess NaOH, which matches the observation. 4. Write the ionic equation for the formation of iron(II) hydroxide.

Answer: The transition metal ion present in solution 'X' is Fe²⁺. The ionic equation for the initial precipitation is: Fe²⁺(aq) + 2OH⁻(aq) → Fe(OH)₂(s).

Teacher's Tip

Always consider both the initial precipitate colour and its behaviour with excess reagent to confidently identify the ion. Remember that Fe(OH)₂ can turn brown on standing due to oxidation, so the initial observation is key.

2. Explaining complex formation with copper(II) ions

Problem: Describe the observations when aqueous ammonia is added dropwise to an aqueous solution of copper(II) sulfate until in excess. Write ionic equations for the reactions that occur.

Steps: 1. Identify the initial ion: Cu²⁺. 2. Predict the initial reaction with aqueous ammonia (which provides OH⁻ ions) and the colour of the precipitate formed. 3. Predict the reaction with excess aqueous ammonia and the colour of the complex formed. 4. Write the ionic equation for the precipitation of copper(II) hydroxide. 5. Write the ionic equation for the formation of the tetraamminecopper(II) complex ion.

Answer: Initially, a few drops of aqueous ammonia cause a light blue precipitate to form. This precipitate is copper(II) hydroxide, Cu(OH)₂.Upon adding excess aqueous ammonia, the light blue precipitate redissolves to form a deep blue solution, which is the tetraamminecopper(II) complex ion.Ionic equations:1. Precipitation: Cu²⁺(aq) + 2OH⁻(aq) → Cu(OH)₂(s)2. Complex formation: Cu(OH)₂(s) + 4NH₃(aq) → [Cu(NH₃)₄(H₂O)₂]²⁺(aq) + 2OH⁻(aq) (or, more simply showing direct ligand exchange from hydrated ion: [Cu(H₂O)₆]²⁺(aq) + 4NH₃(aq) → [Cu(NH₃)₄(H₂O)₂]²⁺(aq) + 4H₂O(l))

Teacher's Tip

When writing complex formation equations, it's often simpler to show the hydrated ion reacting directly with the ligand, displacing water molecules. Ensure the charge is balanced and the number of ligands is correct for the coordination number (usually 4 or 6 for transition metals).

3. Distinguishing between two ions

Problem: You have two unlabelled test tubes, one containing Cr³⁺(aq) and the other Fe³⁺(aq). Explain how you would use aqueous sodium hydroxide to distinguish between them, including expected observations and relevant ionic equations.

Steps: 1. Recall the reaction of Cr³⁺ with NaOH and excess NaOH. 2. Recall the reaction of Fe³⁺ with NaOH and excess NaOH. 3. Describe the observations for each step for both ions. 4. Write the ionic equations for the precipitation and complex formation of Cr³⁺ with NaOH. 5. Write the ionic equation for the precipitation of Fe³⁺ with NaOH.

Answer: To distinguish between Cr³⁺(aq) and Fe³⁺(aq) using aqueous sodium hydroxide:1. Add aqueous sodium hydroxide dropwise to each solution.2. In both test tubes, a precipitate will form initially: * For Cr³⁺(aq): A grey-green precipitate (Cr(OH)₃) will form. * For Fe³⁺(aq): A reddish-brown precipitate (Fe(OH)₃) will form.3. Continue adding aqueous sodium hydroxide in excess to both test tubes: * For the solution that contained Cr³⁺(aq), the grey-green precipitate (Cr(OH)₃) will redissolve, forming a dark green solution (due to the formation of the hexahydroxochromate(III) complex ion, [Cr(OH)₆]³⁻). * For the solution that contained Fe³⁺(aq), the reddish-brown precipitate (Fe(OH)₃) will remain insoluble.Ionic Equations:For Cr³⁺:Cr³⁺(aq) + 3OH⁻(aq) → Cr(OH)₃(s)Cr(OH)₃(s) + 3OH⁻(aq) → [Cr(OH)₆]³⁻(aq)For Fe³⁺:Fe³⁺(aq) + 3OH⁻(aq) → Fe(OH)₃(s)

Teacher's Tip

When distinguishing, make sure to describe both the initial observation and the observation with excess reagent if there's a difference. Always specify the colours accurately.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Water Treatment: Precipitation reactions are used in water purification to remove heavy metal ions (e.g., lead, mercury) by converting them into insoluble hydroxides or carbonates, which can then be filtered out.
  • Qualitative Analysis: In analytical chemistry, these characteristic precipitation and complex formation reactions are crucial for identifying the presence of specific transition metal ions in unknown samples.
  • Pigments and Dyes: Many transition metal compounds, including their hydroxides and complex ions, exhibit vibrant colours and are used as pigments in paints, ceramics, and textiles (e.g., chromium compounds for green pigments).
  • Pharmaceuticals: Some transition metal complexes are used in medicine, for example, as anti-cancer drugs (like cisplatin) or diagnostic agents, where their specific coordination chemistry is vital.
  • Metallurgy: Understanding precipitation helps in extracting and purifying metals from their ores, as selective precipitation can separate desired metal ions from impurities.

High Yield — Comes Up Every Year

  • ⭐ Which precipitates redissolve in excess NaOH or NH₃, and the colour of the resulting complex solution (Cu²⁺ with excess NH₃; Cr³⁺ with excess NaOH and NH₃).

Pay close attention to the phrasing 'dropwise' versus 'in excess' in questions. These often dictate different outcomes (precipitation vs. complex formation) and examiners look for precise descriptions of observations at each stage.

  • ❌ Forgetting to include state symbols (aq), (s), (l) in ionic equations.
  • ❌ Not knowing which precipitates redissolve in excess NaOH or NH₃, and which do not.
  • ❌ Assuming all transition metal hydroxides form complex ions with excess reagents.
  • ❌ Incorrectly balancing charges in ionic equations, especially for complex ions.
  • ❌ Not recognising that aqueous ammonia produces hydroxide ions in solution, leading to precipitation.

Q9. (3 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~3 min)

Explain, in terms of ligand exchange, why copper(II) hydroxide redissolves in excess aqueous ammonia but not in excess aqueous sodium hydroxide.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Copper(II) hydroxide, Cu(OH)₂, redissolves in excess aqueous ammonia because ammonia molecules (NH₃) are suitable ligands. They are able to displace the hydroxide ligands (and coordinated water molecules) from the copper(II) centre to form the stable, soluble tetraamminecopper(II) complex ion, [Cu(NH₃)₄(H₂O)₂]²⁺. In contrast, copper(II) hydroxide does not redissolve in excess aqueous sodium hydroxide because hydroxide ions themselves are not sufficiently effective ligands to form a stable, soluble anionic complex with copper(II) that would displace the existing hydroxide ligands.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Aqueous ammonia (NH₃) molecules are effective ligands.
  • ✅ They displace the hydroxide (and water) ligands from Cu(OH)₂ (or hydrated Cu²⁺) to form the stable tetraamminecopper(II) complex ion, [Cu(NH₃)₄(H₂O)₂]²⁺, which is soluble.
  • ✅ Hydroxide ions (OH⁻) are not strong enough ligands to displace other OH⁻ ligands from Cu(OH)₂ to form a soluble anionic complex with copper(II).

⚠️ Examiner Note: Students often just state 'it forms a complex' without explaining why it forms with ammonia but not hydroxide, in terms of ligand exchange/stability.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This requires understanding the nature of ligands and the stability of different complexes. Emphasise that NH₃ is a better ligand than OH⁻ for Cu²⁺, in this specific context.

Q9. (3 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~3 min)

Explain why transition metal ions typically form coloured complexes, unlike Group 1 or Group 2 metal ions.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Transition metal ions typically form coloured complexes because they have incompletely filled d-orbitals. When ligands coordinate to the central metal ion, they create an electric field that causes the d-orbitals to split into two sets of different energy levels. Electrons in the lower energy d-orbitals can absorb specific wavelengths (frequencies) of visible light, gaining energy to jump to the higher energy d-orbitals (this is known as a d-d transition). The colour observed is the complementary colour of the light absorbed, leading to the characteristic vibrant colours of transition metal complexes. Group 1 and Group 2 metal ions, on the other hand, do not have incompletely filled d-orbitals (they have either empty or completely filled shells), so these d-d transitions cannot occur, and their compounds are typically colourless unless bonded to a coloured anion.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Transition metals have incompletely filled d-orbitals.
  • ✅ Ligands split the d-orbitals into different energy levels.
  • ✅ Electrons absorb specific frequencies of visible light to jump between these split d-orbitals (d-d transitions), transmitting the complementary colour.

⚠️ Examiner Note: Students often vaguely mention 'd-orbitals' without explaining 'splitting' or 'd-d transitions' and the absorption/transmission of light.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This delves into the reason behind transition metal colours, linking to d-orbital splitting (crystal field theory concepts, even if not named explicitly). It's a fundamental concept for understanding transition metal chemistry.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The study of coloured precipitates and their dissolution to form complexes dates back to the early days of analytical chemistry, playing a crucial role in qualitative analysis. Alfred Werner, a Swiss chemist, is considered the father of coordination chemistry, winning the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1913 for his work on the structures of metal complexes. His theory explained the nature of the bonds and structures of these fascinating compounds, laying the groundwork for understanding ligand exchange and complex formation, which are central to these precipitation reactions.

⚖️ Ethics

While seemingly straightforward lab reactions, the handling and disposal of transition metal compounds have significant ethical and environmental implications. Many heavy metal ions are toxic pollutants. For instance, copper and chromium compounds, while forming beautiful colours, can be harmful to aquatic life and humans if released into the environment. The responsible use, waste management, and recycling of these chemicals in industrial processes (e.g., electroplating, dye manufacturing) are critical to minimise environmental impact and protect public health. This underscores the importance of understanding their chemical behaviour to develop effective remediation strategies.

🚀 Advanced Theory

The ability of certain metal hydroxides to redissolve in excess alkali (amphoteric behaviour) can be explained by considering the Lewis acid-base properties of the metal ion and the stability of the formed complexes. For amphoteric hydroxides like Cr(OH)₃, the central metal ion can act as a Lewis acid, accepting electron pairs from hydroxide ligands to form anionic complexes (e.g., [Cr(OH)₆]³⁻). The stability of these complexes is governed by factors such as the charge density of the metal ion, the nature of the ligand, and entropy changes. The distinction between forming a complex with excess NaOH versus excess NH₃ relates to the relative strengths of OH⁻ and NH₃ as ligands for a particular metal ion, as well as the geometry and charge preferred by the metal center. For instance, Cu²⁺ prefers the neutral NH₃ ligand in excess to form a stable square planar complex, displacing OH⁻, while Cr³⁺ can form stable octahedral complexes with both OH⁻ and NH₃. The difference in colour between [Cr(OH)₆]³⁻ (dark green) and [Cr(NH₃)₆]³⁺ (violet) is due to the different ligand field splitting effects of OH⁻ and NH₃ on the d-orbitals of Cr³⁺, leading to different wavelengths of light absorbed and transmitted.

Pour small volumes (approx. 2 cm³) of each transition metal ion solution into separate test tubes. 2. ⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. Precipitation: This is a double displacement reaction where the metal cations (e.g., Cu²⁺(aq)) react with hydroxide anions (OH⁻(aq)) to form an insoluble ionic compound, the metal hydroxide (e.g., Cu(OH)₂(s)). The driving force is the formation of a solid which separates from the solution. This is often viewed as a ligand exchange where water ligands are replaced by hydroxide ligands, forming a neutral, insoluble complex that then precipitates.
  2. Complex Formation (Redissolution): When excess ligand (e.g., NH₃ or OH⁻) is added, the insoluble metal hydroxide can act as the source of the central metal ion. A ligand exchange reaction occurs where the ligands (often water or hydroxide in the precipitate) around the central metal ion are replaced by the new ligands from the excess reagent (e.g., NH₃ or OH⁻). If the new complex ion formed (e.g., [Cu(NH₃)₄(H₂O)₂]²⁺ or [Cr(OH)₆]³⁻) is soluble in water, the precipitate redissolves. This is a reversible process governed by the stability constants of the different complexes.

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Temperature: Room temperature (typically 20-25°C) for most observations.
  • Pressure: Atmospheric pressure.
  • Catalyst: No specific catalysts are typically required for these precipitation and complex formation reactions, though oxidation of Fe(OH)₂ and Mn(OH)₂ by atmospheric oxygen is a redox process.
  • Solvent: Aqueous solutions are essential for both the transition metal ions and the added reagents (sodium hydroxide, ammonia).
  • Concentration: Typical laboratory concentrations (e.g., 0.1 M transition metal salts, 1.0 M NaOH, 2.0 M NH₃) are used to ensure clear observations of precipitates and colour changes.

🔦 Element Spotlight

While this topic covers multiple transition metals, Chromium (Cr) stands out due to its unique amphoteric behaviour and formation of differently coloured complexes with excess NaOH and NH₃.Chromium (atomic number 24) has an electron configuration of [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹. As Cr³⁺, its electron configuration is [Ar] 3d³. The presence of partially filled d-orbitals is what allows it to form coloured compounds and participate in ligand exchange reactions. Cr³⁺ ions in aqueous solution are typically green or violet. Its hydroxide, Cr(OH)₃, is amphoteric, meaning it can act as both an acid and a base. This property allows it to redissolve in both strong acids and strong bases (like excess aqueous NaOH) to form soluble complex ions, in contrast to most other transition metal hydroxides discussed in this topic.
