🧠 Conceptual Understanding

Acids, bases, and buffers are at the heart of chemical reactivity and biological processes. As a teacher, I'd explain that an acid is fundamentally a 'proton donor' and a base is a 'proton acceptor' (Brønsted-Lowry theory). This simple idea helps us understand reactions where protons (H⁺ ions) are transferred. We measure the acidity or alkalinity of a solution using the pH scale, which is essentially a measure of the hydrogen ion concentration. While strong acids and bases dissociate completely, weak acids and bases only partially dissociate, leading to an equilibrium. This equilibrium is quantified by the acid dissociation constant, Ka, and the ionic product of water, Kw. The real magic happens with buffer solutions. Imagine a chemical 'sponge' that can soak up added acid or base, preventing large changes in pH. That's what a buffer does! These systems, typically made from a weak acid and its conjugate base, are vital for maintaining stable pH environments, from industrial processes to the delicate balance in our own blood. Finally, pH titration curves provide a visual representation of how pH changes during an acid-base reaction, allowing us to determine unknown concentrations or the pKa of weak acids.

📌 Core Theory

  • A Brønsted-Lowry acid is a proton (H⁺) donor; a Brønsted-Lowry base is a proton (H⁺) acceptor.
  • Conjugate acid-base pairs differ by a single proton (e.g., CH₃COOH/CH₃COO⁻).
  • Acids can be monobasic (donates one H⁺), dibasic (donates two H⁺), or tribasic (donates three H⁺).
  • Acids react with metals, carbonates, metal oxides, and alkalis, often involving H⁺ ions, to produce salt and water (and CO₂ with carbonates).
  • pH is a measure of hydrogen ion concentration: pH = –log[H⁺].
  • The ionic product of water, Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻], and its value is 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ mol² dm⁻⁶ at 298 K.
  • The acid dissociation constant, Ka, quantifies the extent of dissociation for weak acids, with pKa = –logKa.
  • Calculations of pH for strong monobasic acids, strong bases (using Kw), and weak monobasic acids (using approximations) are essential.
  • Approximations for weak acid calculations (e.g., [HA]initial ≈ [HA]equilibrium) are valid for very weak acids but become less accurate for 'stronger' weak acids.
  • A buffer solution minimises pH changes upon addition of small amounts of acid or base.
  • Buffer solutions are formed from a weak acid and its conjugate base (salt) or an excess of a weak acid and a strong alkali.
  • The conjugate acid-base pair in a buffer system acts to neutralise added H⁺ or OH⁻ ions, maintaining pH.
  • The Henderson-Hasselbalch equation (pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])) can be used to calculate the pH of buffer solutions.
  • The carbonic acid-hydrogencarbonate buffer system (H₂CO₃/HCO₃⁻) controls blood pH.
  • pH titration curves illustrate the change in pH during an acid-base titration, showing distinct shapes for different acid-base combinations.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

At its core, understanding acids and bases revolves around proton transfer. A Brønsted-Lowry acid, like HCl, readily donates a proton, while a base, such as NH₃, readily accepts one. This proton transfer creates a 'conjugate' pair: when an acid loses a proton, it forms its conjugate base, and when a base gains a proton, it forms its conjugate acid. The strength of an acid or base depends on its extent of dissociation in water. Strong acids (e.g., HCl, H₂SO₄) dissociate completely, making pH calculations straightforward. Weak acids (e.g., CH₃COOH, H₂CO₃) dissociate only partially, establishing an equilibrium described by the acid dissociation constant, Ka. The smaller the Ka (or larger the pKa), the weaker the acid. Water itself undergoes a slight self-ionisation, defining the ionic product of water, Kw, which is fundamental for calculating pH of strong bases. The genius of buffer solutions lies in their ability to 'resist' drastic pH changes. A typical buffer contains a weak acid and its conjugate base. If H⁺ ions are added, the conjugate base reacts with them. If OH⁻ ions are added, the weak acid neutralises them. This dual action maintains a relatively stable pH. This mechanism is critical in biological systems, such as the regulation of blood pH, and in many industrial processes. Finally, pH titration curves provide invaluable information, showing a sharp pH change around the equivalence point, which differs based on the strengths of the acid and base involved. Analysing these curves allows us to determine the pKa of weak acids at the half-equivalence point.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Calculating pH of a Strong Monobasic Acid

Problem: Calculate the pH of a 0.0050 mol dm⁻³ solution of nitric acid (HNO₃).

Steps: 1. Nitric acid is a strong monobasic acid, so it dissociates completely: HNO₃(aq) → H⁺(aq) + NO₃⁻(aq). 2. Therefore, the concentration of H⁺ ions is equal to the concentration of the acid: [H⁺] = 0.0050 mol dm⁻³. 3. Use the formula pH = –log[H⁺]. 4. Substitute the [H⁺] value into the formula: pH = –log(0.0050). 5. Calculate the pH.

Answer: pH = 2.30

Teacher's Tip

For strong monobasic acids, the initial acid concentration is directly equal to the H⁺ concentration. Remember to give pH to two decimal places.

2. Calculating pH of a Weak Monobasic Acid

Problem: Ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH) has a Ka value of 1.75 × 10⁻⁵ mol dm⁻³. Calculate the pH of a 0.100 mol dm⁻³ solution of ethanoic acid.

Steps: 1. Write the dissociation equilibrium for ethanoic acid: CH₃COOH(aq) ⇌ H⁺(aq) + CH₃COO⁻(aq). 2. Write the Ka expression: Ka = [H⁺][CH₃COO⁻]⁄[CH₃COOH]. 3. Assume [H⁺] = [CH₃COO⁻] = x, and [CH₃COOH] ≈ 0.100 - x ≈ 0.100 (approximation, as x is small for a weak acid). 4. Substitute these into the Ka expression: 1.75 × 10⁻⁵ = x × x⁄0.100. 5. Rearrange and solve for x: x² = 1.75 × 10⁻⁵ × 0.100 = 1.75 × 10⁻⁶. 6. x = √1.75 × 10⁻⁶ = 1.3229 × 10⁻³ mol dm⁻³ (this is [H⁺]). 7. Calculate pH using pH = –log[H⁺].

Answer: pH = 2.88

Teacher's Tip

Always check if the approximation is valid: if x is less than 5% of the initial acid concentration, the approximation is usually acceptable. Here, 1.3229 × 10⁻³ / 0.100 = 0.0132 or 1.32%, which is valid.

3. Calculating pH of a Buffer Solution

Problem: A buffer solution is prepared by mixing 0.20 mol of ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH) and 0.20 mol of sodium ethanoate (CH₃COONa) in 1.0 dm³ of water. The Ka for ethanoic acid is 1.75 × 10⁻⁵ mol dm⁻³. Calculate the pH of the buffer.

Steps: 1. Identify the weak acid and its conjugate base concentrations: [CH₃COOH] = 0.20 mol dm⁻³ and [CH₃COO⁻] = 0.20 mol dm⁻³. 2. Use the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]⁄[HA]). 3. First, calculate pKa from Ka: pKa = –log(1.75 × 10⁻⁵) = 4.757. 4. Substitute the values into the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation: pH = 4.757 + log(0.20⁄0.20). 5. Simplify the logarithm term: log(1) = 0. 6. Calculate the pH.

Answer: pH = 4.76 (to 2 decimal places)

Teacher's Tip

When [A⁻] = [HA], the pH of the buffer is equal to the pKa of the weak acid. This is the most effective buffering capacity.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Blood pH Regulation: The human body maintains a very narrow pH range (7.35-7.45) in the blood using buffer systems, primarily the carbonic acid-hydrogencarbonate buffer. Deviations can lead to serious health issues like acidosis or alkalosis.
  • Antacids: These common medicines use bases (e.g., magnesium hydroxide, calcium carbonate) to neutralise excess stomach acid (HCl), relieving indigestion and heartburn. This is a direct application of acid-base neutralisation.
  • Soil pH Control: Farmers and gardeners often adjust soil pH to optimise plant growth. Different plants thrive at different pH levels, and soil buffers (like humic acids) or added lime/sulfur are used to maintain the desired range.
  • Food Preservation: Many foods are acidic to inhibit bacterial growth. The addition of buffering agents can help maintain this acidity over time, extending shelf life.
  • Swimming Pool Maintenance: Pool water pH needs to be carefully maintained (typically 7.2-7.8) for swimmer comfort and disinfectant effectiveness. Buffer systems are often added to prevent large pH swings caused by contaminants or chemicals.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing 'strong' with 'concentrated': A strong acid dissociates fully, regardless of concentration. A concentrated acid simply has many acid molecules per unit volume.
  • ❌ Believing buffers completely prevent pH change: Buffers minimise, but do not eliminate, pH changes. Their capacity is limited.
  • ❌ Incorrectly applying approximations for weak acids: The approximation [HA]initial ≈ [HA]equilibrium is only valid if the acid is very weak or very dilute.
  • ❌ Misinterpreting Kw: Students often forget Kw applies to any aqueous solution, not just pure water, and its value changes with temperature.
  • ❌ Assuming pH 7 is always neutral: pH 7 is only neutral at 298 K. As temperature changes, Kw changes, and thus the pH of pure water (where [H⁺]=[OH⁻]) changes.
  • ❌ Confusing equivalence point with end point in titrations: Equivalence point is the theoretical point where moles of acid = moles of base. End point is where the indicator changes colour.
Memory Anchor

Think of a buffer solution as a 'chemical shock absorber'. Just as a car's shock absorbers smooth out bumps in the road, a buffer absorbs sudden changes in acidity or alkalinity, keeping the pH relatively stable.

Q2. (3 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~3 min)

Explain why ethanoic acid is considered a weak acid, whereas hydrochloric acid is a strong acid.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Ethanoic acid is classified as a weak acid because it only partially dissociates (ionises) when dissolved in water, establishing an equilibrium between the undissociated ethanoic acid molecules and its ions. In contrast, hydrochloric acid is a strong acid because it dissociates completely in water. This complete dissociation means that for a given concentration, a strong acid will produce a much higher concentration of H⁺ ions than a weak acid, leading to a lower pH.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Ethanoic acid only partially dissociates/ionises in water (1 mark).
  • ✅ Hydrochloric acid dissociates/ionises completely in water (1 mark).
  • ✅ Weak acids establish an equilibrium between undissociated molecules and ions, resulting in a lower [H⁺] compared to a strong acid of the same concentration (1 mark).

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: Emphasise the concept of 'partial' vs. 'complete' dissociation and the resulting H⁺ concentration.

Q7. (3 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~3 min)

Distinguish between a strong acid and a weak acid in terms of their dissociation in water and their conjugate bases.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: A strong acid, such as HCl, undergoes complete dissociation in water, meaning all its molecules donate their protons to water molecules. This results in the equilibrium lying far to the right, forming a high concentration of H⁺ ions. Its conjugate base (e.g., Cl⁻) is very weak and has negligible ability to accept a proton. In contrast, a weak acid, like CH₃COOH, only partially dissociates in water, establishing an equilibrium where most acid molecules remain undissociated. Its conjugate base (e.g., CH₃COO⁻) is relatively stronger, meaning it can accept a proton to reform the weak acid, which is crucial for buffer action.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Strong acids dissociate completely, weak acids partially (1 mark).
  • ✅ Strong acid equilibrium lies far to the right, weak acid equilibrium lies to the left (1 mark).
  • ✅ Strong acids have very weak conjugate bases; weak acids have relatively stronger conjugate bases (1 mark).

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: Connect dissociation to equilibrium position and the strength of the conjugate base. Strong acids have weak conjugate bases, and vice versa.

Q10. (3 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~3 min)

Explain the limitations of using approximations (e.g., [HA]equilibrium ≈ [HA]initial) when calculating the pH of 'stronger' weak acids.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: The approximation [HA]equilibrium ≈ [HA]initial assumes that the extent of dissociation of the weak acid is negligible, meaning the concentration of H⁺ ions produced is very small compared to the initial concentration of the acid. This simplifies the Ka expression. However, for 'stronger' weak acids, or very dilute weak acids, the concentration of H⁺ ions produced becomes a more significant proportion of the initial acid concentration. If this change is ignored, the calculated equilibrium concentration of HA will be too high, leading to an underestimation of [H⁺] and thus an inaccurate pH calculation.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Approximation assumes [H⁺] is negligible compared to [HA]initial (1 mark).
  • ✅ For stronger weak acids, [H⁺] becomes a significant proportion of [HA]initial (1 mark).
  • ✅ Ignoring the change in [HA] leads to inaccurate [H⁺] and therefore inaccurate pH calculations (1 mark).

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This topic often appears in exam questions. Students should understand when the approximation is appropriate (typically when [H⁺] is less than 5% of [HA]initial).

Q3. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

A pH titration curve is recorded for the reaction of a weak acid with a strong base. Explain how the pKa of the weak acid can be determined from this curve and describe the key features of the curve.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: For the titration of a weak acid with a strong base, the pH titration curve starts at a higher pH compared to a strong acid of the same concentration due to partial dissociation. As the strong base is added, there is an initial gradual rise in pH, followed by a 'buffer region' where the pH changes very slowly. This occurs because significant amounts of both the weak acid and its conjugate base are present. The equivalence point, where the weak acid has been completely neutralised, occurs at a pH greater than 7 due to the hydrolysis of the conjugate base. To determine the pKa of the weak acid, locate the half-equivalence point on the curve. This is the point where exactly half of the original weak acid has been neutralised by the strong base. At this point, the concentration of the weak acid ([HA]) is equal to the concentration of its conjugate base ([A⁻]). According to the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation (pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])), if [A⁻] = [HA], then log([A⁻]/[HA]) = log(1) = 0. Therefore, at the half-equivalence point, pH = pKa.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Initial pH is higher than for strong acid (1 mark).
  • ✅ Buffer region observed (1 mark).
  • ✅ Equivalence point is above pH 7 (1 mark).
  • ✅ Mid-point/half-equivalence point identified where half of the weak acid has been neutralised (1 mark).
  • ✅ At the mid-point, [HA] = [A⁻] (1 mark).
  • ✅ Therefore, at the mid-point, pH = pKa (1 mark).

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This question requires a thorough understanding of the weak acid-strong base titration curve. Students often forget the significance of the half-equivalence point.

🔢 Working: 1. Initial moles of CH₃COOH = concentration × volume = 0.100 mol dm⁻³ × (100/1000) dm³ = 0.0100 mol. 2. Q9. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

The value of Kw is 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ mol² dm⁻⁶ at 298 K. Given that the autoionisation of water is an endothermic process, how would the pH of pure water change if the temperature was increased to 373 K (100 °C)? Explain your reasoning.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: The autoionisation of water (H₂O ⇌ H⁺ + OH⁻) is an endothermic process. According to Le Chatelier's principle, if the temperature is increased, the equilibrium will shift to favour the endothermic direction to absorb the added heat. This means that the autoionisation of water will increase, leading to higher concentrations of both H⁺ and OH⁻ ions. Consequently, the ionic product of water, Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻], will increase (become larger than 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴). In pure water, [H⁺] always equals [OH⁻], so it remains neutral. However, since [H⁺] = √Kw, an increase in Kw means an increase in [H⁺]. A higher [H⁺] results in a lower pH value. Therefore, the pH of pure water would decrease at 373 K, but it would still be considered neutral because [H⁺] = [OH⁻].

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Increasing temperature shifts equilibrium to favour the endothermic direction (1 mark).
  • ✅ Therefore, Kw increases at higher temperatures (1 mark).
  • ✅ In pure water, [H⁺] = [OH⁻] = √Kw (1 mark).
  • ✅ Since Kw increases, [H⁺] increases, leading to a decrease in pH, but the water remains neutral (1 mark).

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This is a challenging concept that links Le Chatelier's principle to Kw and pH neutrality. Ensure students understand that 'neutral' means [H⁺] = [OH⁻], not necessarily pH 7.

Q10. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

Explain why the equivalence point pH for the titration of a strong acid with a strong base is 7, but for a weak acid with a strong base, it is above 7.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: In the titration of a strong acid with a strong base (e.g., HCl + NaOH), the reaction products are a neutral salt (e.g., NaCl) and water. Neither the cation (Na⁺) nor the anion (Cl⁻) of the salt undergoes hydrolysis (reaction with water) to produce excess H⁺ or OH⁻ ions. Therefore, the solution at the equivalence point is neutral, with a pH of 7. In contrast, for the titration of a weak acid with a strong base (e.g., CH₃COOH + NaOH), the products are a salt (e.g., CH₃COONa) and water. The cation (Na⁺) is still neutral, but the anion, which is the conjugate base of the weak acid (e.g., CH₃COO⁻), is a relatively strong base. This conjugate base reacts with water in a hydrolysis reaction (CH₃COO⁻(aq) + H₂O(l) ⇌ CH₃COOH(aq) + OH⁻(aq)), producing hydroxide ions. The presence of these excess OH⁻ ions makes the solution alkaline, resulting in an equivalence point pH greater than 7.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Strong acid-strong base produces a neutral salt and water (1 mark).
  • ✅ Neither product undergoes hydrolysis to affect pH (1 mark).
  • ✅ Weak acid-strong base produces a salt where the conjugate base is relatively strong (1 mark).
  • ✅ This conjugate base hydrolyses water to produce OH⁻ ions, making the solution alkaline (1 mark).

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This is a key conceptual difference that explains the various shapes of titration curves. Students need to understand the concept of salt hydrolysis.

Answer

B) 12.40 Potassium hydroxide (KOH) is a strong base, meaning it fully dissociates in water, so [OH-] = 0.025 M. First, calculate pOH: pOH = -log[OH-] = -log(0.025) = 1.60. Then, use the relationship pH + pOH = 14 (at 298 K): pH = 14 - pOH = 14 - 1.60 = 12.40.

6. A weak monobasic acid, HA, has a Ka value of 1.8 x 10^-5 mol dm^-3. What is the pH of a 0.10 M solution of this acid? (Assume the approximation [HA]initial is valid.) A) 1.00 B) 2.87 C) 4.74 D) 5.00

Answer

B) 2.87 For a weak monobasic acid, HA <=> H+ + A-, Ka = [H+][A-] / [HA]. Assuming [H+] = [A-] and [HA]equilibrium

is equal to [HA]initial, we have Ka = [H+]^2 / [HA]initial. Rearranging for [H+], [H+] = sqrt(Ka * [HA]initial). [H+] = sqrt((1.8 x 10^-5) * 0.10) = sqrt(1.8 x 10^-6) = 1.34 x 10^-3 M. Then, pH = -log[H+] = -log(1.34 x 10^-3) = 2.87.

9. An acid buffer solution is prepared using ethanoic acid (CH3COOH) and sodium ethanoate (CH3COONa). How does this buffer resist a decrease in pH upon the addition of a small amount of strong acid? 🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The concept of acids and bases has evolved significantly. Early definitions, like Robert Boyle's in the 17th century, were based on observable properties. Svante Arrhenius (late 19th century) proposed that acids produce H⁺ in water and bases produce OH⁻. While revolutionary, it was limited to aqueous solutions. Johannes Brønsted and Thomas Lowry (1923), independently, developed the more general proton-transfer theory, defining acids as proton donors and bases as proton acceptors, applicable across different solvents. Gilbert Lewis (also 1923) further expanded this with the electron-pair concept, where a Lewis acid accepts an electron pair and a Lewis base donates one, encompassing even more reactions beyond proton transfer.

⚖️ Ethics

The understanding of acids and bases has significant ethical implications. For instance, acid rain (formed from atmospheric pollutants like SO₂ and NOₓ) acidifies lakes and soils, harming ecosystems and infrastructure. Ocean acidification, caused by increased atmospheric CO₂ dissolving in oceans to form carbonic acid, threatens marine life, particularly organisms with calcium carbonate shells. Industrial discharges of acidic or alkaline waste into water bodies can have devastating environmental impacts. Ethically, industries and governments have a responsibility to monitor and neutralise waste, control emissions, and develop sustainable practices to mitigate these effects. Furthermore, the use of highly corrosive acids and bases in industrial settings requires strict safety protocols to protect workers and prevent accidental releases.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond the monobasic weak acids, polyprotic acids (like H₃PO₄) have multiple dissociation constants (Ka₁, Ka₂, Ka₃), each corresponding to the loss of a successive proton. These result in more complex buffer systems and titration curves with multiple equivalence points. The selection of acid-base indicators for titrations depends on their pKa values, which must fall within the steep pH change region around the equivalence point for accurate results. Furthermore, the activity of ions, rather than just concentration, can influence pH in highly concentrated solutions, leading to deviations from ideal behaviour, a concept explored in advanced physical chemistry.

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

The practical component (PAG11) involves carrying out and interpreting the results of pH titrations. This includes using a pH meter to record pH changes when titrating an acid with a base, plotting pH against the volume of reactant added to produce a titration curve, and using the curve to determine the equivalence point, or the pKa of a weak acid.

Typical Steps for a pH Titration: 1. Calibrate the pH meter using buffer solutions of known pH (e.g., pH 4.00, 7.00, 10.00). 2. Pipette a known volume of the acid (or base) into a conical flask and add a small amount of distilled water to cover the pH probe bulb. 3. Record the initial pH of the solution. 4. Fill a burette with the titrant (base or acid) of known concentration. 5. Add the titrant in small, measured aliquots (e.g., 1.0 cm³ at a time initially, then 0.1 cm³ near the expected equivalence point). 6. After each addition, stir the solution gently and record the pH. 7. Continue additions until the pH changes significantly and then levels off at a new stable pH range. 8. Plot a graph of pH (y-axis) against the volume of titrant added (x-axis). 9. Determine the equivalence point from the steepest part of the curve. If a weak acid is used, the pKa can be found at the half-equivalence point.

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Corrosive Acids and Bases: Concentrated acids (e.g., HCl, H₂SO₄) and strong bases (e.g., NaOH, KOH) are corrosive and can cause severe burns to skin and eyes. Always wear eye protection (safety goggles) and protective gloves.
  • 🛑 Spillage: In case of spills, neutralise acids with a weak base (e.g., sodium carbonate solution) and bases with a weak acid (e.g., dilute ethanoic acid), then wipe up with plenty of water. Refer to COSHH data sheets.
  • 🛑 Fumes: Some concentrated acids (e.g., HCl) produce irritating fumes. Work in a well-ventilated area or fume cupboard.
  • 🛑 Heating: If any reactions involve heating, use appropriate heating apparatus and allow for cooling before handling.
  • 🛑 Broken Glassware: Handle glassware carefully to prevent breakage. Dispose of broken glass in designated bins.

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. Brønsted-Lowry Neutralisation (Proton Transfer): This is the fundamental 'mechanism' of acid-base reactions under the Brønsted-Lowry definition. An acid donates a proton to a base. For example, in the reaction between hydrochloric acid and water: HCl + H₂O → H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻. Here, HCl donates a proton to H₂O, making HCl the acid and H₂O the base. H₃O⁺ is the conjugate acid of H₂O, and Cl⁻ is the conjugate base of HCl. In a neutralisation reaction between an acid and a hydroxide base: H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l). The H⁺ from the acid combines with the OH⁻ from the base to form water.
  2. Buffer Action (Example: Ethanoic acid/Ethanoate buffer):
    • Addition of H⁺: When H⁺ is added, the conjugate base (ethanoate ions) in the buffer reacts with it: CH₃COO⁻(aq) + H⁺(aq) → CH₃COOH(aq). This removes the added H⁺, minimising the pH drop.
    • Addition of OH⁻: When OH⁻ is added, the weak acid (ethanoic acid) in the buffer reacts with it: CH₃COOH(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → CH₃COO⁻(aq) + H₂O(l). This removes the added OH⁻, minimising the pH rise.

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Neutralisation Reactions: Typically occur at room temperature and pressure in aqueous solution. For solid reactants (e.g., metal oxides, carbonates), the acid solution is added directly.
  • Buffer Formation: Can be prepared by mixing a weak acid and a salt of its conjugate base (e.g., ethanoic acid and sodium ethanoate) or by partially neutralising a weak acid with a strong base (e.g., ethanoic acid with limited NaOH) to form both the weak acid and its conjugate base in solution.

🔦 Element Spotlight

The Carbonic Acid-Hydrogencarbonate Buffer System (H₂CO₃/HCO₃⁻) is a crucial chemical system in human physiology. Carbonic acid (H₂CO₃) is formed when carbon dioxide (CO₂) dissolves in water (CO₂(g) + H₂O(l) ⇌ H₂CO₃(aq)). This weak acid then partially dissociates to form hydrogencarbonate ions (HCO₃⁻) and H⁺ ions (H₂CO₃(aq) ⇌ H⁺(aq) + HCO₃⁻(aq)). The balance between dissolved CO₂, carbonic acid, and hydrogencarbonate ions is vital for maintaining blood pH within a narrow range (7.35-7.45). This system works by: 1) If blood pH drops (becomes too acidic), the HCO₃⁻ ions (conjugate base) react with excess H⁺ to form H₂CO₃, which can then be converted to CO₂ and exhaled. 2) If blood pH rises (becomes too alkaline), H₂CO₃ (the weak acid) dissociates further, releasing H⁺, or reacts with excess OH⁻ to form HCO₃⁻ and water. The body also uses the kidneys to regulate HCO₃⁻ levels and the lungs to regulate CO₂ (and thus H₂CO₃) levels, providing a sophisticated control mechanism for this vital buffer.
