🧠 Conceptual Understanding

As your chemistry teacher, I want you to understand that in the real world, reactions rarely go perfectly. When we do a chemical reaction, we're aiming to turn reactants into desired products. But it's not always 100% efficient. We need ways to measure this efficiency: that's where percentage yield and atom economy come in. Percentage yield tells us how much product we actually get compared to the maximum possible amount. It's about 'how much we made'. Atom economy, on the other hand, tells us how much of the starting materials (reactants) ends up in our desired product, rather than in unwanted by-products. It's about 'how much of our atoms were useful'. Both are crucial for making chemical processes cost-effective, sustainable, and environmentally friendly, especially in industries like pharmaceuticals and manufacturing.

📌 Core Theory

  • Percentage yield compares the actual amount of product obtained from a reaction to the maximum theoretical amount that could be formed.
  • Theoretical yield is the maximum mass of product that can be formed from a given amount of reactants, assuming 100% conversion and no loss.
  • Actual yield is the mass of product actually obtained from the reaction.
  • Factors that can reduce percentage yield include incomplete reactions, side reactions forming other products, loss of product during separation/purification, and impurities in reactants.
  • Atom economy measures the efficiency of a chemical reaction in terms of how many of the atoms in the reactants are incorporated into the desired product.
  • High atom economy means less waste (by-products), making the process more sustainable and cost-effective.
  • Reactions with 100% atom economy mean all atoms from the reactants are incorporated into the desired product, like addition reactions.
  • Reactions where atoms are split into desired products and unwanted by-products (like substitution or elimination reactions) will have less than 100% atom economy.
  • Maximising atom economy is a key principle of green chemistry.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

When conducting chemical reactions, it's rare to achieve a 100% conversion of reactants to desired products. Percentage yield quantifies this efficiency by comparing the actual mass of product collected to the theoretical maximum. The theoretical yield is calculated using stoichiometry from the balanced chemical equation and the limiting reactant. Losses can occur due to various reasons: the reaction might not go to completion, unwanted side reactions might occur, or product might be lost during filtration, washing, or evaporation steps. Atom economy, however, looks at efficiency from a different angle. It focuses on how much of the mass of the reactants ends up as the desired product, rather than by-products. A reaction with high atom economy is desirable because it minimises waste and conserves resources, making the process more environmentally friendly and economically viable. For example, in the pharmaceutical industry, a high atom economy means less expensive raw materials are wasted, and less hazardous waste needs to be disposed of, reducing costs and environmental impact.

📖 Key Definitions

Term Definition
Percentage Yield A measure of the efficiency of a chemical reaction, calculated as (actual yield / theoretical yield) x 100%.
Theoretical Yield The maximum mass of product that could possibly be formed from a given amount of reactants, assuming the reaction goes to completion without any losses.
Actual Yield The mass of product actually obtained from a chemical reaction in practice.
Atom Economy A measure of how efficiently reactant atoms are converted into desired product atoms, calculated as (mass of desired product / total mass of reactants) x 100%.
By-product An unwanted product formed in addition to the desired product during a chemical reaction.

📚 Glossary

  • Actual Yield — The mass of product actually obtained from a chemical reaction in practice.
  • Atom Economy — A measure of how efficiently reactant atoms are converted into desired product atoms, calculated as (mass of desired product / total mass of reactants) x 100%.
  • By-product — An unwanted product formed in addition to the desired product during a chemical reaction.
  • Percentage Yield — A measure of the efficiency of a chemical reaction, calculated as (actual yield / theoretical yield) x 100%.
  • Theoretical Yield — The maximum mass of product that could possibly be formed from a given amount of reactants, assuming the reaction goes to completion without any losses.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Calculating Percentage Yield

Problem: When 10.0 g of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) reacts with hydrochloric acid (HCl), 4.5 g of calcium chloride (CaCl₂) is produced. The balanced equation is: CaCO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CaCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g). Calculate the percentage yield of calcium chloride. (Relative atomic masses: Ca=40, C=12, O=16, Cl=35.5)

Steps: 1. Calculate the molar mass of CaCO₃: 40 + 12 + (3 imes 16) = 100 g/mol. 2. Calculate the molar mass of CaCl₂: 40 + (2 imes 35.5) = 111 g/mol. 3. Calculate moles of CaCO₃ used: 10.0 g / 100 g/mol = 0.100 mol. 4. From the balanced equation, 1 mole of CaCO₃ produces 1 mole of CaCl₂. So, theoretical moles of CaCl₂ = 0.100 mol. 5. Calculate the theoretical mass of CaCl₂: 0.100 mol imes 111 g/mol = 11.1 g. 6. Calculate percentage yield: (Actual yield / Theoretical yield) imes 100% = (4.5 g / 11.1 g) imes 100%.

Answer: 40.5%

Teacher's Tip

Always ensure your chemical equation is balanced before starting stoichiometry calculations. Pay attention to significant figures in your final answer.

2. Calculating Atom Economy

Problem: Methanol (CH₃OH) can be produced from carbon monoxide (CO) and hydrogen (H₂) by the reaction: CO(g) + 2H₂(g) → CH₃OH(l). What is the atom economy of this reaction if methanol is the desired product? (Relative atomic masses: C=12, O=16, H=1)

Steps: 1. Calculate the molar mass of the desired product (CH₃OH): 12 + (3 imes 1) + 16 + 1 = 32 g/mol. 2. Calculate the total molar mass of all reactants (CO + 2H₂): (12 + 16) + (2 imes (2 imes 1)) = 28 + 4 = 32 g/mol. 3. Calculate atom economy: (Mass of desired product / Total mass of reactants) imes 100% = (32 g / 32 g) imes 100%.

Answer: 100%

Teacher's Tip

For atom economy, only consider the molar masses of the reactants and the desired product(s). If there are by-products, their mass is included in the 'total mass of reactants' but not in the 'mass of desired product'.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Pharmaceutical Industry: Producing medicines efficiently is vital. A high percentage yield ensures maximum drug production from expensive raw materials, and high atom economy minimises waste, reducing disposal costs and environmental impact.
  • Industrial Chemical Manufacturing: Companies producing bulk chemicals (e.g., sulfuric acid, ammonia) aim for high yield and atom economy to reduce production costs, maximise profits, and lessen their environmental footprint.
  • Petrochemical Industry: Processes like cracking crude oil aim to maximise the yield of valuable products (e.g., ethene, propene) while controlling atom economy to reduce the formation of less desirable by-products.
  • Green Chemistry: Atom economy is a core principle of green chemistry, which seeks to design chemical products and processes that reduce or eliminate the use and generation of hazardous substances. Aiming for 100% atom economy is the ideal.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing Yield and Atom Economy: Students often mix these up. Yield is about how much product is made compared to what could be made. Atom economy is about how much of the atoms in the reactants end up in the desired product.
  • ❌ Ignoring Limiting Reactants: When calculating theoretical yield, students sometimes forget to identify the limiting reactant if amounts for more than one reactant are given, leading to incorrect theoretical yields.
  • ❌ Incorrect Molar Mass Calculations: Errors in calculating M_r for reactants or products directly lead to incorrect yield/atom economy values.
  • ❌ Not Using Balanced Equations: Calculations for theoretical yield rely entirely on correct mole ratios from a balanced chemical equation. An unbalanced equation will give wrong answers.
  • ❌ Assuming 100% Yield/Economy: Many assume reactions are perfect. Real-world reactions almost never achieve 100% yield, and many reactions have less than 100% atom economy due to by-product formation.
Memory Anchor

Think of atom economy as a 'no-waste' chef: how much of the ingredients actually goes into the main dish, and how much is scraps? Yield is how many portions you actually serve compared to how many you could have served if everything went perfectly.

Calculate the percentage yield if 23.0 g of ethanol (C₂H₅OH) is obtained from the fermentation of 45.0 g of glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆). The equation is C₆H₁₂O₆(aq) → 2C₂H₅OH(aq) + 2CO₂(g). (Relative atomic masses: C=12, H=1, O=16)

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: 1. Molar mass of glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) = (6 imes 12) + (12 imes 1) + (6 imes 16) = 72 + 12 + 96 = 180 g/mol. 2. Molar mass of ethanol (C₂H₅OH) = (2 imes 12) + (5 imes 1) + 16 + 1 = 24 + 5 + 16 + 1 = 46 g/mol. 3. Moles of glucose used = 45.0 g / 180 g/mol = 0.25 mol. 4. From the equation, 1 mole of glucose produces 2 moles of ethanol. So, theoretical moles of ethanol = 0.25 mol imes 2 = 0.50 mol. 5. Theoretical mass of ethanol = 0.50 mol imes 46 g/mol = 23.0 g. 6. Percentage yield = (Actual yield / Theoretical yield) imes 100% = (23.0 g / 23.0 g) imes 100% = 100%.

🔢 Working: 1. Calculate molar mass of glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆). 2. Calculate molar mass of ethanol (C₂H₅OH). 3. Calculate moles of glucose reactant. 4. Use mole ratio from balanced equation to find theoretical moles of ethanol. 5. Calculate theoretical mass of ethanol. 6. Calculate percentage yield.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Molar mass of C₆H₁₂O₆ = 180 g/mol (1 mark)
  • ✅ Molar mass of C₂H₅OH = 46 g/mol (1 mark)
  • ✅ Moles of C₆H₁₂O₆ = 45.0 / 180 = 0.25 mol. Theoretical moles of C₂H₅OH = 2 imes 0.25 = 0.50 mol (1 mark for moles of reactant and product).
  • ✅ Theoretical mass of C₂H₅OH = 0.50 imes 46 = 23.0 g. Percentage yield = (23.0 / 23.0) imes 100 = 100% (1 mark for theoretical mass and percentage yield).

📋 Strategy: Break down the calculation into smaller, manageable steps: molar masses, moles, theoretical mass, then percentage.

⚠️ Examiner Note: Students sometimes forget to multiply by the mole ratio (2 in this case) when calculating theoretical moles of product.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This example has an interesting outcome of 100% yield, which can happen if the actual yield happens to match the theoretical. Remind students not to assume 100% yield but to calculate it. Pay attention to mole ratios.

Q3. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

Explain the difference between percentage yield and atom economy.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Percentage yield is a measure of the practical efficiency of a reaction, comparing the actual amount of product collected in an experiment to the maximum theoretical amount that could be formed. It is affected by factors like incomplete reactions and product loss during separation. Atom economy, on the other hand, is a measure of how efficiently the atoms from the reactants are incorporated into the desired product, rather than ending up as unwanted by-products. It is determined solely by the balanced chemical equation and relative formula masses, not by practical losses.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Percentage yield compares actual amount of product obtained to theoretical maximum (1 mark).
  • ✅ Atom economy measures how many atoms in reactants end up in the desired product (1 mark).
  • ✅ Yield considers practical losses/completeness of reaction (1 mark).
  • ✅ Atom economy considers waste produced as by-products from the reaction stoichiometry itself (1 mark).

📋 Strategy: Define each term separately, then explicitly state what each 'measures' and what 'affects' each, drawing clear contrasts.

⚠️ Examiner Note: Students often use the terms interchangeably or only provide one aspect of each definition.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This is a key conceptual distinction. Encourage students to use clear, distinct points for each definition and the factors affecting them.

✏️ Answer: Advantages in terms of atom economy: Addition polymerisation, like the production of poly(ethene) from ethene, inherently has 100% atom economy. This means that all the atoms from the ethene monomers are incorporated into the desired polymer product, poly(ethene), with no waste by-products formed. This is highly efficient, saving raw materials and avoiding the costs and environmental issues associated with waste disposal.

Disadvantages in terms of environmental impact (despite high atom economy): While the production process itself is atom efficient, poly(ethene) (like most plastics) is not biodegradable. This leads to significant environmental problems, as plastic waste accumulates in landfills and pollutes oceans and natural ecosystems for hundreds of years. The process also requires significant energy, contributing to carbon emissions. So, high atom economy for the reaction doesn't mean the product is environmentally benign post-use.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Advantage: Addition polymerisation has 100% atom economy (1 mark).
  • ✅ Advantage: This means no waste by-products are formed (1 mark).
  • ✅ Disadvantage: Poly(ethene) is not biodegradable (1 mark).
  • ✅ Disadvantage: Leads to plastic pollution/landfill issues if not recycled (1 mark).
  • ✅ Disadvantage: Requires energy for production (1 mark).

📋 Strategy: Address both the reaction efficiency (atom economy) and the product's end-of-life impact separately.

⚠️ Examiner Note: Students often only focus on atom economy and miss the wider environmental consequences of the product itself.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This tests the distinction between the efficiency of the reaction (atom economy) and the overall environmental impact of the product. High atom economy doesn't always equal 'green' overall.

Q4. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

A new pharmaceutical drug requires a complex synthesis. Route 1 has a 90% percentage yield in a single step, but an atom economy of 60%. Route 2 is a two-step process, with each step having an 80% percentage yield, and an overall atom economy of 85%. Discuss which route an industrial chemist might choose, considering economic and environmental factors.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Choosing between these two routes involves balancing several factors.

Route 1 offers a higher single-step percentage yield (90%). This is advantageous if the starting materials are very expensive, as it maximises the conversion of these valuable reactants into the desired product. However, its atom economy is only 60%, meaning a large proportion of reactant atoms are converted into unwanted by-products. This would increase costs for waste disposal and raise environmental concerns due to the volume of waste generated.

Route 2, despite being a two-step process, has a higher overall atom economy of 85%, meaning significantly less waste is generated, which is beneficial for the environment and reduces waste disposal costs. However, its overall percentage yield would be lower (0.80 x 0.80 = 0.64 or 64% yield), meaning more raw materials would be needed to produce the same amount of drug, and there's a greater chance of product loss across two stages. This could make it more expensive in terms of raw material consumption and processing time.

An industrial chemist would choose based on specific priorities: If the raw materials are extraordinarily expensive, Route 1 might be favoured for its higher direct conversion despite the waste. However, if environmental regulations are strict, waste disposal is costly, or raw materials are relatively cheap, Route 2 would likely be preferred due to its superior atom economy and sustainability. A holistic assessment considering raw material costs, energy consumption, by-product toxicity, and waste treatment expenses would be crucial.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Route 1: High single-step yield (90%) reduces need for re-processing, good for expensive raw materials (1 mark).
  • ✅ Route 1: Low atom economy (60%) means significant waste, increasing disposal costs and environmental impact (1 mark).
  • ✅ Route 2: Lower single-step yield (80% x 80% = 64% overall yield) means more raw materials needed, and potential for more losses in two steps (1 mark).
  • ✅ Route 2: Higher atom economy (85%) means less waste overall, reducing environmental burden and disposal costs (1 mark).
  • ✅ Decision: If raw materials are extremely expensive, Route 1's higher single-step yield might be preferred despite waste (1 mark).
  • ✅ Decision: If waste disposal is very costly or environmentally sensitive, Route 2's higher atom economy might be preferred, even with lower overall yield (1 mark).
  • ✅ A balanced conclusion considering both factors is required (1 mark).

📋 Strategy: Analyse each route's yield and atom economy, then discuss the specific economic and environmental implications of each. Conclude by suggesting how a decision would be made based on priority factors.

⚠️ Examiner Note: Students often struggle to integrate both atom economy and percentage yield concepts into a coherent evaluation. They might just list pros and cons without a comparative discussion.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This question requires a multi-faceted evaluation, linking both yield and atom economy to real-world industrial constraints. Encourage students to think about trade-offs.

Q5. (5 Marks | Calculation | ⏱️ ~5 min)

A manufacturer wants to produce 500 kg of a pure chemical. They currently use a reaction with an 80% percentage yield. If they switch to a new catalyst that increases the percentage yield to 90%, how much less starting material (reactant) would be required to produce the same amount of product?

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Let's assume the mass of starting material is directly proportional to the theoretical yield.

For the original process (80% yield): Actual yield = 500 kg Percentage yield = 80% Theoretical yield = Actual yield / (Percentage yield / 100) = 500 kg / 0.80 = 625 kg.

For the improved process (90% yield): Actual yield = 500 kg Percentage yield = 90% Theoretical yield = Actual yield / (Percentage yield / 100) = 500 kg / 0.90 = 555.56 kg.

Difference in theoretical yield = 625 kg - 555.56 kg = 69.44 kg.

Therefore, the manufacturer would require approximately 69.4 kg less starting material to produce 500 kg of the pure chemical.

🔢 Working: 1. Calculate theoretical yield required for 500 kg actual product with 80% yield. 2. Calculate theoretical yield required for 500 kg actual product with 90% yield. 3. Find the difference between the two theoretical yields. 4. State the amount of less starting material required.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ For 80% yield: Theoretical yield = 500 kg / 0.80 = 625 kg (1 mark).
  • ✅ For 90% yield: Theoretical yield = 500 kg / 0.90 = 555.56 kg (1 mark).
  • ✅ Difference in theoretical yield = 625 kg - 555.56 kg = 69.44 kg (1 mark).
  • ✅ Assume 1:1 mass ratio for simplicity (or state that mass of reactant is proportional to theoretical yield) (1 mark).
  • ✅ Less starting material required = 69.44 kg (approx 69.4 kg) (1 mark).
  • ✅ Allow use of a specific M_r for a hypothetical reactant/product as long as consistent.

📋 Strategy: Calculate the 'theoretical yield needed' for both scenarios separately, then subtract to find the difference. Assume proportionality for reactant mass.

⚠️ Examiner Note: Students often make errors when rearranging the percentage yield formula or fail to show clear working for both scenarios.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This is a reverse calculation, working from actual yield to theoretical yield. It's a good way to assess understanding of the practical implications of yield.

Q6. (6 Marks | Calculation | ⏱️ ~6 min)

Magnesium reacts with hydrochloric acid to produce magnesium chloride and hydrogen gas: Mg(s) + 2HCl(aq) → MgCl₂(aq) + H₂(g). A student uses 2.43 g of magnesium and 7.30 g of HCl. The actual yield of magnesium chloride (MgCl₂) obtained is 7.50 g. Calculate the percentage yield of MgCl₂. (Relative atomic masses: Mg=24.3, H=1, Cl=35.5)

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: 1. Calculate molar masses: Mg = 24.3 g/mol HCl = 1 + 35.5 = 36.5 g/mol MgCl₂ = 24.3 + (2 imes 35.5) = 24.3 + 71.0 = 95.3 g/mol

  1. Calculate moles of reactants: Moles of Mg = 2.43 g / 24.3 g/mol = 0.100 mol Moles of HCl = 7.30 g / 36.5 g/mol = 0.200 mol

  2. Determine the limiting reactant: From the balanced equation, the ratio of Mg : HCl is 1 : 2. For 0.100 mol of Mg, 2 imes 0.100 = 0.200 mol of HCl is required. Since we have 0.200 mol of HCl, both reactants are present in the exact stoichiometric amounts. There is no limiting reactant in this case (or they both limit each other if completely consumed).

  3. Calculate theoretical moles of MgCl₂: From the equation, 1 mole of Mg produces 1 mole of MgCl₂. Therefore, 0.100 mol of Mg will produce 0.100 mol of MgCl₂.

  4. Calculate theoretical mass of MgCl₂: Theoretical mass = Moles imes M_r = 0.100 mol imes 95.3 g/mol = 9.53 g.

  5. Calculate percentage yield: Percentage yield = (Actual yield / Theoretical yield) imes 100% = (7.50 g / 9.53 g) imes 100% = 78.7% (to 3 significant figures).

🔢 Working: 1. Calculate molar masses for Mg, HCl, and MgCl₂. 2. Calculate moles of Mg used. 3. Calculate moles of HCl used. 4. Determine the limiting reactant (in this case, they are stoichiometric). 5. Calculate theoretical moles of MgCl₂. 6. Calculate theoretical mass of MgCl₂. 7. Calculate percentage yield.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Molar mass Mg = 24.3 g/mol (1 mark).
  • ✅ Molar mass HCl = 36.5 g/mol (1 mark).
  • ✅ Molar mass MgCl₂ = 95.3 g/mol (1 mark).
  • ✅ Moles Mg = 2.43 / 24.3 = 0.100 mol (1 mark).
  • ✅ Moles HCl = 7.30 / 36.5 = 0.200 mol (1 mark).
  • ✅ Limiting reactant: Mg:HCl ratio is 1:2. Moles HCl needed for 0.100 mol Mg is 0.200 mol. Since 0.200 mol HCl is present, both are fully consumed. No limiting reactant. (Allow 'Mg is limiting' if HCl is slightly in excess or vice-versa, as long as reasoning is consistent with their calculated moles) (1 mark).
  • ✅ Theoretical moles MgCl₂ = 0.100 mol (from Mg) (1 mark).
  • ✅ Theoretical mass MgCl₂ = 0.100 imes 95.3 = 9.53 g (1 mark).
  • ✅ Percentage yield = (7.50 / 9.53) imes 100 = 78.7% (1 mark).
  • ✅ Maximum 6 marks.

📋 Strategy: Follow a clear stoichiometry pathway: M_r, moles, identify limiting reactant (if any), theoretical moles, theoretical mass, then percentage yield.

⚠️ Examiner Note: Misidentifying the limiting reactant (or failing to show working for it) is a frequent error leading to incorrect theoretical yield.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This problem includes identifying the limiting reactant, which is a common challenge. In this specific case, the reactants are in stoichiometric proportions, which can sometimes confuse students looking for an 'excess'.

Q7. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

Discuss how chemists can improve both the percentage yield and the atom economy of a chemical synthesis, outlining the challenges involved.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: To improve percentage yield, chemists can optimise reaction conditions such as temperature, pressure, and reactant concentrations to ensure the reaction goes as close to completion as possible. The use of appropriate catalysts can also speed up the desired reaction without increasing side reactions. Furthermore, refining separation and purification techniques, for example, using more efficient distillation or recrystallization, can minimise product loss. Ensuring reactant purity also helps prevent unwanted side reactions.

To improve atom economy, chemists primarily focus on designing new reaction pathways or choosing existing ones that produce fewer or no by-products. Addition reactions are ideal for this, as all reactant atoms are incorporated into a single product. For reactions that inherently produce by-products, chemists might explore ways to find commercial uses for these by-products, effectively turning 'waste' into valuable co-products, or develop methods to recycle them back into the process as reactants.

Challenges in improving both include the significant research and development costs and time required to discover new catalysts or entirely new, more atom-economical reaction routes. Optimising conditions can be complex, involving trade-offs between yield, reaction rate, and safety. Furthermore, even if by-products can be used, finding a viable market or a cost-effective recycling method for them is not always straightforward. Ultimately, any improvements must also consider overall cost-effectiveness, safety, and environmental impact.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Improve % Yield: Optimise reaction conditions (temperature, pressure, catalyst, concentration) (1 mark).
  • ✅ Improve % Yield: Improve separation/purification techniques to minimise loss (1 mark).
  • ✅ Improve % Yield: Use pure reactants to prevent side reactions (1 mark).
  • ✅ Improve Atom Economy: Choose alternative reaction pathways/mechanisms that produce fewer by-products (e.g. addition instead of substitution) (1 mark).
  • ✅ Improve Atom Economy: Find uses for by-products (e.g., selling them, recycling them as reactants) (1 mark).
  • ✅ Challenges: Optimising conditions can be time-consuming/expensive (1 mark).
  • ✅ Challenges: New catalysts/reaction routes require significant research and development (1 mark).
  • ✅ Challenges: Finding uses for by-products may not always be feasible/economic (1 mark).
  • ✅ Challenges: Environmental concerns and safety must be maintained (1 mark).

📋 Strategy: Break the answer into 'improving yield', 'improving atom economy', and 'challenges', with specific points for each.

⚠️ Examiner Note: Many students can list factors but struggle to fully discuss the 'how' and 'challenges' aspects comprehensively.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This is a comprehensive 'discuss' question requiring knowledge of both concepts and their practical implications. It's a good synthesis question.

🤖 Go Deeper (AI Prompt): "Elaborate on specific experimental techniques used in organic synthesis to maximise yield and discuss how computational chemistry is used to predict and design more atom-economical reaction pathways."

Q8. (3 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~3 min)

Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) can decompose into water and oxygen: 2H₂O₂(aq) → 2H₂O(l) + O₂(g). Is the atom economy for water production in this reaction 100%? Justify your answer. (Relative atomic masses: H=1, O=16)

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: 1. Molar mass of desired product (2H₂O) = 2 imes (2 imes 1 + 16) = 2 imes 18 = 36 g/mol. 2. Total molar mass of reactants (2H₂O₂) = 2 imes (2 imes 1 + 2 imes 16) = 2 imes 34 = 68 g/mol. 3. Atom economy for water = (36 g / 68 g) imes 100% = 52.9%.

No, the atom economy for water production is not 100%. This is because oxygen gas (O₂) is also produced as a by-product. This means that a significant portion of the atoms from the hydrogen peroxide reactants (specifically the oxygen atoms that form O₂) do not end up in the desired water product, thus reducing the atom economy.

🔢 Working: 1. Calculate the molar mass of the desired product (water, 2H₂O). 2. Calculate the total molar mass of the reactants (hydrogen peroxide, 2H₂O₂). 3. Calculate the atom economy. 4. Justify the answer based on the presence of a by-product (oxygen).

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Molar mass of 2H₂O = 36 g/mol (1 mark).
  • ✅ Molar mass of 2H₂O₂ = 68 g/mol (1 mark).
  • ✅ Atom economy for H₂O = (36 / 68) x 100 = 52.9% (1 mark).
  • ✅ Justification: No, it's not 100% because oxygen (O₂) is also produced as a by-product, meaning not all atoms from hydrogen peroxide are incorporated into the desired water product (1 mark).
  • ✅ Award 3 marks for correct calculation and clear justification.

📋 Strategy: Perform the calculation, then clearly explain why it's not 100%, linking it to by-product formation.

⚠️ Examiner Note: Students might incorrectly state 100% atom economy if they don't recognise O₂ as a separate product/by-product.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This problem highlights that even simple decomposition reactions can have less than 100% atom economy if the desired product isn't the only product.

🧩 Quick Quiz (MCQ)

1. Which of the following would lead to a lower percentage yield in a chemical reaction? A) Using a catalyst B) Reactants are completely pure C) Product is lost during filtration D) The reaction is exothermic

Answer

C) Product is lost during filtration Product loss during filtration directly reduces the actual amount of product obtained, thus lowering the percentage yield.

2. Atom economy is calculated as: A) (Actual yield / Theoretical yield) x 100% B) (Mass of desired product / Total mass of reactants) x 100% C) (Mass of reactants / Mass of products) x 100% D) (Moles of desired product / Moles of reactants) x 100%

Answer

B) (Mass of desired product / Total mass of reactants) x 100% Atom economy specifically measures the mass of the desired product relative to the total mass of all reactants, reflecting how efficiently reactant atoms are utilised.

3. A reaction producing a single product from multiple reactants (an addition reaction) will always have: A) A low percentage yield B) 100% atom economy C) Many by-products D) An endothermic energy change

Answer

B) 100% atom economy In an addition reaction, all the atoms from the reactants combine to form a single desired product, meaning there are no by-products and thus 100% atom economy.

4. If the theoretical yield of a reaction is 25 g and the actual yield is 20 g, what is the percentage yield? A) 80% B) 20% C) 125% D) 5%

Answer

A) 80% Percentage yield = (Actual yield / Theoretical yield) x 100% = (20 g / 25 g) x 100% = 80%.

⚡ Cram Sheet (3-3-1)

Key Facts
  • 📌 Percentage yield is (Actual yield / Theoretical yield) x 100%, measuring practical efficiency and affected by losses and incomplete reactions.
  • 📌 Atom economy is (Mass of desired product / Total mass of reactants) x 100%, measuring how much reactant mass is incorporated into the desired product, determined by reaction stoichiometry.
  • 📌 High atom economy means less waste, better for environment and costs; 100% atom economy is characteristic of addition reactions.
Red Flags
  • 🚩 Don't confuse percentage yield with atom economy – they measure different aspects of efficiency.
  • 🚩 Always use a balanced chemical equation and correct molar masses for calculations. Remember to identify the limiting reactant if amounts for more than one reactant are given.
  • 🚩 A percentage yield >100% indicates impurities in the product or measurement errors, not true super-efficiency.
Main Takeaway

Understanding yield and atom economy allows chemists to design and evaluate reactions for maximum efficiency, minimising waste and optimising resource use for both economic and environmental benefit.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The concept of atom economy was formally introduced by Barry Trost in the early 1990s as a key principle of green chemistry. Before this, chemists primarily focused on percentage yield. While yield is crucial for economic reasons (getting as much product as possible from expensive starting materials), it didn't explicitly address the environmental cost of waste. Trost's work highlighted the importance of designing syntheses that incorporate as much of the starting materials as possible into the desired product, minimising unwanted by-products.

⚖️ Ethics

Maximising atom economy has significant ethical implications. It promotes sustainable chemistry by reducing the consumption of finite resources and minimising the production of hazardous waste, thereby lessening the environmental burden. This aligns with the ethical responsibility of chemists to develop processes that are safe, environmentally friendly, and economically viable for society. Choosing a reaction with higher atom economy often leads to a 'greener' process, reflecting a commitment to future generations.

🚀 Advanced Theory

At A-level and beyond, the discussion of atom economy often extends to various reaction types in organic chemistry. For example, addition reactions consistently achieve 100% atom economy. In contrast, substitution, elimination, and condensation reactions typically have lower atom economies because they produce small molecules (like water or HCl) as by-products. Advanced chemists also consider 'E-factors' (environmental factor), which is a ratio of the mass of waste to the mass of product, providing an even more comprehensive measure of process greenness than atom economy alone.
