🧠 Conceptual Understanding

In this topic, we delve into two key families of organic compounds: alcohols, characterised by the hydroxyl (-OH) functional group, and haloalkanes, which contain a halogen atom bonded to an alkyl group. Understanding these functional groups is crucial because they dictate a compound's reactivity and physical properties. We'll explore how they are formed, their characteristic reactions, and the mechanisms by which these reactions occur, paying close attention to factors like bond polarity and bond enthalpy. This builds directly on your prior knowledge of organic structures and nomenclature. Furthermore, we'll introduce the powerful techniques of infrared (IR) spectroscopy and mass spectrometry (MS). These instrumental methods are indispensable to modern chemists, allowing them to rapidly identify unknown compounds and confirm structures, providing a 'fingerprint' of molecular vibrations and a measure of molecular mass and fragmentation patterns. This section ties together fundamental organic chemistry with practical analytical skills, showing how theory and instrumentation work hand-in-hand in the lab.

📌 Core Theory

  • Alcohols contain the -OH functional group and are classified as primary, secondary, or tertiary based on the number of alkyl groups attached to the carbon bearing the hydroxyl group.
  • Alcohols can undergo oxidation (to aldehydes, ketones, or carboxylic acids), dehydration (to alkenes), and esterification.
  • Haloalkanes contain a halogen atom (F, Cl, Br, I) bonded to a saturated carbon atom and are classified as primary, secondary, or tertiary.
  • Haloalkanes are susceptible to nucleophilic substitution reactions, where the halogen is replaced by a nucleophile (e.g., OH⁻, CN⁻, NH₃).
  • The C-X bond in haloalkanes is polar, with the carbon being δ⁺ and the halogen δ⁻, making the carbon an electrophilic centre.
  • The strength of the C-X bond (bond enthalpy) decreases down Group 17, influencing the reactivity of haloalkanes.
  • Haloalkanes can also undergo elimination reactions to form alkenes, particularly with hot ethanolic alkali.
  • Infrared (IR) spectroscopy detects specific functional groups by measuring the absorption of infrared radiation, causing characteristic bonds to vibrate at specific frequencies (wavenumbers).
  • Mass spectrometry (MS) determines the molecular mass of a compound and its fragmentation pattern, providing structural information based on the mass-to-charge ratio (m/z) of ions.
  • The molecular ion peak (M⁺) in a mass spectrum corresponds to the molecular mass of the compound.
  • Characteristic fragment ions in MS help deduce the structure of the molecule.
  • Identifying specific bonds (e.g., O-H, C=O, C-X) using IR spectra is key to identifying functional groups.
  • Practical skills include the preparation and purification of organic liquids, such as reflux, distillation, and solvent extraction.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

Alcohols and haloalkanes are fundamental organic compounds whose reactivity is largely governed by their functional groups. Alcohols exhibit hydrogen bonding due to the polar O-H bond, leading to higher boiling points than comparable alkanes. Their reactions often involve the O-H bond (e.g., oxidation, dehydration) or the C-O bond (e.g., substitution). Haloalkanes, on the other hand, are characterised by a polar carbon-halogen bond, where the halogen is electronegative. This polarity makes the carbon atom electron-deficient and vulnerable to attack by nucleophiles. The reactivity of haloalkanes in nucleophilic substitution is influenced by both bond polarity and bond enthalpy; while greater polarity might suggest higher reactivity, the crucial factor is often the weakness of the C-X bond, which allows easier breaking. Elimination reactions, competing with substitution, are favoured by stronger bases and higher temperatures. Instrumental techniques provide powerful insights into these molecules. IR spectroscopy works by exciting molecular vibrations; different bonds absorb IR radiation at specific frequencies, creating a unique 'fingerprint' spectrum that allows identification of functional groups. Mass spectrometry bombards molecules with high-energy electrons, causing them to ionise and fragment. The resulting ions are separated by their mass-to-charge ratio, giving a spectrum that shows the molecular ion and characteristic fragments, which can be pieced together to deduce the compound's structure. These analytical methods are crucial for confirming synthesis products and identifying unknown organic substances.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Naming Alcohols and Identifying Primary/Secondary/Tertiary

Problem: Name the following alcohol and classify it as primary, secondary, or tertiary: CH₃CH(OH)CH₂CH₃

Steps: 1. Identify the longest carbon chain containing the -OH group. In this case, it's a 4-carbon chain. 2. Number the carbon chain to give the -OH group the lowest possible number. Starting from the left gives -OH on carbon 2. 3. Identify any alkyl substituents. None in this case. 4. Formulate the name: Butan-2-ol. 5. Classify the alcohol: The carbon atom bonded to the -OH group is bonded to two other alkyl groups (a methyl and an ethyl group). Therefore, it is a secondary alcohol.

Answer: Butan-2-ol; Secondary alcohol.

Teacher's Tip

To classify an alcohol, count the number of carbon atoms directly attached to the carbon bearing the -OH group. One carbon = primary, two carbons = secondary, three carbons = tertiary.

2. Predicting Products of Haloalkane Hydrolysis

Problem: Predict the product(s) formed when 1-bromopropane reacts with hot aqueous sodium hydroxide.

Steps: 1. Identify the reactants: 1-bromopropane (CH₃CH₂CH₂Br) and hot aqueous sodium hydroxide (NaOH(aq)). 2. Recognise the reaction type: Aqueous NaOH provides OH⁻ ions, which are strong nucleophiles. This indicates a nucleophilic substitution reaction. 3. Identify the leaving group: The bromine atom (Br) will be replaced. 4. Identify the attacking nucleophile: The hydroxide ion (OH⁻) will attack the δ⁺ carbon. 5. Determine the product: The OH⁻ replaces the Br⁻ to form an alcohol. The product will be propan-1-ol. 6. Write the balanced equation: CH₃CH₂CH₂Br + NaOH → CH₃CH₂CH₂OH + NaBr

Answer: Propan-1-ol (CH₃CH₂CH₂OH) and sodium bromide (NaBr).

Teacher's Tip

Aqueous conditions and moderate temperature typically favour substitution in haloalkanes, while hot ethanolic conditions favour elimination.

3. Interpreting IR Spectra for Functional Groups

Problem: An organic compound shows a strong, broad absorption band at 3200-3600 cm⁻¹ and no absorption around 1700 cm⁻¹. Suggest a functional group present and one that is absent.

Steps: 1. Recall characteristic IR absorption ranges for common functional groups. 2. Interpret the 3200-3600 cm⁻¹ broad absorption: This is characteristic of an O-H stretch, specifically from an alcohol or carboxylic acid. 3. Interpret the lack of absorption around 1700 cm⁻¹: This region typically indicates a C=O stretch (carbonyl group), found in aldehydes, ketones, carboxylic acids, and esters. 4. Combine interpretations: The presence of a broad O-H stretch and the absence of a C=O stretch strongly suggest the compound is an alcohol. 5. State conclusions: Functional group present is likely an alcohol (-OH). Functional group absent is a carbonyl (C=O).

Answer: The compound likely contains an alcohol (-OH) functional group and does not contain a carbonyl (C=O) functional group.

Teacher's Tip

Pay attention to both the presence and absence of key peaks. A broad O-H indicates hydrogen bonding, typical of alcohols. The absence of a C=O peak at ~1700 cm⁻¹ rules out many carbonyl-containing compounds.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Antiseptics and Disinfectants: Ethanol (an alcohol) is widely used in hand sanitisers and disinfectants due to its ability to denature proteins in microorganisms.
  • Refrigerants and Fire Extinguishers: Older haloalkanes like chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) were used as refrigerants and propellants, though now largely replaced by less ozone-depleting alternatives like hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs). Halons were used in fire extinguishers.
  • Anaesthetics and Solvents: Some haloalkanes, such as halothane (a fluorinated haloalkane), have been used as general anaesthetics. Many haloalkanes are excellent solvents for organic compounds.
  • Plastic Manufacturing: Haloalkanes like chloroethene (vinyl chloride) are monomers used in the production of poly(chloroethene) (PVC), a common plastic.
  • Forensic Science and Drug Testing: IR spectroscopy and mass spectrometry are indispensable in forensic labs for identifying unknown substances, drug screening, and analysing trace evidence.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing nucleophilic substitution with electrophilic substitution; nucleophiles attack electron-deficient carbons, not electron-rich pi systems.
  • ❌ Incorrectly classifying primary, secondary, and tertiary alcohols/haloalkanes; focus on the carbon atom directly bonded to the functional group.
  • ❌ Mixing up conditions for substitution versus elimination in haloalkane reactions (e.g., aqueous vs. ethanolic alkali).
  • ❌ Assuming a weak C-X bond means lower reactivity; a weaker bond is generally easier to break, leading to higher reactivity.
  • ❌ Misinterpreting IR spectra by looking for all possible peaks instead of focusing on diagnostic functional group regions (e.g., O-H, C=O).
  • ❌ Mistaking a fragment ion peak for the molecular ion peak in mass spectrometry, especially when the molecular ion is small or unstable.

Consequently, tertiary haloalkanes form these stable carbocations more readily, leading to a faster Sɴ1 reaction rate compared to primary haloalkanes, which would form highly unstable primary carbocations.

  • ✅ This increased stability is due to the electron-donating inductive effect of the three alkyl groups.
  • ✅ Alkyl groups push electron density towards the positive charge, delocalising it and making the carbocation more stable.

Q4. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

An unknown organic compound X has the molecular formula C₄H₁₀O. Its IR spectrum shows a broad absorption at 3300 cm⁻¹ and no absorption at 1700 cm⁻¹. When X is reacted with acidified potassium dichromate(VI) under gentle heating and immediate distillation, an organic product Y is formed which turns Tollens' reagent silver. What are the structures of X and Y? Explain your reasoning.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: From the IR spectrum of compound X: * The broad absorption at 3300 cm⁻¹ indicates the presence of an O-H (hydroxyl) group, characteristic of an alcohol. * The absence of absorption at 1700 cm⁻¹ confirms that X is not a carbonyl compound (ketone, aldehyde, or carboxylic acid) before reaction. * The molecular formula C₄H₁₀O is consistent with a saturated alcohol.

When X is reacted with acidified potassium dichromate(VI) under gentle heating and immediate distillation, an organic product Y is formed. This indicates that X is an alcohol that undergoes oxidation to Y, and Y is volatile enough to be distilled off.

Product Y turns Tollens' reagent silver. This is a characteristic test for aldehydes, meaning Y is an aldehyde.

Since X is an alcohol that can be oxidised to an aldehyde, X must be a primary alcohol. Secondary alcohols oxidise to ketones (which don't react with Tollens'), and tertiary alcohols don't oxidise.

Therefore: * Compound X is Butan-1-ol (CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₂OH). * Compound Y is Butanal (CH₃CH₂CH₂CHO).

Interpret reaction conditions (oxidation, distillation). 4. Interpret chemical test result (Tollens' reagent). 5. Combine all information to deduce the type of alcohol (primary, secondary, tertiary) and then the specific structures.

Q5. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

Butan-1-ol reacts with concentrated sulfuric acid to form an alkene. Draw the mechanism for this dehydration reaction, including relevant curly arrows and intermediate(s).

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: The dehydration of butan-1-ol in the presence of concentrated sulfuric acid proceeds via an E1 (elimination unimolecular) mechanism:

Step 1: Protonation of the hydroxyl group. The oxygen atom of the -OH group uses a lone pair of electrons to attack a proton (H⁺) from the concentrated sulfuric acid. This makes the hydroxyl group a better leaving group, transforming it into a water molecule. [Diagram: CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₂-OH + H⁺ → CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₂-OH₂⁺]

Step 2: Loss of a water molecule to form a carbocation. The now positively charged water molecule detaches from the carbon chain as a neutral water molecule (a good leaving group). This leaves behind a primary carbocation. [Diagram: CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₂-OH₂⁺ → CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₂⁺ + H₂O]

Step 3: Deprotonation to form the alkene. A base (e.g., H₂O or HSO₄⁻) abstracts a proton from an adjacent carbon atom (a β-hydrogen). The electrons from the C-H bond then shift to form a C=C double bond, yielding the alkene and regenerating the H⁺ catalyst. For butan-1-ol, the major product will be but-1-ene. [Diagram: CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₂⁺ + Base → CH₃CH₂CH₂=CH₂ + H⁺Base]

(Note: While butan-1-ol typically forms but-1-ene as the major product, carbocation rearrangement can lead to the more stable secondary carbocation and subsequently but-2-ene. For A-level, the primary carbocation and direct elimination to but-1-ene is usually sufficient.)

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Step 1: Protonation of the -OH group by H₂SO₄ (or H⁺). Curly arrow from O lone pair to H⁺.
  • ✅ Step 2: Loss of H₂O (leaving group) to form a primary carbocation. Curly arrow from C-O bond to O.
  • ✅ Step 3: Deprotonation by H₂O (or HSO₄⁻) of an adjacent carbon, forming a C=C double bond.
  • ✅ Curly arrow from C-H bond to form C=C, and from C=C to H⁺.
  • ✅ Shows major product but-1-ene (or but-2-ene if rearrangement considered).
  • ✅ Correctly shows all curly arrows and charges.

Q6. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

A student proposes that 1-bromo-2-methylpropane could undergo Sɴ1 reaction. Evaluate this proposal, considering the structure of the haloalkane and the Sɴ1 mechanism.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: The proposal that 1-bromo-2-methylpropane undergoes an Sɴ1 reaction is largely incorrect. 1-bromo-2-methylpropane is a primary haloalkane, meaning the carbon atom attached to the bromine is only bonded to one other alkyl group (an isobutyl group in this case).

The Sɴ1 mechanism relies on the formation of a carbocation intermediate in its rate-determining step. Primary carbocations, such as the one that would form from 1-bromo-2-methylpropane (CH₃CH(CH₃)CH₂⁺), are highly unstable. This instability arises because there is only one alkyl group (the -CH(CH₃)CH₃ group) available to stabilise the positive charge through the positive inductive effect. The delocalisation of charge is minimal, requiring a very high activation energy for its formation.

Consequently, the formation of such an unstable primary carbocation is energetically unfavourable, making the Sɴ1 pathway highly unlikely. Instead, primary haloalkanes like 1-bromo-2-methylpropane typically undergo nucleophilic substitution via the Sɴ2 mechanism, where the nucleophile attacks the carbon atom from the back, and the leaving group departs simultaneously, avoiding the formation of an unstable carbocation intermediate. This is also favoured by less steric hindrance around the primary carbon.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ 1-bromo-2-methylpropane is a primary haloalkane.
  • ✅ Sɴ1 reactions involve the formation of a carbocation intermediate in the rate-determining step.
  • ✅ Primary carbocations (R-CH₂⁺) are highly unstable due to limited inductive stabilisation from only one alkyl group.
  • ✅ The energy required to form a primary carbocation is very high, making its formation unfavourable.
  • ✅ Therefore, 1-bromo-2-methylpropane is highly unlikely to undergo an Sɴ1 reaction.
  • ✅ It would predominantly react via an Sɴ2 mechanism due to less steric hindrance and the instability of the primary carbocation.

Answer

C) O-H (alcohol) A broad absorption peak in the region 3200-3600 cm⁻¹ is characteristic of the O-H stretch in alcohols, broadened by hydrogen bonding.

Answer

C) Ethanol and ethanoic acid Ethanoic acid (a carboxylic acid) would show a characteristic strong C=O stretch around 1680-1750 cm⁻¹ and a very broad O-H stretch (often overlapping with C-H) in the region 2500-3300 cm⁻¹. Ethanol (an alcohol) would show an O-H stretch around 3200-3600 cm⁻¹ but no C=O stretch, making them clearly distinguishable.

Answer

B) Butan-1-ol The molecular ion peak (M+) in a mass spectrum corresponds to the relative molecular mass of the compound. Butan-1-ol has the molecular formula C4H10O, giving a molecular mass of (4 × 12) + (10 × 1) + (1 × 16) = 48 + 10 + 16 = 74.

10. In the mass spectrum of propan-1-ol (CH3CH2CH2OH), which fragment ion would correspond to the loss of a hydroxyl group (-OH)? A) m/z = 45 B) m/z = 43 C) m/z = 31 D) m/z = 29

Answer

B) m/z = 43 The molecular ion of propan-1-ol has m/z = 60. The loss of a hydroxyl group (-OH), which has a mass of 17 (16 for O + 1 for H), would result in a fragment ion with m/z = 60 - 17 = 43. This corresponds to the propyl cation (CH3CH2CH2+).

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The discovery and study of alcohols trace back centuries, with ethanol being one of the earliest known organic compounds, produced through fermentation. Early synthetic organic chemists, such as Victor Grignard and his famous reagent (organomagnesium halides), revolutionised the synthesis of alcohols and other complex organic molecules in the early 20th century. Haloalkanes gained significant industrial importance with the development of refrigerants (CFCs) in the 1930s, though their environmental impact (ozone depletion) led to their eventual phasing out and the search for safer alternatives. Infrared spectroscopy emerged as a powerful analytical tool in the mid-20th century, allowing chemists to quickly identify functional groups, a task that was once tedious and time-consuming using only chemical tests. Mass spectrometry, initially developed for isotope analysis, evolved into a crucial tool for structural elucidation, especially with advancements in ionisation techniques like electrospray ionisation in the late 20th century.

⚖️ Ethics

The widespread use of haloalkanes, particularly chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) and halons, for refrigerants, aerosols, and fire extinguishers, led to severe environmental consequences, specifically ozone layer depletion and contribution to the greenhouse effect. This raised critical ethical questions about industrial responsibility, the long-term impact of synthetic chemicals, and the balance between technological convenience and ecological preservation. The Montreal Protocol (1987) stands as a landmark international agreement addressing these issues. In contrast, ethanol, while a renewable fuel source, also presents ethical challenges related to its use as an intoxicating substance and the moral implications of its production (e.g., land use for fuel crops vs. food production). The development of safer, 'green' solvents to replace toxic haloalkanes is a continuous ethical drive in chemistry.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond the basic Sɴ1 and Sɴ2 mechanisms, the stereochemistry of these reactions is crucial. Sɴ2 reactions proceed with inversion of configuration at the chiral centre, while Sɴ1 reactions typically lead to racemisation due to the planar carbocation intermediate. The choice between Sɴ1/Sɴ2 and E1/E2 (elimination) pathways is dictated by a complex interplay of the substrate's structure (primary, secondary, tertiary), the strength and bulkiness of the nucleophile/base, and the solvent properties (polar protic vs. polar aprotic). For alcohols, dehydration can involve carbocation rearrangements (e.g., hydride or alkyl shifts) to form more stable carbocations, leading to unexpected alkene products (Zaitsev's rule). Advanced IR interpretation can also involve studying solvent effects on stretching frequencies or coupling modes, while high-resolution mass spectrometry can differentiate between compounds with identical nominal molecular masses by precisely measuring isotopic masses.

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

For alcohols and haloalkanes, practicals often involve synthesis and purification. Key methods include:

  1. Preparation of a haloalkane from an alcohol (e.g., 1-bromobutane from butan-1-ol):

    • Steps: Mix alcohol with a halide salt (e.g., NaBr) and concentrated sulfuric acid. Heat under reflux to ensure complete reaction and prevent loss of volatile compounds. Allow to cool. Wash the crude product with sodium carbonate solution (to remove excess acid), then water. Dry with an anhydrous salt (e.g., MgSO₄). Purify by distillation, collecting the fraction boiling within the known range of the product.
    • Safety notes: Concentrated acids are corrosive. Haloalkanes and alcohols are flammable. Work in a fume cupboard. Use appropriate PPE (gloves, eye protection). Reflux apparatus prevents volatile vapours from escaping into the lab.

  2. Oxidation of an alcohol (e.g., ethanol to ethanal or ethanoic acid):

    • Steps (to ethanal): Add acidified potassium dichromate(VI) dropwise to ethanol. Heat gently and distil immediately to collect the aldehyde as it forms, preventing further oxidation.
    • Steps (to ethanoic acid): Add excess acidified potassium dichromate(VI) to ethanol. Heat under reflux to ensure complete oxidation.
    • Safety notes: Oxidising agents are harmful. Flammable alcohols. Work in a fume cupboard. Use eye protection.

  3. Tests for functional groups and halide ions:

    • O-H (alcohol): Add acidified potassium dichromate(VI) and warm. Primary/secondary alcohols turn orange to green. Tertiary remains orange.
    • C=O (aldehyde/ketone): Add 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazine (2,4-DNPH). Orange/yellow precipitate indicates C=O. Distinguish aldehyde from ketone using Tollens' reagent (silver mirror for aldehyde) or Fehling's solution (red precipitate for aldehyde).
    • Halide ions (e.g., after hydrolysis of haloalkane): Acidify with dilute nitric acid, then add aqueous silver nitrate. AgCl (white ppt), AgBr (cream ppt), AgI (yellow ppt). Confirm with ammonia solution (AgCl dissolves readily, AgBr sparingly, AgI insoluble).

  • 🛑 Alcohols: Flammable (especially lower alcohols like ethanol, methanol). Can be irritants or toxic if ingested in large quantities (e.g., methanol is highly toxic).

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. Nucleophilic Substitution (Sɴ1): This is a two-step mechanism typically for tertiary haloalkanes. Step 1 (slow, rate-determining): The C-X bond breaks heterolytically, forming a planar carbocation intermediate and a halide ion. Step 2 (fast): A nucleophile attacks the electron-deficient carbon of the carbocation from either side, forming the product. This results in racemisation if the carbon is chiral.
  2. Nucleophilic Substitution (Sɴ2): This is a one-step, concerted mechanism typically for primary haloalkanes. The nucleophile attacks the partially positive carbon atom (δ⁺) from the side opposite to the leaving halogen (backside attack). Simultaneously, the C-X bond breaks, and the halogen leaves as a halide ion. This occurs via a five-coordinate transition state and results in an inversion of configuration at a chiral carbon.
  3. Elimination (E2): This is a one-step, concerted mechanism for haloalkanes when reacted with a strong base (e.g., hot ethanolic KOH). The base abstracts a hydrogen atom from a carbon adjacent to the C-X bond (a β-hydrogen). Simultaneously, a C=C double bond forms, and the halide ion leaves. This leads to the formation of an alkene.
  4. Alcohol Dehydration (E1): This is typically an acid-catalysed, two-step mechanism. Step 1: The alcohol's -OH group is protonated by the acid catalyst, forming an -OH₂⁺ group. Step 2: The -OH₂⁺ group leaves as a water molecule, forming a carbocation. Step 3: A base (e.g., water or HSO₄⁻) abstracts a proton from an adjacent carbon, and the electrons form a C=C double bond, yielding an alkene and regenerating the acid catalyst. Carbocation rearrangements can occur for more stable products.
  5. Alcohol Oxidation: This involves the removal of hydrogen atoms from the carbon bearing the -OH group and/or an adjacent carbon. Primary alcohols are oxidised to aldehydes (loss of 2H), then to carboxylic acids (gain of O). Secondary alcohols are oxidised to ketones (loss of 2H). Tertiary alcohols do not oxidise as they lack hydrogens on the carbon bearing the -OH group and on the alpha carbon that could be removed without breaking a C-C bond.

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Nucleophilic Substitution (Haloalkanes): Aqueous alkali (e.g., NaOH(aq)), warming gently. For Sɴ1, polar protic solvents (e.g., water, alcohols). For Sɴ2, polar aprotic solvents (e.g., acetone, DMF).
  • Elimination (Haloalkanes): Hot ethanolic alkali (e.g., KOH in ethanol), often heating under reflux.
  • Oxidation of Primary Alcohols (to Aldehyde): Acidified potassium dichromate(VI) (K₂Cr₂O₇/H₂SO₄), heat gently and distil immediately.
  • Oxidation of Primary Alcohols (to Carboxylic Acid): Acidified potassium dichromate(VI) (K₂Cr₂O₇/H₂SO₄), heat under reflux.
  • Oxidation of Secondary Alcohols (to Ketone): Acidified potassium dichromate(VI) (K₂Cr₂O₇/H₂SO₄), heat under reflux.
  • Dehydration of Alcohols: Concentrated sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) or concentrated phosphoric acid (H₃PO₄), heat to high temperatures (e.g., 170°C).
  • Hydrolysis of Haloalkanes for Halide Test: Aqueous silver nitrate solution, warmed (rate varies with halogen: I>Br>Cl). Ethanol is often added to ensure miscibility.

🔦 Element Spotlight

Oxygen in Alcohols: Oxygen is a highly electronegative element (Pauling scale ~3.44). In alcohols, the oxygen atom is bonded to a carbon atom and a hydrogen atom, forming the hydroxyl (-OH) functional group. This makes both the C-O and O-H bonds polar. The high electronegativity of oxygen creates a significant partial negative charge (δ⁻) on the oxygen and partial positive charges (δ⁺) on the attached carbon and hydrogen. The polarity of the O-H bond enables alcohols to form hydrogen bonds with other alcohol molecules and with water, leading to higher boiling points and solubility in water compared to alkanes of similar size. The lone pairs of electrons on the oxygen also make it a nucleophilic site. In reactions, the O-H bond can be broken (e.g., in reactions with Na to form alkoxides) or the C-O bond can be broken (e.g., in dehydration or substitution reactions). The ability of the oxygen to be protonated (e.g., by H⁺ from acid) to form -OH₂⁺ is crucial for making the hydroxyl group a good leaving group (as H₂O) in substitution and elimination reactions.
