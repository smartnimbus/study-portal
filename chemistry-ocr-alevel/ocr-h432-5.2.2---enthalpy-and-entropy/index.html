🧠 Conceptual Understanding

As your chemistry teacher, let's unpack enthalpy and entropy. You already know about enthalpy changes (ΔH) – whether a reaction releases heat (exothermic, ΔH < 0) or absorbs it (endothermic, ΔH > 0). But heat isn't the only factor determining if a reaction will happen. That's where entropy (ΔS) comes in. Entropy is a measure of the dispersal of energy in a system, often linked to the 'disorder' or 'randomness' of particles. Think of it: solid particles are highly ordered, liquid particles are less ordered, and gas particles are very disordered. So, gases have higher entropy than liquids, which have higher entropy than solids. Reactions that lead to more disorder, or more gaseous molecules, generally have a positive entropy change (ΔS > 0). The interplay between enthalpy (ΔH), entropy (ΔS), and temperature (T) dictates the overall feasibility of a reaction, through the Gibbs free energy equation (ΔG = ΔH - TΔS). A process is considered feasible, or spontaneous, if ΔG is negative. However, remember that 'feasible' doesn't mean 'fast' – kinetics (reaction rates) are a separate factor.

📌 Core Theory

  • Entropy (ΔS) is a measure of the dispersal of energy in a system; a more disordered system has greater entropy.
  • The magnitude of entropy increases with disorder: solids < liquids < gases.
  • Reactions that increase the number of gaseous molecules generally result in a positive entropy change (ΔS > 0) due to increased disorder.
  • The entropy change of a system (ΔS) can be calculated using the standard entropies of reactants and products: ΔS° = ΣS°(products) - ΣS°(reactants).
  • The feasibility of a process depends on both the enthalpy change (ΔH) and the entropy change (ΔS), influenced by temperature (T).
  • The Gibbs free energy change (ΔG) combines these factors: ΔG = ΔH - TΔS (where T is in Kelvin).
  • A process is feasible (spontaneous) when ΔG has a negative value (ΔG < 0).
  • If ΔG = 0, the system is at equilibrium.
  • If ΔG > 0, the process is not feasible under the given conditions, but the reverse reaction might be.
  • Predictions of feasibility by ΔG do not account for reaction kinetics; a feasible reaction may still be very slow.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

Enthalpy and entropy are fundamental thermodynamic concepts that determine the spontaneity of chemical and physical processes. Enthalpy (ΔH) quantifies the heat change at constant pressure, while entropy (ΔS) quantifies the energy dispersal and the extent of disorder within a system. For a reaction to be spontaneous, it generally prefers to release energy (exothermic, -ΔH) and increase disorder (+ΔS). However, these two factors often work in opposition. This is where the Gibbs free energy (ΔG) becomes crucial. The Gibbs equation, ΔG = ΔH - TΔS, provides a comprehensive criterion for feasibility. The TΔS term represents the energy associated with the entropy change at a given temperature. If ΔG is negative, the reaction is spontaneous; if positive, it is non-spontaneous. If ΔG is zero, the system is at equilibrium. It's vital to note that temperature (T) must always be in Kelvin for these calculations. A key insight is that at high temperatures, the TΔS term becomes more significant, meaning reactions with a positive ΔS become more likely to be feasible, even if they are endothermic. Conversely, at low temperatures, the ΔH term dominates. Furthermore, the Gibbs free energy only predicts whether a reaction can occur, not how fast it will occur. A highly feasible reaction might still have a very high activation energy and thus proceed incredibly slowly (e.g., the combustion of diamond).

📖 Key Definitions

Term Definition
Enthalpy Change (ΔH) The heat change measured at constant pressure, typically associated with bond breaking and bond forming.
Entropy (S) A measure of the dispersal of energy within a system, often related to the degree of disorder or randomness.
Entropy Change (ΔS) The change in the dispersal of energy or disorder of a system during a chemical or physical process.
Gibbs Free Energy Change (ΔG) A thermodynamic potential that measures the maximum reversible work that may be performed by a thermodynamic system at a constant temperature and pressure. It determines the feasibility (spontaneity) of a process.
Feasibility (Spontaneity) The tendency of a process to occur without continuous external intervention; a reaction is feasible if its ΔG is negative.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Calculating Standard Entropy Change (ΔS°)

Problem: Calculate the standard entropy change (ΔS°) for the combustion of methane: CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) given the standard molar entropies (S°) at 298 K:S°[CH₄(g)] = 186.2 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹S°[O₂(g)] = 205.0 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹S°[CO₂(g)] = 213.7 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹S°[H₂O(l)] = 69.9 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹

Steps: 1. Write down the balanced chemical equation: CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l). 2. Identify reactants and products and their stoichiometric coefficients. 3. Use the formula: ΔS° = ΣS°(products) - ΣS°(reactants). 4. Calculate ΣS°(products): (1 × S°[CO₂(g)]) + (2 × S°[H₂O(l)]) = (1 × 213.7) + (2 × 69.9) = 213.7 + 139.8 = 353.5 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹. 5. Calculate ΣS°(reactants): (1 × S°[CH₄(g)]) + (2 × S°[O₂(g)]) = (1 × 186.2) + (2 × 205.0) = 186.2 + 410.0 = 596.2 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹. 6. Subtract the sum of reactant entropies from the sum of product entropies: ΔS° = 353.5 - 596.2 = -242.7 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹.

Answer: ΔS° = -242.7 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹

Teacher's Tip

Pay close attention to the state symbols (g), (l), (s) as they significantly affect entropy values. Also, remember to multiply by stoichiometric coefficients.

2. Calculating Gibbs Free Energy Change (ΔG) and Predicting Feasibility

Problem: For a reaction, ΔH = -150 kJ mol⁻¹ and ΔS = -50 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹. Determine if the reaction is feasible at 25 °C. If not, suggest a temperature at which it might become feasible.

Steps: 1. Convert ΔH to J mol⁻¹: ΔH = -150 kJ mol⁻¹ = -150,000 J mol⁻¹. 2. Convert temperature to Kelvin: T = 25 °C + 273 = 298 K. 3. Use the Gibbs equation: ΔG = ΔH - TΔS. 4. Substitute the values: ΔG = -150,000 J mol⁻¹ - (298 K × -50 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹). 5. Calculate the TΔS term: 298 × -50 = -14,900 J mol⁻¹. 6. Calculate ΔG: ΔG = -150,000 - (-14,900) = -150,000 + 14,900 = -135,100 J mol⁻¹. 7. Convert ΔG back to kJ mol⁻¹ for easier interpretation: ΔG = -135.1 kJ mol⁻¹. 8. Since ΔG is negative, the reaction is feasible at 25 °C. 9. To find a temperature where it might become non-feasible (ΔG > 0) or reach equilibrium (ΔG = 0), set ΔG = 0: 0 = ΔH - TΔS. 10. Rearrange to solve for T: T = ΔH / ΔS. 11. Substitute values: T = (-150,000 J mol⁻¹) / (-50 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹) = 3000 K. 12. This means the reaction is feasible below 3000 K. Therefore, it is feasible at 25 °C. If the question had asked for a temperature where it is not feasible, it would be above 3000 K.

Answer: At 25 °C (298 K), ΔG = -135.1 kJ mol⁻¹. Since ΔG is negative, the reaction is feasible at this temperature. This reaction remains feasible until approximately 3000 K.

Teacher's Tip

Always convert enthalpy to Joules and temperature to Kelvin before using the Gibbs equation to avoid common unit errors. Remember that a negative ΔG indicates feasibility.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Combustion of fuels: Highly exothermic (negative ΔH) and increases gaseous products (positive ΔS), making it highly feasible and a primary energy source.
  • Rusting of iron: A spontaneous oxidation reaction (ΔG < 0) that occurs slowly over time, demonstrating the difference between feasibility and reaction rate.
  • Dissolving solids in liquids: Many dissolution processes are endothermic (positive ΔH), but become feasible at higher temperatures due to a significant increase in entropy (positive ΔS) as particles disperse into solution.
  • Refrigeration and air conditioning: These processes require energy input because they decrease the entropy of the system (cooling makes molecules less disordered) and are therefore non-spontaneous (positive ΔG) without work done on the system.
  • Biological processes: Maintaining highly ordered structures (low entropy) in living organisms requires continuous energy input (food) to overcome the natural tendency towards disorder and achieve a negative ΔG for crucial reactions.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing feasibility with reaction rate: A reaction with a negative ΔG is feasible, but it might still be kinetically slow.
  • ❌ Forgetting to convert temperature to Kelvin when using ΔG = ΔH - TΔS.
  • ❌ Incorrectly interpreting the sign of ΔS: A positive ΔS means increased disorder, not necessarily a 'better' reaction.
  • ❌ Assuming that exothermic reactions are always spontaneous, ignoring the role of entropy.
  • ❌ Assuming that ΔG = 0 means the reaction stops; it actually means the system is at equilibrium, with forward and reverse rates being equal.

In the Gibbs equation, ΔG = ΔH - TΔS, if ΔS is negative, then -TΔS becomes positive. However, because ΔH is so strongly negative, its magnitude significantly outweighs the positive -TΔS term even at higher temperatures. This ensures that ΔG remains negative across all practical temperatures, making the combustion of hydrogen always thermodynamically feasible.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Combustion is highly exothermic, so ΔH is very large and negative (1 mark). There is a decrease in the number of moles of gas (3 to 0, if H₂O(l)) so ΔS is negative (1 mark). Since ΔH is very large and negative, it outweighs the positive TΔS term (as -TΔS is positive here) (1 mark). Therefore, ΔG remains negative at all relevant temperatures, making the reaction always feasible (1 mark).

⚠️ Examiner Note: Students often focus only on the decrease in gas moles (negative ΔS) and miss the crucial magnitude of the negative ΔH.

Q6. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

The Haber process, N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g), is typically carried out at high pressures and moderate temperatures (around 450 °C). Explain why these conditions are chosen, considering both thermodynamic feasibility and reaction kinetics.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: The Haber process is a classic example of balancing thermodynamics and kinetics.Thermodynamically, the forward reaction N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) is exothermic (ΔH < 0) and involves a decrease in the number of gaseous moles (4 moles to 2 moles), leading to a negative entropy change (ΔS < 0). According to ΔG = ΔH - TΔS, a negative ΔG (feasibility) is favored at low temperatures, where the negative ΔH term dominates the positive TΔS term.Kinetically, however, low temperatures result in very slow reaction rates due to insufficient kinetic energy for reactant molecules to overcome the activation energy barrier effectively.To achieve a commercially viable process, a compromise temperature of around 450 °C is used. This temperature is higher than what is thermodynamically optimal for maximum yield but is high enough to ensure a sufficiently fast reaction rate.High pressures are used for two main reasons: firstly, from a thermodynamic perspective, increasing pressure shifts the equilibrium to the side with fewer moles of gas (products), thereby increasing the yield of ammonia (Le Chatelier's principle). Secondly, kinetically, higher pressure increases the concentration of reactant molecules, leading to more frequent collisions and thus a faster reaction rate.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Thermodynamics: ΔH for forward reaction is negative (exothermic) (1 mark). ΔS for forward reaction is negative (4 moles gas to 2 moles gas) (1 mark). Therefore, to make ΔG negative, low temperatures are thermodynamically favoured (ΔG = -ΔH - TΔS, so T needs to be small) (1 mark). Kinetics: Low temperatures lead to slow reaction rates due to lower kinetic energy and fewer successful collisions (1 mark). A moderate temperature of 450 °C is a compromise: it provides a high enough rate (sufficient kinetic energy) (1 mark), while still allowing a reasonable yield, despite being above the purely thermodynamic optimal temperature (1 mark). High pressure shifts equilibrium to the right (more gaseous moles on left), increasing yield and thus feasibility and rate (1 mark, max 6).

Q7. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

Explain why the formation of ozone, 3O₂(g) → 2O₃(g), has a positive ΔH but a negative ΔS. Why is it only feasible under specific conditions (e.g., UV radiation)?

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: The formation of ozone is an endothermic process (positive ΔH) because it requires a significant energy input to break the strong O=O double bonds in oxygen molecules. Simultaneously, it has a negative entropy change (ΔS < 0) because 3 moles of gaseous reactants (O₂) convert into 2 moles of gaseous product (O₃), leading to a decrease in the number of gas molecules and thus a decrease in disorder.According to the Gibbs equation, ΔG = ΔH - TΔS. With a positive ΔH and a negative ΔS, the equation becomes ΔG = (+ΔH) - T(-ΔS), which simplifies to ΔG = (+ΔH) + T|ΔS|. Since both terms on the right are positive, ΔG will always be positive, indicating that the formation of ozone is never thermodynamically feasible under standard conditions. It is only observed due to the continuous input of high-energy UV radiation, which provides the necessary activation energy and drives the non-spontaneous reaction forward.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ ΔH is positive because energy is required to break O=O bonds and form O-O bonds, with the net process being endothermic (1 mark). ΔS is negative because 3 moles of gaseous reactants form 2 moles of gaseous product, decreasing disorder (1 mark). For ΔG = ΔH - TΔS to be negative (feasible), the positive ΔH and positive -TΔS terms (since ΔS is negative) would always result in a positive ΔG (1 mark). Therefore, this reaction is non-spontaneous and requires continuous energy input, such as UV radiation, to drive it (1 mark).

⚠️ Examiner Note: Students often miss that the positive ΔH and negative ΔS together make ΔG always positive, or they don't explicitly link external energy input to overcoming non-feasibility.

Q8. (5 Marks | Calculation | ⏱️ ~5 min)

Given a reaction with ΔH = +120 kJ mol⁻¹ and ΔS = +250 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹. Calculate ΔG at 500 K. Based on your answer, comment on the feasibility of the reaction at this temperature and at 200 K.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: First, convert ΔH to Joules: ΔH = +120 kJ mol⁻¹ = +120,000 J mol⁻¹.

Calculate ΔG at 500 K: ΔG = ΔH - TΔS ΔG = 120,000 J mol⁻¹ - (500 K × 250 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹) ΔG = 120,000 J mol⁻¹ - 125,000 J mol⁻¹ ΔG = -5000 J mol⁻¹ or -5 kJ mol⁻¹. At 500 K, ΔG is negative, so the reaction is feasible.

Now, calculate ΔG at 200 K: ΔG = 120,000 J mol⁻¹ - (200 K × 250 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹) ΔG = 120,000 J mol⁻¹ - 50,000 J mol⁻¹ ΔG = +70,000 J mol⁻¹ or +70 kJ mol⁻¹. At 200 K, ΔG is positive, so the reaction is not feasible.

Comment: The reaction is endothermic (ΔH > 0) and has a positive entropy change (ΔS > 0). It becomes feasible at higher temperatures where the TΔS term is large enough to overcome the positive ΔH. At 500 K, it is feasible, but at 200 K, it is not.

🔢 Working: 1. ΔH = +120,000 J mol⁻¹. 2. At 500 K: TΔS = 500 × 250 = 125,000 J mol⁻¹. 3. ΔG (500 K) = 120,000 - 125,000 = -5,000 J mol⁻¹. 4. Feasibility at 500 K: Yes, because ΔG < 0. 5. At 200 K: TΔS = 200 × 250 = 50,000 J mol⁻¹. 6. ΔG (200 K) = 120,000 - 50,000 = +70,000 J mol⁻¹. 7. Feasibility at 200 K: No, because ΔG > 0.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Convert ΔH to J: 120,000 J mol⁻¹ (1 mark)
  • ✅ Calculate ΔG at 500 K: ΔG = 120,000 - (500 × 250) = 120,000 - 125,000 = -5000 J mol⁻¹ (1 mark)
  • ✅ Comment on feasibility at 500 K: feasible (since ΔG < 0) (1 mark)
  • ✅ Calculate ΔG at 200 K: ΔG = 120,000 - (200 × 250) = 120,000 - 50,000 = +70,000 J mol⁻¹ (1 mark)
  • ✅ Comment on feasibility at 200 K: not feasible (since ΔG > 0) (1 mark)

Q9. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

Consider the vaporization of water: H₂O(l) → H₂O(g). At 100 °C and 1 atm, this process is at equilibrium. Using the Gibbs free energy equation, explain how this indicates that water boils at 100 °C at standard pressure.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: For the vaporization of water, H₂O(l) → H₂O(g), at 100 °C and 1 atm, the system is at equilibrium. By definition, at equilibrium, the Gibbs free energy change (ΔG) is zero.Therefore, at 100 °C (373 K), the Gibbs equation becomes: 0 = ΔH - TΔS, which rearranges to ΔH = TΔS.This means that at 100 °C, the energy required to convert liquid water into gaseous steam (the positive enthalpy change, ΔH) is exactly balanced by the energy associated with the increase in disorder (the positive TΔS term) as water molecules gain more freedom of movement. When ΔG = 0, neither the forward nor the reverse process is spontaneous, allowing for the phase transition (boiling) to occur reversibly, which defines the normal boiling point.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ At equilibrium, ΔG = 0 (1 mark). So, at 100 °C (373 K) for H₂O(l) → H₂O(g), ΔH - TΔS = 0 (1 mark). This means ΔH = TΔS (1 mark). This specific temperature (100 °C) is where the energy absorbed for vaporization (ΔH) is exactly balanced by the energy dispersed due to increased entropy (TΔS), making ΔG = 0 and thus allowing boiling (phase change) to occur reversibly (1 mark).

⚠️ Examiner Note: Students often state ΔG=0 but fail to explain what this physically means in terms of the balance between ΔH and TΔS.

Q10. (3 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~3 min)

When a solid dissolves in a liquid, the entropy change is usually positive. However, some dissolution processes are endothermic (ΔH > 0). Explain how these endothermic dissolution processes can still be spontaneous.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: The dissolution of a solid in a liquid typically leads to a positive entropy change (ΔS > 0) because the highly ordered crystal lattice breaks down, and the ions/molecules disperse into the solvent, increasing the overall disorder and energy dispersal of the system. For an endothermic dissolution (ΔH > 0) to be spontaneous (ΔG < 0), the TΔS term in the Gibbs equation (ΔG = ΔH - TΔS) must be sufficiently positive (making -TΔS sufficiently negative) to overcome the positive ΔH. This condition is usually met at higher temperatures, where the magnitude of TΔS increases, making the reaction entropically driven and therefore feasible.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Dissolution generally leads to a significant increase in disorder / dispersal of energy (ΔS > 0) (1 mark). For an endothermic process (ΔH > 0) to be spontaneous (ΔG < 0), the -TΔS term must be large enough to outweigh the positive ΔH (1 mark). This occurs at higher temperatures, where the TΔS term becomes dominant (1 mark).

🧩 Quick Quiz (MCQ)

1. Which of the following best describes entropy? A) A measure of the total energy in a system. B) A measure of the heat content of a system. C) A measure of the dispersal of energy and disorder in a system. D) A measure of the rate of a chemical reaction.

Answer

C) A measure of the dispersal of energy and disorder in a system. Entropy is fundamentally a measure of the dispersal of energy within a system and the extent of its disorder. A more disordered system with energy spread out more widely has higher entropy.

2. For a given substance, which physical state typically has the highest entropy? A) Solid B) Liquid C) Gas D) Aqueous solution

Answer

C) Gas Gases have the highest entropy because their particles are widely spaced and move randomly, leading to the greatest dispersal of energy and disorder compared to liquids (where particles are close but can move) and solids (where particles are fixed in a lattice).

3. Consider the following reaction: 2SO2(g) + O2(g) → 2SO3(g). How does the entropy change (ΔS) for this reaction compare to a reaction where the number of gaseous moles increases? A) ΔS will be positive, indicating an increase in disorder. B) ΔS will be positive, indicating a decrease in disorder. C) ΔS will be negative, indicating an increase in disorder. D) ΔS will be negative, indicating a decrease in disorder.

Answer

D) ΔS will be negative, indicating a decrease in disorder. In this reaction, 3 moles of gaseous reactants (2 SO2 + 1 O2) produce 2 moles of gaseous product (2 SO3). Since the number of gaseous moles decreases, the system becomes more ordered, and thus the entropy change (ΔS) will be negative, indicating a decrease in disorder.

4. Given the standard entropies: S°(N2(g)) = 191.6 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹, S°(H2(g)) = 130.7 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹, S°(NH3(g)) = 192.5 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹. Calculate the standard entropy change (ΔS°) for the reaction: N2(g) + 3H2(g) → 2NH3(g). A) +200.2 J K⁻¹ B) -198.7 J K⁻¹ C) +198.7 J K⁻¹ D) -200.2 J K⁻¹

Answer

B) -198.7 J K⁻¹ ΔS° = ΣS°(products) - ΣS°(reactants). ΔS° = (2 × S°(NH3)) - (S°(N2) + 3 × S°(H2)). ΔS° = (2 × 192.5) - (191.6 + 3 × 130.7) = 385.0 - (191.6 + 392.1) = 385.0 - 583.7 = -198.7 J K⁻¹. The negative value is expected as 4 moles of gas produce 2 moles of gas, leading to a decrease in disorder.

5. What two key factors, besides temperature, determine the feasibility of a process? A) Only the enthalpy change (ΔH). B) Only the entropy change (ΔS). C) The enthalpy change (ΔH) and the entropy change (ΔS). D) The activation energy and the concentration of reactants.

Answer

C) The enthalpy change (ΔH) and the entropy change (ΔS). The feasibility of a process depends on the balance between the enthalpy change (ΔH), the entropy change (ΔS), and the absolute temperature (T). These are combined in the Gibbs free energy equation.

6. Which equation correctly represents the Gibbs free energy change (ΔG)? A) ΔG = ΔH + TΔS B) ΔG = TΔS - ΔH C) ΔG = ΔH - TΔS D) ΔG = ΔS - TΔH

Answer

C) ΔG = ΔH - TΔSThe Gibbs free energy equation, which determines the feasibility of a reaction, is given by ΔG = ΔH - TΔS, where ΔH is the enthalpy change, T is the absolute temperature, and ΔS is the entropy change.

7. A chemical reaction is considered feasible (spontaneous) when the Gibbs free energy change (ΔG) has which characteristic? A) ΔG > 0 B) ΔG = 0 C) ΔG < 0 D) ΔG = TΔS

Answer

C) ΔG < 0 A process is considered feasible or spontaneous when the Gibbs free energy change (ΔG) is negative (ΔG < 0). If ΔG is positive, the reverse reaction is feasible, and if ΔG is zero, the system is at equilibrium.

8. For a reaction with ΔH = +100 kJ mol⁻¹ and ΔS = +250 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹, at what temperature will the reaction become feasible? A) Below 400 K B) Exactly 400 K C) Above 400 K D) The reaction will never be feasible.

Answer

C) Above 400 KFor feasibility, ΔG must be < 0. So, ΔH - TΔS < 0. 100 kJ mol⁻¹ - T(250 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹) < 0. Convert ΔS to kJ K⁻¹ mol⁻¹: 250 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ = 0.250 kJ K⁻¹ mol⁻¹. So, 100 - T(0.250) < 0. 100 < T(0.250). T > 100 / 0.250. T > 400 K. The reaction becomes feasible above 400 K.

9. Calculate the Gibbs free energy change (ΔG) for a reaction at 298 K, given ΔH = -50 kJ mol⁻¹ and ΔS = -100 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹. A) -79.8 kJ mol⁻¹ B) -20.2 kJ mol⁻¹ C) +20.2 kJ mol⁻¹ D) +79.8 kJ mol⁻¹

Answer

B) -20.2 kJ mol⁻¹ ΔG = ΔH - TΔS. First, convert ΔS to kJ K⁻¹ mol⁻¹: -100 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ = -0.100 kJ K⁻¹ mol⁻¹. ΔG = -50 kJ mol⁻¹ - (298 K × -0.100 kJ K⁻¹ mol⁻¹) = -50 - (-29.8) = -50 + 29.8 = -20.2 kJ mol⁻¹.

10. A reaction has a negative ΔG, indicating it is feasible. However, in practice, it does not appear to occur at room temperature. What is the most likely reason for this discrepancy? A) The reaction is actually reversible. B) The concentrations of reactants are too low. C) The reaction has a high activation energy. D) The temperature is too high for the reaction to proceed.

Answer

C) The reaction has a high activation energy. A negative ΔG indicates thermodynamic feasibility, meaning the reaction can occur. However, it does not provide information about the reaction rate. If a reaction has a high activation energy, it may proceed very slowly or not at all at room temperature, even if it is thermodynamically feasible. Kinetics (reaction rate) is independent of thermodynamics (feasibility).

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The concept of entropy was first introduced by Rudolf Clausius in 1850s, deriving it from the Greek word for 'transformation' or 'content of transformation'. Josiah Willard Gibbs, an American physicist, later developed the concept of 'free energy' in the 1870s, now known as Gibbs free energy, which unified enthalpy and entropy into a single criterion for spontaneity. This work laid the foundation for modern chemical thermodynamics and its application in understanding chemical reactions and phase transitions.

⚖️ Ethics

Understanding enthalpy and entropy is crucial for designing energy-efficient processes. For instance, optimizing industrial reactions to minimize wasted energy (heat loss, increasing entropy of surroundings) has significant environmental and economic implications. In the context of climate change, the thermodynamics of energy conversion and storage (e.g., hydrogen fuel cells, batteries) relies heavily on these principles, aiming to maximize useful work and minimize entropy generation in non-useful forms.

🚀 Advanced Theory

At a more advanced level, entropy can be understood through statistical mechanics using Ludwig Boltzmann's definition: S = k ln W, where k is the Boltzmann constant and W is the number of microstates (arrangements of particles and their energies) available to the system. A system with more microstates has higher entropy. This provides a microscopic foundation for the macroscopic observation of increasing disorder. Furthermore, the relationship between Gibbs free energy and the equilibrium constant (ΔG° = -RT ln K) links thermodynamics directly to chemical equilibrium, allowing for quantitative prediction of equilibrium positions from standard thermodynamic data.
