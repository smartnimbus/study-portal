🧠 Conceptual Understanding

Atoms are the basic units of matter, composed of protons, neutrons, and electrons. Understanding their structure, particularly isotopes, is foundational to chemistry. From these tiny particles, we build molecules and compounds, and chemists need to quantify these substances using the 'mole' concept, which acts as a bridge between the microscopic world of atoms and the macroscopic world of grams and litres. This allows us to predict reaction outcomes through stoichiometry and write balanced chemical equations. This topic also introduces you to fundamental reaction types: acid-base reactions, which are essentially proton transfers, and redox reactions, which involve the transfer of electrons and changes in oxidation states. Mastering these concepts provides the essential toolkit for understanding all subsequent chemistry.

📌 Core Theory

  • Atoms consist of a nucleus containing protons and neutrons, surrounded by electrons in electron shells.
  • Protons have a relative mass of 1 and a charge of +1; neutrons have a relative mass of 1 and no charge; electrons have a negligible mass and a charge of -1.
  • Atomic number (Z) is the number of protons and defines the element; mass number (A) is the total number of protons and neutrons.
  • Isotopes are atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons (different mass numbers).
  • Relative atomic mass (Aᵣ) is the weighted average mass of isotopes in a sample of an element, compared to 1/12th the mass of a carbon-12 atom.
  • The mole is the amount of substance that contains the Avogadro constant (6.022 × 10²³) of particles.
  • Molar mass (Mᵣ) is the mass of one mole of a substance, expressed in g mol⁻¹.
  • Chemical equations must be balanced to obey the law of conservation of mass.
  • Calculations involving moles, mass, concentration, and gas volumes at standard conditions (molar gas volume) are crucial for stoichiometry.
  • Empirical formula shows the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound; molecular formula shows the actual number of atoms.
  • Acids are proton (H⁺) donors; bases are proton acceptors.
  • Neutralisation reactions involve an acid and a base reacting to form a salt and water.
  • Redox reactions involve both oxidation and reduction occurring simultaneously.
  • Oxidation is the loss of electrons or an increase in oxidation number; reduction is the gain of electrons or a decrease in oxidation number.
  • Oxidation numbers are assigned to atoms in compounds to track electron transfer during redox reactions.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

Building on the basic atomic structure, this topic formalises how we quantify matter. The mole concept is central: one mole of any substance always contains the same number of particles (Avogadro's constant), making it a universal counting unit for chemists. We use molar mass, which is simply the relative molecular mass in grams, to convert between mass and moles, and molar gas volume for gases. These calculations underpin stoichiometry, allowing us to predict the amounts of reactants needed and products formed in chemical reactions. Understanding chemical reactions extends beyond just quantities to reaction types. Acid-base reactions are critical for many processes, with neutralisation forming salts. Redox reactions are fundamentally about electron transfer, and we use oxidation numbers to systematically identify which species are oxidised and reduced, even in complex reactions. Mastery of these quantitative and qualitative aspects is essential for advanced chemical study.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Calculating Relative Atomic Mass from Isotopic Abundances

Problem: Chlorine has two main isotopes: chlorine-35 (³⁵Cl) with an abundance of 75.77% and chlorine-37 (³⁷Cl) with an abundance of 24.23%. Calculate the relative atomic mass of chlorine.

Steps: 1. Multiply the mass of each isotope by its percentage abundance. 2. Sum these values. 3. Divide the total by 100 to get the weighted average.

Answer: Aᵣ(Cl) = 35.48

Teacher's Tip

Remember to use the exact isotopic masses if given, or the mass number if not. The sum of abundances should always be 100%.

2. Calculating Moles from Mass

Problem: Calculate the number of moles in 20.0 g of calcium carbonate, CaCO₃. (Relative atomic masses: Ca=40.1, C=12.0, O=16.0)

Steps: 1. Calculate the molar mass (Mᵣ) of CaCO₃. 2. Use the formula: moles = mass / molar mass.

Answer: 0.200 mol (3 s.f.)

Teacher's Tip

Always state the units for molar mass (g mol⁻¹) and moles (mol). Be careful with significant figures – typically match the least precise measurement given in the question.

3. Stoichiometry Calculation

Problem: What mass of magnesium oxide (MgO) is produced when 12.15 g of magnesium (Mg) is completely burned in oxygen? (Aᵣ: Mg=24.3, O=16.0)

Steps: 1. Write a balanced chemical equation for the reaction. 2. Calculate the moles of the known substance (Mg). 3. Use the stoichiometric ratio from the balanced equation to find the moles of the unknown substance (MgO). 4. Convert moles of the unknown substance to mass.

Answer: 20.15 g (2 d.p.)

Teacher's Tip

Balancing the equation is the critical first step! A mistake here will cascade through all subsequent calculations.

4. Determining Oxidation Numbers

Problem: Determine the oxidation number of sulfur in H₂SO₄ and in SO₄²⁻.

Steps: 1. Recall the rules for assigning oxidation numbers: H is +1 (except in metal hydrides), O is -2 (except peroxides/fluorine). 2. For a neutral compound (H₂SO₄), the sum of oxidation numbers must be zero. 3. For an ion (SO₄²⁻), the sum of oxidation numbers must equal the charge of the ion.

Answer: In H₂SO₄, S is +6. In SO₄²⁻, S is +6.

Teacher's Tip

Practice these rules! They are fundamental for identifying redox reactions. Remember, oxidation numbers can be positive, negative, or zero.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Industrial Production: Stoichiometry is critical for optimising industrial processes, ensuring maximum yield of desired products and minimising waste, from pharmaceutical synthesis to fertilizer production.
  • Environmental Monitoring: Acid-base reactions are fundamental to understanding and mitigating acid rain, soil pH, and water treatment processes. Titrations are used to measure pollutants or nutrient levels.
  • Batteries and Fuel Cells: Redox reactions are at the heart of all electrochemical cells, powering everything from our phones to electric vehicles, by converting chemical energy into electrical energy.
  • Corrosion Prevention: Understanding redox processes helps engineers design methods to prevent rust (oxidation of iron) in structures and vehicles, extending their lifespan.
  • Food Preservation: Many food preservation techniques, like antioxidants, involve controlling or preventing undesirable oxidation reactions that lead to spoilage.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing atomic number (protons) with mass number (protons + neutrons).
  • ❌ Incorrectly calculating molar mass by missing subscripts or incorrectly interpreting brackets in formulae (e.g., Ca(OH)₂).
  • ❌ Failing to balance chemical equations correctly, leading to incorrect stoichiometric ratios.
  • ❌ Mixing up oxidation (loss of electrons, increase in oxidation number) and reduction (gain of electrons, decrease in oxidation number).
  • ❌ Assuming all reactions producing CO₂ are combustion; they could be acid-carbonate reactions.
Memory Anchor

Think of the 'mole' as the chemist's 'dozen' – just like a baker's dozen always means 13, a chemist's mole always means 6.022 × 10²³ particles. It's just a much, much bigger number!

Therefore, copper is the reducing agent.2. Nitrogen (N) in Nitric Acid (HNO₃) and Nitrogen Dioxide (NO₂): - In HNO₃, assign H as +1 and O as -2. So, +1 + N + 3(-2) = 0, which means N has an oxidation number of +5. - In NO₂, assign O as -2. So, N + 2(-2) = 0, which means N has an oxidation number of +4. - The oxidation number of nitrogen decreases from +5 to +4. This decrease signifies that nitrogen has gained one electron and has undergone reduction. Therefore, nitric acid (specifically the nitrogen in it) is the oxidising agent. Since both oxidation and reduction occur, it is definitively a redox reaction.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ This is a redox reaction.
  • ✅ Oxidation numbers: Cu (0 to +2), N in HNO₃ (+5 to +4 in NO₂).
  • ✅ Cu is oxidised (loss of 2 electrons, oxidation number increases from 0 to +2).
  • ✅ N in HNO₃ is reduced (gain of 1 electron, oxidation number decreases from +5 to +4).
  • ✅ Copper is the reducing agent, nitric acid is the oxidising agent.

Q6. (6 Marks | Calculation | ⏱️ ~6 min)

10.0 g of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) is reacted with 50.0 cm³ of 2.00 mol dm⁻³ hydrochloric acid (HCl). Calculate the mass of carbon dioxide (CO₂) produced. Which reactant is in excess and by how many moles? (Aᵣ: Ca=40.1, C=12.0, O=16.0, H=1.0, Cl=35.5)

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: 1. Write the balanced chemical equation: CaCO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CaCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g). 2. Calculate moles of each reactant: - Mᵣ(CaCO₃) = 40.1 + 12.0 + (3 × 16.0) = 100.1 g mol⁻¹. - Moles CaCO₃ = 10.0 g / 100.1 g mol⁻¹ = 0.0999 mol. - Moles HCl = concentration × volume = 2.00 mol dm⁻³ × (50.0/1000) dm³ = 0.100 mol. 3. Determine the limiting reactant: - From the equation, 1 mole of CaCO₃ reacts with 2 moles of HCl. - If all 0.0999 mol of CaCO₃ reacted, it would require 2 × 0.0999 = 0.1998 mol of HCl. We only have 0.100 mol HCl, so HCl is the limiting reactant. - Alternatively, if all 0.100 mol of HCl reacted, it would require 0.100 / 2 = 0.0500 mol of CaCO₃. We have 0.0999 mol CaCO₃, which is more than needed. Therefore, HCl is the limiting reactant, and CaCO₃ is in excess. 4. Calculate the mass of CO₂ produced (based on the limiting reactant, HCl): - From the equation, 2 moles of HCl produce 1 mole of CO₂. So, 0.100 mol HCl produces 0.100 / 2 = 0.0500 mol CO₂. - Mᵣ(CO₂) = 12.0 + (2 × 16.0) = 44.0 g mol⁻¹. - Mass CO₂ = moles × Mᵣ = 0.0500 mol × 44.0 g mol⁻¹ = 2.20 g. 5. Calculate the moles of the excess reactant (CaCO₃): - Moles of CaCO₃ reacted = 0.0500 mol. - Moles of CaCO₃ in excess = Initial moles - Moles reacted = 0.0999 mol - 0.0500 mol = 0.0499 mol. Mass of CO₂ produced is 2.20 g. Calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) is in excess by 0.0499 mol.

🔢 Working: 1. Write balanced equation. 2. Calculate moles of both reactants. 3. Identify the limiting reactant. 4. Calculate moles of CO₂ based on limiting reactant. 5. Calculate mass of CO₂. 6. Calculate moles of excess reactant.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Balanced equation: CaCO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CaCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g).
  • ✅ Mᵣ(CaCO₃) = 100.1 g mol⁻¹.
  • ✅ Moles CaCO₃ = 10.0 g / 100.1 g mol⁻¹ = 0.0999 mol.
  • ✅ Moles HCl = 2.00 mol dm⁻³ × (50.0/1000) dm³ = 0.100 mol.
  • ✅ From equation, 1 mole CaCO₃ reacts with 2 moles HCl. For 0.0999 mol CaCO₃, 2 × 0.0999 = 0.1998 mol HCl needed. We only have 0.100 mol HCl, so HCl is limiting.
  • ✅ Using HCl as limiting reactant: 2 moles HCl produces 1 mole CO₂. So 0.100 mol HCl produces 0.0500 mol CO₂.
  • ✅ Mass CO₂ = 0.0500 mol × 44.0 g mol⁻¹ = 2.20 g.
  • ✅ Moles CaCO₃ reacted = 0.100 mol HCl / 2 = 0.0500 mol CaCO₃.
  • ✅ Moles CaCO₃ in excess = 0.0999 mol (initial) - 0.0500 mol (reacted) = 0.0499 mol CaCO₃.

Q7. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

Describe how you would distinguish between a strong acid and a weak acid in a school laboratory, including observations and chemical equations.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: To distinguish between a strong acid and a weak acid of the same concentration in a school laboratory, two main methods can be used:1. Measuring pH: * Method: Use a pH meter or universal indicator solution. Add a few drops of universal indicator to samples of each acid, or dip a calibrated pH meter into each acid solution. * Observation: The strong acid (e.g., 0.1 mol dm⁻³ HCl) will show a very low pH, typically around 1, or turn universal indicator red. The weak acid (e.g., 0.1 mol dm⁻³ CH₃COOH) will show a higher pH, typically around 3-5, or turn universal indicator orange/yellow. * Explanation: Strong acids, like HCl, dissociate almost completely in water: HCl(aq) → H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq), producing a high concentration of H⁺ ions. Weak acids, like ethanoic acid, dissociate only partially: CH₃COOH(aq) ⇌ H⁺(aq) + CH₃COO⁻(aq), resulting in a lower concentration of H⁺ ions. 2. Measuring Electrical Conductivity: * Method: Set up a conductivity meter (or a circuit with a power supply, electrodes, and a light bulb) and place the electrodes into separate samples of each acid. * Observation: The strong acid solution will show high electrical conductivity (e.g., the bulb will shine brightly), while the weak acid solution will show low electrical conductivity (e.g., the bulb will be dim or off). * Explanation: Electrical conductivity depends on the concentration of free, mobile ions in the solution. Since strong acids dissociate almost fully, they produce a high concentration of H⁺ and anion ions, allowing current to flow easily. Weak acids, due to their partial dissociation, produce a much lower concentration of ions, leading to poor conductivity.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Use a pH meter or universal indicator to measure pH.
  • ✅ Strong acid will have a very low pH (e.g., pH 1), weak acid will have a higher pH (e.g., pH 3-5) for same concentration.
  • ✅ Reason: Strong acids fully dissociate (e.g., HCl → H⁺ + Cl⁻), weak acids partially dissociate (e.g., CH₃COOH ⇌ H⁺ + CH₃COO⁻).
  • ✅ Measure electrical conductivity.
  • ✅ Strong acid will have high conductivity, weak acid will have low conductivity.
  • ✅ Reason: Strong acids produce more free mobile ions when dissociated, allowing greater charge flow.

🧩 Quick Quiz (MCQ)

1. An atom of element X has 17 protons, 18 neutrons, and 17 electrons. Which of the following statements is correct regarding this atom? A) Its atomic number is 18. B) Its mass number is 17. C) It is an isotope of an element with 18 protons. D) It has a mass number of 35.

Answer

D) It has a mass number of 35. The atomic number is equal to the number of protons (17). The mass number is the sum of protons and neutrons (17 + 18 = 35). Isotopes have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.

2. Which statement correctly describes two isotopes of the same element? A) They have the same mass number but different atomic numbers. B) They have different numbers of protons but the same number of neutrons. C) They have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons. D) They have different chemical properties due to their different masses.

Answer

C) They have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons. Isotopes are atoms of the same element, meaning they have the same atomic number (number of protons). However, they have different numbers of neutrons, leading to different mass numbers. Their chemical properties are the same because they have the same electron configuration.

3. What is the correct chemical formula for magnesium nitrate? A) MgNO3 B) Mg(NO3)2 C) Mg2NO3 D) Mg2(NO3)3

Answer

B) Mg(NO3)2 Magnesium is in Group 2, forming a Mg2+ ion. The nitrate ion is NO3-. To balance the charges, two nitrate ions are needed for every one magnesium ion, hence Mg(NO3)2.

4. When aluminium reacts with oxygen, aluminium oxide (Al2O3) is formed. What are the correct stoichiometric coefficients to balance the equation: Al(s) + O2(g) → Al2O3(s)? A) 2Al + O2 → Al2O3 B) 4Al + 3O2 → 2Al2O3 C) 3Al + 2O2 → Al2O3 D) Al + O2 → Al2O3 (already balanced)

Answer

B) 4Al + 3O2 → 2Al2O3 To balance the equation, we need 4 aluminium atoms and 6 oxygen atoms on both sides. So, 4Al + 3O2 → 2Al2O3.

5. What is the mass of 0.25 moles of sodium chloride (NaCl)? (Ar: Na=23, Cl=35.5) A) 58.5 g B) 14.625 g C) 234 g D) 117 g

Answer

B) 14.625 g The molar mass of NaCl is 23 + 35.5 = 58.5 g/mol. Mass = Moles × Molar Mass = 0.25 mol × 58.5 g/mol = 14.625 g.

6. In the reaction: CuO(s) + H2(g) → Cu(s) + H2O(l), what mass of copper oxide (CuO) would be needed to produce 6.35 g of copper (Cu)? (Ar: Cu=63.5, O=16) A) 6.35 g B) 7.95 g C) 12.7 g D) 3.175 g

Answer

B) 7.95 g Molar mass of Cu = 63.5 g/mol. Moles of Cu = 6.35 g / 63.5 g/mol = 0.1 mol. From the balanced equation, 1 mole of CuO produces 1 mole of Cu. So, 0.1 mol of CuO is needed. Molar mass of CuO = 63.5 + 16 = 79.5 g/mol. Mass of CuO = 0.1 mol × 79.5 g/mol = 7.95 g.

7. Which of the following is an example of a neutralisation reaction? A) Sodium reacting with water to produce sodium hydroxide and hydrogen. B) Calcium carbonate decomposing to form calcium oxide and carbon dioxide. C) Hydrochloric acid reacting with potassium hydroxide to form potassium chloride and water. D) Iron rusting in the presence of oxygen and water.

Answer

C) Hydrochloric acid reacting with potassium hydroxide to form potassium chloride and water. A neutralisation reaction is the reaction between an acid and a base (or alkali) to form a salt and water. Hydrochloric acid is an acid, potassium hydroxide is an alkali, and they react to form potassium chloride (a salt) and water.

8. Which of the following statements about bases and alkalis is correct? A) All bases are soluble in water. B) An alkali is a soluble acid. C) A base is a substance that neutralises an acid. D) All alkalis are strong bases.

Answer

C) A base is a substance that neutralises an acid. A base is a substance that neutralises an acid. Alkalis are soluble bases that produce hydroxide ions in water. Not all bases are soluble, and alkalis are not acids. Alkalis can be strong or weak bases.

9. In the reaction: Mg(s) + CuCl2(aq) → MgCl2(aq) + Cu(s), which species is being oxidised? A) Mg(s) B) CuCl2(aq) C) Cu(s) D) Cl- (from CuCl2)

Answer

A) Mg(s) Oxidation is the loss of electrons. Magnesium (Mg) starts as a neutral atom (oxidation state 0) and becomes part of MgCl2, where it has an oxidation state of +2 (Mg2+). This means it has lost two electrons, so Mg is oxidised.

10. Consider the reaction: 2Fe2O3(s) + 3C(s) → 4Fe(s) + 3CO2(g). Which of the following correctly identifies the species being reduced? A) Carbon (C) B) Iron(III) oxide (Fe2O3) C) Carbon dioxide (CO2) D) Iron (Fe)

Answer

B) Iron(III) oxide (Fe2O3) Reduction is the gain of electrons or a decrease in oxidation number. In Fe2O3, iron has an oxidation state of +3. In Fe(s), iron has an oxidation state of 0. The oxidation state of iron decreases from +3 to 0, indicating a gain of electrons, so Fe2O3 is reduced. Carbon's oxidation state increases from 0 to +4 in CO2, so carbon is oxidised.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The journey to understanding atoms and reactions is rich with scientific discovery. John Dalton (early 19th century) proposed the first modern atomic theory. Amedeo Avogadro (1811) hypothesised that equal volumes of gases at the same temperature and pressure contain the same number of molecules, leading to Avogadro's constant. Later, Michael Faraday's work on electrolysis helped establish the particulate nature of electricity, linking it to the movement of ions. Svante Arrhenius (late 19th century) first defined acids and bases in terms of H⁺ and OH⁻ ions in solution, later refined by Brønsted and Lowry with their proton transfer theory. The concept of oxidation numbers was developed to systematise the analysis of redox reactions.

⚖️ Ethics

The ability to control and quantify chemical reactions has profound ethical implications. Optimising industrial processes for maximum yield (stoichiometry) can reduce waste, but there's a balance between economic efficiency and environmental impact. The production of chemicals like fertilisers and pesticides, while boosting agricultural output, raises concerns about eutrophication and environmental contamination. Redox reactions underpin energy storage (batteries), where the ethical sourcing of raw materials (e.g., cobalt for lithium-ion batteries) and the safe disposal/recycling of spent batteries are major issues. Understanding acid-base chemistry is critical for managing industrial effluents and waste treatment to prevent ecological damage.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond the basics, stoichiometry extends to calculations involving percentage yield and atom economy, crucial for green chemistry. Acid-base chemistry deepens into pH calculations, buffer solutions, and acid-base titrations with complex indicators. Redox reactions can be explored through electrochemical cells (voltaic and electrolytic), standard electrode potentials, and the Nernst equation, which quantifies how concentration changes affect cell potentials. Reaction mechanisms for both acid-base and redox reactions become more intricate, involving electron curly arrows and understanding intermediates.

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

A key practical for this topic is acid-base titration. The general methodology involves: 1. Preparing a standard solution of known concentration. 2. Using a pipette to accurately measure a fixed volume of one reactant into a conical flask. 3. Adding a suitable indicator to the conical flask. 4. Filling a burette with the other reactant (of unknown concentration). 5. Titrating slowly, adding the reactant from the burette dropwise until the indicator changes colour (the endpoint). 6. Recording the volume added from the burette (titre). 7. Repeating the titration until concordant results (within 0.1 cm³) are obtained. This allows calculation of the unknown concentration using mole ratios.

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Acids and Bases (e.g., HCl, NaOH): Corrosive. Causes severe skin burns and eye damage. Always wear eye protection and gloves. If contact occurs, wash immediately with plenty of water. Neutralise spills with appropriate neutralising agents.
  • 🛑 Glassware (burettes, pipettes, volumetric flasks): Fragile. Handle with care to prevent breakages and cuts. Always check for chips or cracks before use.
  • 🛑 Heating: Use appropriate heating apparatus (Bunsen burner, hot plate) carefully. Always point test tubes away from yourself and others. Wear heat-resistant gloves if handling hot equipment.
  • 🛑 Chemical Waste: Dispose of all chemical waste according to school/laboratory guidelines. Do not pour strong acids/bases down the sink without neutralising and diluting.

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. Neutralisation (Brønsted-Lowry concept): This is a proton (H⁺) transfer reaction. An acid (proton donor) donates an H⁺ ion to a base (proton acceptor). For example, in the reaction of hydrochloric acid (HCl) with sodium hydroxide (NaOH), the H⁺ ion from HCl is transferred to the OH⁻ ion from NaOH to form water. The overall ionic equation is H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l).
  2. Redox (Electron Transfer): Redox reactions involve the simultaneous transfer of electrons. Oxidation is the loss of electrons, and reduction is the gain of electrons. For instance, in the reaction 2Na(s) + Cl₂(g) → 2NaCl(s), sodium atoms lose electrons (Na → Na⁺ + e⁻, oxidation) and chlorine molecules gain electrons (Cl₂ + 2e⁻ → 2Cl⁻, reduction). These half-reactions occur simultaneously.

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Neutralisation Reactions: Typically carried out in aqueous solution at room temperature. No specific catalysts or pressures are usually required.
  • Combustion Reactions (e.g., for empirical formula determination): Requires oxygen (or air) and initiation by heat (e.g., a flame).
  • Reactions involving gases (e.g., Molar Gas Volume calculations): Conditions are usually specified as room temperature and pressure (r.t.p., 20 °C and 1 atm, where molar gas volume is 24.0 dm³ mol⁻¹) or standard temperature and pressure (s.t.p., 0 °C and 1 atm, where molar gas volume is 22.4 dm³ mol⁻¹).
  • Redox Titrations (e.g., KMnO₄ with FeSO₄): Often carried out in acidic solution (e.g., using dilute H₂SO₄) to ensure the reduction of MnO₄⁻ to Mn²⁺ and to prevent unwanted side reactions. Temperature is typically room temperature.

🔦 Element Spotlight

Hydrogen (H): Atomic number 1. The simplest and most abundant element in the universe. Electron configuration is 1s¹. It exists as H₂ gas in its elemental form. In reactions, hydrogen can exhibit unique behaviour. As H⁺, it is the defining species for acids, acting as a proton donor. In metal hydrides, its oxidation number is -1, gaining an electron. In compounds with non-metals (like H₂O, HCl), it has an oxidation number of +1, losing an electron. Its isotopes, protium (¹H), deuterium (²H, D), and tritium (³H, T), are significant in nuclear chemistry and scientific research.
