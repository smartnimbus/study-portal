🧠 Conceptual Understanding

This topic introduces you to key organic nitrogen compounds: amines, amides, and amino acids. We'll explore their structures, properties, and reactions. A crucial concept introduced here is chirality and optical isomerism, which explains why some molecules have non-superimposable mirror images and different biological activities. You'll also learn about different types of polymerisation, focusing on condensation polymerisation, and how it compares to addition polymerisation. Finally, we'll delve into the art of organic synthesis, specifically stressing the importance of forming carbon–carbon bonds and designing multi-stage routes to create complex organic products from simpler starting materials.

📌 Core Theory

  • Amines are organic compounds containing a nitrogen atom bonded to one or more alkyl or aryl groups, classified as primary, secondary, or tertiary.
  • Amines are bases due to the lone pair of electrons on the nitrogen atom, which can accept a proton.
  • Amides contain a carbonyl group bonded to a nitrogen atom, and can be formed by the reaction of acyl chlorides or acid anhydrides with amines.
  • Amino acids are molecules containing both an amine (-NH₂) group and a carboxylic acid (-COOH) group, typically on the same carbon atom (α-amino acids).
  • Amino acids are amphoteric, acting as both acids and bases, and can exist as zwitterions.
  • Chirality occurs in molecules with a chiral centre (a carbon atom bonded to four different groups), leading to optical isomers (enantiomers).
  • Enantiomers rotate plane-polarised light in opposite directions but have identical chemical properties in non-chiral environments.
  • Condensation polymerisation involves the joining of monomers with the elimination of a small molecule (e.g., H₂O, HCl), forming polymers like polyamides (e.g., nylon) and polyesters (e.g., Terylene).
  • Addition polymerisation involves monomers with C=C double bonds adding together without the loss of any atoms, forming long chain polymers (e.g., poly(ethene)).
  • Organic synthesis focuses on constructing complex molecules, with carbon–carbon bond formation being a fundamental strategy to increase chain length or create cyclic structures.
  • Multi-stage synthesis requires careful planning, considering reaction conditions, reagents, and the order of steps to achieve a desired product with good yield and purity.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

Organic nitrogen compounds are central to biochemistry and industrial chemistry. Amines are derived from ammonia (NH₃) by replacing hydrogen atoms with alkyl or aryl groups; their basicity stems from nitrogen's lone pair. Amides, on the other hand, are less basic due to the electron-withdrawing effect of the carbonyl group. Amino acids, the building blocks of proteins, exemplify amphoteric behaviour and zwitterion formation. The concept of chirality is vital in understanding drug specificity and biological processes, as enantiomers can have vastly different physiological effects. Polymerisation methods differentiate how monomers link together: addition polymerisation simply adds unsaturated monomers, while condensation polymerisation forms new bonds by eliminating small molecules. The strategic planning of multi-stage organic synthesis is akin to solving a puzzle, demanding a comprehensive understanding of various reaction types and functional group interconversions to build complex structures efficiently.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Identifying Chiral Centres

Problem: Identify any chiral centres in the following molecule: 2-aminopropanoic acid (alanine).

Steps: 1. Draw the full displayed formula for 2-aminopropanoic acid. 2. Locate all carbon atoms in the molecule. 3. For each carbon atom, examine the four groups directly attached to it. 4. If a carbon atom is bonded to four different groups, it is a chiral centre.

Answer: The carbon atom at position 2 (alpha-carbon) is bonded to a hydrogen atom (-H), a methyl group (-CH₃), an amine group (-NH₂), and a carboxylic acid group (-COOH). Since all four groups are different, C2 is a chiral centre.

Teacher's Tip

Always draw out the full structure to clearly see all four groups attached to each carbon. Remember that -CH₃, -CH₂CH₃, -OH, -COOH, -NH₂, -Cl, etc., are all considered different groups.

2. Drawing a Condensation Polymer

Problem: Draw the repeating unit of the polymer formed from the condensation reaction of hexane-1,6-diamine and hexanedioic acid.

Steps: 1. Identify the functional groups involved in the reaction: an amine group (-NH₂) and a carboxylic acid group (-COOH). 2. Recognise that a peptide (amide) bond will form with the elimination of water. 3. Draw out one monomer of hexane-1,6-diamine (H₂N-(CH₂)₆-NH₂) and one monomer of hexanedioic acid (HOOC-(CH₂)₄-COOH). 4. Show the removal of H₂O (one H from -NH₂ and -OH from -COOH) to form the amide linkage (-CONH-). 5. Connect the remaining parts of the monomers, indicating the bonds extending to further repeating units with brackets and 'n'.

Answer: The repeating unit is -[HN-(CH₂)₆-NH-CO-(CH₂)₄-CO]-. This polymer is Nylon 6,6.

Teacher's Tip

When drawing repeating units, make sure the 'ends' of the repeating unit correctly show where the bond continues to the next monomer, ensuring the correct stoichiometry.

3. One-step Synthesis of a Primary Amine

Problem: Suggest reagents and conditions for the synthesis of propan-1-amine from 1-bromopropane.

Steps: 1. Identify the starting material (1-bromopropane) and the target product (propan-1-amine). 2. Recognise that this is a nucleophilic substitution reaction where the bromine atom is replaced by an amine group. 3. Recall the reagent used for forming primary amines via nucleophilic substitution from haloalkanes. 4. State the necessary conditions to favour primary amine formation and minimise further substitution.

Answer: Reagents: Excess ethanolic ammonia (NH₃ in ethanol). Conditions: Heat under pressure in a sealed tube. (Excess ammonia minimises further substitution to form secondary/tertiary amines).

Teacher's Tip

Using excess ammonia is crucial to ensure that the primary amine product reacts with ammonia rather than with another molecule of the haloalkane, which would lead to secondary or tertiary amines and quaternary ammonium salts.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Pharmaceuticals: Many drugs contain amine or amide functional groups (e.g., adrenaline, paracetamol). Chirality is critical, as often only one enantiomer is pharmacologically active or safe (e.g., thalidomide disaster).
  • Proteins and Enzymes: Amino acids are the monomers that link via peptide (amide) bonds to form proteins, which are fundamental to all biological life and function as enzymes, structural components, and transporters.
  • Synthetic Polymers: Polyamides (like nylon) are used in textiles, carpets, and engineering plastics due to their strength and durability. Polyesters (like Terylene/PET) are used in clothing, plastic bottles, and films.
  • Dyes and Pigments: Many synthetic dyes and pigments contain nitrogen-containing functional groups, particularly aromatic amines, contributing to their vibrant colours.
  • Agrochemicals: Nitrogen compounds are integral to herbicides and insecticides, where their specific structures and reactivity are designed to target pests.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing addition and condensation polymerisation: Students often forget that condensation polymerisation involves the elimination of a small molecule.
  • ❌ Incorrectly identifying chiral centres: A carbon atom must be bonded to four different groups, not just four different atoms. For example, -CH₂Cl is different from -CH₃.
  • ❌ Assuming all amines are strong bases: Aromatic amines are weaker bases than aliphatic amines due to delocalisation of the nitrogen lone pair into the benzene ring.
  • ❌ Forgetting the 'excess' reagent in amine synthesis: If ammonia is not in excess when reacting with a haloalkane, you will get a mixture of primary, secondary, tertiary amines and a quaternary ammonium salt.
  • ❌ Mixing up conditions for amide formation (acyl chlorides/acid anhydrides with amines) versus ester formation (carboxylic acids with alcohols).
  • ❌ Not appreciating the multi-step nature of organic synthesis: Students may focus on single reactions rather than planning a sequence of reactions to achieve a target molecule.
Memory Anchor

Imagine 'Chirality' as a pair of hands – they are mirror images but cannot be superimposed. For polymers, 'Condensation' polymerisation condenses out water, while 'Addition' polymerisation simply adds monomers together.

✏️ Answer: The reaction proceeds via nucleophilic acyl substitution: 1. The lone pair of electrons on the nitrogen atom of methylamine acts as a nucleophile, attacking the electrophilic carbonyl carbon of ethanoyl chloride. The π-bond of the carbonyl group breaks, and electrons move to the oxygen atom, forming a tetrahedral intermediate with a negative charge on the oxygen. 2. The lone pair on the negatively charged oxygen reforms the C=O double bond, simultaneously expelling the chloride ion (Cl⁻) as a good leaving group. 3. A proton is then transferred from the positively charged nitrogen to the chloride ion (or another methylamine molecule), regenerating the amine's lone pair and forming N-methylethanamide and hydrogen chloride (HCl).

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Correct structures for ethanoyl chloride and methylamine.
  • ✅ Curly arrow from lone pair on nitrogen of methylamine to carbonyl carbon of ethanoyl chloride.
  • ✅ Curly arrow from C=O bond breaking, electrons move to oxygen.
  • ✅ Formation of tetrahedral intermediate with negative charge on oxygen.
  • ✅ Curly arrow from O⁻ reforming C=O bond, pushing out Cl⁻ as leaving group.
  • ✅ Proton transfer from N⁺ to Cl⁻ (or another methylamine molecule) to form N-methylethanamide and HCl.
  • ✅ Correct structure of N-methylethanamide.

Q5. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

Lactic acid (2-hydroxypropanoic acid) is a chiral molecule. Explain why, but also discuss why the body typically only produces and utilises the (S)-enantiomer.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Lactic acid (2-hydroxypropanoic acid) is a chiral molecule because its central carbon atom (C2) is bonded to four different groups: a hydrogen atom (-H), a hydroxyl group (-OH), a methyl group (-CH₃), and a carboxylic acid group (-COOH). This chiral centre results in two non-superimposable mirror image isomers, (R)- and (S)-lactic acid.

The body typically only produces and utilises the (S)-enantiomer due to the inherent chirality of biological systems. Enzymes, which catalyse almost all metabolic reactions, are proteins themselves composed of chiral amino acids and thus possess specific, chiral active sites. These active sites are designed to interact with only one specific enantiomer of a substrate, much like a lock and key. Only the (S)-lactic acid can correctly bind to and be processed by the enzymes involved in metabolism (e.g., lactate dehydrogenase), making the reactions stereospecific. The (R)-enantiomer either cannot bind or binds ineffectively, thus it is not metabolised in the same way or at all.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Lactic acid is chiral because its central carbon (C2) is bonded to four different groups: -H, -OH, -CH₃, and -COOH.
  • ✅ This gives rise to two enantiomers: (R)-lactic acid and (S)-lactic acid.
  • ✅ Living systems (enzymes) are themselves chiral.
  • ✅ Enzymes have specific active sites that are complementary in shape to only one specific enantiomer (like a lock and key).
  • ✅ Therefore, metabolic pathways and enzyme-catalysed reactions are stereospecific.
  • ✅ Only the (S)-enantiomer fits and reacts with the enzymes involved in glycolysis and muscle metabolism, making it the biologically active form.

Q6. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

Predict the structure of the product when propanoyl chloride reacts with excess ethylamine. Justify the use of excess ethylamine.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: When propanoyl chloride reacts with excess ethylamine, the product formed is N-ethylpropanamide (CH₃CH₂CONHCH₂CH₃).

Excess ethylamine is used for two main reasons. Firstly, the reaction produces hydrogen chloride (HCl) as a byproduct. HCl is an acid and would react with the basic ethylamine reactant, forming an ammonium salt (CH₃CH₂NH₃⁺Cl⁻). This would remove ethylamine from the reaction mixture, making it unavailable to react with the propanoyl chloride, thereby reducing the yield of N-ethylpropanamide. Secondly, using excess ethylamine ensures that there is always sufficient nucleophile present to react completely with the propanoyl chloride, further maximising the yield of the desired amide product.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Product: N-ethylpropanamide (CH₃CH₂CONHCH₂CH₃).
  • ✅ Justification for excess ethylamine:
  • ✅ Reaction forms HCl as a byproduct.
  • ✅ HCl would react with the amine (ethylamine) to form an ammonium salt (CH₃CH₂NH₃⁺Cl⁻).
  • ✅ This removes ethylamine from the reaction, reducing the yield of the desired amide.
  • ✅ Excess ethylamine neutralises the HCl formed, ensuring that there is always sufficient amine to react with the propanoyl chloride and maximise the yield of the amide.

Q7. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

Outline a multi-step synthesis for converting cyclohexene into cyclohexanone oxime. You should include reagents and conditions for each step.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: To convert cyclohexene to cyclohexanone oxime, a two-step synthesis is required:

Step 1: Oxidation of cyclohexene to cyclohexanone. Reagents: Acidified potassium dichromate(VI) (K₂Cr₂O₇ in dilute H₂SO₄) or hot, concentrated potassium manganate(VII) (KMnO₄). Conditions: Heat under reflux. Cyclohexene, an alkene, is oxidised to the corresponding ketone, cyclohexanone.

Step 2: Reaction of cyclohexanone with hydroxylamine. Reagent: Hydroxylamine (NH₂OH) - typically supplied as hydroxylamine hydrochloride (NH₂OH·HCl) and a buffer to maintain a slightly acidic pH (e.g., pH 5-6). Conditions: Warm, in aqueous ethanol. The ketone reacts with hydroxylamine to form the oxime and water. Equation: C₆H₁₀O + NH₂OH → C₆H₁₀NOH + H₂O

Q9. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

Describe how you would distinguish between a primary, secondary, and tertiary amine in a laboratory setting using chemical tests.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: To distinguish between primary, secondary, and tertiary amines:

  1. Reaction with Acyl Chloride (e.g., Ethanoyl Chloride):

    • Primary amines (RNH₂) will react vigorously with ethanoyl chloride, producing white fumes of HCl and forming a N-substituted amide.
    • Secondary amines (R₂NH) will also react vigorously with ethanoyl chloride, producing white fumes of HCl and forming a N,N-disubstituted amide.
    • Tertiary amines (R₃N) do not react with ethanoyl chloride because they lack a hydrogen atom bonded directly to the nitrogen, which is required for the substitution reaction. This test allows for the identification of a tertiary amine by its lack of reaction.

  2. Reaction with Nitrous Acid (from NaNO₂/HCl at 0-5°C):

    • Once tertiary amines are ruled out, this test can differentiate between primary and secondary amines.
    • Primary amines react with cold nitrous acid to form an unstable diazonium salt, which rapidly decomposes to produce a colorless gas (nitrogen, N₂) and usually an alcohol. Effervescence (gas bubbles) is observed.
    • Secondary amines react with cold nitrous acid to form an oily yellow liquid called a N-nitrosamine.

By performing these two tests sequentially, primary, secondary, and tertiary amines can be distinguished.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Test for primary/secondary amines: Reaction with acyl chloride (e.g., ethanoyl chloride).
  • ✅ Primary amine: Vigorous reaction, white fumes of HCl, forms N-substituted amide.
  • ✅ Secondary amine: Vigorous reaction, white fumes of HCl, forms N,N-disubstituted amide.
  • ✅ Tertiary amine: No reaction with acyl chloride as it lacks a N-H bond.
  • ✅ Test to differentiate primary/secondary (after identifying tertiary): Reaction with nitrous acid (from NaNO₂/HCl at 0-5°C).
  • ✅ Primary amine: Effervescence (N₂ gas evolved), forms an unstable diazonium salt which decomposes.
  • ✅ Secondary amine: Forms an oily yellow liquid (N-nitrosamine).
  • ✅ Tertiary amine: Forms a soluble salt with nitrous acid, but no N-nitrosamine or N₂ gas unless warming (which decomposes it).

🧩 Quick Quiz (MCQ)

1. Which statement correctly describes a primary amine? A) A compound containing a carbon atom bonded to two nitrogen atoms. B) A compound containing a nitrogen atom bonded to one alkyl group and two hydrogen atoms. C) A compound containing a nitrogen atom within a benzene ring. D) A compound containing a nitrogen atom bonded to three different alkyl groups.

Answer

B) A compound containing a nitrogen atom bonded to one alkyl group and two hydrogen atoms. A primary amine has a nitrogen atom bonded to one alkyl (or aryl) group and two hydrogen atoms. For example, methylamine (CH3NH2).

2. Amides can be formed by the reaction between which two types of organic compounds? A) An alcohol and a carboxylic acid. B) An amine and a halogenoalkane. C) A carboxylic acid and an amine. D) An aldehyde and an alcohol.

Answer

C) A carboxylic acid and an amine. Amides are typically formed by the condensation reaction between a carboxylic acid (or an acyl chloride/acid anhydride) and an amine, where a molecule of water (or HCl/carboxylic acid) is eliminated.

3. Which of the following amino acid structures contains a chiral carbon atom? A) Glycine (NH2-CH2-COOH) B) Alanine (CH3-CH(NH2)-COOH) C) Valine (CH(CH3)2-CH(NH2)-COOH) D) Both Alanine and Valine.

Answer

D) Both Alanine and Valine. A chiral carbon atom is bonded to four different groups. Glycine's alpha-carbon is bonded to two hydrogen atoms, so it is not chiral. Alanine's alpha-carbon is bonded to -H, -NH2, -COOH, and -CH3, so it is chiral. Valine's alpha-carbon is bonded to -H, -NH2, -COOH, and -CH(CH3)2, so it is also chiral. Therefore, both Alanine and Valine contain a chiral carbon atom.

4. Which type of isomerism is exhibited by molecules containing a chiral centre? A) Geometric isomerism. B) Positional isomerism. C) Optical isomerism. D) Chain isomerism.

Answer

C) Optical isomerism. Molecules with a chiral centre (a carbon atom bonded to four different groups) are non-superimposable mirror images of each other. These are known as enantiomers and exhibit optical isomerism, meaning they rotate plane-polarised light in opposite directions.

5. What is the key difference between addition polymerisation and condensation polymerisation? A) Addition polymerisation forms a polymer with a higher molecular mass, while condensation polymerisation forms a polymer with a lower molecular mass. B) Addition polymerisation involves the joining of monomers without the loss of any atoms, while condensation polymerisation involves the joining of monomers with the elimination of a small molecule. C) Addition polymerisation requires monomers with at least two different functional groups, while condensation polymerisation requires only one functional group. D) Addition polymerisation forms thermosetting polymers, while condensation polymerisation forms thermoplastics.

Answer

B) Addition polymerisation involves the joining of monomers without the loss of any atoms, while condensation polymerisation involves the joining of monomers with the elimination of a small molecule. Addition polymerisation involves monomers (usually alkenes) adding to each other without losing any atoms, meaning the polymer is the sum of all monomer units. Condensation polymerisation, however, involves the elimination of a small molecule (like water, HCl, or methanol) during the formation of each new bond between monomers.

6. Which pair of monomers could react to form a polyester? A) Ethene and propene. B) A dicarboxylic acid and a diamine. C) A diol and a dicarboxylic acid. D) An amino acid.

Answer

C) A diol and a dicarboxylic acid. Polyesters are formed by condensation polymerisation between monomers containing two alcohol (-OH) groups (a diol) and monomers containing two carboxylic acid (-COOH) groups (a dicarboxylic acid), with the elimination of water.

7. Carbon-carbon bond formation is crucial in organic synthesis primarily because it allows for the: A) Introduction of halogen atoms into a molecule. B) Conversion of alcohols into aldehydes. C) Increase in the complexity and size of carbon skeletons. D) Breaking down of larger molecules into smaller fragments.

Answer

C) Increase in the complexity and size of carbon skeletons. Carbon-carbon bond formation reactions are fundamental for building up larger and more complex organic molecules from simpler starting materials, thereby increasing the carbon skeleton's size and complexity. This is essential for synthesizing a wide range of organic compounds.

8. Consider a multi-stage synthesis starting from ethene to produce ethanol, then ethanal, and finally ethanoic acid. Which of the following accurately describes a reagent needed for one of these steps? A) Concentrated sulfuric acid and steam for ethene to ethanol. B) Tollens' reagent for ethanol to ethanal. C) Hydrogen gas with a nickel catalyst for ethanal to ethanoic acid. D) Aqueous sodium hydroxide for ethanol to ethanal.

Answer

A) Concentrated sulfuric acid and steam for ethene to ethanol. Ethene can be converted to ethanol by hydration, typically using steam and concentrated phosphoric acid or concentrated sulfuric acid as a catalyst. Tollens' reagent tests for aldehydes but doesn't convert ethanol to ethanal. Ethanol is oxidised to ethanal using acidified potassium dichromate(VI), not hydrogen gas or aqueous sodium hydroxide.

9. Which statement about amino acids is correct? A) They are typically acidic due to the presence of both an amine and a carboxyl group. B) They are able to form zwitterions in solution due to the presence of both acidic and basic functional groups. C) They only exist as charged ions at very high pH values. D) They are generally insoluble in water.

Answer

B) They are able to form zwitterions in solution due to the presence of both acidic and basic functional groups. Amino acids contain both a basic amine (-NH2) group and an acidic carboxylic acid (-COOH) group. In solution, these groups can ionise, with the amine group accepting a proton to form -NH3+ and the carboxylic acid group losing a proton to form -COO-, resulting in a neutral molecule with both positive and negative charges, known as a zwitterion. This also contributes to their good water solubility.

10. Nylon 6,6 is a synthetic polymer. What type of polymerisation forms Nylon 6,6? A) Addition polymerisation. B) Free radical polymerisation. C) Condensation polymerisation. D) Substitution polymerisation.

Answer

C) Condensation polymerisation. Nylon 6,6 is formed by the condensation reaction between two different monomers: a diamine (hexamethylenediamine) and a dicarboxylic acid (adipic acid). During the formation of each amide link, a molecule of water is eliminated.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The history of organic nitrogen compounds is rich, starting with Friedrich Wöhler's synthesis of urea (an amide) in 1828, which famously disproved vitalism. The late 19th and early 20th centuries saw significant advancements in amine chemistry and the understanding of proteins as polymers of amino acids. The discovery of nylon by Wallace Carothers in the 1930s revolutionised materials science, marking a pivotal moment in the development of synthetic polymers and leading to the 'plastic age'. The recognition of chirality's biological importance emerged prominently with the tragic thalidomide disaster in the 1950s, highlighting the critical need for stereospecific drug synthesis.

⚖️ Ethics

The ethics surrounding nitrogen compounds and polymers are multifaceted. The mass production of synthetic polymers like polyamides and polyesters has led to widespread environmental pollution, prompting ethical debates about corporate responsibility, consumer behaviour, and sustainable alternatives. The pharmaceutical industry grapples with the ethical implications of chiral drugs: ensuring only the therapeutically active enantiomer is produced and marketed, especially after lessons learned from thalidomide. Furthermore, the use of nitrogen compounds in agrochemicals raises questions about their impact on biodiversity, water quality, and human health.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond the basic understanding, advanced studies in this area delve into stereoselective synthesis, where chemists develop methods to synthesise only one specific enantiomer of a chiral molecule, often using chiral catalysts or auxiliaries (e.g., Sharpless epoxidation, enantioselective hydrogenation). Peptide synthesis involves complex protecting group strategies to build specific protein sequences efficiently. Polymer chemistry explores advanced polymer architectures (e.g., block copolymers), smart polymers, and the kinetics and thermodynamics of polymerisation processes. Multi-stage synthesis also incorporates more sophisticated reactions for carbon–carbon bond formation (e.g., Heck, Suzuki, Sonogashira couplings) and advanced retrosynthetic analysis.

For amine synthesis: Reacting a haloalkane with excess ethanolic ammonia in a sealed tube under heat. The product (a mixture of amines) can then be separated. For amide synthesis: Reacting an acyl chloride with a primary or secondary amine in a fume cupboard. Safety: Amines are often corrosive and have unpleasant odours. Acyl chlorides are highly reactive and react vigorously with water, releasing HCl fumes. Both require careful handling in a fume cupboard with appropriate PPE (gloves, eye protection).

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Amines (especially lower molecular weight ones) are often corrosive, toxic, and highly flammable. Work in a fume cupboard.
  • 🛑 Acyl chlorides are highly reactive, corrosive, and release corrosive hydrogen chloride gas upon reaction with water or amines. Handle with extreme care in a fume cupboard.
  • 🛑 Hydrogen cyanide (HCN) used to make nitriles is extremely toxic. Potassium cyanide (KCN) is also highly toxic. Reactions involving cyanides must be carried out in a fume cupboard with extreme caution.
  • 🛑 Lithium aluminium hydride (LiAlH₄) is a powerful reducing agent that reacts violently with water and protic solvents, producing hydrogen gas (flammable). Handle in dry, inert conditions.
  • 🛑 Strong acids (HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃) and bases (NaOH) are corrosive. Wear eye protection and gloves.
  • 🛑 Organic solvents are often flammable and may be toxic. Ensure good ventilation.

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. Nucleophilic substitution for amine synthesis from haloalkanes: The lone pair on ammonia (or an amine) acts as a nucleophile, attacking the δ⁺ carbon of the haloalkane, leading to the departure of the halide ion.
  2. Nucleophilic acyl substitution for amide formation: The lone pair on an amine attacks the electrophilic carbonyl carbon of an acyl chloride or acid anhydride, forming a tetrahedral intermediate. The leaving group (Cl⁻ or carboxylate) departs, followed by deprotonation of the nitrogen.
  3. Reduction of nitrobenzene: Involves a series of electron and proton transfers. Often simplified as 6[H] in equations, where [H] represents nascent hydrogen from Sn/HCl.
  4. Condensation reaction for polyamide/polyester formation: The nucleophilic attack of an amine's lone pair (for polyamide) or an alcohol's oxygen (for polyester) on a carbonyl carbon of a carboxylic acid or derivative, followed by the elimination of water or HCl.
  5. Formation of nitriles: Nucleophilic substitution of a haloalkane by a cyanide ion (CN⁻) or addition of HCN to a carbonyl compound followed by dehydration.

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Amine synthesis from haloalkanes: Excess ethanolic ammonia, sealed tube, heat (e.g., 100°C).
  • Reduction of nitrobenzene to phenylamine: Tin (Sn) and concentrated hydrochloric acid (HCl), heat under reflux, followed by neutralisation with NaOH (aq).
  • Amide formation from acyl chlorides/acid anhydrides: Room temperature, anhydrous conditions (often in an organic solvent like ether), sometimes with a base to absorb HCl byproduct.
  • Reduction of nitriles to primary amines: Lithium aluminium hydride (LiAlH₄) in dry ether, followed by aqueous acid/water workup; or H₂ gas with a Ni catalyst and heat/pressure.
  • Nitration of benzene: Concentrated nitric acid and concentrated sulfuric acid, 50-60°C.
  • Condensation polymerisation: Heating monomers (often diacids/diesters and diamines/diols) under specific temperatures and sometimes vacuum to remove the small molecule byproduct and drive the equilibrium.

🔦 Element Spotlight

Nitrogen (N) is a p-block element with electron configuration [He] 2s²2p³. It typically forms three covalent bonds and possesses a lone pair of electrons when neutral, as seen in ammonia and amines. This lone pair makes nitrogen compounds nucleophilic and basic. Its versatility allows it to form single, double, and triple bonds (e.g., in nitriles) and exist in various oxidation states, making it central to a wide array of organic reactions and biological molecules.
