🧠 Conceptual Understanding

As your chemistry teacher, I want you to understand that 'pure' water isn't just H2O; it's about making water suitable for a specific purpose. For drinking, we need 'potable' water, which means it's safe and pleasant to drink, free from harmful microorganisms and excessive dissolved substances. This practical is all about how we test water samples for their pH and the amount of dissolved solids present, and then how we can purify them, specifically using distillation. You'll learn that water from different sources, like a river versus tap water, will have different characteristics, and understanding these differences is crucial for ensuring public health and safety.

📌 Core Theory

  • Potable water is water that is safe to drink, containing appropriate levels of dissolved salts and no harmful microbes.
  • Chemically pure water (H2O) contains only water molecules and is not necessarily potable, as it lacks essential dissolved minerals and can be tasteless.
  • Water samples from different sources (e.g., tap, river, pond, distilled) will have varying compositions due to different impurities and dissolved substances.
  • The pH of a water sample indicates its acidity or alkalinity; potable water generally has a pH between 6.5 and 8.5.
  • Dissolved solids in water contribute to its hardness and taste; excessive levels can be harmful, while too few make it unpalatable.
  • Distillation is a physical separation technique used to purify water by separating it from dissolved solids and non-volatile impurities.
  • The process of distillation involves heating water to its boiling point, turning it into steam, and then condensing the steam back into liquid water, leaving impurities behind.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

When we analyze water, we're primarily concerned with two key properties: pH and the concentration of dissolved solids. pH is measured using a pH probe or universal indicator, which tells us how acidic or alkaline the water is. Potable water should be close to neutral, around pH 7. Dissolved solids are usually salts or minerals picked up as water flows over rocks and soil. We can determine their presence and approximate quantity by evaporating a known volume of water to dryness and weighing the residue. The greater the mass of the residue, the more dissolved solids were present. For purifying water, especially to remove dissolved salts, distillation is a highly effective method. This involves heating the impure water in a flask until it boils. The water turns into steam, leaving the non-volatile dissolved solids behind. The steam then passes into a condenser, which is cooled by circulating cold water, causing the steam to condense back into liquid water. This collected liquid, called the distillate, is pure water, largely free of dissolved impurities.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Analysing pH of Water Samples

Problem: A student tests three water samples: Sample A from a river, Sample B from a tap, and Sample C from a rain puddle. They record the following pH values using a universal indicator: Sample A = 6, Sample B = 7.5, Sample C = 5. Interpret these results and suggest which sample is most suitable for drinking.

Steps: 1. Interpret pH values: pH 7 is neutral, pH < 7 is acidic, pH > 7 is alkaline. 2. Assess Sample A (pH 6): Slightly acidic. 3. Assess Sample B (pH 7.5): Slightly alkaline, close to neutral. 4. Assess Sample C (pH 5): Acidic. 5. Determine suitability for drinking: Potable water should have a pH between 6.5 and 8.5 and be free from harmful substances. Sample B falls within this range.

Answer: Sample A is slightly acidic. Sample B is slightly alkaline, close to neutral. Sample C is acidic. Sample B (tap water) has a pH (7.5) within the range typically considered safe for drinking (6.5-8.5), making it the most suitable based solely on pH. However, further tests for microbes and other contaminants would be needed to confirm potability.

Teacher's Tip

Remember that pH is just one factor for potability; other tests like dissolved solids and microbial content are also vital.

2. Determining Dissolved Solids by Evaporation

Problem: A student wants to compare the amount of dissolved solids in tap water and river water. They take 50.0 cm3 of tap water and 50.0 cm3 of river water. After evaporating each sample to dryness in pre-weighed evaporating dishes, they record the following masses:

Steps: 1. Initial mass of empty evaporating dish for tap water = 35.25 g 2. Mass of dish + residue for tap water = 35.31 g 3. Initial mass of empty evaporating dish for river water = 36.10 g 4. Mass of dish + residue for river water = 36.28 g 5. Calculate the mass of dissolved solids for tap water: 35.31 g - 35.25 g = 0.06 g. 6. Calculate the mass of dissolved solids for river water: 36.28 g - 36.10 g = 0.18 g. 7. Compare the masses: River water has 0.18 g of dissolved solids, while tap water has 0.06 g.

Answer: The mass of dissolved solids in 50.0 cm3 of tap water is 0.06 g. The mass of dissolved solids in 50.0 cm3 of river water is 0.18 g. This indicates that the river water sample contains a significantly higher concentration of dissolved solids compared to the tap water sample.

Teacher's Tip

Always use a control (e.g., distilled water) when comparing samples to ensure your evaporating dishes are clean and to get a baseline for 'zero' dissolved solids.

3. Describing the Distillation Process

Problem: Describe the main steps involved in using simple distillation to obtain pure water from a solution of salt water.

Steps: 1. Pour the salt water into a distillation flask and set it up with a condenser attached to the side arm and a receiving beaker/flask to collect the distillate. 2. Heat the salt water using a Bunsen burner. The water will boil and turn into steam, leaving the non-volatile salt behind in the flask. 3. The steam travels up into the neck of the flask and then into the condenser. 4. Cold water is circulated through the outer jacket of the condenser, cooling the inner tube. 5. As the steam passes through the cooled inner tube of the condenser, it loses heat and condenses back into liquid water. 6. This purified liquid water (distillate) is collected in the receiving beaker/flask. 7. Stop heating when only a small volume of liquid remains in the distillation flask, or when no more distillate is being collected.

Answer: To obtain pure water from salt water by simple distillation, first, the salt water is placed in a distillation flask and heated. As the water boils, it turns into steam, leaving the non-volatile salt dissolved in the flask. The steam then enters a condenser, which is cooled by a continuous flow of cold water, causing the steam to condense back into liquid water. This purified liquid water, known as the distillate, is collected in a separate beaker. This process effectively separates the water from the dissolved salt.

Teacher's Tip

Ensure the condenser is set up correctly with water flowing in at the bottom and out at the top to maximise cooling efficiency.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Municipal Water Treatment: Large-scale water treatment plants use filtration, sedimentation, chlorination, and sometimes distillation (though less common due to energy costs) to produce potable water for homes and industries.
  • Emergency Water Purification: In disaster zones or survival situations, simple distillation or boiling is used to make contaminated water safe to drink, removing pathogens and dissolved solids.
  • Laboratory Use: Pure distilled or deionised water is essential in chemistry laboratories for preparing solutions and carrying out experiments, as dissolved ions could interfere with reactions.
  • Industrial Processes: Many industries, such as power generation and pharmaceuticals, require high-purity water to prevent scaling, corrosion, or contamination of products.
  • Desalination: In arid regions, seawater can be desalinated (purified) using large-scale distillation plants or reverse osmosis to produce fresh drinking water.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Distilled water is potable: Distilled water is chemically pure H2O, but it lacks essential minerals and can taste flat. It's not necessarily safe to drink without further treatment if contaminants that boil at similar temperatures to water (e.g., some volatile organic compounds) were present in the original sample.
  • ❌ Boiling water makes it perfectly pure: Boiling water kills most microorganisms, but it doesn't remove dissolved salts, heavy metals, or other non-volatile chemical pollutants. It concentrates dissolved solids as water evaporates.
  • ❌ All clear water is safe to drink: Water can appear perfectly clear but still contain harmful bacteria, viruses, or dissolved toxic chemicals that are colourless and odourless.
  • ❌ pH 7 is always ideal for drinking water: While 7 is neutral, potable water has a healthy range, typically 6.5-8.5. Slight variations within this range are normal and safe due to natural minerals.
Memory Anchor

Imagine water going on a 'spa day' to get rid of its 'baggage' (impurities). Distillation is like giving it a steam bath (evaporation) then a cold shower (condensation) to emerge refreshed and 'pure', leaving all the worries behind.

Q4. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

Distillation is often considered a 'last resort' for large-scale water purification due to its high energy requirements. Discuss alternative modern methods of water purification used on a large scale for potable water, explaining how they work and their advantages over distillation.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: While distillation is effective, its high energy cost makes it impractical for large-scale potable water purification. Modern alternatives offer more efficient solutions. One common approach is multi-stage filtration, which includes sedimentation, sand filtration, and activated carbon filtration. Sedimentation allows heavy particles to settle, sand filtration removes finer suspended solids, and activated carbon removes dissolved organic compounds, tastes, and odours. This method is significantly less energy-intensive and faster than distillation. Following filtration, chlorination or UV treatment is used for disinfection. Chlorination involves adding chlorine to kill bacteria and viruses, while UV light denatures their DNA. These methods are highly effective against pathogens, which distillation also removes, but they are much cheaper and faster for large volumes. For desalination or removal of dissolved salts, reverse osmosis (RO) is a leading alternative. In RO, high pressure forces water through a semi-permeable membrane, which allows water molecules to pass but blocks dissolved salts and larger impurities. RO is considerably more energy-efficient than distillation for desalination as it doesn't require phase changes. Another method is ozonation, where ozone gas (O3) is bubbled through water, oxidising and breaking down pollutants and disinfecting effectively, leaving no harmful residues. These alternative methods collectively offer advantages such as lower energy consumption, faster processing speeds for large volumes, and the ability to selectively remove specific contaminants while often retaining beneficial minerals (unlike distillation, which removes everything).

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Mention multi-stage filtration (sedimentation, sand filtration, activated carbon). Removes suspended solids, some chemicals. Cheaper, less energy.
  • ✅ Mention chlorination/UV treatment. Kills microorganisms. Essential for safety, but doesn't remove dissolved solids.
  • ✅ Mention reverse osmosis (RO). Forces water through a semi-permeable membrane, leaving dissolved salts/impurities behind. Much less energy-intensive than distillation for desalination, more efficient for large scale.
  • ✅ Mention ozonation. Uses ozone to oxidise and break down pollutants, also disinfects. Effective disinfectant, leaves no residue.
  • ✅ Advantages of alternatives: lower energy consumption, faster, more efficient for bulk processing, can be tailored to specific contaminants, retain beneficial minerals (except RO to some extent).

Q6. (2 Marks | Short Answer | ⏱️ ~2 min)

Explain why it is advisable to add a few anti-bumping granules to the distillation flask before heating.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: It is advisable to add a few anti-bumping granules to the distillation flask before heating to ensure smooth and even boiling. These granules provide nucleation sites where bubbles of steam can form steadily. Without them, the liquid can become superheated, meaning it heats above its boiling point without boiling, then suddenly boils violently (known as 'bumping'). Bumping can cause the apparatus to jump, spill hot liquid, or even break, making the experiment unsafe.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Anti-bumping granules (or boiling chips) provide nucleation sites for bubbles to form smoothly.
  • ✅ This prevents 'bumping' or 'superheating', where liquid heats above its boiling point without boiling, then suddenly boils violently, which can be dangerous and damage apparatus.

✏️ Answer: Simple distillation has significant limitations for purifying river water contaminated with both dissolved salts and volatile organic pollutants with boiling points below 100°C. Because they have boiling points lower than or close to that of water, they would vaporise either before the water or alongside it, meaning they would condense and be collected in the distillate, rendering the 'purified' water still contaminated with these harmful substances. This could then be followed by simple distillation (or reverse osmosis) to remove the dissolved salts and any remaining non-volatile impurities and pathogens. For mixtures with closely boiling components, fractional distillation (though more complex) could be considered to achieve better separation of volatile compounds.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Limitation 2: It will NOT effectively remove volatile organic pollutants.

  • ✅ Suggestion: A more suitable strategy would be fractional distillation to separate components with close boiling points, OR pre-treatment with activated carbon to adsorb organic pollutants.
  • ✅ Suggestion: This could be followed by boiling/distillation to kill pathogens and remove salts, or reverse osmosis for broad removal.

Q9. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

Outline the main stages involved in producing potable water from a contaminated river source for a large city, beyond simple distillation.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: To produce potable water from a contaminated river source for a large city, a multi-stage process is typically used, which is more efficient than simple distillation. The first stage is screening, where large debris like leaves and branches are removed. This is followed by sedimentation and coagulation, where chemicals such as aluminium sulfate and calcium hydroxide are added to cause tiny suspended particles to clump together (flocculation), forming larger particles that settle to the bottom as sludge. Next, the water undergoes filtration, passing through beds of sand and gravel to remove any remaining suspended solids. After filtration, the critical step of disinfection takes place, where harmful microorganisms (bacteria, viruses) are killed, typically using chlorine, ozone, or ultraviolet (UV) light. Finally, the pH may be adjusted to ensure it's within the safe potable range, and sometimes fluoride is added for dental health before the water is stored and distributed.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Screening: Remove large debris (leaves, branches).
  • ✅ Sedimentation/Coagulation: Add chemicals (e.g., aluminium sulfate, calcium hydroxide) to make small particles clump together (flocculation), then allow them to settle as sludge.
  • ✅ Filtration: Pass water through beds of sand and gravel to remove remaining suspended solids.
  • ✅ Disinfection: Kill harmful microorganisms using chlorine, ozone, or UV light.
  • ✅ pH Adjustment / Fluoridation (optional): Adjust pH to be within potable range, add fluoride for dental health if desired.
  • ✅ Storage/Distribution: Store in reservoirs and distribute through pipes.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The earliest forms of water purification date back to ancient civilisations, with records from ancient India and Egypt describing methods like boiling, filtering through charcoal, and storing water in copper vessels. Hippocrates, around 400 BC, invented the 'Hippocratic sleeve', a cloth filter. Modern water treatment began in the 19th century with sand filtration in Scotland (Glasgow, 1804; Paisley, 1832) to combat cholera epidemics. Chlorination as a widespread disinfectant began in the early 20th century. Distillation has been known since ancient times for producing 'aqua vitae' (alcohol) but its application for water purification scaled up significantly in naval vessels and arid regions.

⚖️ Ethics

The ethics surrounding water purification revolve around access to clean, safe drinking water as a fundamental human right. Who should bear the cost of water purification (consumers, government, industry)? How do we balance effective purification with environmental impact (e.g., energy consumption of desalination, disposal of chemical sludge)? There are also ethical considerations in balancing necessary disinfection (like chlorination) with potential by-products, and ensuring equitable distribution of purified water globally, especially in regions facing water scarcity or pollution.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond simple distillation, more advanced separation techniques include fractional distillation for separating liquids with close boiling points (e.g., ethanol from water), and vacuum distillation for heat-sensitive compounds, where reducing pressure lowers the boiling point. For dissolved solids, reverse osmosis uses a semi-permeable membrane to remove ions and molecules by applying pressure greater than osmotic pressure, making it highly effective for desalination with less energy than distillation. Ion exchange resins can also selectively remove specific ions (e.g., for water softening or deionisation) through chemical reactions. For organic pollutants, advanced oxidation processes (AOPs) use highly reactive species like hydroxyl radicals to break down complex organic molecules into less harmful compounds, often involving UV light, ozone, or hydrogen peroxide.

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

Required Practical 8: Analysis and purification of water samples

Part 1: Analysis of Water Samples (pH and Dissolved Solids)

Equipment List: * Three different water samples (e.g., tap water, river/pond water, distilled water) * Universal indicator solution or pH paper/probe * Test tubes/beakers * Evaporating dishes (3 per student/group) * Tripod stands, gauze mats, Bunsen burners, heatproof mats * Measuring cylinders (e.g., 50 cm3) * Electronic balance (accurate to 2 decimal places) * Tongs * Marker pen/labels

Procedure for pH Testing: 1. Pour a small amount (e.g., 10 cm3) of each water sample into separate, labelled test tubes or small beakers. 2. Add a few drops of universal indicator solution to each sample and stir gently. Alternatively, dip a strip of universal indicator paper into each sample. 3. Observe the colour change in each sample. 4. Compare the colour to a standard pH colour chart to determine the pH value of each sample. 5. Record the pH values and note whether each sample is acidic, neutral, or alkaline.

Procedure for Dissolved Solids Testing: 1. Clean and dry three evaporating dishes. Accurately weigh each empty dish using an electronic balance and record its mass. Label each dish (e.g., 'Tap', 'River', 'Distilled'). 2. Using a measuring cylinder, add a known, precise volume (e.g., 50.0 cm3) of each water sample to its respective labelled evaporating dish. 3. Place each evaporating dish on a gauze mat supported by a tripod stand, directly over a Bunsen burner on a heatproof mat. 4. Gently heat each dish to evaporate the water. Use a low to medium flame to prevent spitting and ensure slow, even evaporation. Continue heating until all the water has evaporated and a dry residue remains at the bottom of the dish. 5. Remove the dish from the heat using tongs and allow it to cool completely to room temperature (this is critical for accurate weighing). 6. Accurately reweigh each evaporating dish with the solid residue and record the mass. 7. Calculate the mass of dissolved solids for each sample by subtracting the initial mass of the empty dish from the final mass of the dish plus residue. 8. Compare the masses of dissolved solids for the different water samples.

Part 2: Purification by Simple Distillation

Equipment List: * Distillation flask (e.g., 250 cm3 round-bottom flask) * Condenser (Liebig condenser) * Receiving flask/beaker * Thermometer (0-110°C) * Bunsen burner, tripod stand, gauze mat, heatproof mat * Retort stands and clamps (2-3) * Rubber tubing (for condenser water inlet/outlet) * Tap water supply and sink for cooling water * Impure water sample (e.g., salt solution, or one of the tested samples from Part 1) * Anti-bumping granules

Procedure for Simple Distillation: 1. Set up the distillation apparatus as shown in standard diagrams (distillation flask on tripod/gauze, thermometer in neck of flask with bulb level with side arm, condenser attached to side arm, receiving flask at the end of the condenser). 2. Secure all glassware with clamps, ensuring the setup is stable. 3. Connect the rubber tubing to the condenser: water should flow in at the bottom and out at the top to ensure the condenser jacket fills completely and provides efficient cooling. 4. Pour the impure water sample into the distillation flask. Add a few anti-bumping granules to ensure smooth boiling. 5. Start the flow of cold water through the condenser. 6. Begin heating the distillation flask gently with the Bunsen burner. The impure water will boil, producing steam. 7. Observe the thermometer. It should rise to 100°C (at standard pressure) as steam passes into the condenser. 8. The steam will condense in the cold condenser and drip as purified water (distillate) into the receiving flask. 9. Collect the distillate until most of the water has evaporated from the distillation flask, but do not heat to complete dryness as some impurities might decompose or become difficult to clean. 10. Stop heating and turn off the condenser water flow.

Safety Notes (COSHH Considerations): * Eye protection (safety goggles) MUST be worn at all times. * Hot apparatus: Bunsen burner flames, hot glassware (flask, dish, condenser) can cause severe burns. Use tongs and allow equipment to cool before handling. * Hot liquids: Boiling water can spit or splash. Do not lean over evaporating dishes or distillation flasks. * Glassware: Handle carefully to prevent breakages. Ensure retort stands and clamps are stable and secure. * Flammable materials: Keep flammable materials away from the Bunsen burner flame. * Water Samples: If using river/pond water, assume it contains harmful bacteria and chemicals. Do not ingest, and wash hands thoroughly after handling. Use a fume cupboard if concerned about volatile impurities. * Mercury thermometers: If using old thermometers, be aware of mercury hazard in case of breakage. Modern thermometers are alcohol-filled. * Anti-bumping granules: Non-hazardous, but do not ingest.

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Hot apparatus and liquids: Flasks, dishes, and condensers become very hot. Use tongs and allow to cool before handling. Risk of burns.
  • 🛑 Spitting/splashing liquids: Especially during evaporation of dissolved solids, hot water can spit. Wear eye protection and do not lean over.
  • 🛑 Broken glassware: Risk of cuts. Handle glassware carefully, ensure stable setups.
  • 🛑 Bunsen burner flame: Risk of burns to skin or flammable materials. Keep hair tied back, ensure no loose clothing.
  • 🛑 Untreated water samples: River/pond water may contain harmful microorganisms or chemical pollutants. Do not ingest. Wash hands thoroughly.
  • 🛑 Acid/alkali exposure: If testing highly acidic or alkaline samples, wear gloves and eye protection. Neutralisation reactions can be exothermic.

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. Neutralisation (for pH adjustment): If water is too acidic or alkaline, it can be neutralised. For acidic water, a base (e.g., Ca(OH)2) reacts with H+ ions: H+(aq) + OH-(aq) -> H2O(l). For alkaline water, an acid reacts with OH- ions. This is a general acid-base reaction.
  2. Evaporation: This is a physical process, not a chemical reaction mechanism. When water is heated, its molecules gain kinetic energy and overcome intermolecular forces to escape as water vapour (H2O(l) -> H2O(g)). Non-volatile dissolved solids are left behind.
  3. Condensation: This is also a physical process. When water vapour cools, its molecules lose kinetic energy, intermolecular forces reform, and it changes back into liquid water (H2O(g) -> H2O(l)).

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Distillation: Requires heating the impure water to its boiling point (approx. 100°C at standard pressure). The condenser must be continuously supplied with cold water to ensure efficient cooling and condensation of steam.
  • Evaporation for Dissolved Solids: Gentle heating is applied to evaporate water to dryness, leaving a solid residue. No specific temperature, just enough to boil water.
  • pH Testing: Room temperature is generally used. No specific conditions other than ensuring probes are calibrated or indicator is fresh.

🔦 Element Spotlight

While not a single element, water (H2O) is central. It's a compound of hydrogen and oxygen, formed by covalent bonds. Water is a polar molecule, meaning it has a slight positive charge on the hydrogen atoms and a slight negative charge on the oxygen atom. This polarity makes it an excellent solvent, able to dissolve a wide range of ionic compounds (salts) and other polar molecules. Its high specific heat capacity and high boiling point are also crucial properties that make it vital for life and many chemical processes. In its pure form (H2O), it's colourless, odourless, and tasteless. Its ability to act as a solvent is why it picks up so many dissolved solids from its environment, necessitating purification for different uses.
