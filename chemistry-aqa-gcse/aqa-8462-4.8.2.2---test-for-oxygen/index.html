🧠 Conceptual Understanding

As your chemistry teacher, I want you to understand that identifying gases is a fundamental skill in the lab. For oxygen, we don't just 'see' it, we test its property of supporting combustion. Oxygen is vital for things to burn, so our test relies on this. When you have an unknown gas, you introduce a 'glowing' splint—meaning it has a smouldering ember but no flame. If the gas is oxygen, it provides the fuel for that ember to burst back into a flame. It's a quick, simple, and distinctive chemical test that confirms the presence of oxygen.

📌 Core Theory

  • The test for oxygen involves placing a glowing splint into a sample of the gas.
  • A 'glowing splint' is a wooden splint that has been lit and then blown out, so it has a red-hot tip but no flame.
  • Oxygen is a gas that supports combustion.
  • When a glowing splint is introduced to oxygen, it will relight, bursting into a small flame.
  • This observation is a positive result for the presence of oxygen.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

The test for oxygen leverages its unique property as a supporter of combustion. While oxygen itself is not flammable, it is crucial for other substances to burn. When a wooden splint is lit and then extinguished, it leaves behind a small ember that is still very hot and smouldering. This ember contains unburnt carbon that is slowly reacting with the tiny amount of oxygen in the air. Upon introduction to an atmosphere rich in oxygen, the increased concentration of oxygen molecules rapidly accelerates the combustion reaction at the glowing tip of the splint. This rapid combustion releases enough energy to reignite the splint, causing it to burst back into a visible flame. This distinct 'relighting' is the definitive positive result for the presence of oxygen.

📖 Key Definitions

Term Definition
Glowing splint A wooden splint that has been ignited and then blown out, leaving a red-hot, smouldering tip.
Combustion A high-temperature exothermic redox chemical reaction between a fuel and an oxidant (usually oxygen) that produces oxidized, often gaseous products, in a mixture termed smoke. It typically involves a visible flame.
Supporter of combustion A substance, typically a gas like oxygen, that enables or accelerates the burning of another substance.

📚 Glossary

  • Combustion — A high-temperature exothermic redox chemical reaction between a fuel and an oxidant (usually oxygen) that produces oxidized, often gaseous products, in a mixture termed smoke. It typically involves a visible flame.
  • Glowing splint — A wooden splint that has been ignited and then blown out, leaving a red-hot, smouldering tip.
  • Supporter of combustion — A substance, typically a gas like oxygen, that enables or accelerates the burning of another substance.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Performing the Oxygen Test

Problem: A student has collected a gas in a test tube and suspects it might be oxygen. Describe the procedure to confirm this.

Steps: 1. Light a wooden splint using a Bunsen burner or lighter. 2. Allow the splint to burn briefly, then blow out the flame, leaving a glowing ember at the tip. 3. Carefully insert the glowing splint into the test tube containing the suspected gas. 4. Observe any changes to the glowing splint.

Answer: If the gas is oxygen, the glowing splint will relight, bursting into a small flame.

Teacher's Tip

Ensure the splint is genuinely 'glowing' and not still flaming or completely out. A fully flaming splint will just continue to burn, and a cold splint will do nothing.

2. Interpreting Gas Test Results

Problem: A scientist performs a gas test on an unknown sample. They insert a glowing splint, and it immediately relights. What conclusion can be drawn about the unknown gas?

Steps: 1. Recall the standard test for oxygen. 2. Identify the characteristic observation for oxygen during this test. 3. Relate the observation to the presence of oxygen.

Answer: The unknown gas is oxygen, as a glowing splint relights in its presence.

Teacher's Tip

Always state both the observation and the conclusion clearly in your answer.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Used in industrial settings to check for oxygen leaks or purity in gas tanks.
  • Medical professionals might use similar principles to verify oxygen supply in hospitals or emergency equipment.
  • Firefighters understand oxygen's role in combustion and work to remove it to extinguish fires.
  • In space exploration, understanding and testing for oxygen is critical for life support systems.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Mistaking oxygen as a flammable gas; it supports combustion but does not burn itself.
  • ❌ Using a lit splint instead of a glowing splint, which will simply continue to burn and not give a distinctive 'relight' observation.
  • ❌ Confusing the oxygen test with the hydrogen test (lit splint makes a 'squeaky pop').
  • ❌ Believing that any gas that allows a fire to burn is oxygen; other oxidising gases can also support combustion, but the glowing splint test is specific for oxygen at GCSE level.
Memory Anchor

Imagine 'O' for Oxygen, and also 'O' for 'On fire again' - the splint goes back 'on' and glows again!

Q4. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

📋 Mark Scheme:

Always wear eye protection. Ensure the gas sample is handled carefully and any flames used to light the splint are controlled and extinguished safely after use.

14. What term describes a substance that helps other substances burn?

A substance that helps other substances burn is described as an oxidizer or a supporter of combustion. Oxygen is a primary example of such a substance.

Tags: OxygenTest, Combustion, GCSEChemistry, AQA, Definition

15. How does the result of the oxygen test differ from testing an inert gas like nitrogen?

In the oxygen test, a glowing splint relights. If tested with an inert gas like nitrogen, the glowing splint would simply extinguish because nitrogen does not support combustion.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The discovery of oxygen is often credited to Joseph Priestley in 1774, though Carl Wilhelm Scheele independently discovered it earlier. Priestley famously conducted experiments where he heated 'red calx of mercury' (mercury(II) oxide) and found it produced a gas in which a candle burned brighter and a mouse lived longer. He called it 'dephlogisticated air'. Antoine Lavoisier later gave it the name 'oxygen' (meaning 'acid-former'), recognizing its role in combustion and acid formation.

⚖️ Ethics

Oxygen is critical for life and widely used in medicine (oxygen therapy, life support). Ethical considerations arise regarding its equitable distribution during medical crises, the cost of medical oxygen, and safety regulations for its handling in hospitals and industries, as concentrated oxygen environments significantly increase fire risk.

🚀 Advanced Theory

At a higher level, the glowing splint test can be understood in terms of reaction kinetics. The combustion of the wooden splint is an exothermic reaction. In a normal atmosphere (21% O₂), the rate of reaction is sufficient to maintain a glowing ember. In pure oxygen (100% O₂), the increased concentration of reactant (O₂) significantly increases the frequency of effective collisions between oxygen molecules and the carbon atoms in the splint. This rapid increase in reaction rate causes a sharp rise in temperature and energy release, quickly reaching the ignition temperature for the rest of the splint and causing it to burst into flame. This highlights the relationship between reactant concentration and reaction rate.

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

The test for oxygen is typically a quick confirmation test after gas collection. Prepare a glowing splint by lighting a wooden splint and then blowing out the flame, ensuring only a red-hot ember remains. Hold the test tube containing the gas firmly, and quickly insert the glowing splint into the gas. Observe immediately. Safety: Always wear eye protection. Ensure the area is clear of flammable materials when lighting the splint.

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Fire hazard: A glowing splint is hot and can ignite flammable materials. Oxygen itself increases fire risk.
  • 🛑 Eye protection: Essential when handling flames and gas samples.
  • 🛑 Hot glassware: Test tubes might become warm during gas collection or if exothermic reactions occur.

  • Wooden splints
  • Bunsen burner
  • Test tubes (with stopper or bung if collecting gas)
  • Safety goggles
