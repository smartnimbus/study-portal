🤜 In a Nutshell

The VSEPR theory is a fundamental model used to predict molecular shapes and bond angles by considering the repulsion between electron pairs (bonding and lone pairs) around a central atom. Lone pairs exert greater repulsion, distorting ideal geometries and compressing bond angles (e.g., from 109.5° in tetrahedral CH₄ to 107° in pyramidal NH₃ and 104.5° in bent H₂O). Mastering VSEPR is crucial for understanding molecular properties and reactivity.

🧠 Conceptual Understanding

The shape of a molecule or ion is crucial to its properties and reactivity. This topic introduces you to the Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) theory, a simple yet powerful model used to predict the three-dimensional arrangement of atoms around a central atom. The core idea is that electron pairs, whether they are involved in bonding (bonded pairs) or not (lone pairs), repel each other and will arrange themselves as far apart as possible to minimise these repulsions. Lone pairs of electrons exert a greater repulsive force than bonded pairs, which means they 'squash' the bonded pairs closer together, leading to deviations from ideal bond angles. By counting the total number of electron domains (each lone pair or bond, regardless of multiplicity, counts as one domain) around the central atom, we can predict the electron geometry. Then, by considering only the bonded pairs, we can determine the molecular geometry and approximate bond angles for common structures like linear, trigonal planar, tetrahedral, pyramidal, and non-linear.

📌 Core Theory

  • Molecular shapes are determined by the repulsion between electron pairs in the valence shell of the central atom.
  • The VSEPR theory states that electron pairs arrange themselves to minimise repulsion, resulting in maximum separation.
  • Electron pairs can be either bonding pairs (involved in a covalent bond) or lone pairs (non-bonding electron pairs).
  • Each bond (single, double, or triple) counts as one 'electron domain' or 'region of electron density'. Each lone pair also counts as one electron domain.
  • The total number of electron domains around the central atom determines the electron geometry (e.g., 2 domains = linear, 3 = trigonal planar, 4 = tetrahedral).
  • Lone pairs repel more strongly than bonding pairs (lone pair-lone pair > lone pair-bonding pair > bonding pair-bonding pair).
  • Increased lone pair repulsion causes bond angles to be smaller than in structures with only bonding pairs.
  • Molecular geometry describes the arrangement of atoms, while electron geometry describes the arrangement of electron domains.
  • Common shapes to predict include linear (180°), trigonal planar (120°), tetrahedral (109.5°), pyramidal (approx. 107°), non-linear (approx. 104.5°), and octahedral (90°).
  • Examples for specific bond angles: CH₄ (tetrahedral, 109.5°), NH₃ (pyramidal, 107°), H₂O (non-linear, 104.5°).

🔬 Detailed Explanation

The VSEPR theory provides a straightforward method for predicting molecular shapes. First, you need to draw the Lewis structure of the molecule or ion to identify the central atom and all its valence electrons. Next, count the total number of electron domains around the central atom. Each single, double, or triple bond counts as one bonding domain, and each lone pair counts as one non-bonding domain. Once you have the total number of electron domains, you can determine the electron geometry: 2 domains lead to a linear arrangement, 3 to trigonal planar, 4 to tetrahedral, 5 to trigonal bipyramidal (though not explicitly required at this level), and 6 to octahedral. The electron domains will naturally position themselves as far apart as possible in 3D space. Finally, to determine the molecular geometry, you consider only the positions of the atoms (i.e., the bonded pairs), taking into account that lone pairs exert greater repulsive forces. This increased repulsion 'pushes' bonded pairs closer together, reducing the bond angles from the ideal electron geometry angles. For example, methane (CH₄) has four bonding pairs and no lone pairs, leading to a perfect tetrahedral shape with 109.5° bond angles. Ammonia (NH₃) also has four electron domains (three bonding pairs and one lone pair), giving it a tetrahedral electron geometry, but the lone pair's stronger repulsion compresses the H-N-H bond angles to approximately 107°, resulting in a pyramidal molecular shape. Water (H₂O) has four electron domains (two bonding pairs and two lone pairs), again leading to a tetrahedral electron geometry, but the two lone pairs' combined repulsion reduces the H-O-H bond angle even further to approximately 104.5°, making it a non-linear or bent molecule.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Predicting the shape and bond angle of Ammonia (NH₃)

Problem: Using VSEPR theory, predict the electron geometry, molecular geometry, and approximate bond angle in an ammonia (NH₃) molecule.

Steps: 1. Draw the Lewis structure for NH₃. Nitrogen (N) is the central atom with 5 valence electrons. Each hydrogen (H) has 1 valence electron. Total valence electrons = 5 + (3 × 1) = 8 electrons. 2. Form single bonds between N and each H, using 6 electrons (3 bonds). This leaves 2 electrons (1 lone pair) on the nitrogen atom. 3. Count the electron domains around the central N atom: 3 bonding pairs (N-H) + 1 lone pair = 4 electron domains. 4. Based on 4 electron domains, the electron geometry is tetrahedral. 5. Considering the molecular geometry, there are 3 bonding pairs and 1 lone pair. The lone pair exerts a greater repulsion than the bonding pairs. 6. This increased repulsion compresses the H-N-H bond angles from the ideal 109.5° (tetrahedral) to approximately 107°. 7. The molecular geometry is pyramidal.

Answer: Electron geometry: Tetrahedral. Molecular geometry: Pyramidal. Bond angle: Approximately 107°.

Teacher's Tip

Always start with the Lewis structure to correctly identify bonding and lone pairs. Don't forget to count lone pairs when determining electron domains.

2. Predicting the shape and bond angle of Water (H₂O)

Problem: Using VSEPR theory, predict the electron geometry, molecular geometry, and approximate bond angle in a water (H₂O) molecule.

Steps: 1. Draw the Lewis structure for H₂O. Oxygen (O) is the central atom with 6 valence electrons. Each hydrogen (H) has 1 valence electron. Total valence electrons = 6 + (2 × 1) = 8 electrons. 2. Form single bonds between O and each H, using 4 electrons (2 bonds). This leaves 4 electrons (2 lone pairs) on the oxygen atom. 3. Count the electron domains around the central O atom: 2 bonding pairs (O-H) + 2 lone pairs = 4 electron domains. 4. Based on 4 electron domains, the electron geometry is tetrahedral. 5. Considering the molecular geometry, there are 2 bonding pairs and 2 lone pairs. The two lone pairs exert significant repulsion. 6. This repulsion compresses the H-O-H bond angles even more than in NH₃, from the ideal 109.5° (tetrahedral) to approximately 104.5°. 7. The molecular geometry is non-linear (or bent).

Answer: Electron geometry: Tetrahedral. Molecular geometry: Non-linear (Bent). Bond angle: Approximately 104.5°.

Teacher's Tip

Remember that two lone pairs cause even greater compression of bond angles than one lone pair.

3. Predicting the shape of Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)

Problem: Using VSEPR theory, predict the electron geometry and molecular geometry of a carbon dioxide (CO₂) molecule.

Steps: 1. Draw the Lewis structure for CO₂. Carbon (C) is the central atom with 4 valence electrons. Each oxygen (O) has 6 valence electrons. Total valence electrons = 4 + (2 × 6) = 16 electrons. 2. Form double bonds between C and each O (C=O), using 8 electrons (2 double bonds). Distribute remaining electrons to oxygen atoms to satisfy octets. Each oxygen gets 2 lone pairs. 3. Count the electron domains around the central C atom: Each double bond counts as one electron domain. So, 2 bonding domains (C=O) + 0 lone pairs = 2 electron domains. 4. Based on 2 electron domains, the electron geometry is linear. 5. With 2 bonding pairs and 0 lone pairs, the molecular geometry is also linear. 6. The O=C=O bond angle is 180°.

Answer: Electron geometry: Linear. Molecular geometry: Linear. Bond angle: 180°.

Teacher's Tip

Double and triple bonds are treated as a single electron domain for VSEPR theory.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Drug Design: The 3D shape of a drug molecule is critical for its ability to bind to specific receptors in the body, which dictates its effectiveness and side effects. VSEPR helps predict these shapes.
  • Protein Structure and Function: Proteins fold into precise 3D shapes, and understanding the geometry around individual atoms (like nitrogen or carbon) is fundamental to understanding how proteins interact and function in biological processes.
  • Material Science: The properties of polymers and other materials (e.g., strength, flexibility) are directly influenced by the shapes of the repeating molecular units and how they pack together.
  • Taste and Smell: Our senses of taste and smell rely on specific molecules fitting into receptor sites. The molecular shape is paramount for this 'lock and key' mechanism, determining if a substance tastes sweet or smells pungent.
  • Catalysis: Catalysts work by providing a surface or active site with a specific shape that allows reactant molecules to bind efficiently, facilitating a chemical reaction.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing electron geometry with molecular geometry. Electron geometry includes lone pairs, while molecular geometry describes only the arrangement of atoms.
  • ❌ Incorrectly counting electron domains, especially with multiple bonds (a double or triple bond counts as ONE domain).
  • ❌ Forgetting that lone pairs occupy space and exert greater repulsion than bonding pairs, leading to smaller bond angles.
  • ❌ Assuming all molecules with a central atom and four substituents are tetrahedral (e.g., NH₃ and H₂O are not perfectly tetrahedral due to lone pairs).
  • ❌ Drawing 2D structures and not visualising the 3D repulsion, which is key to VSEPR.

Consider the molecule ozone (O₃). Explain its molecular geometry and approximate bond angle, identifying the central atom.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

Q9. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

A molecule has the formula AB₃. It has a dipole moment, indicating it is polar. Given this information, what are the possible molecular geometries for AB₃, and which would be non-polar?

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: For a molecule with the formula AB₃, there are two common possible molecular geometries: trigonal planar (if there are 3 bonding pairs and 0 lone pairs on the central atom A) and trigonal pyramidal (if there are 3 bonding pairs and 1 lone pair on the central atom A). A trigonal planar AB₃ molecule, like BF₃, would be non-polar because its symmetrical structure allows the individual bond dipoles to cancel each other out. However, a trigonal pyramidal AB₃ molecule, like NH₃, is polar because its asymmetrical shape, combined with the presence of a lone pair (which contributes to the dipole) and non-cancelling bond dipoles, results in a net dipole moment. Since the question states the molecule has a dipole moment, it must be trigonal pyramidal.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Possible geometries for AB₃ with 3 bonding pairs are trigonal planar (if 0 lone pairs) or trigonal pyramidal (if 1 lone pair). (2 marks)
  • ✅ Trigonal planar AB₃ (e.g., BF₃) would be non-polar because the bond dipoles cancel out due to symmetry. (1 mark)
  • ✅ Trigonal pyramidal AB₃ (e.g., NH₃) would be polar because the bond dipoles do not cancel, and the lone pair also contributes to overall polarity, leading to a net dipole moment. (1 mark)

🧩 Quick Quiz (MCQ)

1. Which statement best explains why the bond angle in ammonia (NH3) is smaller than the bond angle in methane (CH4)? A) Nitrogen is more electronegative than carbon. B) Ammonia has a lone pair of electrons which repels bonding pairs more strongly than bonding pairs repel each other. C) Methane has four bonding pairs, leading to greater repulsion. D) Hydrogen atoms in ammonia are smaller than in methane.

Answer

B) Ammonia has a lone pair of electrons which repels bonding pairs more strongly than bonding pairs repel each other. Lone pairs of electrons exert a greater repulsive force than bonding pairs. Ammonia has one lone pair and three bonding pairs, whereas methane has four bonding pairs. The stronger lone pair-bonding pair repulsion in ammonia reduces its bond angle from the ideal tetrahedral angle of 109.5° to approximately 107°.

2. A central atom surrounded by four electron pairs, two of which are lone pairs, will have which molecular shape? A) Tetrahedral B) Pyramidal C) Non-linear (bent) D) Trigonal planar

Answer

C) Non-linear (bent) Two lone pairs and two bonding pairs result in four electron domains around the central atom. Due to the stronger repulsion from the lone pairs, the bonding pairs are pushed closer together, resulting in a non-linear (bent) molecular shape.

3. What is the approximate bond angle in a water molecule (H2O)? A) 180° B) 120° C) 109.5° D) 104.5°

Answer

D) 104.5° Water has two bonding pairs and two lone pairs around the central oxygen atom. The two lone pairs exert stronger repulsion than the bonding pairs, reducing the ideal tetrahedral angle (109.5°) further to approximately 104.5°.

4. Which of the following molecules has a pyramidal shape? A) CO2 B) NH3 C) CH4 D) BF3

Answer

B) NH3 Ammonia (NH3) has three bonding pairs and one lone pair of electrons around its central nitrogen atom. The lone pair repels the bonding pairs more strongly than bonding pairs repel each other, distorting the tetrahedral electron geometry into a pyramidal molecular shape.

5. An ion with a central atom surrounded by six bonding pairs of electrons and no lone pairs will have which molecular shape? A) Tetrahedral B) Trigonal bipyramidal C) Octahedral D) Square planar

Answer

C) Octahedral Six bonding pairs of electrons around a central atom will arrange themselves to minimise repulsion, leading to an octahedral geometry.

6. In terms of electron pair repulsion, what is the correct order from strongest to weakest repulsion? A) Lone pair-bonding pair > Lone pair-lone pair > Bonding pair-bonding pair B) Bonding pair-bonding pair > Lone pair-bonding pair > Lone pair-lone pair C) Lone pair-lone pair > Lone pair-bonding pair > Bonding pair-bonding pair D) Lone pair-lone pair > Bonding pair-bonding pair > Lone pair-bonding pair

Answer

C) Lone pair-lone pair > Lone pair-bonding pair > Bonding pair-bonding pair The order of repulsion strength is: lone pair-lone pair > lone pair-bonding pair > bonding pair-bonding pair. This explains why lone pairs cause greater distortions to bond angles.

7. What is the approximate bond angle in a molecule with a trigonal planar shape, such as BF3? A) 90° B) 109.5° C) 120° D) 180°

Answer

C) 120° A trigonal planar molecule has three bonding pairs of electrons around the central atom and no lone pairs. These three electron pairs arrange themselves as far apart as possible in a plane, resulting in bond angles of 120°.

8. Which of the following molecules has a linear shape? A) H2O B) SO2 C) CO2 D) NH3

Answer

C) CO2 Carbon dioxide (CO2) has two double bonds around the central carbon atom. These two electron domains repel each other to be as far apart as possible, resulting in a linear arrangement with a 180° bond angle.

9. Which statement is true for a molecule with a tetrahedral shape, like methane (CH4)? A) It has three bonding pairs and one lone pair. B) All bond angles are approximately 109.5°. C) It has a central atom surrounded by two bonding pairs. D) Its molecular geometry is planar.

Answer

B) All bond angles are approximately 109.5°. A tetrahedral molecule has four bonding pairs of electrons around the central atom, with no lone pairs. These four pairs repel each other equally, resulting in bond angles of approximately 109.5° and a 3D tetrahedral structure.

10. A molecule with a central atom having two bonding pairs and one lone pair of electrons will exhibit which molecular shape? A) Linear B) Trigonal planar C) Non-linear (bent) D) Pyramidal

Answer

C) Non-linear (bent) With three electron domains (two bonding pairs and one lone pair), the electron geometry is trigonal planar. However, the lone pair occupies more space and repels the bonding pairs, resulting in a non-linear (bent) molecular shape. An example is SO2.

1. What is electron pair repulsion theory (VSEPR)?

It states that electron pairs around a central atom repel each other, arranging themselves as far apart as possible to minimize repulsion, thus determining the molecule's shape.

All electrons carry a negative charge. Like charges repel, so the electron pairs in the valence shell of a central atom push away from each other to achieve maximum separation.

Tags: Repulsion, Electrons, Basics

A lone pair is a pair of valence electrons that are not shared with another atom in a covalent bond. They are localized on the central atom.

Tags: Lone Pair, Definition, Electrons

Lone pairs repel more strongly than bonded pairs because they are held closer to the central atom and are not shared between two nuclei. This increased repulsion compresses bond angles.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The VSEPR model was developed by Nevil Sidgwick and Herbert Powell in 1940 and later refined by Ronald Gillespie and Ronald Nyholm in 1957. Gillespie, in particular, was instrumental in popularizing the theory and highlighting the significance of lone pairs in determining molecular shapes, challenging the then-dominant valence bond theory for its simplicity and predictive power regarding geometry. Their work provided a remarkably simple and accurate method for predicting molecular geometries that is still widely used in introductory chemistry courses today.

⚖️ Ethics

While VSEPR theory itself is a fundamental concept in pure chemistry and doesn't directly raise ethical concerns, the understanding of molecular shape derived from it has profound ethical implications in applied fields. For example, in pharmaceutical drug development, accurately predicting the 3D shape of potential drug molecules is crucial for ensuring they bind effectively to target proteins (e.g., enzymes, receptors) while minimizing off-target binding that could lead to harmful side effects. Misunderstanding or misapplying molecular shape predictions could lead to ineffective or dangerous drugs. Similarly, in toxicology, the shape of a pollutant molecule can dictate how it interacts with biological systems, affecting its toxicity and persistence in the environment.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond VSEPR, more advanced theories like Hybridisation Theory provide a quantum mechanical explanation for molecular shapes and bond angles. Hybridisation involves the mixing of atomic orbitals (s, p, d) on the central atom to form new hybrid orbitals that are degenerate (equal in energy) and spatially oriented to minimize repulsion. For example, in methane (CH₄), the carbon atom undergoes sp³ hybridisation, forming four equivalent sp³ hybrid orbitals that point towards the corners of a tetrahedron, explaining the 109.5° bond angles. In ethene (C₂H₄), carbon is sp² hybridised, leading to trigonal planar geometry around each carbon. The concept of formal charge and electronegativity can also be used to refine bond angle predictions slightly, as they influence the 'size' or electron density of bonding pairs, affecting repulsion strengths. For molecules with more than 4 electron domains, such as those involving expanded octets (e.g., PCl₅, SF₆), d-orbitals are often involved in hybridisation, leading to more complex geometries like trigonal bipyramidal (sp³d) and octahedral (sp³d²).
