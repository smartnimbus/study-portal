🤜 In a Nutshell

Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons featuring a reactive carbon-carbon double bond, enabling them to undergo characteristic addition reactions like hydrogenation, hydration, and halogenation. This reactivity is key to their industrial uses, such as plastic production and margarine manufacturing, and allows for the bromine water test to distinguish them from alkanes, which only undergo substitution.

🧠 Conceptual Understanding

Alkenes are a family of hydrocarbons that contain at least one carbon-carbon double bond (C=C). This double bond makes them 'unsaturated', meaning they have the capacity to form more bonds. Unlike alkanes, which are saturated and relatively unreactive, the C=C double bond in alkenes is a region of high electron density, making it susceptible to attack by other molecules. This allows alkenes to undergo 'addition reactions', where atoms are added across the double bond, breaking it and forming a single C-C bond, along with two new single bonds. This reactivity is crucial for many industrial processes, such as making plastics or converting vegetable oils into margarine. They also undergo combustion like other hydrocarbons, but often incompletely due to their higher carbon content.

📌 Core Theory

  • Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons containing at least one carbon-carbon double bond (C=C).
  • The general formula for alkenes with one double bond is C {n}H .
  • The carbon-carbon double bond is a functional group, responsible for the characteristic reactions of alkenes.
  • Alkenes undergo combustion reactions with oxygen, producing carbon dioxide and water if combustion is complete, or carbon monoxide/soot if incomplete.
  • Due to their higher carbon-to-hydrogen ratio compared to alkanes, alkenes tend to burn with smoky flames, indicating incomplete combustion.
  • The characteristic reactions of alkenes are addition reactions, where the C=C double bond breaks, and new atoms are added to each carbon atom of the original double bond.
  • Common addition reactions include reaction with hydrogen (hydrogenation), steam (hydration), and halogens (halogenation).
  • Hydrogenation converts an alkene into an alkane; hydration converts an alkene into an alcohol; halogenation converts an alkene into a haloalkane.
  • The test for unsaturation uses bromine water: an alkene will decolourise orange-brown bromine water as it undergoes an addition reaction with bromine.
  • The first four alkenes are ethene (C {2}H ), propene (C {3}H ), but-1-ene (C {4}H ), and pent-1-ene (C {5}H ).

🔬 Detailed Explanation

Alkenes are fundamentally defined by the presence of a carbon-carbon double bond. This double bond consists of one strong sigma (σ) bond and one weaker pi (π) bond. It's the readily broken pi bond that makes alkenes far more reactive than alkanes. In addition reactions, this pi bond 'opens up', allowing two new single bonds to form with incoming atoms, effectively converting the C=C double bond into a C-C single bond. For instance, in hydrogenation, hydrogen atoms add across the double bond to form an alkane. In hydration, water (as steam) adds across the double bond to form an alcohol. In halogenation, halogen atoms (like bromine or chlorine) add across the double bond to form a dihaloalkane. These reactions do not produce any other small molecules, hence the term 'addition'. The conditions for these reactions vary, but all fundamentally rely on the reactivity of the double bond. Alkenes also undergo combustion, but due to their relatively higher carbon content, they often undergo incomplete combustion, producing a sooty flame and toxic carbon monoxide, alongside carbon dioxide and water.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Drawing the Product of Ethene and Bromine Reaction

Problem: Draw the displayed structural formula of the product formed when ethene reacts with bromine.

Steps: 1. Start with the displayed structural formula of ethene: H {2}C=CH . 2. Identify the carbon-carbon double bond. 3. Remember that bromine (Br _{2}) is added across the double bond, breaking it. 4. Change the double bond to a single bond between the two carbon atoms. 5. Add one bromine atom to each of the two carbon atoms that were part of the double bond. 6. Ensure each carbon atom still has four bonds in total.

Answer: H {2}BrC-CBrH (1,2-dibromoethane)

Teacher's Tip

Always check that each carbon atom in your final structure has exactly four bonds. For addition reactions, the carbons involved in the original double bond are where the new atoms attach.

2. Identifying an Alkene Using Bromine Water

Problem: A student has two unknown colourless liquid hydrocarbons, Sample A and Sample B. When a few drops of orange-brown bromine water are added to Sample A, the colour disappears. When added to Sample B, the colour persists. Identify which sample is an alkene and explain your reasoning.

Steps: 1. Recall the chemical test for unsaturation: bromine water decolourises in the presence of an alkene. 2. Apply this knowledge to Sample A: the decolourisation indicates the presence of a carbon-carbon double bond. 3. Conclude that Sample A is an alkene because it undergoes an addition reaction with bromine, removing the coloured bromine molecules from solution. 4. Apply this knowledge to Sample B: the persistent colour indicates the absence of a carbon-carbon double bond. 5. Conclude that Sample B is likely an alkane, as alkanes do not readily react with bromine water under normal conditions.

Answer: Sample A is an alkene. This is because alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons with a C=C double bond. They react with bromine via an addition reaction, causing the orange-brown bromine colour to disappear as the bromine is used up.

Teacher's Tip

When explaining observations in chemistry, always link the observation (e.g., colour change) to the chemical property (e.g., presence of a double bond) and the type of reaction occurring (e.g., addition).

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Margarine production: Vegetable oils (which contain unsaturated fatty acids, i.e., alkenes) are hydrogenated to make them solid at room temperature, producing margarine.
  • Plastic manufacturing: Alkenes, particularly ethene and propene, are used as monomers to produce polymers like poly(ethene) (polythene) and poly(propene) (polypropylene) through addition polymerisation.
  • Ripening of fruits: Ethene (C {2}H ) is a natural plant hormone that promotes the ripening of fruits. It's sometimes used commercially to ripen fruits evenly.
  • Ethanol production: Ethene is hydrated with steam in the presence of a catalyst to produce ethanol, an important industrial solvent and fuel.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing addition reactions with substitution reactions: Addition reactions only occur with unsaturated compounds and add atoms, while substitution reactions occur with saturated compounds and replace atoms.
  • ❌ Forgetting that halogens (like bromine and chlorine) exist as diatomic molecules (Br

{2}, Cl ) in their elemental form, and two halogen atoms add to the alkene.

  • ❌ Assuming alkenes always undergo complete combustion; often they undergo incomplete combustion due to their high carbon content.
  • ❌ Incorrectly drawing displayed formulae, especially failing to ensure each carbon has four bonds.
  • ❌ Not knowing the specific conditions required for different addition reactions (e.g., catalyst for hydrogenation).
Memory Anchor

Alkenes are 'greedy' with their double bond - they want to ADD more atoms, unlike 'satisfied' alkanes!

Q9. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

Describe the difference between an addition reaction and a substitution reaction in organic chemistry, explaining why alkenes undergo addition but alkanes undergo substitution.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: In organic chemistry, an addition reaction is one where atoms are added across a carbon-carbon double (or triple) bond, breaking the multiple bond and forming a single bond. All reactant atoms are incorporated into a single product molecule, with nothing else formed. A substitution reaction, however, involves one atom or group of atoms being replaced by another atom or group, typically forming two products.

Alkenes undergo addition reactions because they possess a carbon-carbon double bond. This double bond is a region of high electron density and contains a relatively weak pi bond that can easily break, providing a site for new atoms to attach. Alkanes, on the other hand, are saturated hydrocarbons, meaning they only have strong carbon-carbon and carbon-hydrogen single bonds. These single bonds are difficult to break, so alkanes do not readily undergo addition reactions. Instead, under specific conditions (like UV light with halogens), they can undergo substitution reactions, where a hydrogen atom is replaced by a halogen atom.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Addition reaction: Atoms are added across a double (or triple) bond, breaking it and forming a single bond, with no atoms lost (all reactants incorporated into one product).
  • ✅ Substitution reaction: An atom or group of atoms is replaced by another atom or group of atoms, usually forming two products.
  • ✅ Alkenes have a C=C double bond (unsaturated).
  • ✅ The C=C double bond is a region of high electron density / contains a weaker pi bond that is easily broken, allowing addition.
  • ✅ Alkanes have only C-C and C-H single bonds (saturated).
  • ✅ These single bonds are strong and unreactive, so breaking them requires more energy, making substitution (replacement) more favourable under specific conditions (e.g., UV light with halogens) than adding across them.

📋 Strategy: Define each reaction type first. Then, explain the structural reasons for the different reactivities of alkenes and alkanes.

Key Facts
  • 📌 Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons containing at least one carbon-carbon double bond (C=C).
  • 📌 Alkenes undergo addition reactions where atoms are added across the C=C double bond, converting it to a single C-C bond.
  • 📌 Alkenes burn in air with smoky flames due to incomplete combustion, producing carbon and carbon monoxide alongside carbon dioxide and water.

The presence of the carbon-carbon double bond dictates the characteristic addition reactions and incomplete combustion properties of alkenes.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The discovery of alkenes dates back to the early 19th century, with ethene (then called 'olefiant gas') being isolated. Its unique reactivity was observed early on. The industrial significance of hydrogenation was pioneered by Paul Sabatier and Jean-Baptiste Senderens in the late 19th century, who discovered that nickel could catalyse the addition of hydrogen to unsaturated compounds, a discovery for which Sabatier won the Nobel Prize. The understanding of the carbon-carbon double bond's structure and its role in addition reactions evolved throughout the 20th century with advancements in organic chemistry.

⚖️ Ethics

The widespread use of alkenes in plastic production presents significant ethical and environmental challenges. While plastics are versatile, their persistence in the environment leads to pollution (microplastics, waste accumulation) and harm to ecosystems. The reliance on fossil fuels as the primary source for alkenes also contributes to climate change and raises questions about sustainability. Additionally, the incomplete combustion of alkenes can produce harmful pollutants like carbon monoxide and soot, impacting air quality and human health.

🚀 Advanced Theory

At A-level, the mechanism of addition reactions, particularly electrophilic addition, is studied using 'curly arrows' to show electron movement. The pi bond of the alkene acts as a nucleophile (electron donor) and attacks an electrophile (electron acceptor), such as the slightly positive end of a bromine molecule or a proton from an acid. This forms a carbocation intermediate, which then rapidly reacts with a nucleophile (e.g., Br ^{-} or H _{2}O) to complete the addition. This detailed mechanism explains regioselectivity (Markovnikov's rule) and stereochemistry, which are beyond GCSE scope but build directly on the idea of the double bond's reactivity.

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. Addition Reaction General Principle: The carbon-carbon double bond (C=C) is a region of high electron density due to the presence of a sigma bond and a pi bond. The pi bond is weaker and more accessible to attacking reagents. In an addition reaction, the pi bond breaks, and the two carbon atoms that were part of the double bond each form a new single bond with an incoming atom or group of atoms. The overall result is that the molecule becomes saturated (or less unsaturated) and only one product is formed.
  2. Hydrogenation: A hydrogen molecule (H _{2}) adds across the C=C double bond. This typically occurs in the presence of a nickel catalyst and heat. The catalyst weakens the H-H bond and helps break the C=C bond, allowing one hydrogen atom to bond to each carbon of the original double bond, converting the alkene into an alkane.
  3. Halogenation: A halogen molecule (e.g., Br {2}, Cl ) adds across the C=C double bond. This reaction usually proceeds readily at room temperature and does not require a catalyst. One halogen atom adds to each carbon atom of the original double bond, forming a dihaloalkane (e.g., 1,2-dibromoethane from ethene and bromine).
  4. Hydration: A water molecule (H _{2}O, typically as steam) adds across the C=C double bond. This industrial process requires specific conditions: a phosphoric acid catalyst, high temperature, and high pressure. The H atom from water adds to one carbon and the OH group adds to the other carbon of the original double bond, forming an alcohol (e.g., ethanol from ethene and steam).

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Combustion: Requires oxygen (air). Complete combustion requires excess oxygen; incomplete combustion occurs with limited oxygen.
  • Hydrogenation: Hydrogen gas (H _{2}), Nickel (Ni) catalyst, and heat (e.g., 150°C).
  • Hydration: Steam (gaseous H {2}O), Concentrated phosphoric acid (H PO _{4}) catalyst, High temperature (e.g., 300°C), and High pressure (e.g., 60-70 atmospheres).
  • Halogenation: Halogen (e.g., bromine liquid/aqueous, chlorine gas). Occurs readily at room temperature. Does not require UV light (unlike alkanes).

🔦 Element Spotlight

Ethene (C {2}H ) is the simplest and arguably most important alkene. Its structure consists of two carbon atoms joined by a double bond, with each carbon also bonded to two hydrogen atoms. This C=C bond gives ethene its characteristic reactivity. Ethene is a colourless, sweet-smelling gas at room temperature. It is highly flammable. Industrially, ethene is a crucial feedstock, primarily used to produce poly(ethene) plastic through addition polymerisation, and also for the synthesis of ethanol via hydration. Its role as a plant hormone for fruit ripening highlights its biological significance too.
