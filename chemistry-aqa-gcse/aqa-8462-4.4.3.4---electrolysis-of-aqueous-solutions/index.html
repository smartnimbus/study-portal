🤜 In a Nutshell

Electrolysis of aqueous solutions involves a 'competition' between ions from the dissolved salt and ions from water (H⁺ and OH⁻) at both the cathode and anode. Product prediction relies on the reactivity series (for cations vs. H⁺) and the halide rule (for anions vs. OH⁻). This process is vital for producing important industrial chemicals like chlorine, hydrogen, and sodium hydroxide.

🧠 Conceptual Understanding

When we electrolyse an ionic compound in an aqueous solution, things get a bit more complicated than with molten compounds. That's because water itself contains ions: H⁺ and OH⁻, which are in equilibrium with water molecules (H₂O ⇌ H⁺ + OH⁻). This means that at both the positive anode and the negative cathode, there's a 'competition' between different ions to be discharged. The product formed at each electrode depends on the relative reactivity of the metal ions (at the cathode) and the type of non-metal ions present (at the anode). Understanding this competition is key to predicting the products, which is a common exam requirement.

📌 Core Theory

  • Electrolysis of aqueous solutions involves competition between ions from the dissolved salt and ions from water (H⁺ and OH⁻) at both electrodes.
  • At the negative electrode (cathode), metal ions or hydrogen ions (from water) can be discharged. Hydrogen gas is produced if the metal is more reactive than hydrogen (e.g., Na⁺, K⁺). If the metal is less reactive than hydrogen (e.g., Cu²⁺, Ag⁺), the metal itself is deposited.
  • At the positive electrode (anode), non-metal ions or hydroxide ions (from water) can be discharged. Oxygen gas is produced from hydroxide ions unless halide ions (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) are present in high enough concentration, in which case the halogen gas is produced.
  • The discharge of H⁺ ions at the cathode leads to the formation of H₂ gas: 2H⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → H₂(g).
  • The discharge of OH⁻ ions at the anode leads to the formation of O₂ gas: 4OH⁻(aq) → O₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) + 4e⁻.
  • For halide ions, the halogen is produced: 2X⁻(aq) → X₂(g) + 2e⁻ (where X = Cl, Br, I).
  • Spectator ions remain in solution, causing the pH to change depending on which ions are removed.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

Electrolysis of aqueous solutions requires careful consideration of the species present in the electrolyte. Not only do you have the cation and anion from the dissolved ionic compound (e.g., Na⁺ and Cl⁻ from NaCl), but you also have H⁺ and OH⁻ ions from the dissociation of water. These four types of ions (two cations, two anions) compete at their respective electrodes. At the cathode (negative electrode), positively charged ions are attracted. If the metal ion from the dissolved salt is more reactive than hydrogen (like sodium, potassium, or calcium), hydrogen ions from water will be preferentially discharged, forming hydrogen gas. However, if the metal ion is less reactive than hydrogen (like copper, silver, or lead), the metal ions will gain electrons and deposit as the solid metal. At the anode (positive electrode), negatively charged ions are attracted. Hydroxide ions from water are usually discharged, producing oxygen gas and water, unless halide ions (chloride, bromide, iodide) are present. Halide ions are preferentially discharged over hydroxide ions if they are present in sufficient concentration, producing the respective halogen gas. Sulfate and nitrate ions are rarely discharged and remain as spectator ions. The concentration of halide ions can play a role; for dilute halide solutions, oxygen might still be produced.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Predicting Products for Aqueous Copper(II) Sulfate

Problem: Predict the products at the anode and cathode when aqueous copper(II) sulfate (CuSO₄) is electrolysed using inert electrodes. Write half-equations for the reactions.

Steps: 1. Identify the ions present in the aqueous solution: Cu²⁺, SO₄²⁻, H⁺ (from water), OH⁻ (from water). 2. At the cathode (negative electrode), two positively charged ions compete: Cu²⁺ and H⁺. Copper is less reactive than hydrogen. 3. Therefore, Cu²⁺ ions will gain electrons and be deposited as copper metal. Half-equation: Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s). 4. At the anode (positive electrode), two negatively charged ions compete: SO₄²⁻ and OH⁻. Sulfate ions are not halides. 5. Therefore, OH⁻ ions will lose electrons and be discharged, producing oxygen gas and water. Half-equation: 4OH⁻(aq) → O₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) + 4e⁻. 6. The products are copper metal at the cathode and oxygen gas at the anode.

Answer: Cathode product: Copper metal (Cu). Anode product: Oxygen gas (O₂).

Teacher's Tip

Always list all possible ions (from the salt and from water) first. Then use the reactivity series for the cathode and the halide rule for the anode to determine the products.

2. Predicting Products for Aqueous Sodium Chloride

Problem: Predict the products at the anode and cathode when aqueous sodium chloride (NaCl) is electrolysed using inert electrodes. Explain why these products are formed.

Steps: 1. Identify the ions present: Na⁺, Cl⁻, H⁺ (from water), OH⁻ (from water). 2. At the cathode (negative electrode), Na⁺ and H⁺ ions compete. Sodium is more reactive than hydrogen. 3. Therefore, H⁺ ions will gain electrons to form hydrogen gas. Half-equation: 2H⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → H₂(g). 4. At the anode (positive electrode), Cl⁻ and OH⁻ ions compete. Chloride is a halide ion. 5. Therefore, Cl⁻ ions will lose electrons to form chlorine gas. Half-equation: 2Cl⁻(aq) → Cl₂(g) + 2e⁻. 6. The products are hydrogen gas at the cathode and chlorine gas at the anode.

Answer: Cathode product: Hydrogen gas (H₂). Anode product: Chlorine gas (Cl₂).

Teacher's Tip

Remember the halide rule is crucial for the anode. Chlorine is highly reactive and readily discharged.

Therefore, OH⁻ ions will be discharged, forming oxygen gas and water. Half-equation: 4OH⁻(aq) → O₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) + 4e⁻. 6. The products are hydrogen gas at the cathode and oxygen gas at the anode.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Chlor-alkali Process: The electrolysis of concentrated aqueous sodium chloride (brine) is a major industrial process used to produce chlorine gas (Cl₂), hydrogen gas (H₂), and sodium hydroxide (NaOH), which are all vital chemicals.
  • Purification of Metals: Electrolysis can be used to purify metals like copper. Impure copper acts as the anode, and pure copper as the cathode. Copper ions from the anode dissolve and are deposited as pure copper at the cathode, while more reactive impurities remain in solution and less reactive impurities fall to the bottom as 'anode sludge'.
  • Electroplating: Although often done with specific metal salts, the principle of depositing a less reactive metal onto a surface via electrolysis can involve aqueous solutions.
  • Production of Hydrogen and Oxygen: The electrolysis of water (or dilute aqueous acids/alkalis to increase conductivity) is a method to produce high-purity hydrogen and oxygen gases, particularly relevant for fuel cells or medical applications.

🎯 Exam Strategy

High Yield — Comes Up Every Year
  • ⭐ Predicting the products at both electrodes (cathode and anode) for a given aqueous ionic solution.
  • ⭐ Writing balanced half-equations for the reactions occurring at each electrode.
  • ⭐ Explaining why certain products are formed based on the reactivity series and the presence of halide ions.
  • ⭐ Understanding how the pH of the remaining solution changes as electrolysis proceeds (e.g., increased OH⁻ concentration if H⁺ is removed).
Examiner's Tip

When predicting products, always consider the four possible ions: the cation and anion from the salt, and H⁺ and OH⁻ from water. Use the reactivity series for cations (less reactive metal or hydrogen wins) and the halide rule for anions (halide wins over hydroxide).

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing the products of electrolysis of molten salts with aqueous solutions (e.g., thinking sodium metal will form from aqueous NaCl).
  • ❌ Forgetting the role of water and its ions (H⁺ and OH⁻) in the competition at the electrodes.
  • ❌ Not knowing the reactivity series for predicting cathode products or the halide rule for anode products.
  • ❌ Incorrectly writing half-equations, especially for oxygen or hydrogen production, often missing water or incorrectly balancing electrons.
  • ❌ Assuming that sulfate or nitrate ions will be discharged at the anode.
Memory Anchor

Think of the electrodes as a 'battleground' for ions. At the cathode, it's a 'reactivity rumble' - the less reactive positive ion gets reduced. At the anode, it's 'halide or oxygen' - if there's a halide, it gets oxidised, otherwise, oxygen does.

Q10. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

If a student used copper electrodes instead of inert electrodes to electrolyse aqueous copper(II) sulfate, what would happen at the anode and how would this affect the concentration of the copper(II) sulfate solution over time?

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: If copper electrodes were used instead of inert electrodes, the anode would not produce oxygen gas. Instead, the copper anode itself would react. Copper atoms from the anode would lose electrons (be oxidised) to form copper(II) ions (Cu²⁺), which would dissolve into the solution (Cu(s) → Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻). This setup is effectively copper refining. At the cathode, Cu²⁺ ions from the solution would still be deposited as copper metal. However, because new Cu²⁺ ions are constantly entering the solution from the dissolving anode at roughly the same rate as they are removed at the cathode, the concentration of copper(II) sulfate in the solution would remain relatively constant over time.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ At the anode, the copper electrode would react/dissolve (1 mark)
  • ✅ Cu atoms from the anode would lose electrons to form Cu²⁺ ions (Cu(s) → Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻) (1 mark)
  • ✅ The concentration of copper(II) sulfate solution would remain relatively constant (1 mark)
  • ✅ Because Cu²⁺ ions are removed at the cathode and replaced at the anode (1 mark)

🧩 Quick Quiz (MCQ)

1. During the electrolysis of an aqueous solution using inert electrodes, which statement correctly describes the products formed? A) A) A metal less reactive than hydrogen is always produced at the cathode. B) B) Oxygen is always produced at the anode. C) C) If halide ions are present, the halogen is produced at the anode. D) D) Hydrogen is always produced at the cathode.

Answer

C) C) If halide ions are present, the halogen is produced at the anode. At the anode, if halide ions (Cl-, Br-, I-) are present, they are preferentially discharged over hydroxide ions (from water) to produce the corresponding halogen. Otherwise, oxygen is produced from hydroxide ions.

2. What are the products at the cathode and anode, respectively, during the electrolysis of aqueous sodium chloride using inert electrodes? A) A) Sodium and chlorine B) B) Hydrogen and chlorine C) C) Sodium and oxygen D) D) Hydrogen and oxygen

Answer

B) B) Hydrogen and chlorine At the cathode, sodium is more reactive than hydrogen, so hydrogen gas is produced. At the anode, chloride ions are halide ions, so chlorine gas is produced in preference to oxygen.

3. When aqueous copper(II) sulfate is electrolysed using inert electrodes, what are the products at the cathode and anode? A) A) Copper and sulfur dioxide B) B) Hydrogen and oxygen C) C) Copper and oxygen D) D) Hydrogen and sulfur

Answer

C) C) Copper and oxygen At the cathode, copper is less reactive than hydrogen, so copper metal is deposited. At the anode, sulfate ions are not halide ions, so hydroxide ions from water are discharged to produce oxygen gas.

4. Which products would you expect to form at the electrodes during the electrolysis of aqueous potassium bromide? A) A) Potassium and bromine B) B) Hydrogen and bromine C) C) Potassium and oxygen D) D) Hydrogen and oxygen

Answer

B) B) Hydrogen and bromine At the cathode, potassium is more reactive than hydrogen, so hydrogen gas is produced. At the anode, bromide ions are halide ions, so bromine liquid/gas is produced.

5. In the electrolysis of aqueous zinc chloride, why is hydrogen gas produced at the cathode instead of zinc metal? A) A) Zinc ions are attracted to the anode. B) B) Hydrogen is more reactive than zinc. C) C) Zinc is more reactive than hydrogen. D) D) Chloride ions prevent zinc from being discharged.

Answer

C) C) Zinc is more reactive than hydrogen. At the cathode, the metal (zinc) is more reactive than hydrogen, so hydrogen ions from water are preferentially discharged to form hydrogen gas.

6. During the electrolysis of aqueous lithium nitrate, oxygen gas is produced at the anode. Which is the best explanation for this observation? A) A) Nitrate ions are oxidised to oxygen. B) B) Lithium ions are oxidised to oxygen. C) C) Hydroxide ions from water are preferentially discharged. D) D) Water itself is reduced to oxygen.

Answer

C) C) Hydroxide ions from water are preferentially discharged. Nitrate ions are not halide ions. Therefore, at the anode, hydroxide ions (OH-) from the dissociation of water are preferentially discharged over nitrate ions to produce oxygen gas and water.

7. In an aqueous solution, water molecules break down. What ions are produced from water that can be discharged at the electrodes during electrolysis? A) A) H+ and O2- B) B) H+ and OH- C) C) H- and O2+ D) D) O2- and OH-

Answer

B) B) H+ and OH- Water molecules dissociate to a small extent into hydrogen ions (H+) and hydroxide ions (OH-), which can then be attracted to and discharged at the respective electrodes.

8. Predict the products at the cathode and anode during the electrolysis of aqueous silver nitrate using inert electrodes. A) A) Silver and oxygen B) B) Hydrogen and nitrogen dioxide C) C) Silver and hydrogen D) D) Hydrogen and oxygen

A) A) Silver and oxygen At the cathode, silver is less reactive than hydrogen, so silver metal is deposited. At the anode, nitrate ions are not halide ions, so hydroxide ions from water are discharged to produce oxygen gas.

9. Which of the following aqueous solutions would produce a metal at the cathode during electrolysis with inert electrodes? A) A) Sodium chloride B) B) Potassium iodide C) C) Lead(II) nitrate D) D) Magnesium sulfate

Answer

C) C) Lead(II) nitrate Lead is less reactive than hydrogen, so lead ions (Pb2+) would be preferentially discharged at the cathode to form lead metal. Sodium, potassium, and magnesium are all more reactive than hydrogen.

10. At the anode, which of these aqueous solutions would produce a halogen gas when electrolysed with inert electrodes? A) A) Copper(II) sulfate B) B) Potassium nitrate C) C) Lithium bromide D) D) Sodium hydroxide

Answer

C) C) Lithium bromide Bromide ions (Br-) are halide ions, so they would be discharged at the anode to produce bromine gas. Sulfate, nitrate, and hydroxide ions would result in the production of oxygen gas.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The foundations of electrolysis were laid by Michael Faraday in the early 19th century. He coined many of the terms still used today, like 'electrode', 'anode', 'cathode', 'ion', and 'electrolyte'. His meticulous experimental work led to Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis, which quantitatively relate the amount of substance produced to the charge passed. While Faraday's initial work focused on basic principles, the application to aqueous solutions, and the complexities introduced by water, became a significant area of later study and industrial development, particularly with the chlor-alkali process.

⚖️ Ethics

The industrial applications of aqueous electrolysis, such as the chlor-alkali process, raise significant ethical and environmental concerns. The production of chlorine, for instance, is vital but also produces a highly toxic gas that requires careful handling and disposal to prevent environmental contamination and health risks to workers. The energy intensity of electrolysis processes also contributes to discussions around sustainable energy sources and the carbon footprint of chemical manufacturing. Balancing the demand for essential chemicals with environmental protection and worker safety is an ongoing ethical challenge.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond GCSE, the prediction of products in aqueous electrolysis can be refined using standard electrode potentials (E⁰ values). These values quantify the tendency of a species to gain electrons (be reduced). At the cathode, the species with the more positive (or less negative) E⁰ value will be reduced. At the anode, the species with the more negative (or less positive) E⁰ value will be oxidised. However, factors like overpotential (the extra voltage needed to overcome activation energy barriers for gas evolution), concentration, and the nature of the electrode material can sometimes cause deviations from predictions based purely on standard electrode potentials. For example, the high overpotential for oxygen formation often means halide ions are discharged even when their standard oxidation potential is slightly less favourable than hydroxide.

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

For the electrolysis of aqueous solutions (e.g., copper(II) sulfate or sodium chloride), the general procedure involves setting up an electrolytic cell with a beaker containing the electrolyte. Two inert electrodes (typically graphite rods) are immersed in the solution, connected to a DC power supply. Test tubes are inverted over the electrodes to collect any gases produced. Observations are made at each electrode, and the products are identified using appropriate chemical tests (e.g., lit splint for H₂, glowing splint for O₂, damp litmus for Cl₂). Safety notes: Always wear eye protection. Ensure good ventilation, especially when producing chlorine gas. Avoid touching live electrodes. Use low voltage DC power supplies. Dispose of chemicals responsibly, especially solutions containing heavy metal ions.

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Hydrogen gas: Highly flammable, forms explosive mixtures with air.
  • 🛑 Oxygen gas: Supports combustion, increases fire risk.
  • 🛑 Chlorine gas: Toxic, corrosive, respiratory irritant, greenish-yellow gas with pungent smell.
  • 🛑 Bromine liquid/gas: Toxic, corrosive, brown/orange liquid with choking fumes.
  • 🛑 Iodine solid/vapour: Irritant, purple vapour.
  • 🛑 Corrosive/Irritant solutions: Electrolytes can be acidic, alkaline, or irritant. Wear eye protection and gloves.
  • 🛑 Electrical hazards: Ensure safe handling of power supplies and connections to prevent electric shock.

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. At the Cathode (Negative Electrode - Reduction): Positively charged ions (cations) are attracted. These include the metal cation from the salt (e.g., Na⁺, Cu²⁺) and hydrogen ions (H⁺) from water. There is a 'competition' for electrons. The ion that is more easily reduced (has a higher tendency to gain electrons) is discharged. This is determined by the reactivity series: if the metal is more reactive than hydrogen (e.g., Na, K, Ca), then H⁺ ions gain electrons (2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂). If the metal is less reactive than hydrogen (e.g., Cu, Ag, Pb), then the metal cation gains electrons (Mⁿ⁺ + ne⁻ → M).
  2. At the Anode (Positive Electrode - Oxidation): Negatively charged ions (anions) are attracted. These include the non-metal anion from the salt (e.g., Cl⁻, SO₄²⁻, NO₃⁻) and hydroxide ions (OH⁻) from water. There is a 'competition' to lose electrons. The ion that is more easily oxidised (has a higher tendency to lose electrons) is discharged. This follows the 'halide rule': if halide ions (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) are present in sufficient concentration, they are preferentially oxidised to form the respective halogen gas (2X⁻ → X₂ + 2e⁻). If no halide ions are present (or if the halide is very dilute, or if other anions like SO₄²⁻ or NO₃⁻ are present), then hydroxide ions are oxidised, producing oxygen gas and water (4OH⁻ → O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻).

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Electrolyte: The ionic compound must be in an aqueous solution.
  • Electrodes: Typically inert electrodes are used (e.g., graphite or platinum) unless an active electrode is specifically required (e.g., in electrorefining).
  • Power Supply: A direct current (DC) power supply is essential to ensure unidirectional flow of electrons and distinct products at each electrode.
  • Temperature: Room temperature is generally used, although higher temperatures might increase reaction rates.
  • Concentration: The concentration of the electrolyte can influence the products, especially at the anode when halide ions compete with hydroxide ions (e.g., dilute vs. concentrated NaCl).
  • Pressure: Atmospheric pressure is typically used for lab experiments.

🔦 Element Spotlight

While no single element is the sole focus, the products of aqueous electrolysis often include key elements or compounds. Hydrogen (H₂) is a light, colourless, flammable gas often produced at the cathode. Its production involves H⁺ ions from water gaining electrons. Oxygen (O₂) is a colourless gas that supports combustion, typically produced at the anode from the oxidation of hydroxide ions from water. Chlorine (Cl₂) is a toxic, corrosive, greenish-yellow gas with a pungent smell, produced at the anode when concentrated chloride ions are present. These three products highlight the versatility and significance of aqueous electrolysis.
