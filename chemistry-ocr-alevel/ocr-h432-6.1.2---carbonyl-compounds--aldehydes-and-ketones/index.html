🤜 In a Nutshell

Carbonyl compounds, including aldehydes and ketones, feature a reactive C=O group that undergoes characteristic nucleophilic addition reactions, leading to products like alcohols and hydroxynitriles. Aldehydes are uniquely distinguished from ketones by their susceptibility to oxidation, forming carboxylic acids, which is leveraged in identification tests like Tollens' reagent. Understanding their structure and reactivity is fundamental to organic synthesis and analytical chemistry.

🧠 Conceptual Understanding

Alright class, today we're diving into the fascinating world of carbonyl compounds – specifically aldehydes and ketones. The heart of these molecules is the 'carbonyl group', a carbon atom double-bonded to an oxygen atom (C=O). This group is highly polar because oxygen is more electronegative than carbon, pulling electron density towards itself. This makes the carbon atom partially positive (δ+) and the oxygen atom partially negative (δ-). This polarity is key to their reactivity: the δ+ carbon is a prime target for nucleophiles (electron-rich species), leading to a characteristic reaction called nucleophilic addition. The difference between an aldehyde and a ketone lies in what's attached to this carbonyl carbon: aldehydes have at least one hydrogen atom directly bonded to the carbonyl carbon, while ketones have two alkyl (or aryl) groups. This structural difference means aldehydes can be easily oxidised, unlike ketones, providing a neat way to distinguish them. Understanding these fundamental differences and their characteristic reactions is crucial for building more complex organic molecules and identifying unknown compounds in the lab.

📌 Core Theory

  • Carbonyl compounds contain the C=O functional group.
  • Aldehydes have the carbonyl carbon bonded to at least one hydrogen atom (RCHO), while ketones have the carbonyl carbon bonded to two alkyl or aryl groups (RCOR').
  • The C=O bond is polar due to the electronegativity difference between carbon and oxygen, making the carbon electrophilic (δ+) and the oxygen nucleophilic (δ-).
  • The characteristic reaction of carbonyl compounds is nucleophilic addition.
  • Aldehydes can be oxidised to carboxylic acids using suitable oxidising agents like acidified dichromate(VI) (Cr₂O₇²⁻/H⁺), whereas ketones generally resist oxidation under these conditions.
  • Common nucleophilic addition reactions include reduction with sodium tetrahydridoborate(III) (NaBH₄) to form alcohols, and reaction with hydrogen cyanide (HCN) to form hydroxynitriles.
  • The mechanism for nucleophilic addition involves the attack of a nucleophile on the electrophilic carbonyl carbon and protonation of the oxygen.
  • 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazine (2,4-DNPH) is used to detect the presence of a carbonyl group (aldehyde or ketone), forming an orange/yellow precipitate (a 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazone). The melting point of this derivative can identify the specific carbonyl compound.
  • Tollens' reagent (ammoniacal silver nitrate) is used to distinguish between aldehydes and ketones. Aldehydes reduce Tollens' reagent to silver metal (forming a 'silver mirror'), while ketones do not react.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

The carbonyl group's reactivity stems from the significant polarity of the carbon-oxygen double bond. The carbon atom, being electron-deficient, is highly susceptible to attack by nucleophiles. This attack breaks the C=O π-bond, forming a new C-Nu bond and an intermediate with a negatively charged oxygen atom (alkoxide ion). This alkoxide ion then rapidly reacts with an electrophile, often H⁺, to form the final product. In the case of reduction with NaBH₄, the hydride ion (H⁻) acts as the nucleophile, leading to an alcohol. When reacting with HCN, the cyanide ion (CN⁻) is the nucleophile, forming a hydroxynitrile. The difference in reactivity between aldehydes and ketones in oxidation reactions is due to the presence of a hydrogen atom on the carbonyl carbon of aldehydes. This C-H bond can be easily broken and replaced by a C-O bond during oxidation, forming a carboxylic acid. Ketones lack this hydrogen atom, making them much more resistant to oxidation without breaking stronger carbon-carbon bonds.

📖 Key Definitions

Aldehyde An organic compound containing a carbonyl group bonded to at least one hydrogen atom and one alkyl (or aryl) group (RCHO).
Ketone An organic compound containing a carbonyl group bonded to two alkyl (or aryl) groups (RCOR').
Nucleophilic addition A type of addition reaction where an electron-rich species (nucleophile) attacks an electron-deficient carbon atom, typically in a polar multiple bond, leading to the formation of a single bond and subsequent addition of an electrophile.

Oxidation (of aldehydes) The reaction of an aldehyde with an oxidising agent (e.g., acidified dichromate(VI)) to form a carboxylic acid, involving the gain of oxygen or loss of hydrogen/electrons.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Reduction of Propanone using NaBH₄

Problem: Describe the nucleophilic addition reaction of propanone with sodium tetrahydridoborate(III) (NaBH₄) followed by dilute acid, including the mechanism and naming the product.

Steps: 1. Draw the structure of propanone, showing the polar C=O bond. 2. Show the hydride ion (H⁻) from NaBH₄ acting as a nucleophile, attacking the δ+ carbonyl carbon. 3. Draw a curly arrow from the C=O π-bond breaking and electrons moving to the oxygen atom, forming an alkoxide ion intermediate. 4. In a separate step, show the alkoxide ion reacting with H⁺ from the dilute acid (protonation of the oxygen atom). 5. Draw the final product and name it. The product is propan-2-ol.

Answer: Propanone undergoes nucleophilic addition with NaBH₄ followed by H⁺ to form propan-2-ol. The hydride ion (H⁻) attacks the carbonyl carbon, breaking the C=O π-bond and forming an alkoxide ion. This intermediate is then protonated by H⁺ to yield propan-2-ol.

Teacher's Tip

Always show the full curly arrows originating from a lone pair or the middle of a bond, and pointing to where the electrons are moving. Remember NaBH₄ provides H⁻, not H⁺.

2. Formation of 2-hydroxynitrile from Ethanal

Problem: Outline the mechanism for the reaction between ethanal and hydrogen cyanide (HCN) in the presence of a small amount of KCN, and name the organic product.

Steps: 1. Draw ethanal, showing the polar C=O bond. Include the lone pair on the oxygen. 2. Show the cyanide ion (CN⁻) as the nucleophile (produced from KCN acting as a catalyst), attacking the δ+ carbonyl carbon. 3. Draw a curly arrow from the C=O π-bond breaking and electrons moving to the oxygen atom, forming an alkoxide intermediate. 4. Show the alkoxide ion abstracting a proton from a molecule of HCN (or H⁺ in solution), regenerating the CN⁻ catalyst. 5. Draw the final product, 2-hydroxypropanenitrile, clearly showing the new -OH and -CN groups.

Answer: Ethanal undergoes nucleophilic addition with HCN (catalysed by CN⁻) to form 2-hydroxypropanenitrile. The cyanide ion nucleophilically attacks the carbonyl carbon, followed by protonation of the oxygen. The product is 2-hydroxypropanenitrile.

Teacher's Tip

HCN itself is a weak nucleophile; the reaction is catalysed by CN⁻ ions, usually from NaCN or KCN. Remember the product is a hydroxynitrile, with both -OH and -CN groups on the same carbon atom.

3. Oxidation of Propanal

Problem: Describe how propanal can be oxidised to its corresponding carboxylic acid, including the reagents, conditions, and the observation expected if acidified potassium dichromate(VI) is used.

Steps: 1. Identify the reactant: propanal, an aldehyde. 2. State the oxidising agent: acidified potassium dichromate(VI) (K₂Cr₂O₇/H₂SO₄) or Tollens' reagent. 3. State the conditions: Reflux with acidified dichromate(VI) for good yield. 4. Describe the observation: If dichromate(VI) is used, the orange Cr₂O₇²⁻ solution turns green as Cr³⁺ ions are formed. 5. Write the balanced equation for the reaction, including the organic product: Propanal is oxidised to propanoic acid.

Answer: Propanal can be oxidised to propanoic acid using acidified potassium dichromate(VI). The conditions involve heating under reflux. The observation for this reaction is that the orange dichromate(VI) solution turns green as the Cr₂O₇²⁻ ions are reduced to Cr³⁺ ions. The equation is CH₃CH₂CHO + [O] → CH₃CH₂COOH.

Teacher's Tip

Remember that aldehydes are easily oxidised, while ketones are not. Acidified dichromate(VI) is a strong oxidising agent; for milder oxidation or distinction, Tollens' or Fehling's reagents can be used.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Formaldehyde (Methanal): Used extensively in the production of resins, polymers, and as a preservative for biological specimens (embalming fluid).
  • Acetone (Propanone): A common industrial solvent, found in nail polish remover, paint thinners, and as a cleaning agent.
  • Flavourings and Fragrances: Many aldehydes and ketones have distinctive smells and are used in the food and perfume industry, such as vanillin (a benzaldehyde derivative) and cinnamaldehyde.
  • Pharmaceutical Synthesis: Carbonyl compounds are important intermediates in the synthesis of many pharmaceutical drugs, where nucleophilic addition reactions are key steps in building complex molecular structures.
  • Breathalysers: Early breathalysers used the oxidation of ethanol (an alcohol) to ethanal (an aldehyde) by acidified potassium dichromate(VI). While not a direct aldehyde/ketone reaction, it highlights the importance of redox chemistry involving these functional groups.

Q5. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Ethanal has a planar carbonyl group, meaning the carbon atom is sp² hybridised, and the atoms directly bonded to it (methyl group, hydrogen, and oxygen) lie in the same plane. Since both attack pathways are equally likely, an equimolar mixture of these two enantiomers, known as a racemic mixture, is produced. This results in an optically inactive product, as the optical rotations of the two enantiomers cancel each other out.

  • ✅ Both faces are equally accessible, so attack is equally likely from either side.
  • ✅ This results in the formation of a racemic mixture (an equimolar mixture) of two enantiomers (R and S isomers) of 2-hydroxypropanenitrile.

Protonation of hydroxyl: In the presence of acid, the hydroxyl group (-OH) is protonated to form -OH₂⁺, which is a good leaving group. 4. Elimination of water: The lone pair on the nitrogen atom pushes out the water molecule (H₂O), forming a C=N double bond and a protonated imine. 5. Deprotonation: The protonated imine loses a proton to form the neutral imine product (R-N=CH-CH₃).

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Step 1: Lone pair on N of RNH₂ attacks carbonyl carbon.
  • ✅ Step 1: C=O π-bond electrons move to oxygen.
  • ✅ Step 1: Formation of tetrahedral intermediate (zwitterionic or after proton transfer).
  • ✅ Step 2: Proton transfer from N⁺ to O⁻ (or solvent mediated).
  • ✅ Step 3: Protonation of -OH group to -OH₂⁺ (good leaving group).
  • ✅ Step 4: Loss of H₂O (leaving group).
  • ✅ Step 5: Deprotonation of N to form C=N imine. (Alternatively, direct lone pair on N pushes out water, then deprotonation).
  • ✅ All curly arrows correct and all intermediate structures drawn correctly.

Q7. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

Explain why the nucleophilic addition of HCN to propanone results in a racemic mixture of 2-hydroxy-2-methylpropanenitrile, even though the starting propanone is achiral.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Propanone is an achiral molecule, and its carbonyl carbon is sp² hybridised, meaning it is planar. When the nucleophile, cyanide ion (CN⁻), attacks this planar carbon, it can approach with equal probability from either the 'top' face or the 'bottom' face of the molecule. This equal probability of attack from both sides leads to the formation of two different stereoisomers (enantiomers) of 2-hydroxy-2-methylpropanenitrile. Since these two enantiomers are produced in equal amounts (a 50:50 ratio), the resulting mixture is a racemic mixture. A racemic mixture is optically inactive because the rotation of plane-polarised light caused by one enantiomer is exactly cancelled out by the opposite rotation caused by the other.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Propanone is achiral and has a planar carbonyl carbon (sp² hybridised).
  • ✅ The nucleophile (CN⁻) can attack the carbonyl carbon from either side (above or below the plane).
  • ✅ Both attack pathways are equally probable.
  • ✅ This leads to the formation of two enantiomers (R and S forms) in equal amounts, resulting in a racemic mixture which is optically inactive.

Q9. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

An unknown organic compound X, with the molecular formula C₃H₆O, gives an orange precipitate with 2,4-DNPH. When reacted with aqueous potassium cyanide and dilute sulfuric acid, it forms a product Y that contains a chiral centre. Deduce the structure of X and Y, and explain why Y has a chiral centre.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: The molecular formula C₃H₆O suggests an aldehyde (propanal) or a ketone (propanone) as the most likely carbonyl compounds. The positive test with 2,4-DNPH (orange precipitate) confirms that X is indeed a carbonyl compound. The crucial piece of information is that reaction with aqueous potassium cyanide and dilute sulfuric acid (forming a hydroxynitrile) yields product Y which contains a chiral centre. If X were propanone, the product would be 2-hydroxy-2-methylpropanenitrile, which does not have a chiral centre (the carbon attached to -OH and -CN is also attached to two identical -CH₃ groups). Therefore, X must be propanal (CH₃CH₂CHO). When propanal reacts with HCN, the product Y is 2-hydroxybutanenitrile (CH₃CH₂CH(OH)CN). This molecule has a chiral centre at the carbon atom bonded to the hydroxyl (-OH) group and the nitrile (-CN) group, as this carbon is attached to four different groups: a hydrogen atom (-H), a hydroxyl group (-OH), a nitrile group (-CN), and an ethyl group (-CH₂CH₃).

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ C₃H₆O indicates a carbonyl (aldehyde or ketone) or an unsaturated alcohol.
  • ✅ Orange ppt with 2,4-DNPH confirms carbonyl.
  • ✅ Forms a chiral product with HCN/H⁺, so X must be propanal (an aldehyde that forms a chiral hydroxynitrile). (Propanone would not form a chiral centre in its hydroxynitrile).
  • ✅ Structure of X: Propanal (CH₃CH₂CHO).
  • ✅ Structure of Y: 2-hydroxybutanenitrile (CH₃CH₂CH(OH)CN).
  • ✅ Explanation of chiral centre in Y: The carbon atom bonded to -CN, -OH, -H, and -CH₂CH₃ is bonded to four different groups, making it a chiral centre.

Q10. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

Consider the nucleophilic addition of HCN to propanone. Draw the full mechanism for this reaction, including curly arrows and the structure of the intermediate.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Step 1: The cyanide ion (CN⁻), acting as a nucleophile, attacks the electrophilic carbon of the carbonyl group in propanone. Simultaneously, the pi electrons from the C=O bond move to the oxygen atom, forming an alkoxide ion intermediate. CN⁻ → (attacks (CH₃)₂C=O carbon) ; C=O pi bond electrons → O⁻. Intermediate: (CH₃)₂C(O⁻)CN. Step 2: The negatively charged oxygen (alkoxide ion) then abstracts a proton (H⁺) from a molecule of HCN (or from the solvent), forming the stable hydroxynitrile product, 2-hydroxy-2-methylpropanenitrile. O⁻ → (attacks H⁺)The final product is 2-hydroxy-2-methylpropanenitrile.

  • ✅ Step 1: Curly arrow from CN⁻ to carbonyl carbon.
  • ✅ Step 1: Curly arrow from C=O π-bond to oxygen atom.
  • ✅ Step 1: Correct intermediate (alkoxide ion, (CH₃)₂C(O⁻)CN) formed.
  • ✅ Step 2: Curly arrow from O⁻ of alkoxide ion to H⁺ (from HCN or solvent).

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

Formaldehyde, the simplest aldehyde, was first synthesised by Butlerov in 1859, but its structure was correctly determined by August Hofmann in 1867. Acetone (propanone) has been known since ancient times, used by alchemists for various purposes, and was industrially produced in the 19th century. The understanding of the nucleophilic addition mechanism evolved with the development of electron theory in organic chemistry in the early 20th century, notably by Ingold, providing a fundamental framework for explaining carbonyl reactivity.

⚖️ Ethics

The use of hydrogen cyanide (HCN) in organic synthesis, particularly in forming hydroxynitriles, raises significant safety and ethical concerns due to its extreme toxicity. HCN is a potent respiratory poison. Industrial processes must implement stringent safety protocols, including closed systems, highly ventilated areas, and efficient waste disposal. The disposal of cyanide-containing waste products is also a major environmental and ethical challenge, requiring careful treatment to prevent release into ecosystems.

🚀 Advanced Theory

While NaBH₄ is a common reducing agent, the stereochemical outcome of nucleophilic addition to prochiral carbonyls (like unsymmetrical ketones or aldehydes) can be controlled using chiral reducing agents (e.g., CBS reduction reagents or certain chiral phosphine ligands with transition metal catalysts). These reagents can preferentially deliver the hydride to one face of the carbonyl, leading to an enantioselective reduction and the formation of a single enantiomer of the alcohol product, which is crucial in pharmaceutical synthesis where stereospecificity is often vital for drug activity and minimising side effects. The concept of kinetic vs. thermodynamic control can also be applied to certain carbonyl reactions under different conditions.

🧪 Required Practical

Test for Carbonyl Group using 2,4-Dinitrophenylhydrazine (2,4-DNPH)

Equipment: Test tubes, dropping pipettes, water bath, melting point apparatus. Reagents: 2,4-DNPH reagent solution (acidified with H₂SO₄), unknown organic liquid (aldehyde or ketone). Safety: 2,4-DNPH is toxic, flammable, and corrosive; wear eye protection and gloves, work in a fume cupboard. Avoid skin contact. Keep away from ignition sources. Method: 1. Add 2 cm³ of 2,4-DNPH reagent to a test tube. 2. Add 3-5 drops of the unknown organic liquid to the test tube. 3. Shake the mixture gently. 4. Observe for the formation of an orange or yellow precipitate. If no precipitate forms at room temperature, warm gently in a water bath. 5. If a precipitate forms, filter it off, wash with cold distilled water, and recrystallise from a suitable solvent (e.g., ethanol/water mixture) to purify. 6. Dry the purified derivative and determine its melting point using a melting point apparatus. 7. Compare the measured melting point to known values in a data book to identify the specific carbonyl compound.

Distinguishing Aldehydes and Ketones using Tollens' Reagent

Equipment: Test tubes, dropping pipettes, water bath. Reagents: Tollens' reagent (freshly prepared ammoniacal silver nitrate solution), aldehyde, ketone. Preparation of Tollens' Reagent: To aqueous silver nitrate, add aqueous sodium hydroxide until a brown precipitate of Ag₂O forms. Then, add dilute aqueous ammonia dropwise, with shaking, until the precipitate just redissolves. (Must be freshly prepared as it can form explosive silver nitride on standing). Safety: Tollens' reagent is corrosive. Ammonia solution is irritant. Dispose of waste immediately and correctly (do not store). Wear eye protection and gloves. Method: 1. Add 2 cm³ of freshly prepared Tollens' reagent to a clean test tube. 2. Add 5 drops of the aldehyde or ketone to the test tube. 3. Place the test tube in a water bath at about 60-70 °C and warm gently for several minutes. 4. Observe for the formation of a silver mirror on the inside of the test tube. Aldehydes will give a silver mirror; ketones will show no visible reaction. Note: Ensure test tubes are scrupulously clean for a good silver mirror to form.

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Hydrogen Cyanide (HCN): Extremely poisonous gas. Formed from cyanide salts in acid. Must be handled in a fume cupboard with extreme care and appropriate PPE. Causes respiratory failure.
  • 🛑 Cyanide salts (e.g., NaCN, KCN): Highly toxic. Avoid ingestion and skin contact. Must not be mixed with acids.
  • 🛑 Sodium Tetrahydridoborate(III) (NaBH₄): Flammable solid, reacts violently with water and acids to release hydrogen gas (flammable). Keep dry and away from ignition sources.
  • 🛑 2,4-Dinitrophenylhydrazine (2,4-DNPH): Irritant, toxic, flammable. Can be explosive when dry (usually supplied wetted). Handle in a fume cupboard, wear gloves and eye protection.
  • 🛑 Tollens' reagent: Can form explosive silver nitride (Ag₃N) on standing, especially when concentrated or alkaline. Must be freshly prepared and disposed of immediately after use by acidifying with dilute acid (e.g., HCl) to convert Ag⁺ to AgCl precipitate, which is less hazardous, then flushing with plenty of water. Corrosive.
  • 🛑 Acidified Potassium Dichromate(VI) (Cr₂O₇²⁻/H⁺): Strong oxidising agent, corrosive, toxic, and a suspected carcinogen. Wear gloves and eye protection, avoid skin contact.
  • 🛑 General Organic Solvents: Many are flammable (e.g., ethanol, propanone), irritant, or toxic. Work in a fume cupboard, avoid ignition sources, wear appropriate PPE.

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. Nucleophilic Addition (General): The electrophilic carbonyl carbon (δ+) is attacked by a nucleophile (Nu⁻). The electrons from the C=O π-bond move onto the oxygen, forming an alkoxide intermediate (C-O⁻). This alkoxide then reacts with an electrophile (E⁺), usually a proton (H⁺), to form the final product (C-O-E).
  2. Nucleophilic Addition with NaBH₄ (Reduction): The hydride ion (H⁻), derived from NaBH₄, acts as the nucleophile. It attacks the carbonyl carbon, and the C=O π-bond breaks, forming an alkoxide. This alkoxide is then protonated by H⁺ from a dilute acid 'work-up' to yield an alcohol.
  3. Nucleophilic Addition with HCN: The cyanide ion (CN⁻), typically from a cyanide salt (e.g., KCN) acting as a catalyst, attacks the carbonyl carbon. The C=O π-bond breaks, forming an alkoxide. This alkoxide then abstracts a proton from a molecule of HCN (or H⁺ from the solution) to form a hydroxynitrile, regenerating the CN⁻ catalyst.
  4. Oxidation of Aldehydes: While not a 'curly arrow' mechanism in the same vein as nucleophilic addition, the oxidation involves the breaking of the C-H bond on the carbonyl carbon and the formation of a C-O bond. The aldehyde is oxidised to a carboxylic acid, with the oxidising agent (e.g., Cr₂O₇²⁻/H⁺ or Ag⁺ in Tollens' reagent) being reduced.

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Reduction with NaBH₄: Typically carried out in aqueous or alcoholic solvents (e.g., methanol, ethanol) at room temperature. Followed by addition of dilute acid (H⁺) for protonation of the intermediate alkoxide.
  • Addition of HCN: Requires the presence of a small amount of cyanide ions (e.g., from KCN or NaCN) as a catalyst. Usually carried out at room temperature. The pH needs to be slightly alkaline (around pH 9-10) to generate sufficient CN⁻ but not too high to hydrolyse the nitrile.
  • Oxidation of Aldehydes (to carboxylic acids):
  • Using Acidified Potassium Dichromate(VI): Heat under reflux (e.g., 60-100 °C) with dilute H₂SO₄.
  • Using Tollens' Reagent: Warm gently in a water bath (e.g., 50-70 °C).
  • Formation of 2,4-Dinitrophenylhydrazone: Typically carried out at room temperature, but gentle warming in a water bath can accelerate the reaction if no precipitate forms immediately.

🔦 Element Spotlight

The Carbonyl Group (C=O) is the star of this show. It consists of a carbon atom double-bonded to an oxygen atom. This bond is highly polar because oxygen is significantly more electronegative than carbon, leading to a partial positive charge (δ+) on the carbon and a partial negative charge (δ-) on the oxygen. This makes the carbonyl carbon an electrophilic site, highly susceptible to attack by nucleophiles. The carbon atom is sp² hybridised, giving the carbonyl group a planar trigonal geometry. This planarity is crucial for understanding the stereochemical outcomes of nucleophilic addition reactions, as nucleophiles can approach from either face of the plane. The C=O bond also has characteristic strong absorption in the infrared (IR) spectrum (around 1680-1750 cm⁻¹), making it easily identifiable by spectroscopic techniques.
