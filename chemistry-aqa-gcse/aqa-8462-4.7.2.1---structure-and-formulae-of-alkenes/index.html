🤜 In a Nutshell

Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons characterised by at least one carbon-carbon double bond (C=C), following the general formula CnH2n. This double bond makes them more reactive than alkanes, allowing them to undergo addition reactions. The first four members are ethene, propene, butene, and pentene, whose names end in '-ene' and reflect their carbon chain length.

🧠 Conceptual Understanding

Alkenes are a family of organic compounds called hydrocarbons, meaning they are made only of carbon and hydrogen atoms. What makes alkenes special is the presence of at least one carbon-carbon double bond (C=C) within their structure. This double bond is key! It means alkenes are 'unsaturated' because they contain fewer hydrogen atoms than an alkane with the same number of carbon atoms, and it makes them much more reactive. The general formula for alkenes with one double bond is CnH2n, which helps us predict their molecular formula. Understanding alkenes is crucial because their unique reactivity makes them important starting materials for producing many valuable chemicals, including plastics.

📌 Core Theory

  • Alkenes are hydrocarbons, meaning they contain only carbon and hydrogen atoms.
  • The defining feature of an alkene is the presence of at least one carbon-carbon double bond (C=C).
  • Because of the C=C double bond, alkenes are described as 'unsaturated'. This means they can undergo addition reactions.
  • The general formula for the homologous series of alkenes (with one double bond) is CnH2n, where 'n' represents the number of carbon atoms.
  • The first four members of the alkene homologous series are ethene (n=2), propene (n=3), butene (n=4), and pentene (n=5).
  • You should be able to recognise alkenes from their names (ending in '-ene') or from given formulae (e.g., C2H4, C3H6) and identify the double bond in displayed formulae.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

The presence of a carbon-carbon double bond (C=C) distinguishes alkenes from alkanes, which only contain carbon-carbon single bonds. This double bond means that each carbon atom involved in the bond is only bonded to two other atoms (two carbon, two hydrogen for ethene), rather than the maximum four single bonds possible for carbon. Consequently, alkenes have two fewer hydrogen atoms compared to an alkane with the same number of carbon atoms, making them 'unsaturated'. This 'extra' bond in the double bond is weaker and more accessible, making alkenes more reactive than alkanes. They readily undergo addition reactions, where atoms are added across the double bond, converting it into a single bond and forming a saturated compound. This reactivity is fundamental to their industrial importance, particularly in polymerisation.

Problem: Draw the displayed formula of propene.

Steps: 1. Identify the prefix 'prop-', which indicates 3 carbon atoms. 2. Identify the suffix '-ene', which indicates an alkene with a C=C double bond. 3. Draw three carbon atoms in a chain. 4. Place a double bond between two adjacent carbon atoms (e.g., C1 and C2). 5. Fill in the remaining single bonds for hydrogen atoms, ensuring each carbon atom has a total of four bonds. 6. Teacher's Tip

Remember that each carbon atom must always form four bonds in total. The double bond counts as two bonds for each carbon involved.

2. Determining the Molecular Formula of Butene

Problem: Determine the molecular formula for butene.

Steps: 1. Identify the prefix 'but-', which indicates 4 carbon atoms. 2. Identify the suffix '-ene', which indicates an alkene with one C=C double bond. 3. Recall the general formula for alkenes with one double bond: CnH2n. 4. Substitute 'n=4' (for 'but-') into the general formula. 5. Calculate the number of hydrogen atoms: 2 imes 4 = 8.

Answer: C4H8

Always check the prefix to find 'n' (number of carbons), then apply the general formula CnH2n for alkenes.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Production of polymers: Ethene is polymerised to make poly(ethene), a common plastic used in packaging and pipes.
  • Ripening of fruits: Ethene gas is naturally produced by some fruits (like bananas and tomatoes) and acts as a hormone to initiate and accelerate ripening.
  • Monomers for various plastics: Propene is used to make poly(propene) for carpets, ropes, and car parts; chloroethene (vinyl chloride) polymerises to form poly(chloroethene) or PVC.
  • Starting materials in the petrochemical industry: Alkenes are crucial intermediates in the production of many organic chemicals, solvents, and detergents.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing the general formula of alkenes (CnH2n) with that of alkanes (CnH2n+2).
  • ❌ Incorrectly counting bonds around carbon atoms, especially those involved in the double bond (a carbon in a C=C bond should still have a total of four bonds).
  • ❌ Not understanding what 'unsaturated' means in terms of reactivity (it means they can add more atoms across the double bond).
  • ❌ Assuming all alkenes are gases at room temperature; larger alkenes can be liquids or solids.
Memory Anchor

Think of the double bond in an alkene like two hands holding, but one hand is a bit loose and ready to grab onto something new. This 'loose hand' makes alkenes reactive and 'unsaturated' (not fully satisfied with just hydrogens).

Q6. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Alkenes possess a carbon-carbon double bond (C=C), making them unsaturated. In contrast, alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons containing only strong C-C and C-H single bonds. These bonds require significant energy to break, making alkanes generally unreactive. When they do react, usually with halogens under UV light, it typically involves a substitution mechanism where a hydrogen atom is replaced by a halogen atom, rather than adding new atoms.

  • ✅ This double bond consists of a sigma and a weaker pi bond, which is easily broken.
  • ✅ The pi bond is a region of high electron density, attractive to electrophiles.
  • ✅ Alkanes have only strong C-C and C-H single bonds (saturated), requiring more energy to break, thus preferring substitution where one atom replaces another.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The history of alkenes is intertwined with the development of organic chemistry and the petroleum industry. Ethene (ethylene) was one of the first organic compounds studied, with its unique reactivity recognised early on. Its industrial importance exploded in the 20th century, particularly with the discovery and development of polymerisation processes, leading to the creation of plastics like poly(ethene). Early chemists struggled to understand the 'unsaturation' and 'affinity' of these compounds until theories of bonding, including the concept of double bonds, became established.

⚖️ Ethics

The widespread use of alkenes as monomers for plastics raises significant ethical and environmental concerns. While plastics offer numerous benefits, their non-biodegradability and reliance on finite fossil fuels contribute to pollution, resource depletion, and climate change. Ethical considerations include balancing industrial output with environmental protection, ensuring responsible waste management, promoting recycling, and investing in research for biodegradable alternatives or renewable feedstocks for alkene production.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond GCSE, the carbon-carbon double bond in alkenes is described in terms of a sigma (σ) bond and a pi (π) bond. The σ bond is formed by the direct overlap of atomic orbitals, while the π bond is formed by the sideways overlap of p-orbitals. This π bond is weaker and more exposed, making it susceptible to attack by electrophiles, leading to electrophilic addition reactions. Students might also explore E/Z isomerism (geometric isomerism) which arises in alkenes due to the restricted rotation around the double bond, creating different spatial arrangements of substituents.

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

The most common practical application of alkene structure is the test for unsaturation using bromine water. Steps: 1. Add a few drops of bromine water (orange/brown) to an unknown organic sample. 2. Shake the mixture. Observation: If the sample is an alkene (unsaturated), the bromine water will rapidly decolourise from orange/brown to colourless. If it is an alkane (saturated), no colour change will be observed. Safety notes: Bromine water is an irritant; avoid contact with skin and eyes. Work in a fume cupboard as organic compounds can be volatile and flammable.

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Alkenes (like ethene, propene) are highly flammable gases/liquids. Keep away from ignition sources.
  • 🛑 Bromine water: Irritant. Use in a fume cupboard, wear eye protection and gloves.
  • 🛑 Organic vapours can be intoxicating or harmful if inhaled in large quantities. Ensure good ventilation.

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. Alkenes undergo addition reactions. The pi (π) bond in the C=C double bond is a region of high electron density and is relatively weak. When an electrophile (an electron-pair acceptor, such as the slightly positive end of a polar molecule like Br-Br or H-Br, or even H+ from H2O/H2SO4) approaches the double bond, the pi electrons are attracted to it. The pi bond breaks, and new single bonds are formed with the atoms being added across the original double bond. For GCSE, the focus is on understanding that the double bond 'opens up' to accept new atoms, rather than a detailed electron pushing mechanism (e.g., electrophilic addition is an A-level concept).

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Reaction with Bromine water (Bromination): Room temperature, no catalyst required (e.g., ethene + Br2(aq)).
  • Hydrogenation (Addition of Hydrogen): High temperature (e.g., 150°C), nickel catalyst (e.g., ethene + H2(g)).
  • Hydration (Addition of Steam): High temperature (e.g., 300°C), high pressure (e.g., 60-70 atm), concentrated phosphoric(V) acid catalyst (e.g., ethene + H2O(g)).
  • Polymerisation: High temperature, high pressure, catalyst (specifics vary depending on type of poly(alkene)).

🔦 Element Spotlight

Carbon is the central element in alkenes, forming the backbone of the molecule. Each carbon atom in an organic compound typically forms four bonds. In alkenes, the presence of a carbon-carbon double bond means that two of these four bonds are used to link two carbon atoms together. The remaining two bonds on each carbon involved in the double bond are typically made with hydrogen atoms (or other carbon atoms in larger molecules). Carbon's ability to form stable chains and rings, combined with its capacity for multiple bonding (single, double, triple), is what gives rise to the vast diversity of organic chemistry, with alkenes being a key example of compounds with high reactivity due to their double bond.
