🤜 In a Nutshell

Addition polymerisation is the process where many unsaturated alkene monomers link together by breaking their carbon-carbon double bonds to form a large, saturated polymer molecule without losing any atoms. This reaction requires high temperatures, high pressures, and a catalyst. The resulting polymers, like poly(ethene), are highly versatile and widely used but pose significant environmental challenges due to their non-biodegradability.

🧠 Conceptual Understanding

Imagine you have lots of identical building blocks, each with a 'sticky' part. Addition polymerisation is like linking all these sticky blocks together, one after another, to form a super long chain. In chemistry, these building blocks are called monomers, and they are typically alkenes – small molecules containing a carbon-carbon double bond (C=C). This double bond is the 'sticky part'. During polymerisation, this double bond breaks open, allowing each monomer to link up with its neighbours, forming a very large molecule called a polymer. Crucially, no other small molecules are produced in this reaction, meaning all the atoms from the monomers end up in the polymer chain. This is different from other types of polymerisation where small molecules like water might be released.

📌 Core Theory

  • Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons containing at least one carbon-carbon double bond (C=C).
  • Addition polymerisation is the process where many small unsaturated monomer molecules join together to form a single, large saturated polymer molecule.
  • During addition polymerisation, the carbon-carbon double bond in each monomer breaks open.
  • New single covalent bonds form between the carbon atoms of adjacent monomers, creating a long chain.
  • The repeating unit of an addition polymer has the same atoms as the monomer, as no atoms are lost during the reaction.
  • Examples include ethene forming poly(ethene) and propene forming poly(propene).
  • The structure of the polymer can be represented by showing the repeating unit within brackets and an 'n' subscript to indicate a large number of units.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

In addition polymerisation, the key feature is the unsaturated carbon-carbon double bond (C=C) present in the monomer. When conditions like high temperature, high pressure, and a catalyst are applied, the pi-bond (one of the two bonds in the double bond) breaks. This creates two 'free' bonding sites on each carbon atom that were part of the double bond. These sites then readily form new single covalent bonds with adjacent monomer molecules, effectively adding them to the growing chain. Because all atoms from the original monomers are incorporated into the polymer, the molecular formula of the repeating unit in the polymer is identical to that of the monomer. For example, ethene (C H 2 =CH 2 ) forms poly(ethene), where the repeating unit is (-CH 2 -CH 2 -). This continuous linking forms a giant covalent structure, which gives polymers their characteristic strength and durability. Recognizing the C=C functional group in a monomer is essential for predicting the structure of the resulting addition polymer and its repeating unit.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Drawing the Repeating Unit of Poly(ethene)

Problem: Draw the displayed formula of ethene and then draw the repeating unit of the polymer formed by addition polymerisation.

Steps: 1. Draw the displayed formula of ethene, showing the C=C double bond and two hydrogen atoms attached to each carbon. 2. Imagine the C=C double bond breaking, leaving two single bonds extending from each carbon atom. 3. Draw the two carbon atoms connected by a single bond, with the original hydrogen atoms still attached. 4. Extend single bonds from the outside carbons to indicate that the unit links to other identical units, then enclose this structure in brackets with a subscript 'n'.

Answer: Ethene: H₂C=CH₂. Repeating unit of Poly(ethene): -[CH₂-CH₂]-n

Teacher's Tip

Always break the double bond and ensure the single bonds extend outside the brackets for the repeating unit to show continuous chain formation.

2. Identifying the Monomer from a Polymer

Problem: A section of an addition polymer is shown: -CH₂-CH(CH₃)-CH₂-CH(CH₃)-. Deduce the displayed formula of the monomer from which it was formed.

Steps: 1. Identify the repeating unit in the polymer chain. In this case, it's -CH₂-CH(CH₃)-. 2. Recognise that in addition polymerisation, the repeating unit has the same atoms as the monomer. 3. To find the monomer, reverse the polymerisation process: place a double bond between the two carbons that formed new single bonds with adjacent units (the carbons within the repeating unit that were originally part of the C=C double bond in the monomer). 4. Draw the original molecule with the C=C double bond. This means the carbons involved in the repeating unit will form the double bond in the monomer. 5. Ensure all valencies are correct (carbon forms 4 bonds).

Answer: The repeating unit is -CH₂-CH(CH₃)-. The monomer is propene: CH₂=CH(CH₃).

Teacher's Tip

Look for the carbons within the repeating unit that were bonded to other units. These two carbons formed the original C=C double bond in the monomer.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Poly(ethene) (polythene) is used to make plastic bags, plastic bottles, and cling film due to its flexibility and inertness.
  • Poly(propene) (polypropylene) is used for plastic crates, ropes, carpets, and medical components because of its strength and resistance to chemicals.
  • Poly(chloroethene) (PVC) is used for drainpipes, window frames, and electrical cable insulation because it is rigid and a good electrical insulator.
  • Polystyrene, formed from styrene monomers, is used in packaging (e.g., disposable cups, protective inserts) and insulation materials.
  • Teflon (poly(tetrafluoroethene)) is used for non-stick coatings on cookware and as a lubricant due to its extremely low friction.

3. In addition polymerisation, how does the repeating unit in the polymer compare to the original monomer molecule? A) The repeating unit has fewer atoms than the monomer. B) The repeating unit has gained extra atoms compared to the monomer. C) The repeating unit has the same atoms as the monomer. D) The repeating unit is a smaller fragment of the monomer.

Answer

C) The repeating unit has the same atoms as the monomer. In addition polymerisation, no atoms are lost during the bonding process, so the repeating unit within the polymer has the exact same atoms as the monomer from which it was formed.

4. Which feature must a molecule possess to undergo addition polymerisation? A) A C-C single bond B) A C=C double bond C) An -OH functional group D) A halogen atom

Answer

B) A C=C double bond Addition polymerisation requires the presence of a carbon-carbon double bond (C=C) in the monomer, as this bond breaks to allow the monomers to link together.

5. Ethene has the formula CH₂=CH₂. Which of the following represents a section of poly(ethene)? A) -CH₂-CH₃-CH₂-CH₃- B) -CH₂-CH₂-CH₂-CH₂- C) -CH=CH-CH=CH- D) -CH₂-CH(OH)-CH₂-CH(OH)-

Answer

B) -CH₂-CH₂-CH₂-CH₂- During addition polymerisation, the C=C double bond in ethene breaks, and each carbon atom forms a single bond with another ethene unit. The repeating unit is -CH₂-CH₂-.

6. A student states that during addition polymerisation, small molecules like water are produced as byproducts. Is this statement correct? A) Yes, water is always produced. B) Yes, sometimes water is produced. C) No, no other molecules are formed. D) No, oxygen is produced instead of water.

Answer

C) No, no other molecules are formed. Addition polymerisation is characterised by the fact that all atoms from the monomers are incorporated into the polymer, and no small molecules (like water) are formed as byproducts.

7. Propene has the structure CH₂=CH-CH₃. What is the repeating unit of poly(propene)? A) -CH₂-CH(CH₃)- B) -CH₂-CH₂-CH₃- C) -CH=CH-CH₃- D) -CH(CH₃)-CH(CH₃)-

Answer

A) -CH₂-CH(CH₃)- The double bond in propene breaks, and the monomers link. The CH₃ group remains as a side chain on one of the carbon atoms that were part of the double bond.

8. A section of an addition polymer is shown: -CH₂-CCl₂-CH₂-CCl₂-. What was the monomer used to form this polymer? A) Chloroethene (CH₂=CHCl) B) 1,1-dichloroethene (CH₂=CCl₂) C) 1,2-dichloroethene (CHCl=CHCl) D) Tetrachloroethene (CCl₂=CCl₂)

Answer

B) 1,1-dichloroethene (CH₂=CCl₂) To find the monomer, identify the repeating unit (-CH₂-CCl₂-). Then, place a double bond between the two carbons in the repeating unit that were originally part of the monomer's double bond. This gives CH₂=CCl₂.

9. Why do addition polymers have very large relative molecular masses? A) They contain very strong ionic bonds. B) They are formed from a single, very heavy monomer. C) Many small monomer molecules join together to form long chains. D) They are always found in solid state.

Answer

C) Many small monomer molecules join together to form long chains. Polymers are macromolecules formed by the covalent bonding of thousands of individual monomer units, leading to very long chains and thus very large molecular masses.

10. The monomer vinyl chloride (chloroethene) has the structure CH₂=CHCl. Which option correctly shows a short section of poly(vinyl chloride), PVC? A) -CH₂-CHCl-CH₂-CHCl- B) -CH=CHCl-CH=CHCl- C) -CH₂-CH₂-CHCl-CHCl- D) -CHCl-CHCl-CHCl-CHCl-

Answer

A) -CH₂-CHCl-CH₂-CHCl- The double bond in vinyl chloride breaks, and the units link end-to-end. The chlorine atom remains as a side group on alternate carbons in the main chain.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The history of polymers began with natural polymers like rubber and cellulose. The first synthetic polymer, Bakelite, was invented by Leo Baekeland in 1907, though it was a thermosetting plastic. The development of addition polymerisation truly took off in the 1930s with the discovery of poly(ethene) (polythene) by Eric Fawcett and Reginald Gibson at ICI. Further breakthroughs came with Karl Ziegler and Giulio Natta in the 1950s, who developed catalysts (Ziegler-Natta catalysts) that allowed for the precise control of polymer structure, enabling the production of high-density poly(ethene) (HDPE) and isotactic poly(propene), for which they shared the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1963.

⚖️ Ethics

The widespread adoption of addition polymers has brought immense benefits but also significant ethical challenges. The major concern is plastic pollution, driven by their non-biodegradability and over-reliance on single-use items. This leads to environmental damage, harm to wildlife, and the pervasive problem of microplastics entering food chains. Ethical considerations involve balancing the convenience and economic benefits of plastics against their long-term environmental costs. This includes responsibilities for sustainable production (e.g., using renewable feedstocks), effective waste management, and the development of truly biodegradable alternatives.

🚀 Advanced Theory

At a more advanced level, addition polymerisation mechanisms can be free radical, cationic, or anionic. Free radical polymerisation is common for poly(ethene) and involves initiation, propagation, and termination steps. The properties of addition polymers are also deeply influenced by factors like tacticity (the stereochemical arrangement of side groups along the polymer chain, e.g., isotactic, syndiotactic, atactic poly(propene)) and the degree of branching. Highly branched polymers (like LDPE) have lower densities and melting points due to less efficient chain packing, while linear polymers (like HDPE) pack more tightly, leading to higher density and rigidity. Understanding these nuances allows for the tailored design of polymers with specific properties for diverse applications.

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Monomers (e.g., ethene, propene) are often flammable gases. Store in well-ventilated areas, away from ignition sources.
  • 🛑 High temperatures and pressures used in industrial polymerisation pose explosion and burn risks. Requires robust containment and cooling systems.
  • 🛑 Some catalysts can be toxic or corrosive; handle with appropriate PPE (gloves, safety goggles) and in a fume cupboard.

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

    1. Initiation: A small amount of initiator (e.g., a free radical) breaks the C=C double bond in an alkene monomer, forming a reactive species.
    1. Propagation: The reactive species adds to another alkene monomer, extending the chain. This process repeats rapidly as more monomers are added.
    1. Termination: Two growing polymer chains, or a growing chain and an initiator fragment, combine or disproportionate, ending the chain growth and forming a stable polymer molecule. (Note: GCSE only requires understanding C=C breaks and new C-C bonds form, not detailed steps).

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • High temperature (e.g., 200-300°C for ethene polymerisation)
  • High pressure (e.g., 1000-3000 atmospheres for ethene polymerisation)
  • Presence of a catalyst (e.g., a small amount of oxygen, organic peroxides for LDPE; Ziegler-Natta catalysts for HDPE/poly(propene))
  • Absence of oxygen (for some catalysts) or controlled oxygen (for radical initiators)
