🤜 In a Nutshell

Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons featuring a reactive C=C double bond, comprising a sigma and a pi bond, leading to a trigonal planar geometry and restricted rotation essential for E/Z stereoisomerism. Their electron-rich pi bond makes them susceptible to electrophilic addition reactions, where Markownikoff's rule predicts major products based on carbocation stability, making them fundamental building blocks for polymers and other organic syntheses.

🧠 Conceptual Understanding

Alright class, let's talk about alkenes. Think of them as the 'active' members of the hydrocarbon family, unlike their 'saturated' cousins, the alkanes. What makes them so special? It's that carbon-carbon double bond (C=C). This isn't just one bond; it's actually two different types: a strong sigma (σ) bond and a weaker pi (π) bond. The presence of this 'extra' π bond makes alkenes 'unsaturated' because they have fewer hydrogens than they could potentially hold. This π bond is the key to their reactivity – it's exposed and easily broken, making alkenes hungry for electrons. This 'electron-rich' nature means they readily undergo addition reactions, where the double bond 'opens up' and new atoms attach across it. This property is vital, from making plastics to synthesizing new drugs, and understanding its mechanism, called electrophilic addition, is fundamental to organic chemistry. We'll also dive into how the rigid structure of the double bond can lead to fascinating 'E/Z' isomers, creating molecules with the same formula but different 3D arrangements, impacting their properties.

📌 Core Theory

  • Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons containing at least one carbon-carbon double bond (C=C).
  • The C=C bond consists of one sigma (σ) bond and one pi (π) bond.
  • The σ bond is formed by the direct, head-on overlap of atomic orbitals.
  • The π bond is formed by the sideways overlap of p-orbitals above and below the plane of the σ bond.
  • The π bond restricts rotation around the C=C bond, leading to the possibility of stereoisomerism.
  • The region around each carbon atom in the C=C bond is trigonal planar, with bond angles of approximately 120°, due to electron pair repulsion between the three bonding regions.
  • Stereoisomers are compounds with the same structural formula but a different arrangement of atoms in space.
  • E/Z isomerism (geometric isomerism) occurs in alkenes when each carbon atom of the C=C bond is bonded to two different groups.
  • The Cahn-Ingold-Prelog (CIP) priority rules are used to assign E (entgegen, opposite) or Z (zusammen, together) configurations to stereoisomers.
  • Cis-trans isomerism is a special case of E/Z isomerism where identical groups are on the same side (cis) or opposite sides (trans) of the double bond, only applicable when each carbon has at least one identical group.
  • Alkenes are reactive because the π bond is weaker than the σ bond and more exposed, making it susceptible to attack by electron-deficient species.
  • Alkenes undergo addition reactions, where the π bond breaks and atoms are added across the double bond.
  • Typical addition reactions include hydrogenation (with H₂), halogenation (with X₂, e.g., Br₂ water as a test for unsaturation), hydrohalogenation (with HX), and hydration (with H₂O/steam).
  • An electrophile is an electron-pair acceptor, often positively charged or possessing a partial positive charge.
  • Electrophilic addition is the characteristic reaction mechanism for alkenes, involving the heterolytic fission of the π bond.
  • Markownikoff's rule predicts the major product in addition reactions of H-X to unsymmetrical alkenes: the hydrogen atom adds to the carbon atom of the C=C bond that already has the greater number of hydrogen atoms.
  • Carbocation stability (tertiary > secondary > primary) influences the major product formation in electrophilic addition, as the more stable carbocation intermediate is formed more readily.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

The unsaturated nature of alkenes stems directly from the carbon-carbon double bond. This bond is not merely a 'stronger single bond'; it's a combination of a robust sigma (σ) bond and a more diffuse, weaker pi (π) bond. The σ bond provides the primary link, formed by axial overlap of hybrid orbitals. The π bond, on the other hand, results from the sideways overlap of unhybridised p-orbitals, existing above and below the plane of the molecule. This spatial arrangement locks the groups attached to the double-bonded carbons into a rigid, planar geometry, preventing free rotation. This rigidity is crucial for E/Z isomerism, where different arrangements of substituents around the double bond lead to distinct compounds with different properties. The electron density of the π bond is readily accessible, making it a nucleophilic site attractive to electrophiles (electron-pair acceptors). In electrophilic addition, the electrophile attacks the π bond, causing it to break heterolytically and form a carbocation intermediate. This intermediate then rapidly reacts with a nucleophile to form the final addition product. For unsymmetrical alkenes reacting with unsymmetrical reagents like H-X, Markownikoff's rule guides the regioselectivity, ensuring the most stable carbocation intermediate is formed, leading to a predictable major product. The relative instability and exposed nature of the π bond explain why alkenes are far more reactive than alkanes, making them invaluable building blocks in organic synthesis.

1. Assigning E/Z Configuration to a Stereoisomer

Problem: Determine if the following molecule is an E or Z isomer: CH₃CH=C(Cl)CH₂CH₃

Steps: 1. First, draw the molecule showing the double bond and all substituents clearly. 2. Identify the two carbon atoms involved in the double bond. Let's call them C1 and C2. 3. For C1, identify the two groups attached: H and CH₃. Assign priorities based on atomic number: C (in CH₃) has higher atomic number than H. So, CH₃ is higher priority. 4. For C2, identify the two groups attached: Cl and CH₂CH₃. Assign priorities: Cl has atomic number 17, C (in CH₂CH₃) has atomic number 6. So, Cl is higher priority. 5. Compare the positions of the higher priority groups. If they are on the same side of the double bond (both above or both below), it's Z (zusammen, together). If they are on opposite sides, it's E (entgegen, opposite). 6. Assuming the structure is (CH₃)C=C(Cl)(CH₂CH₃) with H on C1 and Cl on C2 on opposite sides, and CH₃ on C1 and CH₂CH₃ on C2 on opposite sides. Let's clarify drawing: Assume the question implies (CH₃)C(H)=C(Cl)(CH₂CH₃). Priorities: C1: CH₃ > H. C2: Cl > CH₂CH₃. If CH₃ and Cl are on the same side, it's Z. If on opposite sides, it's E. 7. Let's represent it clearly: Imagine C1-CH₃ and C1-H. C2-Cl and C2-CH₂CH₃. If CH₃ is 'up' and Cl is 'up', then Z. If CH₃ is 'up' and Cl is 'down', then E. For example, if it's (E)-2-chloro-2-pentene.

Answer: To determine E/Z isomerism for CH₃CH=C(Cl)CH₂CH₃ (which is 2-chloro-2-pentene), let's consider the groups on each carbon of the double bond:On C2 (left carbon of C=C): The groups are -H and -CH₃. Priority: -CH₃ (C atom) > -H (H atom).On C3 (right carbon of C=C): The groups are -Cl and -CH₂CH₃. Priority: -Cl (Cl atom) > -CH₂CH₃ (C atom).If the two higher-priority groups (-CH₃ and -Cl) are on the same side of the double bond, it is the Z isomer. If they are on opposite sides, it is the E isomer. The problem statement does not give the spatial arrangement, only the molecular formula, so both E and Z forms are possible isomers. A specific drawing would be needed to assign. Let's assume a common representation or pick one. For example, if -CH₃ and -Cl are on opposite sides, it would be the E-isomer.

Teacher's Tip

Always explicitly draw out the molecule in 2D to clearly see the positions of the substituents relative to the double bond. Use a dashed wedge for 'back' and solid wedge for 'front' if needed for clarity in 3D, but for E/Z, a simple planar drawing suffices.

2. Predicting Major Product via Markownikoff's Rule

Problem: Predict the major organic product formed when propene reacts with hydrogen bromide (HBr).

Steps: 1. Draw the structural formula of propene (CH₂=CH-CH₃). 2. Identify the two carbon atoms involved in the double bond. Note that it's an unsymmetrical alkene. 3. Identify the reagent: HBr. It will split into H⁺ (electrophile) and Br⁻ (nucleophile). 4. Apply Markownikoff's rule: The hydrogen atom (H⁺) from HBr will add to the carbon atom of the C=C that already has the greater number of hydrogen atoms. The terminal carbon (CH₂) has two hydrogens, the middle carbon (CH) has one. So, H adds to CH₂. 5. This addition forms a carbocation intermediate. Adding H to CH₂ results in a secondary carbocation (CH₃-CH⁺-CH₃). Adding H to CH would result in a primary carbocation (CH₂⁺-CH₂-CH₃). The secondary carbocation is more stable. 6. The bromide ion (Br⁻) then adds to the positively charged carbon of the more stable carbocation. 7. Draw the final major product.

Answer: The major organic product is 2-bromopropane (CH₃CHBrCH₃).

Teacher's Tip

Markownikoff's rule is all about carbocation stability. Secondary carbocations are more stable than primary ones, and tertiary are more stable than secondary. Always choose the pathway that forms the most stable carbocation intermediate.

3. Drawing the Electrophilic Addition Mechanism

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Polymer Production: Alkenes are monomers (small repeating units) that undergo addition polymerisation to form polymers, such as poly(ethene) (plastic bags, bottles) from ethene and poly(propene) from propene.
  • Margarine Production: Unsaturated vegetable oils (containing C=C bonds) are hydrogenated in the presence of a nickel catalyst to produce solid or semi-solid margarines. This reduces the number of C=C bonds, increasing the melting point.
  • Ripening of Fruits: Ethene (ethylene) is a natural plant hormone that triggers the ripening process in fruits. It is used commercially to ripen fruits like bananas after transport.
  • Industrial Alcohol Synthesis: Ethene can be hydrated (reacted with steam) to produce ethanol, an important industrial solvent and biofuel. This is a large-scale industrial process.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing cis-trans isomerism with E/Z isomerism: Cis-trans is a specific case of E/Z, applicable only when identical groups are present on each carbon of the double bond. E/Z is more general.
  • ❌ Incorrectly drawing curly arrows in mechanisms: Arrows must originate from electron density (bonds or lone pairs) and point to electron-deficient sites. Double-headed arrows indicate movement of an electron pair.
  • ❌ Believing the double bond is 'just' two single bonds: Not understanding the distinct nature and reactivity of the π bond compared to the σ bond.
  • ❌ Forgetting that the C=C bond prevents rotation: This is key to understanding stereoisomerism.
  • ❌ Applying Markownikoff's rule incorrectly or to symmetrical alkenes/reagents: It applies to unsymmetrical alkenes and unsymmetrical H-X reagents.
  • ❌ Drawing carbocation intermediates incorrectly: The positive charge must be on a carbon, and the carbon must have only three bonds.
Memory Anchor

Remember 'MERP': Markownikoff's Electrophilic Rule for Protonation. The H⁺ goes to the carbon with More Hydrogens, forming the more stable carbocation, leading to the Major Product. Also, 'Zame Zide, E-pposite' for E/Z isomers and higher priority groups.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: When 2-methylpropene (CH₂=C(CH₃)₂) reacts with HCl, the major organic product formed is 2-chloro-2-methylpropane. [Diagram 1: Two carbon atoms, each with three sp² orbitals (one pointing to other C, two to H's) forming sigma bonds, and one p-orbital perpendicular to the plane. B) Each carbon atom of the C=C double bond must be attached to two different groups. For E/Z isomerism to occur, there must be restricted rotation around the C=C bond and each carbon atom involved in the double bond must be bonded to two different groups.

6. Consider the molecule 1,2-dichloroethene. Which statement is true regarding its E/Z isomerism? A) It exhibits E/Z isomerism, and the cis isomer is the Z isomer. B) It exhibits E/Z isomerism, and the cis isomer is the E isomer. C) It does not exhibit E/Z isomerism because it is symmetrical. D) It only exhibits cis-trans isomerism, not E/Z.

Answer

A) It exhibits E/Z isomerism, and the cis isomer is the Z isomer. 1,2-dichloroethene exhibits E/Z isomerism. When the two high-priority groups (chlorine atoms) are on the same side of the double bond, it is the Z isomer, which corresponds to the cis arrangement.

7. Why are alkenes generally more reactive than alkanes towards electrophiles? A) Alkenes have a higher overall bond enthalpy. B) The pi (π) bond in alkenes is relatively strong and unreactive. C) The pi (π) bond in alkenes is an electron-rich region and is relatively easily broken. D) Alkenes are saturated, allowing for easier bond breaking.

Answer

C) The pi (π) bond in alkenes is an electron-rich region and is relatively easily broken. The pi (π) bond in alkenes is a region of high electron density and has a relatively low bond enthalpy compared to a sigma bond, making it susceptible to attack by electrophiles and easily broken.

8. Which of the following best describes an electrophile? A) An electron-rich species that donates a pair of electrons. B) An electron-deficient species that accepts a pair of electrons. C) A species with an unpaired electron. D) A species that initiates a free radical reaction.

Answer

B) An electron-deficient species that accepts a pair of electrons. An electrophile is an 'electron-loving' species that is electron-deficient and seeks an electron pair, acting as a Lewis acid.

9. When propene reacts with hydrogen bromide (HBr), what is the major organic product formed? A) 1-bromopropane B) 2-bromopropane C) Propan-1-ol D) Propane

Answer

B) 2-bromopropane According to Markownikoff's rule, in the addition of HBr to unsymmetrical propene, the hydrogen adds to the carbon with more hydrogens already (CH2), and the bromine adds to the carbon with fewer hydrogens (CH), resulting in 2-bromopropane as the major product.

10. According to Markownikoff's rule, in the addition of H-X to an unsymmetrical alkene, which carbon atom of the C=C bond does the hydrogen atom preferentially attach to? A) The carbon atom with fewer hydrogen atoms already attached. B) The carbon atom with more hydrogen atoms already attached. C) The carbon atom closest to the end of the chain. D) The carbon atom that is bonded to the largest alkyl group.

Answer

B) The carbon atom with more hydrogen atoms already attached. Markownikoff's rule states that the hydrogen atom of the hydrogen halide (H-X) adds to the carbon atom of the alkene that already has the greater number of hydrogen atoms attached, leading to the more stable carbocation intermediate.

🗂️ Flashcards (15)

1. What is an alkene?

Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons containing at least one carbon-carbon double bond (C=C). This double bond makes them more reactive than alkanes.

Tags: definition, hydrocarbons, structure, unsaturation

2. Describe the C=C bond in alkenes.

The C=C bond consists of one strong sigma (σ) bond and one weaker pi (π) bond. The σ bond forms by head-on overlap of orbitals, while the π bond forms by sideways overlap of p-orbitals.

Tags: bonding, structure, pi-bond, sigma-bond

3. Why is rotation restricted around a C=C bond?

The pi (π) bond in the C=C prevents free rotation around the bond axis. Breaking the π bond would require significant energy, which is why rotation is restricted at room temperature.

Tags: bonding, structure, stereoisomerism

4. Explain the shape and bond angle around each carbon in a C=C bond.

Each carbon atom in a C=C bond is surrounded by three regions of electron density (two single bonds and one double bond). According to VSEPR theory, these repel each other to give a trigonal planar shape with bond angles of approximately 120 degrees.

Tags: VSEPR, shape, bonding, structure

5. What are stereoisomers?

Stereoisomers are molecules that have the same structural formula and bonding but a different spatial arrangement of atoms. They cannot be interconverted without breaking and reforming bonds.

Tags: definition, isomerism, stereoisomerism

6. What is E/Z isomerism?

E/Z isomerism is a type of stereoisomerism found in alkenes due to restricted rotation around the C=C bond and different groups attached to each carbon of the double bond. 'E' (entgegen) means opposite, and 'Z' (zusammen) means together for higher priority groups.

Tags: definition, isomerism, E/Z

7. How does E/Z isomerism differ from cis-trans isomerism?

Cis-trans isomerism is a specific case of E/Z isomerism, applicable only when each carbon of the double bond has at least one hydrogen atom. E/Z isomerism is a more general system that uses Cahn-Ingold-Prelog (CIP) priority rules for any groups.

Tags: compare, isomerism, E/Z, cis-trans

8. How are Cahn–Ingold–Prelog (CIP) rules used to identify E/Z isomers?

For each carbon in the C=C, assign priority to the two attached groups based on atomic number of the first atom. If the two higher-priority groups are on the same side, it's the Z-isomer; if on opposite sides, it's the E-isomer.

Tags: process, isomerism, E/Z, CIP

9. When can E/Z isomerism occur in alkenes?

E/Z isomerism occurs when there is restricted rotation around the C=C double bond, and each carbon of the double bond is attached to two different groups. If either carbon has two identical groups, E/Z isomerism is not possible.

Tags: isomerism, E/Z, conditions

10. Why are alkenes generally more reactive than alkanes?

Alkenes are more reactive due to the presence of the pi (π) bond in the C=C double bond. This π bond has a lower bond enthalpy than a sigma bond and is more exposed, making it susceptible to attack by electrophiles.

Tags: reactivity, bonding, pi-bond

11. Describe the test for unsaturation using bromine water.

Add aqueous bromine (orange-brown) to the organic sample. If an alkene is present, the bromine water will rapidly decolorize as bromine adds across the C=C double bond in an electrophilic addition reaction.

Tags: test, addition reaction, halogenation, PAG

12. Outline the addition reaction of an alkene with hydrogen.

Alkenes react with hydrogen gas in the presence of a nickel catalyst (or platinum/palladium) at high temperature and pressure. Hydrogen adds across the C=C bond, converting the alkene into a saturated alkane.

Tags: addition reaction, hydrogenation, catalysis

13. Outline the general electrophilic addition of an alkene with H-X (where X is a halogen).

The π electrons of the alkene attack the partially positive hydrogen of H-X, forming a carbocation and a halide ion. The halide ion then attacks the carbocation, forming a haloalkane.

Tags: addition reaction, mechanism, electrophile

14. What is an electrophile?

An electrophile is an electron-deficient species that is attracted to regions of high electron density. It acts as an electron pair acceptor in a chemical reaction, often initiating addition reactions with alkenes.

Tags: definition, electrophile, mechanism

15. State Markownikoff's rule for addition reactions.

When a hydrogen halide (HX) adds to an unsymmetrical alkene, the hydrogen atom adds to the carbon atom of the double bond that already has the greater number of hydrogen atoms. This typically forms the more stable carbocation intermediate.

Tags: Markownikoff's rule, addition reaction, regioselectivity, carbocation

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The study of alkenes dates back to the early 19th century with the isolation of ethene (then called 'olefiant gas') by Dutch chemists who noticed its oil-forming properties when reacted with chlorine (hence 'oil-forming' or 'olefin'). The concept of the double bond was fully developed later in the century by chemists like Kekulé. Markownikoff's rule, formulated in 1869 by Vladimir Markownikoff, was initially an empirical observation, but its explanation in terms of carbocation stability only became clear with the development of mechanistic organic chemistry in the 20th century.

⚖️ Ethics

The widespread use of alkenes in polymer production, particularly poly(ethene) and poly(propene), presents significant ethical and environmental challenges. While these plastics are incredibly versatile and cheap, their persistence in the environment (plastic pollution), reliance on fossil fuels for production, and difficulties in recycling pose serious concerns. Research into biodegradable polymers, recycling technologies, and sustainable sources (e.g., bio-based ethene) aims to mitigate these impacts. The industrial-scale hydration of ethene for ethanol also raises questions about land use for biomass if it were to become the primary source for fuel ethanol.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond simple carbocation stability, more advanced considerations in electrophilic addition include carbocation rearrangements (e.g., hydride shifts or alkyl shifts) which can occur to form even more stable carbocation intermediates before the nucleophilic attack, leading to unexpected products. Furthermore, the stereochemistry of addition can be influenced by the structure of the alkene and the attacking electrophile; for example, the formation of cyclic intermediates like bromonium ions can enforce anti-addition. Frontier Molecular Orbital (FMO) theory provides a quantum mechanical understanding of why the HOMO of the alkene (the π bond) interacts with the LUMO of the electrophile to initiate the reaction.

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

The primary practical for alkenes is the 'Test for Unsaturated Hydrocarbons using Bromine Water (PAG7)'.

Equipment: Test tubes, dropping pipette, stoppers, samples of alkene (e.g., cyclohexene) and alkane (e.g., cyclohexane), bromine water (aqueous bromine).

Procedure: 1. Add 1-2 cm³ of the unknown organic liquid to a test tube. 2. Add a few drops of orange/brown bromine water to the test tube. 3. Stopper the test tube and shake gently. 4. Observe any colour change.

Expected Observation: If the liquid contains a C=C double bond (an alkene), the orange/brown colour of the bromine water will rapidly disappear (decolorise). If the liquid is saturated (an alkane), the bromine water colour will remain.

Safety Notes: * Bromine water is corrosive and an irritant. Use in a fume cupboard and wear eye protection. * Alkenes and alkanes are typically flammable; keep away from naked flames. * Avoid inhaling vapours.

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Alkenes: Generally flammable. Ethene is a gas, but higher alkenes are volatile liquids. Keep away from ignition sources. Vapours can form explosive mixtures with air.
  • 🛑 Bromine water (Br₂(aq)): Corrosive and an irritant. Vapours are toxic. Causes burns. Use in a fume cupboard with eye protection and gloves.
  • 🛑 Hydrogen halides (HCl, HBr, HI): Corrosive gases/solutions. Cause severe burns and respiratory irritation. Handle in a fume cupboard with appropriate PPE.
  • 🛑 Concentrated sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) / Phosphoric(V) acid (H₃PO₄): Highly corrosive. Strong oxidising and dehydrating agents. Cause severe burns. Handle with extreme care, wearing eye protection and gloves, in a fume cupboard.
  • 🛑 Nickel catalyst: May be an irritant. Finely divided nickel can be pyrophoric (ignite spontaneously in air).

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. Electrophilic Addition (e.g., Ethene + HBr):
  2. Step 1: Electrophilic attack. The electron-rich π bond in ethene is attracted to the partially positive hydrogen (δ⁺H) in HBr. A pair of electrons from the C=C bond attacks the H atom, forming a new C-H bond. Simultaneously, the H-Br bond undergoes heterolytic fission, with both electrons moving to the more electronegative bromine atom, forming a bromide ion (Br⁻). (Curly arrow from C=C bond to H atom of H-Br. Curly arrow from H-Br bond to Br atom.)
  3. Step 2: Carbocation formation. This results in a positively charged carbon species called a carbocation (CH₃CH₂⁺ for ethene). The other product is a bromide ion (Br⁻).
  4. Step 3: Nucleophilic attack. The electron-rich bromide ion (Br⁻), acting as a nucleophile, rapidly attacks the electron-deficient carbocation. A lone pair of electrons from the Br⁻ forms a new C-Br bond with the positively charged carbon atom. (Curly arrow from a lone pair on Br⁻ to the positively charged carbon of the carbocation.)
  5. Step 4: Product formation. This forms the final product, bromoethane (CH₃CH₂Br).
  6. Electrophilic Addition (e.g., Ethene + Br₂ in organic solvent):
  7. Step 1: Induced dipole and electrophilic attack. As a non-polar Br₂ molecule approaches the electron-rich C=C bond, the π electrons induce a temporary dipole in the Br₂ molecule (δ⁺Br-δ⁻Br). The π electrons attack the δ⁺Br, and the Br-Br bond breaks heterolytically, forming a cyclic bromonium ion and a bromide ion (Br⁻).
  8. Step 2: Nucleophilic attack. The bromide ion (Br⁻) then attacks one of the carbon atoms in the bromonium ion from the side opposite to the existing C-Br bond (anti-addition). This opens the ring and forms 1,2-dibromoethane.

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Hydrogenation (addition of H₂): High pressure, 150°C, nickel (Ni) catalyst.
  • Halogenation (addition of X₂, e.g., Br₂): Room temperature, in an organic solvent (e.g., tetrachloromethane, CCl₄, or aqueous solution for bromine water test). No catalyst required.
  • Hydrohalogenation (addition of HX, e.g., HBr): Room temperature, often in an inert solvent or as a gas reaction. No catalyst required.
  • Hydration (addition of H₂O/steam): 300°C, 60-70 atm pressure, concentrated phosphoric(V) acid (H₃PO₄) catalyst, or concentrated sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) followed by hydrolysis.

🔦 Element Spotlight

While not an element, the carbon-carbon double bond is the central feature. Each carbon atom involved in the double bond is sp² hybridised, meaning one 2s orbital mixes with two 2p orbitals to form three sp² hybrid orbitals. These lie in a trigonal planar arrangement (120° bond angles). The remaining unhybridised 2p orbital on each carbon lies perpendicular to this plane. The direct overlap of two sp² orbitals forms the strong σ bond between the carbons, while the sideways overlap of the two unhybridised p-orbitals forms the weaker π bond, residing above and below the σ bond. This unique bonding arrangement dictates the alkene's flat geometry, restricted rotation, and its high reactivity towards electrophiles due to the exposed, electron-rich π cloud.
