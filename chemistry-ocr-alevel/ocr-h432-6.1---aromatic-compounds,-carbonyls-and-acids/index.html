🧠 Conceptual Understanding

This topic expands our understanding of organic functional groups by introducing aromatic compounds, particularly benzene and its derivatives, along with the versatile carbonyl group found in aldehydes and ketones, and the acidic properties of carboxylic acids and their related functional groups like acyl chlorides and esters. We'll explore how the unique delocalised electron system of benzene gives it exceptional stability and dictates its characteristic reactions (electrophilic substitution). We'll also examine the polarity of the carbonyl group, which makes aldehydes and ketones susceptible to nucleophilic addition. Finally, we'll delve into the distinctive acidity of carboxylic acids and understand the synthesis and hydrolysis of their key derivatives, acyl chlorides and esters, which are vital in drug synthesis and polymer chemistry. Understanding these groups is crucial for comprehending more complex organic molecules and reaction pathways.

📌 Core Theory

  • Aromatic compounds, such as benzene (C₆H₆), possess a planar, cyclic structure with delocalised π-electrons, conferring extra stability.
  • The delocalised electron system in benzene makes it resistant to addition reactions, favouring electrophilic substitution reactions instead.
  • Common electrophilic substitution reactions of benzene include nitration, halogenation, sulfonation, and Friedel-Crafts alkylation/acylation.
  • Phenols are aromatic compounds with a hydroxyl group directly attached to the benzene ring, making them more acidic than alcohols due to resonance stabilisation of the phenoxide ion.
  • The carbonyl group (C=O) is present in aldehydes (RCHO) and ketones (RCOR'), which are polar due to the electronegativity difference between carbon and oxygen.
  • Aldehydes and ketones undergo nucleophilic addition reactions at the carbonyl carbon.
  • Carboxylic acids (RCOOH) are weak acids, capable of donating a proton due to the resonance stabilisation of the carboxylate ion.
  • Esters (RCOOR') are derivatives of carboxylic acids formed by esterification with alcohols, and can be hydrolysed back into carboxylic acids and alcohols.
  • Acyl chlorides (RCOCl) are highly reactive carboxylic acid derivatives, readily undergoing nucleophilic acyl substitution reactions with water, alcohols, phenols, and amines.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

Aromatic compounds are characterised by the presence of a benzene ring, a six-membered carbon ring with alternating single and double bonds, but crucially, a delocalised system of six π-electrons. This delocalisation provides significant stability, which means benzene does not readily undergo typical alkene addition reactions. Instead, it undergoes electrophilic substitution, where an electrophile replaces a hydrogen atom on the ring. Phenols, with their -OH group directly attached, show enhanced reactivity towards electrophilic substitution and are more acidic than simple alcohols due to the delocalisation of the lone pair on oxygen into the ring. Carbonyl compounds, specifically aldehydes and ketones, feature a C=O functional group. The polarity of this bond, with a partial positive charge on carbon and a partial negative charge on oxygen, makes the carbonyl carbon an ideal site for nucleophilic attack, leading to nucleophilic addition reactions. Carboxylic acids, with their -COOH group, are weak acids because their conjugate base, the carboxylate ion, is stabilised by resonance. They react with bases, carbonates, and active metals. Derivatives like esters and acyl chlorides are formed from carboxylic acids. Esters are known for their sweet smells and are formed via esterification. Acyl chlorides are highly reactive and are valuable synthetic intermediates for creating other carboxylic acid derivatives, undergoing nucleophilic acyl substitution.

📖 Key Definitions

Aromatic compound A cyclic, planar molecule with a delocalised system of π-electrons following Hückel's rule (4n+2 π-electrons).
Delocalisation The spreading out of electron density over multiple atoms, typically through a system of overlapping p-orbitals, leading to increased stability.
Electrophilic substitution A reaction where an electrophile substitutes a group (usually hydrogen) on an aromatic ring, maintaining the aromatic system.
Nucleophilic addition A reaction where a nucleophile adds across a double or triple bond (e.g., C=O or C=N), typically involving the breaking of the π-bond.
Carbonyl group A functional group consisting of a carbon atom double-bonded to an oxygen atom (C=O).
Esterification The chemical reaction that forms an ester from an alcohol and a carboxylic acid (or a derivative like an acyl chloride).
Acyl chloride A carboxylic acid derivative with a -COCl functional group, known for its high reactivity in nucleophilic acyl substitution reactions.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Nitration of Benzene

Problem: Show the mechanism for the nitration of benzene, including the formation of the electrophile and regeneration of the catalyst.

Steps: 1. Step 1: Formation of the electrophile. Concentrated nitric acid (HNO₃) reacts with concentrated sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) to form the nitronium ion, NO₂⁺, which is the electrophile. HNO₃ + H₂SO₄ → NO₂⁺ + HSO₄⁻ + H₂O. 2. Step 2: Electrophilic attack. The delocalised π-electron cloud of the benzene ring is attracted to the electron-deficient nitronium ion (NO₂⁺). A pair of electrons from the benzene ring attacks the NO₂⁺ ion, forming a dative covalent bond and breaking the delocalised system to form a carbocation intermediate (sigma complex). 3. Step 3: Loss of a proton. The HSO₄⁻ ion (from the sulfuric acid catalyst) acts as a base, abstracting a proton from the carbon atom bonded to the nitro group. This allows the electrons from the C-H bond to reform the delocalised π-system, regenerating the aromatic ring and forming H₂SO₄. 4. Overall Reaction: C₆H₆ + HNO₃ → C₆H₅NO₂ + H₂O

Answer: The mechanism involves the formation of the nitronium ion (NO₂⁺) from nitric and sulfuric acids, which then attacks the benzene ring. A carbocation intermediate is formed, followed by the loss of a proton to restore aromaticity and regenerate the sulfuric acid catalyst.

Teacher's Tip

Always remember to show the movement of electron pairs with curly arrows, especially in the formation of the electrophile, the attack on the ring, and the regeneration of the catalyst.

2. Reduction of Propanal to Propan-1-ol

Problem: Outline the nucleophilic addition mechanism for the reduction of propanal (CH₃CH₂CHO) using sodium borohydride (NaBH₄) followed by dilute acid.

Steps: 1. Step 1: Nucleophilic attack by hydride. The hydride ion (H⁻), derived from NaBH₄, acts as a nucleophile. It attacks the partially positive carbonyl carbon of propanal, while the π-electrons of the C=O bond shift onto the oxygen, forming an alkoxide intermediate (CH₃CH₂CH₂O⁻). 2. Step 2: Protonation of the alkoxide. In a separate step, usually after the initial reduction, the alkoxide intermediate is protonated by dilute acid (H⁺/H₂O) to form the alcohol, propan-1-ol (CH₃CH₂CH₂OH).

Answer: Propanal undergoes nucleophilic addition. A hydride ion (H⁻) from NaBH₄ attacks the carbonyl carbon, forming an alkoxide. Subsequent protonation by dilute acid yields propan-1-ol.

Teacher's Tip

NaBH₄ provides H⁻ nucleophiles, while LiAlH₄ is a stronger reducing agent. Remember that aldehydes reduce to primary alcohols and ketones reduce to secondary alcohols.

3. Esterification of Ethanoic Acid and Ethanol

Problem: Write the balanced equation and describe the conditions for the formation of ethyl ethanoate from ethanoic acid and ethanol.

Steps: 1. Step 1: Write the reactants. Ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH) and ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH). 2. Step 2: Identify the functional groups and products. The -OH from the carboxylic acid and -H from the alcohol combine to form H₂O, and the remaining parts join to form the ester. So, CH₃COOCH₂CH₃ (ethyl ethanoate) and H₂O. 3. Step 3: Balance the equation. The equation is already balanced: CH₃COOH + CH₃CH₂OH ⇌ CH₃COOCH₂CH₃ + H₂O. 4. Step 4: State the conditions. The reaction is heated under reflux with a concentrated strong acid catalyst, typically concentrated H₂SO₄.

Answer: CH₃COOH + CH₃CH₂OH ⇌ CH₃COOCH₂CH₃ + H₂O. The reaction is carried out by heating ethanoic acid and ethanol under reflux in the presence of a concentrated sulfuric acid catalyst.

Teacher's Tip

Esterification is a reversible reaction, hence the equilibrium arrow. Sulfuric acid acts as both a catalyst and a dehydrating agent, shifting the equilibrium to the right by removing water.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Pharmaceuticals: Many drugs, like aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid, an ester) and paracetamol, contain aromatic rings and/or carboxylic acid derivatives. Phenols are used as antiseptics and disinfectants.
  • Fragrances and Flavourings: Esters are responsible for the pleasant scents and tastes of many fruits and flowers, widely used in food and perfume industries (e.g., ethyl acetate, pentyl ethanoate).
  • Polymers and Plastics: Carboxylic acids and their derivatives are key monomers in the production of polymers like polyesters (e.g., PET bottles) and polyamides (e.g., nylon).
  • Solvents: Acetone (a ketone) and ethyl acetate (an ester) are common industrial and laboratory solvents, used in nail polish removers, glues, and paints.
  • Dyes and Pigments: Aromatic compounds are fundamental building blocks for many synthetic dyes due to their stable electron systems that absorb visible light.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing benzene with cyclohexatriene: Benzene is not an alkene and does not undergo addition reactions readily due to delocalisation.
  • ❌ Incorrectly drawing curly arrows in mechanisms: Arrows must originate from a source of electrons (lone pair, bond) and point to an electron-deficient centre.
  • ❌ Forgetting the role of the catalyst (e.g., H₂SO₄ in nitration) in both formation of the electrophile and regeneration.
  • ❌ Assuming all carboxylic acid derivatives have the same reactivity; acyl chlorides are significantly more reactive than esters.
  • ❌ Not distinguishing between aldehydes and ketones in terms of reducing properties (aldehydes reduce Tollens'/Fehling's, ketones do not).
Memory Anchor

Think of Benzene as a 'chemical fortress' due to its delocalised shield. Electrophiles need to sneak in (substitution) rather than blast through (addition). Carbonyls are 'electron magnets', drawing in nucleophiles. Carboxylic acids are 'proton donors' because their 'donated' proton leaves behind a stable, resonating ion.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: The mechanism involves two steps. Firstly, the cyanide ion (CN⁻), acting as a nucleophile, attacks the partially positive carbon atom of the ethanal carbonyl group. Secondly, the alkoxide intermediate is protonated by H⁺ (from HCN or water), leading to the formation of 2-hydroxypropanenitrile.

Q4. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

Compare the acidity of ethanoic acid, phenol, and ethanol. Explain your reasoning.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: The order of acidity is ethanoic acid > phenol > ethanol. Ethanoic acid is the strongest due to the resonance stabilisation of its conjugate base, the ethanoate ion, where the negative charge is delocalised over two oxygen atoms. Phenol is next; its phenoxide ion is stabilised by delocalisation of the negative charge into the benzene ring. Ethanol is the least acidic as its ethoxide ion conjugate base has no resonance stabilisation, meaning the negative charge is localised on the oxygen atom, making it highly unstable.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Order of acidity: Ethanoic acid > Phenol > Ethanol (1 mark).
  • ✅ Ethanoic acid's carboxylate ion is stabilised by resonance, delocalising the negative charge over two oxygen atoms (1 mark).
  • ✅ Phenol's phenoxide ion is stabilised by resonance, delocalising the negative charge into the benzene ring (1 mark).
  • ✅ Ethanol's ethoxide ion has no resonance stabilisation, and the negative charge is localised on a single oxygen atom, making it the least stable (1 mark).

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This is a common comparison question. The key is to explain the stability of the conjugate base using resonance arguments.

Q1. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

Explain, with the aid of a diagram, the delocalised bonding in benzene.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Benzene is a planar, cyclic molecule where each of the six carbon atoms is sp² hybridised. Each carbon forms three σ-bonds: one to a hydrogen atom and two to adjacent carbon atoms. The remaining unhybridised p-orbital on each carbon atom lies perpendicular to the plane of the ring. These six p-orbitals overlap laterally both above and below the plane of the ring. This extensive overlap forms a continuous doughnut-shaped cloud of delocalised π-electrons, distributing the electron density evenly over all six carbon atoms. This delocalisation significantly enhances the stability of the benzene ring. (A diagram showing a hexagon with an inner circle or a clearer representation of p-orbital overlap would be included here.)

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Diagram: Benzene ring with alternating single/double bonds (Kekulé) or hexagon with inner circle (delocalised model) (1 mark).

  • ✅ The remaining p-orbital on each carbon atom overlaps above and below the plane of the ring (1 mark).

Q3. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

Phenol reacts with bromine water to produce 2,4,6-tribromophenol. Benzene, however, requires a halogen carrier (e.g., FeBr₃) and anhydrous conditions to react with bromine. Account for this difference in reactivity.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Phenol is significantly more reactive than benzene towards electrophilic substitution due to the presence of the hydroxyl (-OH) group. The lone pair of electrons on the oxygen atom of the -OH group can be delocalised into the benzene ring, increasing the overall electron density of the aromatic system. This electron-donating effect strongly activates the ring, making it much more susceptible to attack by electrophiles, especially at the 2, 4, and 6 (ortho and para) positions. Consequently, phenol reacts readily with bromine water (a relatively weak electrophile) to form 2,4,6-tribromophenol, without the need for a halogen carrier. Benzene, lacking such an activating group, has a lower electron density and therefore requires a stronger electrophile, generated by a halogen carrier like FeBr₃, to react with bromine.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Phenol has a hydroxyl (-OH) group directly attached to the benzene ring (1 mark).
  • ✅ The lone pair of electrons on the oxygen atom of the -OH group is delocalised into the benzene ring (1 mark).
  • ✅ This increases the electron density of the benzene ring, activating it towards electrophilic attack (1 mark).
  • ✅ This activation is particularly strong at the 2, 4, and 6 positions (ortho and para directing) (1 mark).
  • ✅ Therefore, phenol is sufficiently activated to react with bromine water (a weaker electrophile) without a halogen carrier (1 mark).

Q4. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

Compound A (C₅H₁₀O₂) reacts with warm aqueous NaOH to produce compound B (C₃H₅O₂⁻Na⁺) and compound C (C₂H₅OH). Compound B, when acidified, forms propanoic acid. Deduce the structure of compound A and draw the mechanism for its reaction with NaOH.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Compound A, with molecular formula C₅H₁₀O₂, reacts with warm aqueous NaOH to produce a salt and an alcohol, indicating it is an ester undergoing alkaline hydrolysis. Compound C is ethanol (C₂H₅OH), meaning the alcohol part of the ester is derived from ethanol. Compound B, when acidified, forms propanoic acid (CH₃CH₂COOH), indicating the carboxylic acid part of the ester is propanoic acid. Therefore, compound A is ethyl propanoate (CH₃CH₂COOCH₂CH₃).

Mechanism for alkaline hydrolysis: 1. The hydroxide ion (OH⁻) from NaOH acts as a nucleophile, attacking the partially positive carbonyl carbon of ethyl propanoate. The π-electrons of the C=O bond shift onto the oxygen, forming a tetrahedral intermediate with a negative charge on the oxygen. 2. The tetrahedral intermediate collapses, reforming the carbonyl bond and expelling the ethoxide ion (CH₃CH₂O⁻) as a leaving group. This forms propanoic acid initially, but in alkaline conditions, it immediately deprotonates to form the propanoate ion (CH₃CH₂COO⁻). 3. The ethoxide ion (CH₃CH₂O⁻) will then abstract a proton from water or the propanoic acid (if formed), ultimately yielding ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH) and the propanoate ion.

Q6. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

Starting from benzene, outline a multi-step synthesis for the production of 4-nitrophenol. Include all reagents, conditions, and indicate the functional groups at each stage.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Synthesis of 4-nitrophenol from benzene requires a multi-step approach due to the conflicting directing effects of the hydroxyl and nitro groups. We must introduce the nitro group after the hydroxyl group, or protect the hydroxyl group first. A common route is: 1. Nitration of Benzene: React benzene with concentrated nitric acid and concentrated sulfuric acid, heated below 50°C, to form nitrobenzene. The nitro group is meta-directing. 2. Reduction of Nitrobenzene: Reduce nitrobenzene to phenylamine using tin and concentrated hydrochloric acid, followed by neutralisation with NaOH. The amino group is ortho/para directing and activating. 3. Diazotisation of Phenylamine: React phenylamine with sodium nitrite and dilute hydrochloric acid at 0-10°C to form benzenediazonium chloride. 4. Hydrolysis to Phenol: Warm the benzenediazonium chloride solution with water to produce phenol. 5. Nitration of Phenol: React phenol with dilute nitric acid at room temperature. The hydroxyl group is strongly activating and ortho/para directing, so 4-nitrophenol will be the major product.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Step 1: Nitration of benzene (1 mark). Reagents: Conc. HNO₃, Conc. H₂SO₄. Conditions: Heat below 50°C. Product: Nitrobenzene (1 mark).
  • ✅ Step 2: Reduction of nitrobenzene to phenylamine (1 mark). Reagents: Sn/conc. HCl. Conditions: Heat, then NaOH. Product: Phenylamine (1 mark).
  • ✅ Step 3: Conversion of phenylamine to benzenediazonium chloride (1 mark). Reagents: NaNO₂, dil. HCl. Conditions: 0-10°C. Product: Benzenediazonium chloride.
  • ✅ Step 4: Hydrolysis of benzenediazonium chloride to phenol (1 mark). Reagents: Warm H₂O. Product: Phenol.
  • ✅ Step 5: Nitration of phenol (1 mark). Reagents: Dilute HNO₃. Conditions: Room temperature. Product: 4-nitrophenol (major product) (1 mark).

Q8. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

Explain why nitrobenzene undergoes electrophilic substitution reactions much more slowly than benzene.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Nitrobenzene reacts much more slowly than benzene in electrophilic substitution reactions because the nitro (-NO₂) group is a strong electron-withdrawing group. This group pulls electron density away from the benzene ring, primarily through resonance and inductive effects. This withdrawal of electron density deactivates the ring, making it less 'electron-rich' and thus less attractive to incoming electrophiles. As a result, the formation of the carbocation intermediate (sigma complex) requires a higher activation energy, slowing down the rate of electrophilic substitution.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ The nitro group (-NO₂) is an electron-withdrawing group (1 mark).
  • ✅ It withdraws electron density from the benzene ring via resonance and inductive effects (1 mark).
  • ✅ This deactivates the ring, making it less attractive to electrophiles (1 mark).
  • ✅ Consequently, the carbocation intermediate formed is less stable, increasing the activation energy for the reaction (1 mark).

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This is a direct application of understanding activating/deactivating groups. Emphasise both the electron-withdrawing nature and the consequence for electrophile attraction/activation energy.

🧩 Quick Quiz (MCQ)

1. What is the primary reason for the enhanced stability of benzene compared to a hypothetical cyclic triene with alternating single and double bonds? A) The presence of sp2 hybridised carbon atoms. B) The delocalisation of pi electrons above and below the plane of the ring. C) The strong sigma bonds between carbon atoms. D) The high electronegativity of carbon.

Answer

B) The delocalisation of pi electrons above and below the plane of the ring. Delocalisation of pi electrons forms a stable electron cloud, spreading electron density over the entire ring and lowering the overall energy of the molecule, contributing to its enhanced stability.

2. Why are phenols more acidic than aliphatic alcohols? A) The C-O bond in phenols is stronger. B) The phenoxide ion is stabilised by the delocalisation of the negative charge into the benzene ring. C) Phenols have a higher boiling point than alcohols. D) The hydroxyl group in phenols can form more hydrogen bonds.

Answer

B) The phenoxide ion is stabilised by the delocalisation of the negative charge into the benzene ring. When a phenol loses a proton, the resulting phenoxide ion has its negative charge delocalised into the benzene ring, making it more stable. This increased stability of the conjugate base makes the phenol a stronger acid than an alcohol, where the alkoxide ion cannot be stabilised by delocalisation.

3. Benzene undergoes electrophilic substitution reactions. Which of the following is a common electrophile for the nitration of benzene? A) NO2- B) HNO3 C) NO2+ D) NO+

Answer

C) NO2+ For the nitration of benzene, concentrated nitric acid and concentrated sulfuric acid react to generate the nitronium ion (NO2+), which acts as the electrophile and attacks the electron-rich benzene ring.

4. Which reagent can be used to distinguish between propanal and propanone? A) Acidified potassium dichromate(VI) B) Tollen's reagent C) Fehling's solution D) Both B and C

Answer

D) Both B and CAldehydes (like propanal) can be oxidised to carboxylic acids, while ketones (like propanone) cannot be easily oxidised. Both Tollens' reagent (producing a silver mirror) and Fehling's solution (producing a red precipitate) are mild oxidising agents that react specifically with aldehydes but not ketones.

5. What is the product formed when propanal is reduced using NaBH4 in aqueous solution? A) Propanoic acid B) Propan-1-ol C) Propan-2-ol D) Propane

Answer

B) Propan-1-ol The reduction of an aldehyde (like propanal) using a reducing agent like NaBH4 produces a primary alcohol. Propanal has the carbonyl group at the end of the chain, so it will form propan-1-ol.

6. Arrange the following compounds in order of increasing acidity: phenol, ethanol, ethanoic acid. A) Ethanol < Phenol < Ethanoic acid B) Phenol < Ethanol < Ethanoic acid C) Ethanoic acid < Phenol < Ethanol D) Ethanol < Ethanoic acid < Phenol

Answer

A) Ethanol < Phenol < Ethanoic acid Acidity depends on the stability of the conjugate base formed. The ethanoate ion (from ethanoic acid) is resonance-stabilised, making ethanoic acid the strongest. The phenoxide ion (from phenol) is stabilised by delocalisation into the benzene ring, making phenol more acidic than ethanol. The ethoxide ion (from ethanol) is not stabilised by resonance or delocalisation, making ethanol the weakest acid among the three.

7. Which reagents are typically used to convert a carboxylic acid into an ester via Fischer esterification? A) An alcohol and a strong base. B) An alcohol and a strong acid catalyst. C) An acyl chloride and water. D) An alcohol and a mild oxidising agent.

Answer

B) An alcohol and a strong acid catalyst. Fischer esterification involves reacting a carboxylic acid with an alcohol in the presence of a strong acid catalyst (e.g., concentrated H2SO4) and heating, often with removal of water, to form an ester.

8. Why are acyl chlorides generally more reactive towards nucleophilic substitution than carboxylic acids? A) The carbon-chlorine bond is stronger than the carbon-oxygen bond. B) The chloride ion is a better leaving group than the hydroxide ion. C) Acyl chlorides are less polar than carboxylic acids. D) The carbonyl carbon in acyl chlorides is less electrophilic.

Answer

B) The chloride ion is a better leaving group than the hydroxide ion. Acyl chlorides have a highly electronegative chlorine atom, making the carbonyl carbon very electrophilic. Crucially, the chloride ion (Cl-) is a much better leaving group than the hydroxide ion (OH-) in carboxylic acids, which facilitates nucleophilic attack and substitution.

9. What are the products of the acid hydrolysis of methyl ethanoate? A) Methanol and ethanoic acid B) Methanol and sodium ethanoate C) Ethanol and methanoic acid D) Methane and carbon dioxide

Answer

A) Methanol and ethanoic acid Esters hydrolyse in the presence of acid or base. Acid hydrolysis of an ester breaks the ester bond to reform the original carboxylic acid and alcohol. Methyl ethanoate (CH3COOCH3) will hydrolyse to form ethanoic acid (CH3COOH) and methanol (CH3OH).

10. A compound has the molecular formula C7H6O. It reacts with Tollens' reagent to give a silver mirror and undergoes nitration with concentrated nitric and sulfuric acids. What is the most likely structure of this compound? A) Phenol B) Benzaldehyde C) Benzoic acid D) Phenyl methyl ketone (Acetophenone)

Answer

B) Benzaldehyde Reacting with Tollens' reagent indicates the presence of an aldehyde functional group. Undergoing nitration with concentrated nitric and sulfuric acids suggests an aromatic ring. Combining these, a compound with an aldehyde group attached to a benzene ring (benzaldehyde) fits the description and molecular formula (C6H5CHO).

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The structure of benzene was a long-standing puzzle in chemistry. Friedrich August Kekulé famously proposed the oscillating double bond structure in 1865, reportedly inspired by a dream of a snake biting its own tail. However, this structure couldn't fully explain benzene's unusual stability and lack of reactivity towards addition. Later, Linus Pauling introduced the concept of resonance, and quantum mechanics provided the full explanation of delocalised π-electrons, demonstrating that benzene is a hybrid of contributing structures, more stable than any single Kekulé representation.

⚖️ Ethics

Many aromatic compounds are significant pollutants (e.g., polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons from combustion) and can be carcinogenic. The synthesis of pharmaceuticals often involves aromatic intermediates, raising questions about sustainable synthesis, waste management, and drug accessibility. The use of certain solvents like benzene (a known carcinogen) in industrial processes has been largely phased out or highly regulated, pushing for greener alternatives and better safety protocols.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond simple activating and deactivating groups, the precise electronic effects (inductive and resonance) of substituents on a benzene ring dictate the regioselectivity (ortho, meta, para directing) and reactivity of electrophilic substitution reactions. Hammett plots, which correlate reaction rates or equilibrium constants for substituted benzoic acids and phenols, provide a quantitative measure of these electronic effects. Understanding frontier molecular orbital (FMO) theory can offer deeper insight into why electrophiles attack specific positions on a substituted aromatic ring, relating to the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) electron density distribution.

🧪 Required Practical

For aromatic chemistry, practical aspects involve the nitration of methyl benzoate (safer than benzene itself) or the bromination of phenol to observe reactivity differences. ⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Benzene and aromatic compounds: Many are toxic, carcinogenic, and flammable. Handle in a fume cupboard with appropriate PPE. Nitrobenzene is toxic.
  • 🛑 Concentrated acids: Nitric acid and sulfuric acid are highly corrosive. Wear eye protection and gloves. Handle with care, especially during mixing for electrophile generation (exothermic).
  • 🛑 Acyl chlorides: React violently with water and alcohols, producing corrosive HCl fumes. Highly irritating to skin, eyes, and respiratory system. Must be handled in a fume cupboard in anhydrous conditions.

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. Electrophilic Substitution (e.g., Nitration of Benzene): Involves the formation of a strong electrophile (like NO₂⁺), attack by the π-electron cloud of benzene to form a carbocation intermediate (sigma complex), and subsequent deprotonation to restore aromaticity and regenerate the catalyst.
  2. Nucleophilic Addition (e.g., HCN to Ethanal): A nucleophile (e.g., CN⁻) attacks the partially positive carbonyl carbon, simultaneously moving the π-electrons to the oxygen. The resulting alkoxide intermediate is then protonated to form the final product (e.g., 2-hydroxypropanenitrile).
  3. Fischer Esterification (Acid-Catalysed): Protonation of the carboxylic acid's carbonyl oxygen makes it more electrophilic. An alcohol then acts as a nucleophile, attacking the carbonyl carbon. Proton transfers and elimination of water lead to the ester and regeneration of the catalyst.
  4. Alkaline Hydrolysis of Esters (Saponification): A hydroxide ion (OH⁻) nucleophilically attacks the ester's carbonyl carbon. A tetrahedral intermediate forms, which then expels an alkoxide (OR⁻) leaving group. In alkaline conditions, the resulting carboxylic acid immediately deprotonates to form a carboxylate salt. The alkoxide leaving group becomes the alcohol.
  5. Nucleophilic Acyl Substitution (Acyl Chlorides): Highly reactive acyl chlorides undergo nucleophilic attack at the carbonyl carbon (e.g., by H₂O, ROH, NH₃). A tetrahedral intermediate forms, followed by the elimination of the chloride ion (Cl⁻) as an excellent leaving group, reforming the carbonyl and leading to the new carboxylic acid derivative (e.g., carboxylic acid, ester, amide).

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Nitration of Benzene: Conc. HNO₃, Conc. H₂SO₄, heat below 50°C (to prevent dinitration).
  • Halogenation of Benzene: Halogen (e.g., Br₂), anhydrous halogen carrier (e.g., FeBr₃), room temperature.
  • Friedel-Crafts Acylation/Alkylation: Acyl chloride/alkyl halide, anhydrous AlCl₃, room temperature.
  • Oxidation of Aldehydes to Carboxylic Acids: Reflux with acidified potassium dichromate(VI) or potassium permanganate(VII).
  • Reduction of Carbonyls (Aldehydes/Ketones): NaBH₄ (in aqueous/alcoholic solution) or LiAlH₄ (in dry ether), followed by dilute acid (H⁺/H₂O).
  • Esterification: Carboxylic acid, alcohol, conc. H₂SO₄ catalyst, heat under reflux.
  • Acidic Hydrolysis of Esters: Dilute acid (e.g., H₂SO₄ or HCl), heat under reflux.
  • Alkaline Hydrolysis of Esters: Aqueous NaOH or KOH, heat under reflux.
  • Conversion of Carboxylic Acid to Acyl Chloride: Thionyl chloride (SOCl₂), heat gently.

🔦 Element Spotlight

While not a single element, Benzene (C₆H₆) is the central compound in aromatic chemistry. Its six carbon atoms are sp² hybridised, forming a perfectly planar hexagonal ring. Each carbon is bonded to one hydrogen and two other carbons, leaving one p-orbital on each carbon. These six p-orbitals overlap to form a continuous ring of delocalised π-electrons above and below the plane of the ring. This delocalisation provides significant thermodynamic stability (resonance energy) and dictates benzene's characteristic reactivity: it resists addition reactions that would break the aromatic system, preferring electrophilic substitution to preserve its stability.
