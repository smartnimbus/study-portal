🧠 Conceptual Understanding

Imagine you have two balloons, one filled with hydrogen gas and one with carbon dioxide gas. If both balloons are the same size, at the same temperature, and under the same pressure, what do you think they have in common? This topic explores the fascinating idea that gases, unlike solids or liquids, have a consistent relationship between their amount (in moles) and the volume they occupy. We learn that at standard laboratory conditions, called Room Temperature and Pressure (RTP), one mole of any gas will always take up the exact same volume – 24 dm³. This fundamental principle allows chemists to predict and calculate gas volumes from reactions, which is incredibly useful for industrial processes and everyday applications.

📌 Core Theory

  • Equal amounts in moles of gases occupy the same volume under the same conditions of temperature and pressure (Avogadro's Law).
  • The volume of one mole of any gas at Room Temperature and Pressure (RTP: 20°C and 1 atmosphere pressure) is 24 dm³.
  • The volume of a gas can be calculated from its moles using the formula: Volume (dm³) = Moles × 24.
  • The moles of a gas can be calculated from its volume using the formula: Moles = Volume (dm³) / 24.
  • Volumes of gaseous reactants and products can be calculated from the balanced equation for the reaction, by first finding moles.
  • You should be able to calculate the volume of a gas at RTP from its mass and relative formula mass (Mr) by first converting mass to moles.
  • You should be able to calculate volumes of gaseous reactants and products from a balanced equation and a given volume of a gaseous reactant or product.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

The concept of molar volume stems from Avogadro's Law, which states that equal volumes of all gases, at the same temperature and pressure, contain the same number of molecules. This means that if you have one mole (Avogadro's number of particles) of any gas, it will occupy a fixed volume under specific conditions. In chemistry, these specific conditions are often taken as Room Temperature and Pressure (RTP), which is defined as 20°C and 1 atmosphere of pressure. At RTP, this fixed volume for one mole of any gas is precisely 24 dm³. This value allows us to easily interconvert between moles of gas and the volume it occupies. For example, if we know the moles of a gaseous product formed in a reaction, we can calculate its volume. Conversely, if we measure the volume of a gas, we can determine how many moles are present. This relationship is crucial for understanding gaseous reactions and for quantitative analysis in chemistry, as it bridges the gap between mass-based calculations (using moles and Mr) and volume-based measurements for gases. It's important to remember that this 24 dm³ value is specific to RTP and would change if temperature or pressure were different.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Calculating the volume of a gas from moles

Problem: Calculate the volume occupied by 0.25 moles of carbon dioxide gas at RTP.

Steps: 1. Recall the molar volume of a gas at RTP: 24 dm³. 2. Use the formula: Volume (dm³) = Moles × Molar Volume. 3. Substitute the given values: Volume = 0.25 mol × 24 dm³/mol. 4. Calculate the final volume.

Answer: 6.0 dm³

Teacher's Tip

Always ensure your units are consistent. If the question asks for cm³, remember to convert dm³ to cm³ (1 dm³ = 1000 cm³).

2. Calculating moles from the volume of a gas

Problem: A gas syringe contains 1200 cm³ of oxygen gas at RTP. How many moles of oxygen are present?

Steps: 1. Convert the given volume from cm³ to dm³: 1200 cm³ / 1000 = 1.2 dm³. 2. Recall the molar volume of a gas at RTP: 24 dm³. 3. Use the formula: Moles = Volume (dm³) / Molar Volume. 4. Substitute the values: Moles = 1.2 dm³ / 24 dm³/mol. 5. Calculate the number of moles.

Answer: 0.05 moles

Teacher's Tip

Unit conversion is a common pitfall. Always double-check if your volume is in dm³ before using the 24 dm³/mol factor.

3. Calculating the volume of a gas from its mass and Mr

Problem: What volume does 8.8 g of carbon dioxide (CO₂) occupy at RTP? (A_r: C=12, O=16)

Steps: 1. Calculate the relative formula mass (Mr) of CO₂: Mr = 12 + (2 × 16) = 44. 2. Calculate the number of moles of CO₂: Moles = Mass / Mr = 8.8 g / 44 g/mol. 3. Calculate the volume of CO₂ at RTP: Volume (dm³) = Moles × 24 dm³/mol. 4. Substitute the calculated moles into the volume formula.

Answer: 4.8 dm³

Teacher's Tip

This is a two-step calculation: mass to moles, then moles to volume. Always break down complex problems into simpler steps.

4. Stoichiometry involving gas volumes from a balanced equation

Problem: Hydrogen gas reacts with oxygen gas to produce water vapour. If 60 cm³ of hydrogen gas is reacted completely at RTP, what volume of water vapour is produced? (Balanced equation: 2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(g))

Steps: 1. Identify the molar ratio from the balanced equation between hydrogen (H₂) and water vapour (H₂O): 2 moles H₂ : 2 moles H₂O, which simplifies to 1:1. 2. Apply Avogadro's Law: Since the molar ratio is 1:1, the volume ratio for gases at the same temperature and pressure is also 1:1. 3. Therefore, the volume of water vapour produced will be equal to the volume of hydrogen gas reacted.

Answer: 60 cm³ of water vapour

Teacher's Tip

For gases at the same temperature and pressure, the ratio of volumes is the same as the ratio of moles from the balanced equation. This often bypasses the need to convert to moles explicitly for gas-gas reactions.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Airbag deployment: The rapid production of nitrogen gas from sodium azide decomposition quickly inflates airbags, and engineers need to calculate the exact volume of gas required.
  • Industrial gas production: Companies producing gases like oxygen, nitrogen, or carbon dioxide for industrial use (e.g., welding, food packaging) must calculate the volumes needed for storage and transport.
  • Respiration and diving: Understanding the volumes of gases exchanged in breathing or in scuba diving cylinders is crucial for safety and efficiency.
  • Hot air balloons: Calculating the volume of hot air needed to lift a certain weight depends on the molar volume of hot air vs. cooler ambient air.
  • Rocket propulsion: The volume of gaseous products formed from solid rocket fuel combustion directly impacts thrust and performance.

  • ❌ Forgetting to convert volumes from cm³ to dm³ (divide by 1000) before using the 24 dm³ value.

Q1. (3 Marks | Short Answer | ⏱️ ~3 min)

Why is the molar volume of hydrogen gas (H₂) at RTP the same as the molar volume of carbon dioxide gas (CO₂) at RTP?

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: According to Avogadro's Law, equal moles of any gas occupy the same volume at the same temperature and pressure. This is because the volume of a gas is primarily determined by the space between its widely spaced particles, not the size of the particles themselves. Therefore, 1 mole of H₂ and 1 mole of CO₂ will both contain 6.02 × 10²³ particles and thus occupy 24 dm³ at RTP.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ States Avogadro's Law (equal moles of gases occupy equal volumes)
  • ✅ Mentions 'same temperature and pressure'

🧩 Quick Quiz (MCQ)

1. What is the volume occupied by one mole of any gas at room temperature and pressure (RTP)? A) 22.4 dm³ B) 24 dm³ C) 24 cm³ D) 22.4 cm³

Answer

B) 24 dm³ At room temperature and pressure (20°C and 1 atmosphere), one mole of any gas occupies a volume of 24 dm³.

2. How many moles of carbon dioxide gas would occupy a volume of 48 dm³ at room temperature and pressure? A) 0.5 moles B) 1.0 moles C) 2.0 moles D) 24 moles

Answer

C) 2.0 moles To find the number of moles, divide the given volume by the molar volume at RTP: 48 dm³ / 24 dm³/mol = 2.0 moles.

3. What is the volume occupied by 88 g of carbon dioxide (CO₂) gas at room temperature and pressure? (Relative atomic masses: C = 12, O = 16) A) 24 dm³ B) 44 dm³ C) 48 dm³ D) 88 dm³

Answer

C) 48 dm³ First, calculate the Mr of CO₂: 12 + (2 × 16) = 44. Then, calculate moles: 88 g / 44 g/mol = 2 moles. Finally, calculate volume: 2 moles × 24 dm³/mol = 48 dm³.

4. Consider the reaction: N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) → 2NH₃(g). If 10 dm³ of nitrogen gas reacts completely with hydrogen at constant temperature and pressure, what volume of ammonia gas will be produced? A) 10 dm³ B) 20 dm³ C) 30 dm³ D) 40 dm³

Answer

B) 20 dm³ From the balanced equation, 1 mole of N₂ produces 2 moles of NH₃. For gases at constant temperature and pressure, the ratio of volumes is the same as the ratio of moles. So, 10 dm³ N₂ will produce 2 × 10 dm³ = 20 dm³ NH₃.

5. In the reaction 2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(g), if 50 cm³ of hydrogen gas reacts with 50 cm³ of oxygen gas at constant temperature and pressure, what is the total volume of gas remaining after the reaction? A) 25 cm³ B) 50 cm³ C) 75 cm³ D) 100 cm³

Answer

C) 75 cm³ The ratio H₂ : O₂ : H₂O is 2:1:2. For 50 cm³ of H₂, 25 cm³ of O₂ is needed. So, 50 - 25 = 25 cm³ of O₂ remains unreacted. 50 cm³ of H₂ will produce 50 cm³ of H₂O. Total gas remaining = 25 cm³ (unreacted O₂) + 50 cm³ (H₂O produced) = 75 cm³.

6. What mass of methane (CH₄) is needed to produce 72 dm³ of carbon dioxide at room temperature and pressure, according to the equation: CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l)? (Relative atomic masses: C = 12, H = 1) A) 16 g B) 48 g C) 72 g D) 96 g

Answer

B) 48 g Moles of CO₂ = 72 dm³ / 24 dm³/mol = 3 moles. From the equation, 1 mole of CH₄ produces 1 mole of CO₂, so 3 moles of CH₄ are needed. Mr of CH₄ = 12 + (4 × 1) = 16. Mass of CH₄ = 3 moles × 16 g/mol = 48 g.

7. Which statement is true about the volumes of different gases at the same temperature and pressure? A) Gases with larger relative formula masses always occupy larger volumes. B) Equal masses of different gases occupy equal volumes. C) Equal numbers of moles of different gases occupy equal volumes. D) The volume of a gas depends only on its density.

Answer

C) Equal numbers of moles of different gases occupy equal volumes. Avogadro's law states that equal volumes of all gases, at the same temperature and pressure, have the same number of molecules (or moles).

8. Propane (C₃H₈) burns in oxygen to produce carbon dioxide and water: C₃H₈(g) + 5O₂(g) → 3CO₂(g) + 4H₂O(l). If 60 dm³ of carbon dioxide is produced at RTP, what volume of oxygen was consumed? A) 36 dm³ B) 60 dm³ C) 100 dm³ D) 180 dm³

Answer

C) 100 dm³ From the balanced equation, the mole ratio of O₂ : CO₂ is 5:3. Therefore, the volume ratio is also 5:3. Volume of O₂ = (5/3) × 60 dm³ = 100 dm³.

9. A student collects 6.0 dm³ of hydrogen gas at room temperature and pressure. What mass of magnesium must have reacted with excess dilute hydrochloric acid to produce this volume of hydrogen? (Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂). (Relative atomic mass: Mg = 24) A) 3.0 g B) 6.0 g C) 12.0 g D) 24.0 g

Answer

B) 6.0 g Moles of H₂ = 6.0 dm³ / 24 dm³/mol = 0.25 moles. From the equation, 1 mole of Mg produces 1 mole of H₂, so 0.25 moles of Mg are needed. Mass of Mg = 0.25 moles × 24 g/mol = 6.0 g.

10. In a reaction, 12 dm³ of an unknown gas was experimentally produced at RTP. The theoretical yield for this gas was calculated to be 15 dm³. What is the percentage yield? A) 60% B) 75% C) 80% D) 125%

Answer

C) 80% Percentage Yield = (Actual Yield / Theoretical Yield) × 100%. So, (12 dm³ / 15 dm³) × 100% = 0.8 × 100% = 80%.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The concept of molar volume is directly linked to Amedeo Avogadro's hypothesis (later Avogadro's Law), proposed in 1811. Before Avogadro, chemists like John Dalton struggled to explain why gases reacted in simple whole number ratios by volume (Gay-Lussac's Law of Combining Volumes). Avogadro's insight that equal volumes of gases contain equal numbers of molecules (at the same temperature and pressure) provided the missing piece. It took many decades for his ideas to be fully accepted, notably at the Karlsruhe Congress in 1860, largely due to Stanislao Cannizzaro's advocacy, which finally clarified the concepts of atoms, molecules, and molar masses. The specific value of 24 dm³ at RTP is a later convention for teaching and practical purposes, derived from more precise measurements and the ideal gas law.

⚖️ Ethics

The calculation of gas volumes is critical in industries dealing with hazardous gases, such as the chemical industry (e.g., chlorine, ammonia production), energy sector (natural gas, hydrogen), and healthcare (medical oxygen, anaesthetic gases). Accurate calculations are essential for designing safe storage containers, transport systems, and emergency response protocols. Miscalculations could lead to over-pressurisation, leaks, explosions, or insufficient supply for critical applications. Ethical considerations include ensuring public safety, environmental protection (preventing gas leaks), and responsible resource management through efficient production and storage of gases.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond GCSE, the relationship between pressure (P), volume (V), moles (n), and temperature (T) for gases is described by the Ideal Gas Equation: PV = nRT, where R is the ideal gas constant. This equation provides a more universal way to calculate gas properties under any conditions, not just RTP. The '24 dm³' rule is a specific application of this equation at certain conditions (P=1 atm, T=293 K, R=8.314 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹ converted to appropriate units). Real gases, unlike ideal gases, do not perfectly obey this equation, especially at high pressures and low temperatures, because their particles do have finite volume and experience intermolecular forces, which are ignored in the ideal gas model. The molar volume at Standard Temperature and Pressure (STP: 0°C and 1 atm) is typically 22.4 dm³, which is different from RTP.

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

When collecting gases in the lab, common methods include displacement of water (for insoluble gases) or using a gas syringe (for precise volume measurement or for soluble gases). Safety measures include ensuring good ventilation for hazardous gases, appropriate use of fume hoods, and secure connections to prevent leaks. The gas syringe directly measures volume. For displacement of water, the volume of water displaced is equal to the volume of gas collected. Temperature and pressure should ideally be noted to confirm RTP conditions.

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Many gases are flammable (e.g., hydrogen, methane, propane) – avoid naked flames and sparks.
  • 🛑 Some gases are toxic (e.g., chlorine, carbon monoxide) – ensure good ventilation and use a fume hood.
  • 🛑 Highly reactive gases (e.g., oxygen, chlorine) can cause explosions or rapid reactions when mixed incorrectly.
  • 🛑 Gases under pressure (e.g., from gas cylinders) can be dangerous if valves are faulty or cylinders are knocked over.
  • 🛑 COSHH considerations apply to all chemicals: always check specific hazard symbols and safety data sheets for each gas being used.

  • Trough/bowl of water
  • Delivery tube
  • Conical flask or test tube (for gas generation)
  • Balance (for measuring mass of reactants/products, if needed for initial calculations)
