🤜 In a Nutshell

Amines are basic organic compounds, derivatives of ammonia, characterised by a nitrogen lone pair. Their basicity and nucleophilicity are key, with aliphatic amines being stronger bases than ammonia due to inductive effects, while aromatic amines are weaker due to delocalisation. They are prepared by nucleophilic substitution of haloalkanes (aliphatic) or reduction of nitroarenes (aromatic), processes requiring specific reagents and conditions to control the desired product.

🧠 Conceptual Understanding

Amines are organic compounds derived from ammonia (NH₃), where one or more hydrogen atoms are replaced by alkyl or aryl groups. This gives them a characteristic nitrogen atom with a lone pair of electrons. This lone pair is crucial because it makes amines both basic (proton acceptors) and nucleophilic (electron pair donors). The basicity of amines is a key concept: primary, secondary, and tertiary aliphatic amines are generally stronger bases than ammonia due to the electron-donating effect of alkyl groups, which push electron density towards the nitrogen, making the lone pair more available. Aromatic amines, like phenylamine, are weaker bases than ammonia because the nitrogen's lone pair is delocalised into the benzene ring, making it less available for protonation. Understanding this basicity is fundamental to predicting their reactions with acids to form ammonium salts. Furthermore, we'll explore two primary methods of preparing amines: the nucleophilic substitution of haloalkanes with ammonia (or other amines), and the reduction of nitroarenes, which is the go-to method for synthesizing aromatic amines.

📌 Core Theory

  • Amines are organic derivatives of ammonia (NH₃), classified as primary (RNH₂), secondary (R₂NH), or tertiary (R₃N) based on the number of alkyl/aryl groups attached to the nitrogen atom.
  • The nitrogen atom in amines possesses a lone pair of electrons, making amines both basic (proton acceptors) and nucleophilic (electron pair donors).
  • Basicity of amines: Amines react with dilute acids to form salts. For example, RNH₂ + HCl → RNH₃⁺Cl⁻.
  • Aliphatic amines are stronger bases than ammonia due to the electron-donating inductive effect of alkyl groups, which increases the electron density on the nitrogen, making the lone pair more available for protonation.
  • Aromatic amines are weaker bases than ammonia because the lone pair of electrons on the nitrogen atom is delocalised into the π system of the benzene ring, reducing its availability for protonation.
  • Preparation of aliphatic amines: Nucleophilic substitution of haloalkanes with excess ethanolic ammonia. This reaction can lead to a mixture of primary, secondary, tertiary amines, and quaternary ammonium salts due to successive substitutions.
  • To favour the formation of primary amine, a large excess of ethanolic ammonia is used to minimise further substitution.
  • Preparation of aromatic amines: Reduction of nitroarenes (e.g., nitrobenzene) using tin (Sn) and concentrated hydrochloric acid (HCl), followed by neutralisation with a base (e.g., NaOH).
  • The reduction involves the transfer of hydrogen atoms to the nitro group (-NO₂) to form an amino group (-NH₂).

🔬 Detailed Explanation

Amines are crucial functional groups in organic chemistry due to the presence of a nitrogen atom with a lone pair of electrons. This lone pair dictates their fundamental chemical properties: basicity and nucleophilicity. As bases, amines readily accept a proton (H⁺) from an acid to form a positively charged ammonium salt. The strength of this basicity depends on how readily the lone pair is available. Alkyl groups, being electron-donating, push electron density towards the nitrogen, enhancing the lone pair's availability and thus increasing basicity in aliphatic amines. This explains why tertiary aliphatic amines are often stronger bases than secondary, which are stronger than primary, which are stronger than ammonia. However, in aromatic amines like phenylamine, the lone pair on the nitrogen is conjugated with the delocalised π electrons of the benzene ring. This delocalisation makes the lone pair less available for protonation, significantly reducing the basicity, making aromatic amines weaker bases than ammonia. Industrially, preparing specific amines requires controlled conditions. Aliphatic amines are typically formed by the nucleophilic substitution of haloalkanes. The ammonia acts as a nucleophile, attacking the carbon atom bearing the halogen, displacing it. Using an excess of ethanolic ammonia is critical to limit multiple substitutions, which could lead to secondary, tertiary, and quaternary ammonium salts. For aromatic amines, direct nucleophilic substitution is generally not feasible; instead, a reduction reaction is employed. Nitroarenes, easily formed by nitration of arenes, are reduced using a strong reducing agent like tin and concentrated hydrochloric acid, followed by an alkaline work-up to liberate the free amine.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Reaction of an Aliphatic Amine with an Acid

Problem: Methylamine (CH₃NH₂) is a primary amine. Write an equation to show its reaction with hydrochloric acid (HCl) and name the product formed.

Steps: 1. Identify the amine (methylamine) and the acid (hydrochloric acid). 2. Recognise that the amine's nitrogen lone pair will accept a proton (H⁺) from the acid. 3. Combine the amine and the proton to form the ammonium ion. 4. Combine this ammonium ion with the anion from the acid to form the salt.

Answer: CH₃NH₂ + HCl → CH₃NH₃⁺Cl⁻ (Methylammonium chloride).

Teacher's Tip

Remember that the lone pair on the nitrogen atom is the key to an amine's basicity. It's accepting a proton, not donating one.

2. Preparation of an Aliphatic Amine (Propylamine)

Problem: Outline the synthesis of propylamine (CH₃CH₂CH₂NH₂) from 1-chloropropane (CH₃CH₂CH₂Cl), including necessary conditions.

Steps: 1. Identify the starting material (1-chloropropane) and desired product (propylamine). 2. Recognise that this is an aliphatic amine synthesis from a haloalkane, which proceeds via nucleophilic substitution. 3. State the nucleophile: ammonia (NH₃). 4. Specify the solvent: ethanol (ethanolic ammonia). 5. Specify the conditions to favour primary amine formation: excess ammonia, heated under reflux. 6. Write the overall reaction equation.

Answer: 1-chloropropane reacts with excess ethanolic ammonia, heated under reflux. The ammonia acts as a nucleophile, replacing the chlorine atom. CH₃CH₂CH₂Cl + 2NH₃ → CH₃CH₂CH₂NH₂ + NH₄Cl. (The excess NH₃ mops up the HCl formed).

Teacher's Tip

Always remember 'excess ethanolic ammonia' to minimise further substitution and ensure you get mainly the primary amine. 'Under reflux' is key for organic reactions with volatile reactants.

3. Preparation of an Aromatic Amine (Phenylamine)

Problem: Describe the reduction of nitrobenzene (C₆H₅NO₂) to phenylamine (C₆H₅NH₂), including the reagents and conditions.

Steps: 1. Identify the starting material (nitrobenzene) and desired product (phenylamine). 2. Recognise that this is an aromatic amine synthesis, which requires reduction of the nitro group. 3. State the reducing agents: tin (Sn) and concentrated hydrochloric acid (HCl). 4. Specify the initial conditions: heated under reflux. 5. Explain the work-up step: addition of a strong base (e.g., NaOH) to neutralise the acidic solution and liberate the free amine. 6. Write the overall reaction equation, representing [H] as the reducing agent.

Answer: Nitrobenzene is reduced by heating under reflux with tin (Sn) and concentrated hydrochloric acid (HCl). This forms phenylammonium chloride (C₆H₅NH₃⁺Cl⁻). Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) is then added to the mixture to neutralise the excess acid and convert the phenylammonium chloride salt into phenylamine (C₆H₅NH₂). C₆H₅NO₂ + 6[H] → C₆H₅NH₂ + 2H₂O (where [H] represents the reducing agent system Sn/HCl).

Teacher's Tip

For aromatic amine synthesis, it's a two-stage process: reduction with Sn/HCl, then an alkaline work-up. Don't forget the second step to get the free amine!

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Pharmaceuticals: Many drugs, such as antihistamines, antidepressants, and local anaesthetics (e.g., lidocaine), contain amine functional groups that are crucial for their biological activity.
  • Dyes and Pigments: Aromatic amines are key intermediates in the synthesis of a vast range of synthetic dyes (azo dyes), used in textiles, food colouring, and printing.
  • Polymers: Diamines are essential monomers in the production of polyamides like nylon (e.g., hexamethylenediamine is used with adipic acid to make Nylon 6,6).
  • Cleaning Products: Some amines, like ethanolamines, are used as emulsifiers and surfactants in detergents and cleaning agents.
  • Agrochemicals: Amines are found in various pesticides and herbicides, contributing to their efficacy in agriculture.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing nucleophilicity with basicity: While both involve the lone pair, basicity is proton acceptance, nucleophilicity is attacking an electron-deficient carbon.
  • ❌ Incorrectly ranking amine basicity: Forgetting the electron-donating effect of alkyl groups or the delocalisation effect in aromatic amines.
  • ❌ Omitting 'excess' ammonia for aliphatic amine synthesis, which is crucial for favouring primary amine formation.
  • ❌ Forgetting the alkaline work-up step in the reduction of nitroarenes; without it, the product remains an ammonium salt.
  • ❌ Thinking aromatic amines are strong bases because they are nitrogen-containing: The benzene ring's electron delocalisation makes them weak.
Memory Anchor

Think of an amine's nitrogen like a generous friend with a 'spare chair' (lone pair) who loves to invite a new guest (a proton) to sit down. Aliphatic friends are richer (electron-donating groups) so their chair is always available. Aromatic friends have their chair often busy with other party guests (delocalised ring electrons), making it harder for a new guest to sit.

An organic compound has the formula C₄H₁₁N. Identify all possible primary amine isomers for this formula, draw their structures, and predict their relative basicity.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

Q9. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

Predict the products when N-ethylpropylamine reacts with excess iodoethane. Draw the structure of the final organic product and name it. Explain the type of reaction occurring.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: N-ethylpropylamine is a secondary amine (CH₃CH₂NHCH₂CH₂CH₃). When it reacts with excess iodoethane (CH₃CH₂I), it undergoes successive nucleophilic substitution reactions.

First, the secondary amine reacts with iodoethane to form a tertiary amine, N,N-diethylpropylamine: CH₃CH₂NHCH₂CH₂CH₃ + CH₃CH₂I → (CH₃CH₂)₂NCH₂CH₂CH₃ + HI

Since iodoethane is in excess, this newly formed tertiary amine will then react with more iodoethane. The lone pair on the nitrogen of the tertiary amine (N,N-diethylpropylamine) will act as a nucleophile to attack another molecule of iodoethane, forming a quaternary ammonium salt.

Final organic product: N,N-diethyl-N-propylammonium iodide.

Structure: (CH₃CH₂)₂N⁺(CH₂CH₂CH₃)I⁻ (Nitrogen is positively charged, bonded to two ethyl groups and one propyl group, with an iodide counter-ion).

Type of reaction: Nucleophilic substitution. The amine acts as a nucleophile, donating its lone pair to the electrophilic carbon of the haloalkane, displacing the iodide ion. This process repeats as long as haloalkane is in excess and available lone pairs exist on nitrogen.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ N-ethylpropylamine is a secondary amine.
  • ✅ Reacts with iodoethane via nucleophilic substitution.
  • ✅ Since iodoethane is in excess, multiple substitutions will occur.
  • ✅ The secondary amine will react to form a tertiary amine first: N,N-diethylpropylamine.
  • ✅ Then, the tertiary amine will react with more iodoethane to form a quaternary ammonium salt.
  • ✅ Final organic product: N,N-diethyl-N-propylammonium iodide (a quaternary ammonium salt).
  • ✅ Structure of (CH₃CH₂)₂N⁺(CH₂CH₂CH₃)I⁻.

1. Which of the following best explains why amines act as bases? A) They contain hydrogen atoms that can be donated. B) The nitrogen atom has a lone pair of electrons that can accept a proton. C) The C-N bond is polar, allowing electron donation. D) They are able to hydrolyse water molecules.

Answer

B) The nitrogen atom has a lone pair of electrons that can accept a proton. Amines are basic due to the lone pair of electrons on the nitrogen atom, which can readily accept a proton (H⁺) from an acid.

2. Methylamine (CH₃NH₂) reacts with dilute hydrochloric acid. What is the formula of the organic product formed? A) CH₃NHCl B) CH₃NH₃Cl C) CH₃NH₂Cl D) CH₄NCl

Answer

B) CH₃NH₃ClMethylamine accepts a proton from hydrochloric acid to form a methylammonium ion (CH₃NH₃⁺), which then forms an ionic salt with the chloride ion, CH₃NH₃Cl.

3. When preparing a primary amine from a haloalkane using ammonia, why is it important to use excess ethanolic ammonia? A) To ensure the reaction goes to completion quickly. B) To increase the yield of secondary amines. C) To prevent the primary amine from reacting further to form secondary and tertiary amines. D) To act as a solvent for the haloalkane.

Answer

C) To prevent the primary amine from reacting further to form secondary and tertiary amines. Using excess ammonia ensures that the haloalkane reacts predominantly with ammonia rather than with the primary amine product, which could also act as a nucleophile and lead to the formation of secondary and tertiary amines.

4. Which set of reagents is used to convert nitrobenzene into phenylamine (aniline)? A) Sodium borohydride in ethanol. B) Hydrogen gas with a nickel catalyst. C) Tin and concentrated hydrochloric acid, followed by aqueous NaOH. D) Lithium aluminium hydride in dry ether.

Answer

C) Tin and concentrated hydrochloric acid, followed by aqueous NaOH. Nitrobenzene is reduced to phenylamine by heating with tin and concentrated hydrochloric acid. This forms phenylammonium chloride, which is then treated with a strong base like NaOH to regenerate the free phenylamine.

5. Which statement correctly compares the basicity of methylamine (CH₃NH₂) and phenylamine (C₆H₅NH₂)? A) Phenylamine is a stronger base because the benzene ring stabilises the positive charge on nitrogen. B) Methylamine is a stronger base because the methyl group is electron-donating, making the lone pair more available. C) Both are equally strong bases as they both contain an -NH₂ group. D) Methylamine is a weaker base due to the small size of the methyl group.

Answer

B) Methylamine is a stronger base because the methyl group is electron-donating, making the lone pair more available. Methylamine is a stronger base than phenylamine. The electron-donating methyl group in methylamine increases the electron density on the nitrogen, making its lone pair more available for proton acceptance. In phenylamine, the lone pair on the nitrogen is delocalised into the benzene ring, making it less available to accept a proton.

6. The basicity of an amine is attributed to the presence of: A) A polar C-N bond. B) A highly electronegative nitrogen atom. C) A lone pair of electrons on the nitrogen atom. D) The ability to form hydrogen bonds.

Answer

C) A lone pair of electrons on the nitrogen atom. Amines are basic because the nitrogen atom possesses a lone pair of electrons, which can readily accept a proton (H⁺), acting as a Brønsted-Lowry base.

7. Ethylamine (CH₃CH₂NH₂) reacts with nitric acid (HNO₃). What is the name of the salt formed? A) Ethylnitrate. B) Nitroethane. C) Ethylammonium nitrate. D) Ethylamine nitrite.

Answer

C) Ethylammonium nitrate. Ethylamine, being a base, will accept a proton from nitric acid to form the ethylammonium ion (CH₃CH₂NH₃⁺). This then combines with the nitrate ion (NO₃⁻) to form the salt ethylammonium nitrate.

8. Consider the reaction to form diethylamine from bromoethane. Which other reactant would be used, and in what proportion, to favour the formation of diethylamine? A) Excess ammonia. B) Excess ethylamine. C) Limited ethylamine. D) Excess ethanolic potassium hydroxide.

Answer

B) Excess ethylamine. To favour the formation of a secondary amine like diethylamine, a primary amine (ethylamine) is reacted with a haloalkane (bromoethane). Using excess ethylamine ensures that the bromoethane reacts preferentially with ethylamine rather than the diethylamine product, preventing further substitution to tertiary amines.

9. A compound, X, is an organic base that turns damp red litmus paper blue. It can be prepared by reacting nitrobenzene with tin and concentrated hydrochloric acid, followed by neutralisation. What is compound X? A) Ethanoyl chloride. B) Phenylamine. C) Benzene. D) Benzoic acid.

Answer

B) Phenylamine. The description states X is an organic base (turns litmus blue) and is prepared from nitrobenzene reduction. This preparation method is characteristic of phenylamine (aniline).

10. The preparation of aliphatic amines from haloalkanes and ammonia is an example of what type of organic reaction? A) Electrophilic addition. B) Elimination. C) Free radical substitution. D) Nucleophilic substitution.

Answer

D) Nucleophilic substitution. Ammonia (and amines) acts as a nucleophile due to the lone pair on the nitrogen. It attacks the partially positive carbon atom bonded to the halogen in the haloalkane, displacing the halogen. This is a nucleophilic substitution reaction.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The discovery and synthesis of amines are intertwined with the development of organic chemistry in the 19th century. Early syntheses were often accidental or via reduction reactions. For example, nitrobenzene was first reduced to aniline (phenylamine) in 1840 by Nikolay Zinin. August Wilhelm von Hofmann made significant contributions, including the 'Hofmann degradation' and 'Hofmann elimination', but also the 'Hofmann ammolysis' (reaction of haloalkanes with ammonia) in the 1840s, which was a foundational method for amine synthesis, albeit producing mixtures. The understanding of amine basicity evolved alongside acid-base theories, with the recognition of the nitrogen lone pair as critical.

⚖️ Ethics

Amines are implicated in significant ethical considerations. Many naturally occurring amines act as neurotransmitters (e.g., dopamine, serotonin), and synthetic amines form the basis of many psychoactive drugs, both therapeutic (e.g., selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors, SSRIs) and illicit (e.g., amphetamines, MDMA). This 'dual-use' nature of amine chemistry necessitates careful regulation of precursors and products to prevent diversion for illegal purposes, posing challenges for chemists, regulators, and society regarding freedom of research versus public safety. The environmental impact of amine production and their presence in industrial waste streams also raises concerns, as some amines can be toxic or contribute to air pollution (e.g., in CO₂ capture technologies).

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond simple basicity, the nucleophilicity of amines can be explored in greater depth, including the factors influencing SN1 vs. SN2 mechanisms in alkylation. The concept of 'steric hindrance' can be introduced to explain why tertiary aliphatic amines are sometimes less basic than secondary amines in aqueous solution, despite stronger inductive effects, due to reduced solvation of the bulky ammonium ion. The synthesis of specific amine isomers, particularly secondary and tertiary amines, often involves more sophisticated protecting group chemistry to prevent over-alkylation. Chiral amines are also important, and their synthesis can involve asymmetric catalysis or resolution of racemic mixtures. Furthermore, advanced reactions like reductive amination (forming amines from aldehydes/ketones) and the Gabriel synthesis (for primary amines) offer more selective routes than direct ammolysis of haloalkanes.

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

For the preparation of phenylamine from nitrobenzene: Equipment: Round-bottom flask, condenser, heating mantle, separating funnel, beaker, measuring cylinders. Reagents: Nitrobenzene, tin foil (granulated tin), concentrated hydrochloric acid, sodium hydroxide solution (e.g., 4 M), diethyl ether (or other organic solvent), anhydrous calcium chloride (drying agent). Procedure: 1. Add nitrobenzene, tin, and concentrated HCl to a round-bottom flask. Set up for reflux heating. 2. Heat the mixture under reflux for about 30-60 minutes, until the oily layer of nitrobenzene disappears, indicating completion of reduction. 3. Cool the flask and carefully add concentrated sodium hydroxide solution dropwise until the solution is strongly alkaline (test with universal indicator paper). This neutralises excess acid and liberates the free phenylamine. 4. Transfer the mixture to a separating funnel and extract the phenylamine using diethyl ether. Phenylamine will dissolve in the organic layer. 5. Dry the ethereal layer with an anhydrous drying agent (e.g., anhydrous calcium chloride or magnesium sulfate) to remove any dissolved water. 6. Filter off the drying agent and distil the ether/phenylamine mixture to obtain pure phenylamine. Phenylamine is a colourless oil that turns brown on exposure to air/light. Safety Notes: Nitrobenzene is toxic and absorbed through the skin. Concentrated HCl and NaOH are corrosive. Diethyl ether is highly flammable and its vapours are heavier than air. Work in a fume cupboard. Wear eye protection and gloves. Avoid open flames.

  • 🛑 Nitrobenzene is highly toxic and can be absorbed through the skin. It causes headaches and dizziness.

  • $$Alkaline work-up: C₆H₅NH₃⁺Cl⁻ + NaOH → C₆H₅NH₂ + NaCl + H₂O$$

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. Nucleophilic Substitution (for aliphatic amine synthesis from haloalkanes):
  2. The nitrogen atom of ammonia (or an amine) acts as a nucleophile, using its lone pair of electrons to attack the partially positive carbon atom attached to the halogen in the haloalkane.
  3. A new carbon-nitrogen bond is formed, and simultaneously, the carbon-halogen bond breaks heterolytically, with the halogen atom leaving as a halide ion (the leaving group).
  4. This initially forms a protonated ammonium ion (e.g., RNH₃⁺X⁻). A second molecule of ammonia (or another base) then deprotonates this ion to yield the neutral amine (RNH₂) and an ammonium halide salt.
  5. Reduction of Nitroarenes (e.g., nitrobenzene to phenylamine):
  6. This is a complex multi-step reduction involving the transfer of six electrons and six protons to the nitro group (-NO₂).
  7. The tin (Sn) reacts with concentrated hydrochloric acid (HCl) to produce nascent hydrogen (often represented as [H]), which is a powerful reducing agent.
  8. The nitro group is progressively reduced through several intermediates (e.g., nitrosobenzene, phenylhydroxylamine) until it forms the amino group (-NH₂).
  9. The initial product is the phenylammonium salt (C₆H₅NH₃⁺Cl⁻) due to the presence of excess HCl.
  10. An alkaline work-up (addition of NaOH) is then required to deprotonate this salt and liberate the free phenylamine (C₆H₅NH₂).

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Preparation of Aliphatic Amines from Haloalkanes:
  • Reagents: Haloalkane, excess ethanolic ammonia (or excess primary/secondary amine if synthesising a higher-order amine).
  • Temperature: Heated under reflux (typically 100°C for a primary haloalkane) to ensure a reasonable reaction rate and prevent loss of volatile reactants.
  • Pressure: Atmospheric pressure.
  • Solvent: Ethanol (ethanolic) is crucial to dissolve both polar ammonia and non-polar haloalkane, and to prevent competing hydrolysis reactions that would occur in water.
  • Preparation of Aromatic Amines from Nitroarenes:
  • Reagents: Nitroarene (e.g., nitrobenzene), tin (Sn), concentrated hydrochloric acid (HCl), then concentrated sodium hydroxide (NaOH) for the work-up.
  • Temperature: Heated under reflux (typically 100°C) to ensure an effective reduction.
  • Pressure: Atmospheric pressure.
  • Catalyst: Tin acts as the reducing agent in the presence of acid.
  • Solvent: Often the concentrated HCl itself acts as a solvent and reactant; an aqueous system is typically formed.

🔦 Element Spotlight

The central element in amines is Nitrogen (N). It has an atomic number of 7 and an electron configuration of 1s² 2s² 2p³. Critically, it possesses five valence electrons, forming three covalent bonds and retaining one lone pair of non-bonding electrons. This lone pair is responsible for nitrogen's electron-donating properties, making amines basic (proton acceptors) and nucleophilic (electron pair donors). Nitrogen is more electronegative than carbon and hydrogen, leading to polar N-H and C-N bonds. This polarity, combined with the ability to hydrogen bond (for primary and secondary amines), influences the physical properties like boiling points and solubility. The availability of the lone pair for donation is the key factor determining the reactivity of different types of amines.
