🤜 In a Nutshell

Metal ions can be identified using sodium hydroxide solution, which reacts to form characteristic insoluble metal hydroxide precipitates. Copper(II) forms a blue precipitate, iron(II) a green, and iron(III) a brown. Aluminium, calcium, and magnesium ions all form white precipitates, but only aluminium hydroxide redissolves in excess sodium hydroxide, allowing for its unique identification.

🧠 Conceptual Understanding

When you add sodium hydroxide solution to an unknown solution containing metal ions, a reaction often occurs, forming a precipitate. This precipitate is an insoluble metal hydroxide. The key concept here is that different metal ions react with hydroxide ions to form precipitates with distinct colours or solubilities in excess sodium hydroxide. By observing these characteristics – colour and whether the precipitate redissolves in excess reagent – we can identify the specific metal ion present. This is a fundamental qualitative analysis technique, relying on specific chemical properties to distinguish between different substances, similar to how a unique fingerprint identifies an individual.

📌 Core Theory

  • Sodium hydroxide solution is used to test for the presence of certain dissolved metal ions (cations).
  • When sodium hydroxide is added to solutions containing metal ions, an insoluble metal hydroxide precipitate is formed.
  • Solutions of aluminium ions (Al³⁺), calcium ions (Ca²⁺), and magnesium ions (Mg²⁺) all form white precipitates with sodium hydroxide.
  • Only aluminium hydroxide (Al(OH)₃) precipitate redissolves in excess sodium hydroxide solution to form a colourless solution.
  • Solutions of copper(II) ions (Cu²⁺), iron(II) ions (Fe²⁺), and iron(III) ions (Fe³⁺) form coloured precipitates with sodium hydroxide.
  • Copper(II) ions (Cu²⁺) form a blue precipitate (copper(II) hydroxide, Cu(OH)₂).
  • Iron(II) ions (Fe²⁺) form a green precipitate (iron(II) hydroxide, Fe(OH)₂).
  • Iron(III) ions (Fe³⁺) form a brown precipitate (iron(III) hydroxide, Fe(OH)₃).
  • These reactions are often represented by ionic equations, focusing on the ions directly involved in forming the precipitate.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

The identification of metal ions using sodium hydroxide relies on the principle of precipitation reactions. When a soluble salt of a metal is dissolved in water, the metal exists as a hydrated ion. Upon adding sodium hydroxide, the hydroxide ions (OH⁻) from the NaOH react with these metal ions to form a metal hydroxide. For many metal ions, their hydroxides are insoluble in water, causing them to form a solid precipitate that settles out of the solution. The characteristic colour of this precipitate, such as blue for copper(II) or green for iron(II), provides the first clue to the ion's identity. Furthermore, the solubility of these hydroxides in excess sodium hydroxide is crucial. While most metal hydroxides remain insoluble, aluminium hydroxide is unique. It is an amphoteric hydroxide, meaning it can react with both acids and strong bases. In the presence of excess strong base (like NaOH), the aluminium hydroxide precipitate redissolves to form a soluble complex ion, allowing for its specific identification among other white precipitates like magnesium and calcium hydroxides.

📖 Key Definitions

Term Definition
Precipitate An insoluble solid that forms when two solutions are mixed during a chemical reaction.
Cation A positively charged ion, typically a metal ion, that is attracted to the cathode in electrolysis.
Insoluble Unable to dissolve in a particular solvent.
Amphoteric A substance that can act as both an acid and a base (e.g., aluminium hydroxide, which reacts with both strong acids and strong bases).

  • Amphoteric — A substance that can act as both an acid and a base (e.g., aluminium hydroxide, which reacts with both strong acids and strong bases).
  • Cation — A positively charged ion, typically a metal ion, that is attracted to the cathode in electrolysis.
  • Insoluble — Unable to dissolve in a particular solvent.
  • Precipitate — An insoluble solid that forms when two solutions are mixed during a chemical reaction.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Identifying an unknown solution containing Fe³⁺ ions

Problem: A student is given an unknown solution believed to contain a metal ion. When a few drops of sodium hydroxide solution are added, a reddish-brown precipitate forms. What metal ion is likely present?

Steps: 1. Observe the colour of the precipitate formed upon addition of sodium hydroxide. 2. Compare the observed colour to the known characteristic colours for metal hydroxides. 3. A reddish-brown precipitate is characteristic of iron(III) hydroxide.

Answer: The metal ion likely present is iron(III) (Fe³⁺).

Teacher's Tip

Always pay close attention to the exact colour description, as slight differences can indicate different ions (e.g., green for Fe²⁺ vs. brown for Fe³⁺).

2. Distinguishing between white precipitates of Al³⁺, Ca²⁺, and Mg²⁺

Problem: Three test tubes contain solutions of aluminium nitrate, calcium chloride, and magnesium sulfate, but their labels have fallen off. When sodium hydroxide is added to each, all three form a white precipitate. Describe a further test to identify which test tube contains aluminium ions.

Steps: 1. Recall that aluminium, calcium, and magnesium ions all form white precipitates with sodium hydroxide. 2. Identify the unique property of one of these precipitates with excess sodium hydroxide. 3. Aluminium hydroxide is amphoteric and redissolves in excess sodium hydroxide, while calcium hydroxide and magnesium hydroxide do not. 4. Add excess sodium hydroxide solution to each test tube containing the white precipitate. 5. Observe which precipitate dissolves to form a colourless solution.

Answer: To identify the test tube containing aluminium ions, add excess sodium hydroxide solution to each of the white precipitates. The test tube in which the white precipitate dissolves to form a colourless solution contains aluminium ions.

Teacher's Tip

Remember the 'excess NaOH' test is critical for distinguishing aluminium from calcium and magnesium.

3. Writing an ionic equation for a precipitation reaction

Problem: Write the ionic equation for the reaction between copper(II) ions and sodium hydroxide solution.

Steps: 1. Identify the metal ion and the hydroxide ion that form the precipitate. 2. Recall that copper(II) hydroxide is the precipitate formed, and it is insoluble. 3. Write the ions involved in the reaction: Cu²⁺(aq) and OH⁻(aq). 4. Balance the charges to form the neutral compound Cu(OH)₂. 5. Write the balanced ionic equation including state symbols.

Answer: Cu²⁺(aq) + 2OH⁻(aq) → Cu(OH)₂(s)

Teacher's Tip

Always balance the charges and include correct state symbols (aq for aqueous ions, s for solid precipitate).

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Water Treatment: Identifying and removing heavy metal ions like iron and copper from drinking water to ensure safety and prevent pipe corrosion.
  • Environmental Monitoring: Detecting metal ion pollutants in wastewater or natural water bodies, which is crucial for assessing ecological health and complying with discharge regulations.
  • Industrial Quality Control: Testing for specific metal impurities in raw materials or final products in industries such as metallurgy, pharmaceuticals, and chemical manufacturing.
  • Soil Analysis: Determining the presence of essential or toxic metal ions in soil samples, impacting agriculture and land remediation strategies.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing the colours of iron(II) and iron(III) precipitates (Fe²⁺ is green, Fe³⁺ is brown).
  • ❌ Forgetting the special behaviour of aluminium hydroxide (Al(OH)₃) which redissolves in excess sodium hydroxide.
  • ❌ Assuming all metal hydroxides are insoluble; some are, but the test focuses on the ones that form visible precipitates.
  • ❌ Not including state symbols in ionic equations, or using incorrect ones (e.g., (l) for precipitate instead of (s)).
  • ❌ Confusing the initial solution colour with the precipitate colour (e.g., copper(II) solutions are blue, but the precipitate is also blue).
Memory Anchor

Q7. (3 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~3 min)

Why do metal hydroxides form precipitates when sodium hydroxide is added to solutions of metal ions, rather than remaining dissolved?

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Metal hydroxides form precipitates because most of them are insoluble in water. When a soluble metal salt dissolves, the metal ions are free in solution. Upon adding sodium hydroxide, these metal ions (cations) encounter hydroxide ions (anions). They react to form a neutral metal hydroxide compound. Since this newly formed compound has very low solubility in water, it cannot remain dissolved and therefore solid particles come out of solution, forming a visible precipitate.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Most metal hydroxides are insoluble in water (1 mark).
  • ✅ When metal ions (e.g., M²⁺(aq)) and hydroxide ions (OH⁻(aq)) meet, they combine to form a neutral compound (M(OH)₂(s)) (1 mark).
  • ✅ Because this compound has very low solubility, it comes out of solution as a solid precipitate (1 mark).
  • ✅ This is a general solubility rule for many metal hydroxides (except Group 1 and some Group 2, but GCSE often simplifies this to 'most insoluble').

📋 Strategy: Explain that the product (metal hydroxide) is insoluble, causing it to leave the solution as a solid.

⚠️ Examiner Note: Students often state 'they react' but don't explain why a precipitate forms due to insolubility.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This questions the fundamental chemical principle behind precipitation: insolubility. It's a conceptual stretch beyond just memorising colours.

Q8. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

An unknown solid is thought to be either magnesium oxide or aluminium oxide. Describe how you would use dilute nitric acid and then sodium hydroxide solution to distinguish between these two solids, providing full observations and conclusions.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: To distinguish between magnesium oxide (MgO) and aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃):1. First, add dilute nitric acid to separate small samples of each solid. Both magnesium oxide and aluminium oxide are basic oxides and will dissolve in the acid to form clear, colourless solutions (magnesium nitrate and aluminium nitrate, respectively). This means we now have solutions containing Mg²⁺ ions and Al³⁺ ions.2. Take a small sample of each of these resulting colourless solutions into separate test tubes.3. Add sodium hydroxide solution dropwise to each test tube. In both cases, a white precipitate will form.4. Now, add excess sodium hydroxide solution to each of the white precipitates: * In one test tube, the white precipitate will remain insoluble even in excess sodium hydroxide. This indicates the presence of magnesium ions (Mg²⁺), meaning the original solid was magnesium oxide. * In the other test tube, the white precipitate will dissolve in excess sodium hydroxide to form a clear, colourless solution. This indicates the presence of aluminium ions (Al³⁺), meaning the original solid was aluminium oxide.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Step 1: Add dilute nitric acid to separate samples of each solid. Both MgO and Al₂O₃ will dissolve to form colourless solutions (1 mark for both dissolving).
  • ✅ Step 2: Take samples of these newly formed solutions (containing Mg²⁺ and Al³⁺ respectively) (1 mark).
  • ✅ Step 3: Add sodium hydroxide solution dropwise to each. Both will form white precipitates (1 mark).
  • ✅ Step 4: Add excess sodium hydroxide solution to each white precipitate (1 mark).
  • ✅ Observation: In one solution, the white precipitate remains insoluble in excess NaOH. Conclusion: This was the solution from magnesium oxide, containing Mg²⁺ ions (1 mark).
  • ✅ Observation: In the other solution, the white precipitate dissolves in excess NaOH to form a colourless solution. Conclusion: This was the solution from aluminium oxide, containing Al³⁺ ions (1 mark).

📋 Strategy: Break the problem down into logical stages: first dissolve the solids, then apply the standard NaOH test. For each stage, state the action, observation, and conclusion clearly.

⚠️ Examiner Note: Students often struggle with the multi-step nature, forgetting the initial dissolution in acid or confusing the solubility of the hydroxides.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This is a multi-step problem that tests solubility of oxides in acid, then identification of resulting ions. It requires careful sequencing and detailing all observations and conclusions. Students need to remember both oxides dissolve in acid, then apply the NaOH test.

Q10. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

Explain why it is possible to distinguish between iron(II) ions and iron(III) ions using sodium hydroxide, but not using universal indicator.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: It is possible to distinguish between iron(II) ions (Fe²⁺) and iron(III) ions (Fe³⁺) using sodium hydroxide solution because they react to form distinctly different coloured precipitates. Iron(II) ions produce a green precipitate (iron(II) hydroxide), while iron(III) ions produce a reddish-brown precipitate (iron(III) hydroxide). These characteristic colours allow for clear differentiation.Universal indicator, however, tests the pH of a solution. Solutions of both iron(II) and iron(III) salts are typically acidic due to the hydrolysis of the metal ions (they react with water to produce H⁺ ions). Therefore, adding universal indicator to either solution would likely show a similar acidic pH (e.g., orange or red), providing no basis to distinguish between the two different iron ions.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Sodium hydroxide causes both iron(II) and iron(III) ions to form precipitates (1 mark).
  • ✅ These precipitates have distinct and characteristic colours: green for Fe(OH)₂ and brown for Fe(OH)₃ (1 mark).
  • ✅ Universal indicator tests for pH / acidity/alkalinity of the solution (1 mark).
  • ✅ Both iron(II) and iron(III) salt solutions are typically acidic due to hydrolysis, so a universal indicator would show a similar acidic pH for both, making distinction impossible (1 mark).

📋 Strategy: Explain what NaOH does and its specific observations for both ions. Then, explain what universal indicator does and why it would produce similar, non-discriminating results for both solutions.

⚠️ Examiner Note: Students often struggle to explain why universal indicator wouldn't work, beyond just stating it's not a specific test.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This question requires students to understand the function of both reagents. NaOH is a specific test reagent, while universal indicator is a general pH indicator.

Answer

C) To identify specific metal cations by precipitate formation. C) Magnesium ions (Mg²⁺) Both aluminium and magnesium ions form white precipitates. However, only aluminium hydroxide dissolves in excess sodium hydroxide. Magnesium hydroxide remains as a white precipitate. Copper(II) and iron(II) ions form coloured precipitates.

Calcium and magnesium hydroxides are white precipitates that do not dissolve in excess sodium hydroxide. Iron(III) forms a brown precipitate.

4. Which metal ion will produce a blue precipitate when sodium hydroxide solution is added to its aqueous solution? A) Fe³⁺ B) Fe²⁺ C) Cu²⁺ D) Al³⁺

Answer

C) Cu²⁺ Copper(II) ions (Cu²⁺) react with sodium hydroxide to form a blue precipitate of copper(II) hydroxide, Cu(OH)₂. Fe³⁺ gives brown, Fe²⁺ gives green, and Al³⁺ gives a white precipitate.

This difference in colour allows for their distinction.

A) The solution contains copper(II) ions. B) The solution contains iron(III) ions. C) The solution contains iron(II) ions. D) The solution contains aluminium ions.

Answer

C) The solution contains iron(II) ions. A green precipitate formed with sodium hydroxide solution is characteristic of iron(II) ions (Fe²⁺), which form iron(II) hydroxide (Fe(OH)₂). Copper(II) forms blue, iron(III) forms brown, and aluminium forms white.

7. Which pair of metal ions can be differentiated by observing the precipitate formed with sodium hydroxide, followed by adding excess sodium hydroxide? A) Copper(II) and Iron(II) B) Aluminium and Magnesium C) Iron(II) and Iron(III) D) Calcium and Copper(II)

B) Aluminium and Magnesium Both aluminium and magnesium ions form white precipitates with sodium hydroxide. The other pairs produce differently coloured precipitates initially, so excess NaOH isn't needed for primary distinction.

C) Fe(OH)₃Iron(III) ions (Fe³⁺) form a brown precipitate with sodium hydroxide, which is iron(III) hydroxide, Fe(OH)₃. Cu(OH)₂ is blue, Fe(OH)₂ is green, and Al(OH)₃ is white.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

Qualitative analysis, including precipitation tests, dates back centuries. Early chemists, often alchemists, used observations of colour changes and solid formation to distinguish substances. The systematic development of these tests accelerated in the 18th and 19th centuries with the advent of modern chemistry, becoming a cornerstone of laboratory education and chemical research before instrumental methods became widespread. The amphoteric nature of compounds like aluminium hydroxide was key to understanding complex acid-base chemistry.

⚖️ Ethics

Accurate identification of metal ions has ethical implications in environmental science and public health. Misidentifying toxic heavy metal ions in water supplies (e.g., lead, cadmium, mercury – though not directly in this spec point, the principle applies) could lead to serious health consequences. In industry, ensuring product purity through metal ion testing is crucial for consumer safety and avoids economic fraud. Reliable testing methods are a core component of responsible chemical practice.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond GCSE, the dissolution of amphoteric hydroxides like Al(OH)₃ in excess strong base involves the formation of complex ions. For aluminium, in excess hydroxide, the Al(OH)₃(s) reacts further to form the tetrahydroxoaluminate(III) ion, [Al(OH)₄]⁻(aq), which is soluble and colourless. This is a ligand exchange reaction where hydroxide ions act as ligands. Similarly, the solubility of calcium hydroxide is slightly higher than magnesium hydroxide due to different lattice energies and hydration enthalpies, explaining why Ca(OH)₂ (limewater) is used as a test for CO₂ (as calcium carbonate is less soluble) while Mg(OH)₂ (milk of magnesia) is insoluble enough to be a stomach antacid. Transition metal ions like Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺ have d-orbitals; the characteristic colours arise from electronic transitions within these d-orbitals upon absorption of specific wavelengths of visible light (crystal field theory). The slight difference in d-electron configuration between Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺ accounts for their distinct green and brown colours.

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

To perform these tests: Place approximately 2 cm³ of the unknown solution into a clean test tube. Using a dropping pipette, add sodium hydroxide solution dropwise to the test tube, observing any precipitate formation and its colour. If a white precipitate forms, continue adding sodium hydroxide solution in excess (another 1-2 cm³) and observe if the precipitate dissolves. Record all observations clearly. Safety Note: Sodium hydroxide is corrosive; wear eye protection (goggles) and avoid skin contact. Wash any spills immediately with plenty of water.

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) is corrosive. Causes severe skin burns and eye damage.
  • 🛑 Metal salt solutions may be irritant or harmful (e.g., copper salts are harmful, iron salts can be irritant).
  • 🛑 Wear eye protection (safety goggles) at all times.
  • 🛑 Avoid contact with skin. If contact occurs, wash thoroughly with water.
  • 🛑 If splashes occur in eyes, rinse immediately with plenty of water and seek medical attention.
  • 🛑 Handle all solutions with care, use dropping pipettes to prevent spills.

📐 Key Formulae

  • $$General precipitation: M^{n+}(aq) + nOH^{-}(aq) \rightarrow M(OH)_n(s)$$
  • $$Copper(II) hydroxide: Cu^{2+}(aq) + 2OH^{-}(aq) \rightarrow Cu(OH)_2(s)$$
  • $$Iron(II) hydroxide: Fe^{2+}(aq) + 2OH^{-}(aq) \rightarrow Fe(OH)_2(s)$$
  • $$Iron(III) hydroxide: Fe^{3+}(aq) + 3OH^{-}(aq) \rightarrow Fe(OH)_3(s)$$
  • $$Aluminium hydroxide (formation): Al^{3+}(aq) + 3OH^{-}(aq) \rightarrow Al(OH)_3(s)$$
  • $$Aluminium hydroxide (dissolution in excess NaOH): Al(OH)_3(s) + OH^{-}(aq) \rightarrow [Al(OH)_4]^{-}(aq) \text{ (Advanced, for deep dive)}$$

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. The primary 'mechanism' here is a simple precipitation reaction, where soluble metal cations (e.g., Cu²⁺(aq)) in solution combine with hydroxide anions (OH⁻(aq)) from the added sodium hydroxide solution.
  2. The product, the metal hydroxide (e.g., Cu(OH)₂(s)), has very low solubility in water, causing it to exceed its solubility limit and form a visible solid precipitate.
  3. For amphoteric hydroxides like aluminium hydroxide, the initial precipitation (Al³⁺(aq) + 3OH⁻(aq) → Al(OH)₃(s)) is followed by a redissolution in excess hydroxide: Al(OH)₃(s) + OH⁻(aq) → [Al(OH)₄]⁻(aq). This second step involves the hydroxide ion acting as a ligand, forming a soluble complex ion.

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Room temperature (standard laboratory conditions).
  • Aqueous solutions of metal ions and sodium hydroxide.
  • Dropwise addition initially, followed by excess addition for distinguishing white precipitates.
