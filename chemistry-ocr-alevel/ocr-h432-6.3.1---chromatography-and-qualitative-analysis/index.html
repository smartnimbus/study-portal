🧠 Conceptual Understanding

Chromatography and qualitative analysis are two fundamental pillars in analytical chemistry, essential for both separating mixtures and identifying specific chemical species. Chromatography, like Thin Layer Chromatography (TLC) and Gas Chromatography (GC), works on the principle of differential partitioning, where components of a mixture are separated based on their differing affinities for a stationary phase and a mobile phase. Think of it like a race where different molecules run at different speeds across a surface or through a column. Qualitative analysis, on the other hand, involves performing specific chemical tests to detect the presence of particular functional groups in an unknown organic compound, often yielding a characteristic visual change like a color change or precipitate formation. Together, these techniques allow chemists to unravel the composition and structure of chemical substances, from environmental pollutants to pharmaceutical products, providing vital information in many scientific fields.

📌 Core Theory

  • Thin Layer Chromatography (TLC) separates components based on their polarity and differential adsorption to a stationary phase (e.g., silica gel) and solubility in a mobile phase (solvent).
  • Rf value is calculated as the ratio of the distance travelled by the spot to the distance travelled by the solvent front, providing a characteristic value for a compound under specific conditions.
  • Gas Chromatography (GC) separates volatile components based on their boiling points and affinity for a stationary phase (liquid coated on a solid support) and a mobile phase (inert carrier gas).
  • In GC, retention time is the time taken for a component to pass through the column, used for identification, while peak area is proportional to the amount of the component, used for quantification.
  • Alkenes are identified by decolorizing bromine water (orange to colorless) via electrophilic addition.
  • Haloalkanes are identified by warming with aqueous silver nitrate in ethanol, forming a silver halide precipitate (AgCl white, AgBr cream, AgI yellow) via nucleophilic substitution.
  • Phenols are weakly acidic (react with NaOH but not carbonates) and give characteristic color reactions with FeCl₃, but the OCR syllabus focuses on their weak acidity compared to carboxylic acids.
  • Carbonyl compounds (aldehydes and ketones) react with 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazine (2,4-DNP) to form an orange/yellow precipitate, confirming the presence of a C=O group.
  • Aldehydes are further distinguished from ketones using Tollens' reagent (silver mirror) or Fehling's solution (blue to brick-red precipitate), as aldehydes are easily oxidised.
  • Primary and secondary alcohols, and aldehydes, can be oxidised by acidified potassium dichromate(VI) (orange to green), while tertiary alcohols are not.
  • Carboxylic acids are identified by their strong acidity, reacting with carbonates to produce effervescence (CO₂ gas).
  • Systematic application of these tests allows for the deductive identification of functional groups in an unknown compound.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

Chromatography relies on the principle that components of a mixture will distribute differently between a stationary phase and a mobile phase. In Thin Layer Chromatography (TLC), a polar stationary phase (often silica gel on a plate) and a non-polar mobile phase (solvent) are used. Polar compounds adhere more strongly to the polar stationary phase and travel shorter distances, while less polar compounds are more soluble in the mobile phase and travel further. The Rf value, calculated as the ratio of distance moved by the spot to the distance moved by the solvent front, helps identify compounds by comparing it to known values. Gas Chromatography (GC) separates volatile compounds by injecting them into a heated column. A carrier gas (mobile phase) pushes the vaporised sample through a column packed with a liquid stationary phase. Components with lower boiling points or weaker interactions with the stationary phase travel faster, resulting in shorter retention times. Detectors then measure the eluted compounds, producing a chromatogram where peak position (retention time) identifies the compound and peak area quantifies it. Qualitative analysis, on the other hand, involves a series of specific test-tube reactions. For instance, the C=C double bond in alkenes reacts with bromine water, breaking the double bond and causing the characteristic orange color of bromine to disappear. Haloalkanes undergo nucleophilic substitution with water, releasing halide ions that then precipitate with silver ions. Carbonyl compounds react with 2,4-DNP to form a solid derivative, while aldehydes specifically reduce Tollens' reagent to metallic silver (the silver mirror test) or Fehling's solution, as they are easily oxidised to carboxylic acids. Alcohols can be oxidised by acidified dichromate(VI), with primary and secondary alcohols forming aldehydes/ketones and then carboxylic acids (primary) or remaining ketones (secondary), causing the orange dichromate to turn green (Cr³⁺). Carboxylic acids, being stronger acids than phenols, readily react with carbonates to produce carbon dioxide gas. By combining observations from a systematic series of these tests, the functional groups present in an unknown organic compound can be reliably determined.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Calculating Rf Value from a TLC Chromatogram

Problem: A compound is run on a TLC plate. The spot travels 4.5 cm from the origin, and the solvent front travels 7.5 cm from the origin. Calculate the Rf value for this compound.

Steps: 1. Identify the distance travelled by the spot (d_spot). 2. Identify the distance travelled by the solvent front (d_solvent). 3. Apply the formula: Rf = d_spot / d_solvent. 4. Calculate the Rf value.

Answer: Rf = 0.60

Teacher's Tip

Rf values are always between 0 and 1, and have no units. Ensure you measure from the origin (baseline) for both distances.

2. Interpreting a Gas Chromatogram

Problem: A gas chromatogram shows two peaks. Peak A has a retention time of 2.1 minutes and an area of 120 units. Peak B has a retention time of 3.8 minutes and an area of 360 units. Describe what these results indicate about the sample.

Steps: 1. Interpret retention times for identification. 2. Interpret peak areas for relative quantities. 3. Draw conclusions about the sample's composition.

Answer: The sample contains two different components. Component A has a shorter retention time (2.1 min), suggesting it is either more volatile or interacts less strongly with the stationary phase. Component B has a longer retention time (3.8 min), suggesting it is less volatile or interacts more strongly. Since the area of Peak B (360 units) is three times that of Peak A (120 units), there is three times more of component B present in the mixture than component A.

Teacher's Tip

Remember that retention time is for 'what' and peak area is for 'how much'. Always state both aspects.

3. Systematic Identification of an Unknown Organic Compound

Problem: An unknown liquid compound 'X' is tested. It reacts with 2,4-DNP to form an orange precipitate, but does not give a silver mirror with Tollens' reagent. When warmed with acidified potassium dichromate(VI), the solution remains orange. Deduce the functional group(s) present in compound X.

Steps: 1. Interpret the 2,4-DNP test result. 2. Interpret the Tollens' reagent test result. 3. Interpret the acidified potassium dichromate(VI) test result. 4. Combine the deductions to identify the functional group.

Answer: The reaction with 2,4-DNP to form an orange precipitate indicates the presence of a carbonyl (C=O) functional group, meaning X is either an aldehyde or a ketone. The negative result with Tollens' reagent (no silver mirror) rules out an aldehyde. The observation that acidified potassium dichromate(VI) remains orange confirms that compound X is not an aldehyde or a primary/secondary alcohol. Therefore, compound X must be a ketone.

Teacher's Tip

Always consider the implications of both positive and negative results, systematically eliminating possibilities.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing Rf values with retention times; they are used in different chromatographic techniques and calculated differently.
  • ❌ Assuming a negative result means no functional group is present, rather than perhaps incorrect conditions or a very low concentration.
  • ❌ Not understanding that some tests (e.g., 2,4-DNP) are general for a class of compounds, while others (e.g., Tollens') are specific to a sub-class.
  • ❌ Misinterpreting the colour change for acidified dichromate(VI) or bromine water, or forgetting the initial colour of the reagent.
  • ❌ Failing to use ethanol as a co-solvent when testing for haloalkanes with aqueous silver nitrate, leading to slow or no reaction.

Q5. (3 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~3 min)

Explain the underlying principle of separation in Thin Layer Chromatography.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Thin Layer Chromatography (TLC) separates components based on their differential partitioning between a stationary phase and a mobile phase. The stationary phase, typically a polar material like silica gel, adsorbs components. The mobile phase, a solvent, moves up the plate by capillary action, carrying the components with it. Components that are more polar will interact more strongly with the polar stationary phase and travel shorter distances. Conversely, less polar components will be more soluble in the mobile phase, interacting less with the stationary phase, and thus travel further up the plate.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Separation is based on differential partitioning / differing affinities.
  • ✅ Between the stationary phase (e.g., polar silica gel) and the mobile phase (solvent).
  • ✅ Compounds with higher affinity for the mobile phase travel further, while those with higher affinity for the stationary phase travel less.

📋 Strategy: Explain scientific principle thoroughly.

⚠️ Examiner Note: Lack of detail on 'differential partitioning' or unclear explanation of how polarity affects movement.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: Use keywords like 'differential partitioning', 'adsorption', and 'solubility' when explaining the principle.

This allows them to react with both sodium hydroxide and carbonates, producing effervescence with the latter due to the formation of carbon dioxide gas.

In Gas Chromatography, how does the polarity of the stationary phase and the boiling point of the compounds influence retention time?

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: In Gas Chromatography, both boiling point and the interaction with the stationary phase influence retention time. Compounds with lower boiling points will vaporise more readily and spend more time in the gaseous mobile phase, leading to shorter retention times. Conversely, higher boiling point compounds spend more time in the stationary phase and have longer retention times. Additionally, the polarity of the stationary phase is crucial: if the stationary phase is polar, polar compounds will exhibit stronger intermolecular forces with it and spend more time adsorbed, resulting in longer retention times. If the stationary phase is non-polar, non-polar compounds will have a higher affinity for it, also leading to longer retention times.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Compounds with lower boiling points will vaporise more easily and spend more time in the mobile phase, thus having shorter retention times.
  • ✅ If the stationary phase is polar, polar compounds will have a higher affinity for it (longer retention time).
  • ✅ If the stationary phase is non-polar, non-polar compounds will have a higher affinity for it (longer retention time).

📋 Strategy: Integrate multiple factors influencing separation.

Q2. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

Discuss the advantages and disadvantages of using Thin Layer Chromatography (TLC) compared to Gas Chromatography (GC) for separating organic mixtures.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Thin Layer Chromatography (TLC) offers several advantages: it is a simple, inexpensive, and relatively quick technique, providing a visual result. It is particularly useful for separating non-volatile or heat-sensitive compounds that might decompose in GC. Multiple samples can also be run simultaneously on one plate. However, TLC has disadvantages: it offers lower separation power and sensitivity compared to GC, making it less suitable for highly complex mixtures or precise quantitative analysis. Gas Chromatography (GC), conversely, boasts high separation power and sensitivity, making it excellent for complex mixtures and trace analysis. It also allows for quantitative analysis based on peak areas. The disadvantages of GC include its higher cost and the requirement for samples to be volatile (or derivatised to become volatile); heat-sensitive compounds may decompose during the heating process, and typically only one sample can be run at a time.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ TLC Advantages: Simple, inexpensive, quick, visual result, can be used for non-volatile/heat-sensitive compounds, allows multiple samples simultaneously.
  • ✅ TLC Disadvantages: Less quantitative, less sensitive, lower separation power than GC, difficult for very complex mixtures.
  • ✅ GC Advantages: High separation power, quantitative analysis possible (peak area), very sensitive, works well for volatile compounds.
  • ✅ GC Disadvantages: Requires volatile samples (or derivatisation), expensive equipment, samples can decompose at high temperatures, usually only one sample at a time.

📋 Strategy: Structure answer with clear points for advantages and disadvantages of both techniques.

⚠️ Examiner Note: Students often only list features of each technique without explicitly comparing them or discussing both advantages and disadvantages.

Q4. (4 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~4 min)

Describe how an Rf value is calculated and explain its significance in identifying compounds in Thin Layer Chromatography. What factors must be kept constant for Rf values to be comparable?

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: An Rf value is calculated as the ratio of the distance travelled by the centre of the spot from the origin to the distance travelled by the solvent front from the origin. The formula is: Rf = distance travelled by spot⁄distance travelled by solvent front.

The significance of the Rf value is that it is a characteristic value for a particular compound under a specific set of chromatographic conditions. It allows for the identification of unknown compounds by comparing their Rf values to those of known reference compounds run on the same chromatogram.

For Rf values to be comparable and reliable, several factors must be kept constant, including: the type and thickness of the stationary phase, the composition of the mobile phase (solvent system), the temperature at which the chromatography is performed, and ideally, the amount of sample applied.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Calculation: Rf = distance travelled by spot / distance travelled by solvent front.
  • ✅ Significance: Provides a characteristic value for a compound under specific conditions, aiding in identification by comparison to known values.
  • ✅ Factors to keep constant: Stationary phase (type and thickness), mobile phase (solvent composition), temperature, amount of sample applied.

📋 Strategy: Define, explain significance, and list controlling factors.

⚠️ Examiner Note: Students often forget to mention the need for constant conditions for comparability, or are vague about what 'specific conditions' means.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: Emphasize 'characteristic' and 'under specific conditions' when discussing significance. The factors for comparability are often overlooked.

Q6. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

An unknown organic compound gives an orange precipitate with 2,4-DNP. When heated with acidified potassium dichromate(VI), the solution turns green. However, it does not give a silver mirror with Tollens' reagent. Identify the functional group(s) present in the compound and write the structural formula of a possible compound. Explain your reasoning.

✏️ Answer: 1. 2,4-DNP positive: The formation of an orange precipitate with 2,4-DNP indicates the presence of a carbonyl (C=O) functional group, meaning the compound is either an aldehyde or a ketone. 2. Tollens' negative: The absence of a silver mirror with Tollens' reagent rules out an aldehyde, meaning the compound is likely a ketone. 3. Since it is a ketone, it would not be oxidised further by dichromate under these conditions (unless forcing, which isn't implied). However, this test is also positive for primary and secondary alcohols. The combination of a positive 2,4-DNP test and a positive dichromate test suggests that the original unknown compound was a secondary alcohol, which was first oxidised to a ketone by the dichromate, and that ketone then reacted with 2,4-DNP. A possible compound is Propan-2-ol.

Reasoning: If the original compound was a secondary alcohol, it would be oxidised by acidified dichromate(VI) to a ketone (e.g., propan-2-ol to propanone), causing the orange dichromate to turn green. The resulting ketone would then give a positive test with 2,4-DNP (as all carbonyls do) but a negative test with Tollens' reagent (as ketones do not reduce Tollens' reagent).

  • ✅ Deduction: The compound is a secondary alcohol. (Could be oxidised to a ketone, which then reacts with 2,4-DNP).
  • ✅ Possible compound: e.g., Propan-2-ol or Butan-2-ol.

Q7. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

Design an experimental procedure using qualitative tests to confidently distinguish between benzene, cyclohexene, and cyclohexanol. Include all reagents, conditions, and expected observations.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Here is a systematic procedure to distinguish between benzene, cyclohexene, and cyclohexanol:

  1. Test 1: Bromine Water (or Bromine in CCl₄)

    • Reagent: Aqueous bromine water (orange/yellow).
    • Procedure: Add a few drops of bromine water to separate samples of each compound at room temperature.
    • Expected Observations:
      • Cyclohexene: The orange/yellow bromine water will rapidly decolorize, turning colourless. This is due to the electrophilic addition reaction across the C=C double bond.
      • Benzene: No immediate reaction; the bromine water will remain orange/yellow.
      • Cyclohexanol: No reaction; the bromine water will remain orange/yellow.
    • Deduction: This test positively identifies cyclohexene.

  2. Test 2: Acidified Potassium Dichromate(VI)

    • Reagent: Acidified potassium dichromate(VI) solution (orange).
    • Procedure: Take samples of the two remaining compounds (benzene and cyclohexanol). Add a few drops of acidified potassium dichromate(VI) solution to each and warm gently in a water bath.
    • Expected Observations:
      • Cyclohexanol: The orange dichromate solution will turn green. This is because cyclohexanol, a secondary alcohol, is oxidised to cyclohexanone, reducing the orange Cr₂O₇²⁻ ions to green Cr³⁺ ions.
      • Benzene: No reaction; the orange dichromate solution will remain orange.
    • Deduction: This test positively identifies cyclohexanol.

  3. Test 3: Benzene (by elimination)

    • The compound that gave negative results in both the bromine water test and the acidified dichromate(VI) test is benzene. Benzene is unreactive towards simple qualitative tests due to its delocalised π-electron system and would only react with bromine under UV light (free radical substitution) or with a halogen carrier (electrophilic substitution), not with aqueous bromine water at room temperature.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Test 1: Add aqueous bromine water (or Br₂/CCl₄).
  • ✅ Observation: Cyclohexene will rapidly decolorize bromine water (orange to colorless) due to electrophilic addition. Benzene and cyclohexanol will show no reaction (or very slow reaction for benzene in light). This identifies cyclohexene.
  • ✅ Test 2: Take the remaining two (benzene and cyclohexanol). Add acidified potassium dichromate(VI) and warm.
  • ✅ Observation: Cyclohexanol will cause the orange dichromate solution to turn green (oxidation of secondary alcohol). Benzene will show no reaction. This identifies cyclohexanol.
  • ✅ Test 3: The remaining compound is benzene. No specific simple test, but confirmed by elimination and negative results in previous tests. (Can also confirm with combustion, or more advanced spectroscopy).
  • ✅ Ensure all reagents, conditions (e.g., warming), and full observations (initial and final colours) are provided for each step.

📋 Strategy: Systematic elimination, use specific tests, provide full observations and chemical explanations.

⚠️ Examiner Note: Students often fail to correctly distinguish benzene's lack of reactivity under these conditions or confuse it with alkene reactivity.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This is a comprehensive question requiring knowledge of alkenes, alcohols, and aromatic compounds. Emphasize the distinct reactivity of benzene compared to cyclohexene.

Q8. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

An unknown mixture contains three liquid organic compounds, A, B, and C. A sample of the mixture is run on a Gas Chromatograph. The chromatogram shows three peaks with retention times of 1.2 min, 3.5 min, and 6.8 min. The areas under the peaks are 150, 450, and 300 arbitrary units respectively. The column used has a non-polar stationary phase. Deduce which compound (A, B, or C) is most volatile and which is most polar, providing reasoning for both deductions.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Assuming the peaks correspond to compounds A, B, and C in order of retention time:

  1. Most Volatile Compound: The compound with the shortest retention time (1.2 min, Compound A) is the most volatile. This is because more volatile compounds have lower boiling points and higher vapour pressures, meaning they spend more time in the gaseous mobile phase and less time condensed in the stationary phase. Consequently, they pass through the column more quickly.

  2. Most Polar Compound: The column has a non-polar stationary phase. In GC, compounds with higher affinity for the stationary phase will have longer retention times. Therefore, the compound with the longest retention time (6.8 min, Compound C) is the most non-polar as it interacts most strongly with the non-polar stationary phase. Conversely, the compound with the shortest retention time (1.2 min, Compound A) is the most polar, as it has the lowest affinity for the non-polar stationary phase and is therefore eluted earliest.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Most volatile: Compound with retention time 1.2 min (Compound A, assuming peaks are in order).
  • ✅ Reasoning: More volatile compounds spend less time in the stationary phase and more time in the mobile phase, thus eluting faster and having shorter retention times.
  • ✅ Most polar: Compound with retention time 6.8 min (Compound C).
  • ✅ Reasoning: A non-polar stationary phase will have stronger interactions with non-polar compounds. Therefore, the compound with the longest retention time (6.8 min) will be the most non-polar, having the highest affinity for the non-polar stationary phase. Conversely, the compound with the shortest retention time (1.2 min) will be the most polar, having the lowest affinity for the non-polar stationary phase and thus eluting fastest.

📋 Strategy: Break down into volatility and polarity. Carefully consider how compound polarity interacts with stationary phase polarity.

⚠️ Examiner Note: A common mistake is assuming that 'most polar' means longest retention time regardless of stationary phase polarity. Careful reading of 'non-polar stationary phase' is key.

👩‍🏫 Teacher's Note: This question tests the ability to integrate knowledge of retention time, volatility, and stationary phase polarity. It's crucial to correctly link polarity of compound to affinity for a non-polar stationary phase.

🧩 Quick Quiz (MCQ)

1. A student performs thin-layer chromatography (TLC) on a mixture of compounds. Compound X travels 4.5 cm from the origin, while the solvent front travels 7.5 cm from the origin. What is the Rf value for Compound X? A) A) 0.60 B) B) 1.67 C) C) 0.30 D) D) 0.75

Answer

A) A) 0.60 The Rf value is calculated by dividing the distance travelled by the spot by the distance travelled by the solvent front. So, 4.5 cm / 7.5 cm = 0.60.

2. In a gas chromatogram, what does a longer retention time for a compound primarily indicate? A) A) The compound has a higher boiling point and/or stronger interactions with the stationary phase. B) B) The compound has a lower molecular mass, allowing it to elute faster. C) C) The compound is more volatile than others in the mixture. D) D) The compound is present in a larger quantity in the mixture.

Answer

A) A) The compound has a higher boiling point and/or stronger interactions with the stationary phase. Retention time is influenced by a compound's volatility (related to boiling point) and its affinity for the stationary phase. Compounds with higher boiling points or stronger interactions with the stationary phase will spend more time in the column and thus have longer retention times.

3. A gas chromatogram shows three peaks with areas of 20 arbitrary units, 30 arbitrary units, and 50 arbitrary units respectively. What is the approximate percentage proportion of the component corresponding to the peak with 30 arbitrary units? A) A) 20% B) B) 30% C) C) 50% D) D) 75%

Answer

B) B) 30% The relative amounts of components in a mixture are proportional to their peak areas in a gas chromatogram. The total peak area is 20 + 30 + 50 = 100 units. The proportion of the component with 30 units is (30 / 100) * 100% = 30%.

4. Which reagent would be used to distinguish between an alkene and an alkane, and what would be the observed result for the alkene? A) A) Acidified potassium dichromate(VI), turns green. B) B) Aqueous bromine, colour changes from orange-brown to colourless. C) C) Tollens' reagent, silver mirror forms. D) D) 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazine (2,4-DNP), forms an orange precipitate.

Answer

B) B) Aqueous bromine, colour changes from orange-brown to colourless. Alkenes contain a C=C double bond which reacts with aqueous bromine in an addition reaction, causing the orange-brown colour of the bromine water to disappear (decolourise). Alkanes do not react.

5. To test for a haloalkane, aqueous silver nitrate in ethanol is used. Which observation would confirm the presence of a bromoalkane? A) A) A white precipitate of AgCl forms immediately. B) B) A cream precipitate of AgBr forms slowly over time. C) C) A yellow precipitate of AgI forms immediately. D) D) No precipitate forms, indicating a chloroalkane.

Answer

B) B) A cream precipitate of AgBr forms slowly over time. Bromoalkanes react with aqueous silver nitrate to form a cream precipitate of silver bromide (AgBr). This reaction is typically slower than for iodoalkanes but faster than for chloroalkanes.

6. A student has an unknown compound that they suspect is either a carboxylic acid or a phenol. Which test would definitively distinguish between these two functional groups? A) A) Adding bromine water, looking for decolourisation. B) B) Adding universal indicator, observing a pH of around 7 for both. C) C) Adding aqueous sodium carbonate, observing effervescence for the carboxylic acid. D) D) Heating with acidified potassium dichromate(VI), looking for a colour change from orange to green.

Answer

C) C) Adding aqueous sodium carbonate, observing effervescence for the carboxylic acid. Carboxylic acids are strong enough acids to react with carbonates, producing carbon dioxide gas (effervescence). Phenols are weaker acids and do not react with carbonates.

7. Which reagent is used to confirm the presence of a carbonyl group (aldehyde or ketone) in an unknown organic compound? A) A) Fehling's solution B) B) Tollens' reagent C) C) Acidified potassium dichromate(VI) D) D) 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazine (2,4-DNP)

Answer

D) D) 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazine (2,4-DNP) 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazine (2,4-DNP) reacts with both aldehydes and ketones to form an orange or yellow precipitate, confirming the presence of a carbonyl group.

8. After performing the 2,4-DNP test, a student wants to determine if the carbonyl compound is an aldehyde or a ketone. Which additional test would be appropriate? A) A) Adding aqueous bromine and observing decolourisation. B) B) Adding acidified potassium dichromate(VI) and warming, looking for an orange to green colour change. C) C) Adding aqueous sodium carbonate and looking for effervescence. D) D) Adding Tollens' reagent and warming, looking for a silver mirror.

Answer

D) D) Adding Tollens' reagent and warming, looking for a silver mirror. Tollens' reagent specifically oxidises aldehydes (forming a silver mirror) but does not react with ketones, thus distinguishing between them.

9. A student tests an unknown alcohol with acidified potassium dichromate(VI) and warms the mixture. The solution remains orange. What can be concluded about the alcohol? A) A) It is a primary alcohol. B) B) It is a secondary alcohol. C) C) It is a tertiary alcohol. D) D) It is an aldehyde.

Answer

C) C) It is a tertiary alcohol. Primary and secondary alcohols can be oxidised by acidified dichromate(VI), causing the orange Cr2O72- to be reduced to green Cr3+. Tertiary alcohols cannot be oxidised under these conditions, so the solution remains orange.

10. Chromatography separates components based on their differential partitioning between which two phases? A) A) Solid and liquid B) B) Mobile and stationary C) C) Aqueous and organic D) D) Polar and non-polar

Answer

B) B) Mobile and stationary All chromatographic techniques rely on the separation of components of a mixture based on their differing affinities for a stationary phase and a mobile phase.

Measure the distance from the baseline to the centre of the spot. Then measure the distance from the baseline to the solvent front. Divide the spot's distance by the solvent front's distance to get the Rf value.

4. What is retention time in Gas Chromatography (GC)?

Tags: GC, Chromatography, Retention Time

TLC uses a liquid mobile phase and a solid stationary phase to separate non-volatile compounds, while GC uses an inert gas mobile phase and a liquid or solid stationary phase for volatile compounds. TLC separates based on polarity, GC based on boiling point and affinity for stationary phase.

Carboxylic acids are stronger acids than phenols, so they react with carbonates (CO3^2-) to produce carbon dioxide gas. Phenols are weaker acids and are not acidic enough to react with carbonates, thus no gas is produced.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

Chromatography was first invented in 1900 by the Russian botanist Mikhail Tsvet, who used it to separate plant pigments (chlorophylls and carotenoids) by passing them through a column of calcium carbonate, hence the name 'chromatography' (from Greek 'chroma' meaning colour and 'graphy' meaning writing). Gas chromatography was developed much later in the mid-20th century, revolutionising analytical chemistry by providing highly efficient separations for volatile organic compounds. The qualitative tests for organic functional groups also have a rich history, evolving through empirical observation and mechanistic understanding over decades of organic chemistry research.

⚖️ Ethics

The applications of chromatography and qualitative analysis, particularly in fields like drug testing, forensics, and environmental monitoring, raise several ethical considerations. For instance, the accuracy and reliability of analytical results are paramount; false positives or negatives in drug tests can have severe consequences for individuals. Ensuring proper chain of custody for samples, validating analytical methods, and interpreting results within their statistical limits are ethical necessities. In environmental analysis, accurate detection of pollutants informs policy and public health decisions, carrying significant ethical weight regarding corporate responsibility and public welfare.

🚀 Advanced Theory

Beyond A-level, chromatography expands significantly. High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) uses high pressure to force solvents through much finer stationary phases, offering superior separation for non-volatile or thermally unstable compounds. Chiral chromatography is a specialised technique that separates enantiomers (mirror-image isomers) using a chiral stationary phase, critical in pharmaceutical industries where different enantiomers can have vastly different biological activities. Further quantitative analysis in GC often involves internal standards and calibration curves to achieve highly accurate concentration measurements, accounting for variations in injection volume or detector response. Reaction mechanisms for qualitative tests can also be explored in greater detail, such as the full mechanism for 2,4-DNP derivative formation or the detailed redox half-equations for dichromate oxidation under varying pH conditions.

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

Thin Layer Chromatography (TLC): Spot sample on baseline of a TLC plate (silica gel), place in a developing tank with solvent (mobile phase), allow solvent to run up, remove, dry, and visualise spots (e.g., with UV light or iodine vapour). Measure distances to calculate Rf. Qualitative Functional Group Tests: Typically performed in test tubes. Add specified reagents under given conditions (e.g., warming in a water bath), and observe any colour changes, precipitate formation, or gas evolution. Crucial safety measures include using a fume cupboard for volatile or toxic reagents (e.g., bromine, 2,4-DNP) and handling acids/bases carefully.

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Bromine water: Corrosive, toxic by inhalation and skin contact. Use in a fume cupboard, wear gloves.
  • 🛑 2,4-Dinitrophenylhydrazine (2,4-DNP): May be toxic, irritant. Wear gloves and eye protection.
  • 🛑 Tollens' reagent: Silver nitrate is corrosive. Ammoniacal solutions can form explosive silver fulminate on standing; must be prepared fresh and disposed of immediately.
  • 🛑 Acidified potassium dichromate(VI): Strong oxidising agent, corrosive, toxic, suspected carcinogen. Use in a fume cupboard, wear gloves and eye protection.
  • 🛑 Silver nitrate: Corrosive, stains skin. Wear gloves and eye protection.
  • 🛑 Ethanol: Flammable liquid. Keep away from ignition sources.
  • 🛑 Organic solvents (e.g., hexane, ethyl acetate): Flammable, can be irritants or toxic. Use in a fume cupboard, wear gloves.
  • 🛑 Carboxylic acids: Corrosive, irritant. Wear gloves and eye protection.

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. Alkenes + Bromine Water: Electrophilic addition. The electron-rich C=C double bond attacks the partially positive end of a polarised bromine molecule, forming a carbocation intermediate, followed by attack of the bromide ion. The π bond is broken and two new σ bonds are formed.
  2. Haloalkanes + Aqueous Silver Nitrate (in ethanol): Nucleophilic substitution. Water (or ethanol) acts as a nucleophile, attacking the carbon atom bonded to the halogen, leading to the departure of the halide ion. The halide ion then precipitates with Ag⁺ ions.
  3. Carbonyls + 2,4-DNP: Nucleophilic addition-elimination (condensation). The nitrogen atom of 2,4-DNP acts as a nucleophile, attacking the electrophilic carbon of the carbonyl group. This is followed by elimination of a water molecule to form a 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazone derivative (an imine).
  4. Aldehydes + Tollens' Reagent: Redox reaction. The aldehyde is oxidised to a carboxylic acid (or carboxylate ion in alkaline conditions), while the Ag⁺ ions in Tollens' reagent are reduced to metallic silver (Ag(s)).
  5. Alcohols/Aldehydes + Acidified Dichromate(VI): Redox reaction. Primary alcohols are oxidised to aldehydes, then to carboxylic acids. Secondary alcohols are oxidised to ketones. Aldehydes are oxidised to carboxylic acids. In these reactions, the orange dichromate(VI) ions (Cr₂O₇²⁻) are reduced to green chromium(III) ions (Cr³⁺).
  6. Carboxylic Acids + Carbonates: Acid-base reaction. The carboxylic acid donates a proton to the carbonate ion, forming carbon dioxide gas and water, along with the carboxylate salt. (2RCOOH + CO₃²⁻ → 2RCOO⁻ + H₂O + CO₂).

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Alkenes + Bromine water: Room temperature.
  • Haloalkanes + Aqueous Silver Nitrate in ethanol: Warm gently (e.g., in a water bath at 50°C).
  • Carbonyls + 2,4-DNP: Room temperature, often in acidic solution.
  • Aldehydes + Tollens' Reagent: Warm gently (e.g., in a water bath at 60-70°C).
  • Alcohols/Aldehydes + Acidified Potassium Dichromate(VI): Warm (e.g., reflux for full oxidation of primary alcohols to carboxylic acids, gentle warming for distinguishing tests). Acidified (using dilute sulfuric acid).
  • Carboxylic Acids + Sodium Carbonate: Room temperature.

🔦 Element Spotlight

While not an element, the Carbonyl Group (C=O) is central to several qualitative tests. It consists of a carbon atom double-bonded to an oxygen atom. This double bond makes the carbon atom electrophilic (electron-deficient) due to the higher electronegativity of oxygen, making it susceptible to nucleophilic attack. The presence of the C=O group is detected by 2,4-DNP. Its specific reactivity (or lack thereof) distinguishes aldehydes from ketones, as the aldehyde C-H bond allows for further oxidation (e.g., with Tollens' or Fehling's), which is not present in ketones.
