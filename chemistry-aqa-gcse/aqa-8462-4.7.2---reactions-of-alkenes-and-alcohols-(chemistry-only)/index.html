🤜 In a Nutshell

🧠 Conceptual Understanding

Today, we're going to explore the unique reactivity of two important homologous series: alkenes and alcohols. Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons, meaning they contain at least one carbon-carbon double bond. This double bond makes them much more reactive than alkanes, allowing them to undergo addition reactions where atoms are added across the double bond. Alcohols, on the other hand, are organic compounds containing the hydroxyl (-OH) functional group. This group gives alcohols distinctive chemical properties, including their ability to burn, be oxidised, and react in specific ways. Understanding these reactions is crucial for comprehending organic synthesis and the production of many industrial chemicals and everyday materials.

📌 Core Theory

  • Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons containing at least one carbon-carbon double bond (C=C).
  • The general formula for alkenes with one double bond is C_{n}H_{2n}.
  • Alkenes undergo addition reactions, where atoms add across the C=C double bond, breaking it and forming a single bond.
  • Examples of addition reactions include hydrogenation (addition of H2), halogenation (addition of X2), and hydration (addition of H2O).
  • Hydrogenation of alkenes forms alkanes; it requires a nickel catalyst and high temperature/pressure.
  • Halogenation of alkenes forms dihaloalkanes; it occurs readily at room temperature and is used as a test for unsaturation (e.g., bromine water decolourises).
  • Hydration of alkenes forms alcohols; it requires steam (H2O(g)) and phosphoric(V) acid catalyst (H3PO4) at high temperature and pressure.
  • Alkenes can also undergo addition polymerisation, where many alkene monomers join together to form a long-chain polymer.
  • Alcohols are organic compounds containing the hydroxyl (-OH) functional group.
  • The general formula for straight-chain alcohols is C_{n}H_{2n+1}OH.
  • Alcohols burn in excess oxygen to produce carbon dioxide and water (complete combustion).
  • Alcohols can be oxidised to form carboxylic acids, typically using an oxidising agent like acidified potassium dichromate(VI) (turns from orange to green).
  • Primary alcohols are oxidised first to aldehydes and then to carboxylic acids.
  • Alcohols can be dehydrated to form alkenes, requiring a hot catalyst like aluminium oxide (Al2O3) or concentrated sulfuric acid.

🔬 Detailed Explanation

The unsaturated nature of alkenes, due to the presence of a carbon-carbon double bond, makes them highly reactive. This double bond consists of a sigma (σ) bond and a pi (π) bond, with the pi bond being weaker and more accessible to attack. In addition reactions, the pi bond breaks, allowing two new single bonds to form with incoming atoms or groups. For instance, in hydrogenation, hydrogen atoms add across the double bond to saturate the molecule, converting an alkene into an alkane. Halogenation, such as with bromine water, is a visible test for unsaturation because the orange-brown bromine water rapidly decolourises as bromine adds across the double bond. Alcohols, characterised by their -OH group, exhibit different reactivity. They undergo combustion, releasing energy, making them useful as fuels. Oxidation of primary alcohols is a key reaction, converting them first to aldehydes and then to carboxylic acids, often seen in the brewing industry where ethanol is oxidised to ethanoic acid (vinegar). Dehydration of alcohols, the reverse of alkene hydration, is a useful method for synthesising alkenes by removing a water molecule.

📝 Worked Examples

1. Identifying an Alkene using Bromine Water

Problem: A student is given two unknown liquid hydrocarbons, A and B. When a few drops of orange-brown bromine water are added to liquid A, the colour quickly disappears. When added to liquid B, the colour remains orange-brown. Explain these observations and identify which liquid is an alkene.

Steps: 1. Recall the test for unsaturation: alkenes decolourise bromine water. 2. Relate the observation for liquid A: the bromine water decolourised, indicating liquid A is unsaturated. 3. Relate the observation for liquid B: the bromine water remained orange-brown, indicating liquid B is saturated. 4. Conclude that liquid A is an alkene, and liquid B is likely an alkane.

Answer: Liquid A caused the orange-brown bromine water to decolourise, indicating it is an alkene because alkenes undergo an addition reaction with bromine, breaking the double bond. Liquid B did not decolourise the bromine water, meaning it is a saturated hydrocarbon, likely an alkane.

Teacher's Tip

Remember that the decolourisation of bromine water is a classic test for the presence of a C=C double bond, characteristic of alkenes.

2. Forming an Alcohol from an Alkene

Problem: Describe the industrial process for converting ethene into ethanol, including the reactants, conditions, and type of reaction.

Steps: 1. Identify the starting alkene: ethene. 2. Identify the desired product: ethanol (an alcohol). 3. Recall the reaction that converts an alkene to an alcohol: hydration. 4. State the reactants required for hydration: ethene and steam (water in gaseous form). 5. Recall the specific conditions for industrial hydration: a catalyst (phosphoric(V) acid, H3PO4), high temperature (300°C), and high pressure (60-70 atm). 6. Describe the reaction type as an addition reaction.

Answer: Ethene is converted to ethanol through an industrial hydration reaction. Ethene is reacted with steam (gaseous water) at 300°C and 60-70 atmospheres pressure, in the presence of a phosphoric(V) acid (H3PO4) catalyst. This is an addition reaction where the water molecule adds across the double bond of ethene.

Teacher's Tip

Always specify 'steam' for industrial hydration, not just 'water', as it indicates the correct physical state and temperature conditions.

3. Oxidation of Ethanol

Problem: Ethanol can be oxidised to form ethanoic acid. State the observations you would make during this reaction using acidified potassium dichromate(VI) and explain the role of the dichromate.

Steps: 1. Identify the reactant: ethanol. 2. Identify the product: ethanoic acid. 3. Identify the oxidising agent: acidified potassium dichromate(VI). 4. Recall the colour change of potassium dichromate(VI) upon reduction: orange to green. 5. Explain that the dichromate(VI) acts as an oxidising agent, itself being reduced in the process.

Answer: During the oxidation of ethanol to ethanoic acid using acidified potassium dichromate(VI), the observed colour change would be from orange to green. The acidified potassium dichromate(VI) acts as an oxidising agent, which means it causes the ethanol to lose electrons (be oxidised) while it itself gains electrons (is reduced), leading to the colour change from orange (dichromate(VI) ions) to green (chromium(III) ions).

Teacher's Tip

Remember that oxidation and reduction always occur simultaneously. If one substance is oxidised, another must be reduced.

🌍 Real World Applications

  • Margarine Production (Hydrogenation of Alkenes): Vegetable oils contain unsaturated fatty acids (with C=C bonds). Hydrogenation converts these into saturated fats, which are solid at room temperature, forming margarine.
  • Ethanol Production (Hydration of Ethene): Ethanol, used as a fuel, solvent, and in alcoholic beverages, can be produced industrially by reacting ethene with steam in the presence of a phosphoric(V) acid catalyst.
  • Plastic Manufacturing (Addition Polymerisation): Many common plastics, such as poly(ethene) and poly(propene), are formed by the addition polymerisation of alkene monomers.
  • Vinegar Production (Oxidation of Ethanol): Ethanol in alcoholic drinks can be oxidised by air or bacteria to ethanoic acid (acetic acid), which is the main component of vinegar.
  • Antiseptics and Disinfectants (Alcohols): Alcohols like ethanol and propan-2-ol are widely used as antiseptics and disinfectants due to their ability to denature proteins and kill microorganisms.

Common Misconceptions
  • ❌ Confusing addition reactions (alkenes) with substitution reactions (alkanes). Alkenes have a double bond that breaks to allow addition, alkanes only have single bonds and require substitution.
  • ❌ Incorrectly stating that alcohols undergo addition reactions. Alcohols generally do not have double bonds, so they do not undergo addition reactions. Their reactions involve the -OH group or C-C/C-H bonds (e.g., combustion).
  • ❌ Assuming all alcohols are oxidised to carboxylic acids in one step. Primary alcohols oxidise via aldehydes, while secondary alcohols oxidise to ketones (not covered in GCSE, but useful to know it's not always carboxylic acids). Tertiary alcohols are resistant to oxidation.
  • ❌ Forgetting the specific conditions required for industrial reactions (e.g., catalyst, temperature, pressure).
  • ❌ Mixing up the test for unsaturation (bromine water) with other chemical tests.
Memory Anchor

Think of ALKENES as having 'two left feet' (the double bond) so they are always 'adding' things to become more stable. ALCOHOLS are like an 'old friend' (-OH) that can be 'oxidised' (gets rusty/changes) or 'dehydrated' (loses water).

Q3. (5 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~5 min)

Explain why the industrial production of poly(ethene) from ethene is a more environmentally sustainable process than if it were formed via a condensation reaction, assuming both polymers have similar properties. (Note: Only consider the reaction itself, not the source of ethene.)

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: Addition polymerisation, such as the formation of poly(ethene) from ethene, is generally more environmentally sustainable in terms of the reaction process itself than a condensation reaction would be. In addition polymerisation, the alkene monomers simply add together across their double bonds to form the polymer, with no other atoms or molecules being lost. This means that 100% of the atoms from the starting materials are incorporated into the final polymer, leading to a high atom economy and no undesirable by-products. In contrast, condensation polymerisation reactions always produce a small molecule, such as water or hydrogen chloride, as a by-product. These by-products constitute waste that needs to be disposed of or treated, increasing the environmental footprint of the process. Therefore, for processes yielding similar materials, addition polymerisation is often favoured for its inherent lower waste generation.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Addition polymerisation (of ethene) produces only the polymer.
  • ✅ No small by-products (e.g., water, HCl) are formed.
  • ✅ Condensation reactions produce a small molecule as a by-product.
  • ✅ By-products are waste/require disposal/treatment.
  • ✅ Less waste means more atom economy / more environmentally friendly process.
  • ✅ Therefore, addition polymerisation is more sustainable in terms of waste generation.

For prop-1-ene, this would mean the hydrogen from water adds to the first carbon, and the -OH group adds to the second carbon, primarily forming propan-2-ol as the major product. Selectively forming propan-1-ol as the major product through direct hydration of prop-1-ene is challenging because it goes against the typical regioselectivity of addition reactions. Achieving this would usually require different, more sophisticated catalysts or a multi-step synthesis route, rather than a simple direct hydration under standard GCSE conditions which would favour propan-2-ol.

  • ✅ In theory, two possible products: propan-1-ol and propan-2-ol.

Q7. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

Describe the main steps involved in the industrial production of poly(propene) from crude oil fractions, including any necessary intermediate steps and reaction conditions.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: The industrial production of poly(propene) begins with crude oil. First, crude oil undergoes fractional distillation to separate it into different fractions, including naphtha. Naphtha is a mixture of longer-chain hydrocarbons, which are often alkanes. To obtain propene, the naphtha fraction undergoes cracking. This involves heating the long-chain alkanes to high temperatures (e.g., 400-900°C) with a catalyst or to very high temperatures (700-1000°C) without a catalyst, breaking them down into smaller, more useful molecules, including alkenes like propene. Finally, these propene monomers are subjected to addition polymerisation. This involves reacting many propene molecules under specific conditions of high temperature, high pressure, and in the presence of an initiator or catalyst, causing the carbon-carbon double bonds in each propene molecule to break and link together to form long poly(propene) chains.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Fractional distillation of crude oil to obtain naphtha fraction.
  • ✅ Cracking of naphtha to produce smaller, more reactive alkene molecules like propene.
  • ✅ Description of cracking conditions (high temperature/catalyst).
  • ✅ Propene monomers undergo addition polymerisation.
  • ✅ Conditions for addition polymerisation (high temperature, high pressure, catalyst/initiator).
  • ✅ Propene units link to form long poly(propene) chains.

Q8. (6 Marks | Long Answer | ⏱️ ~6 min)

An alcohol, C4H9OH, can exist as different structural isomers. When one specific isomer of C4H9OH is dehydrated, it produces only but-1-ene. When another isomer is oxidised using acidified potassium dichromate(VI), it forms a ketone (butanone). Identify these two specific isomers of C4H9OH and justify your choices.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: The general formula C4H9OH indicates an isomer of butanol. If one isomer produces only but-1-ene upon dehydration, this suggests the hydroxyl (-OH) group was located at the end of the carbon chain (a primary alcohol), as dehydration would remove water from C1 and C2, forming the double bond between C1 and C2. Thus, this isomer is butan-1-ol. If another isomer forms a ketone (butanone) upon oxidation with acidified potassium dichromate(VI), this signifies that it is a secondary alcohol. In a secondary alcohol, the carbon atom bonded to the -OH group is also bonded to two other carbon atoms. For a four-carbon chain, this means the -OH group is on the second carbon, which would be butan-2-ol. Primary alcohols oxidise to carboxylic acids (via aldehydes), and tertiary alcohols do not readily oxidise, supporting the identification of butan-2-ol as the isomer forming a ketone.

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ C4H9OH is butanol.
  • ✅ Dehydration to but-1-ene implies the -OH group was on C1 (primary alcohol).
  • ✅ Therefore, the first isomer is butan-1-ol (CH3CH2CH2CH2OH).
  • ✅ Oxidation to a ketone implies it is a secondary alcohol.
  • ✅ A secondary alcohol with 4 carbons is butan-2-ol (CH3CH(OH)CH2CH3).
  • ✅ Justification for butan-1-ol: Primary alcohols dehydrate to form alkenes, and if it only forms but-1-ene, the -OH must be at the end.
  • ✅ Justification for butan-2-ol: Secondary alcohols oxidise to ketones.

Q9. (3 Marks | Calculation | ⏱️ ~3 min)

Suggest a complete balanced symbol equation for the complete combustion of propan-1-ol.

✅ Answer & Mark Scheme

✏️ Answer: 2C3H7OH + 9O2 → 6CO2 + 8H2O

🔢 Working: 1. Write the unbalanced equation: C3H7OH + O2 → CO2 + H2O₂. Balance Carbon atoms: 3C on left, so 3CO2 on right: C3H7OH + O2 → 3CO2 + H2O₃. Balance Hydrogen atoms: 8H on left (7+1), so 4H2O on right: C3H7OH + O2 → 3CO2 + 4H2O₄. Balance Oxygen atoms: Left: 1 (from alcohol) + 2x (from O2). Right: (3x2) + (4x1) = 6 + 4 = 10. So 1 + 2x = 10 => 2x = 9 => x = 4.5. So C3H7OH + 4.5O2 → 3CO2 + 4H2O₅. Multiply by 2 to get whole numbers: 2C3H7OH + 9O2 → 6CO2 + 8H2O

📋 Mark Scheme:

  • ✅ Correct reactants: C3H7OH + O2
  • ✅ Correct products: CO2 + H2O⁻ ✅ Correctly balanced equation: 2C3H7OH + 9O2 → 6CO2 + 8H2O

Key Facts
  • 📌 Alkenes (CnH2n, C=C double bond) undergo addition reactions (hydrogenation, halogenation, hydration) due to their unsaturation.
  • 📌 Alcohols (CnH2n+1OH, -OH group) undergo combustion, oxidation (to carboxylic acids), and dehydration (to alkenes).
  • 📌 Bromine water decolourises with alkenes (addition reaction) but not with alkanes or alcohols (test for unsaturation).
Red Flags
  • 🚩 Don't confuse addition reactions of alkenes with substitution reactions of alkanes. The double bond breaking is key.
  • 🚩 Remember the specific conditions (catalyst, T, P) for industrial reactions like hydration of ethene or hydrogenation.
  • 🚩 Oxidation of alcohols produces carboxylic acids (for primary alcohols); observe orange to green colour change of acidified dichromate.
Main Takeaway

Alkenes and alcohols are fundamental organic compounds with distinct reactivities dictated by their functional groups. Understanding their characteristic addition (for alkenes) and transformation (for alcohols) reactions is vital for comprehending organic synthesis and distinguishing between different classes of organic compounds.

🔍 Deep Dive (Extension)

📜 History

The study of alkenes and alcohols has deep roots in organic chemistry. Ethene was first isolated in the late 18th century, and its 'olefiant gas' nature (oil-forming gas) led to the term 'olefin' for alkenes. Ethanol has been known and produced by fermentation for millennia. Major breakthroughs in understanding their structures and reactions came in the 19th and early 20th centuries with the development of organic synthesis and industrial chemistry. The discovery of catalysts like nickel for hydrogenation (Nobel Prize to Sabatier and Grignard in 1912) revolutionised the production of margarine and other products. The industrial synthesis of ethanol from ethene became a cornerstone of the petrochemical industry.

⚖️ Ethics

The production and use of alkenes and alcohols raise several ethical considerations. The reliance on crude oil for ethene production, and subsequently poly(ethene), brings issues of resource depletion, environmental damage from extraction, and carbon emissions. The use of biofuels like ethanol, while renewable, can lead to 'food vs. fuel' dilemmas, where crops are grown for energy instead of sustenance. The disposal of plastics (polymers from alkenes) also poses significant environmental challenges, including landfill burden and microplastic pollution.

🚀 Advanced Theory

At a more advanced level, the addition reactions of alkenes can be understood in terms of electrophilic addition mechanisms, where the electron-rich double bond is attacked by an electron-deficient species (an electrophile). For example, in the reaction with HBr, the H+ acts as the electrophile. This mechanism helps explain regioselectivity (Markovnikov's rule, where the major product is formed by the more stable carbocation intermediate) and stereoselectivity. Alcohol oxidation pathways are also more complex, involving different intermediates (aldehydes, ketones) depending on the alcohol's substitution pattern (primary, secondary, tertiary) and the strength of the oxidising agent. The 'chemistry only' aspect at GCSE often hints at these underlying, more complex principles.

🔬 Science: Lab & Formulae

🧪 Required Practical

For the dehydration of ethanol to ethene: ethanol is soaked onto ceramic wool and heated gently. Aluminium oxide (Al2O3) or broken pot pieces act as a catalyst and are heated strongly in the same test tube. Ethene gas is produced and collected over water via a delivery tube. Safety: Ethene is flammable, ensure good ventilation and no naked flames nearby. Ethanol is also flammable. Ensure the delivery tube is removed from water before heating stops to prevent 'suck-back'.

⚠️ Safety Hazards

  • 🛑 Flammability: Alkenes and alcohols (e.g., ethene, ethanol) are highly flammable. Keep away from naked flames and sources of ignition. Use in a well-ventilated area.
  • 🛑 Irritants/Corrosives: Bromine water can be an irritant. Concentrated sulfuric acid and phosphoric(V) acid are corrosive. Wear eye protection and gloves. Handle with care.
  • 🛑 Toxic fumes: Ensure good ventilation when carrying out any reactions involving organic compounds to avoid inhaling harmful fumes.
  • 🛑 High pressure/temperature: Industrial reactions often use high pressures and temperatures, requiring specialised equipment and strict safety protocols to prevent explosions or leaks.

⚗️ Chemistry: Reactions

🔄 Reaction Mechanisms

  1. Addition Reactions (Alkenes): The carbon-carbon double bond in alkenes is a region of high electron density. When an alkene reacts with a molecule like hydrogen (H2), a halogen (X2), or water (H2O), the pi bond of the double bond breaks. The atoms from the reacting molecule then add across the two carbon atoms that were part of the double bond, converting it into a single bond and forming a saturated product.
  2. Oxidation of Primary Alcohols: Primary alcohols (R-CH2-OH) are oxidised in two stages. First, the alcohol is oxidised to an aldehyde (R-CHO) by the removal of two hydrogen atoms (one from the -OH group and one from the carbon it's attached to). If heating under reflux with excess oxidising agent, the aldehyde is then further oxidised by the addition of an oxygen atom (from the oxidising agent) to form a carboxylic acid (R-COOH).
  3. Dehydration of Alcohols: In the presence of a hot catalyst (like aluminium oxide or concentrated sulfuric acid), alcohols undergo an elimination reaction. A hydrogen atom from a carbon adjacent to the -OH group, along with the -OH group itself, are removed from the alcohol molecule as a molecule of water (H2O). This removal leads to the formation of a carbon-carbon double bond, thus forming an alkene.

🌡️ Reaction Conditions

  • Hydrogenation of Alkenes: Reactant: H2 gas. Conditions: Nickel catalyst, 150°C, moderate pressure.
  • Halogenation of Alkenes (e.g., with Bromine): Reactant: Aqueous halogen (e.g., bromine water). Conditions: Room temperature, no catalyst (spontaneous).
  • Hydration of Alkenes (Industrial): Reactant: Steam (H2O(g)). Conditions: Phosphoric(V) acid (H3PO4) catalyst, 300°C, 60-70 atmospheres pressure.
  • Combustion of Alcohols: Reactant: O2. Conditions: Ignition (e.g., flame).
  • Oxidation of Primary Alcohols to Carboxylic Acids: Reagent: Acidified potassium dichromate(VI) solution. Conditions: Heat under reflux (to ensure complete oxidation to carboxylic acid).
  • Dehydration of Alcohols: Catalyst: Hot aluminium oxide (Al2O3) or concentrated sulfuric acid. Conditions: Strong heating (e.g., 170°C).

🔦 Element Spotlight

Ethanol (C2H5OH) is a key compound in this topic, being both an alcohol and a common product of alkene hydration. Its structure consists of a two-carbon chain, with three hydrogen atoms on the first carbon, two hydrogen atoms on the second carbon, and a hydroxyl (-OH) group attached to the second carbon. Ethanol is a clear, colourless liquid at room temperature with a distinctive smell. Its physical properties are influenced by the hydroxyl group, which allows for hydrogen bonding, leading to a relatively higher boiling point than alkanes of similar size. Chemically, ethanol is highly flammable, burning with a clean flame to produce CO2 and H2O. It can be oxidised to ethanoic acid, and it can be dehydrated to form ethene. It is widely used as a solvent, a fuel, and in alcoholic beverages.
